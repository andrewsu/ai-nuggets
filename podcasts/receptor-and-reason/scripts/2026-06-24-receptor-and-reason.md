We start with the MapLight ML-004 IRIS readout. ML-004 is a selective 5-HT1B and 5-HT1D receptor agonist that MapLight has been advancing for social communication deficits in autism spectrum disorder. The IRIS study was a Phase 2 double-blind, placebo-controlled, twelve-week trial in a hundred-and-sixty-one participants — a hundred-and-two adolescents and fifty-nine adults — with a primary endpoint of change from baseline on the caregiver-reported Autism Behavioral Inventory social-communication-domain score at week twelve. They missed it. The drug did not separate from placebo on the primary. The piece they're pivoting on is a pre-specified analysis in adolescents with moderate-to-severe baseline irritability — defined as Aberrant Behavior Checklist irritability subscale at sixteen or higher — where the care-partner-reported ABC-irritability score showed a between-group effect size of one-point-three-three with a nominal p of point oh-one-three, and the clinician-rated CGI-improvement in that same subgroup showed an effect size of one-point-oh-eight with a nominal p of point oh-three-six. The safety profile is the other thing worth noting — generally well-tolerated, no serious adverse events on active treatment, no extrapyramidal signs, and lower mean weight gain than placebo, which is the right side of the antipsychotic comparator question. The framing matters. If you read this as a failed social-communication trial, the program is in trouble — and the company is heading into an End-of-Phase-2 meeting with the FDA precisely because the primary failure constrains what they can claim. If you read it as a positive irritability signal in a specific adolescent subgroup, ML-004 starts looking like a more selective alternative to aripiprazole and risperidone for ASD-associated irritability, with a cleaner side-effect profile. The intellectually honest version is that the trial was overpowered to find a social-communication signal it never delivered, and the irritability subgroup is a hypothesis-generating finding that has to be confirmed in a new prospective trial designed against ABC-irritability as the primary endpoint — which the FDA will require. The broader lesson here is that selective 5-HT-1B-and-1D agonism is showing up as a mechanism that may modulate irritability circuits without the metabolic and motor liabilities of the dopamine-blocker class, and that is a path worth keeping an eye on even if the social-communication endpoint stays out of reach.

Onto the preclinical side and one of the more important PD mechanism papers of the year so far — a Schnitzer-lab preprint that uses dual-color two-photon calcium imaging in the six-hydroxydopamine mouse model to ask what amantadine is actually doing in the dorsolateral striatum. The classical model — direct-pathway SPNs promote movement, indirect-pathway SPNs suppress it, an imbalance produces hypo- or hyperkinesia — predicts that effective therapies should rebalance dSPN and iSPN activity. L-DOPA fits the model. Amantadine has always been a weird outlier — it improves both hypokinesia in Parkinson's and L-DOPA-induced dyskinesias, and the dual rescue is hard to square with a single balance correction. They simultaneously monitored dSPN and iSPN dynamics in healthy, parkinsonian, and dyskinetic states, and separated two analyses — pathway balance, and action-specific ensemble activity. The clean result is that L-DOPA rescues the dSPN/iSPN imbalance in hypokinetic conditions but fails to restore the locomotion-coding ensemble. Amantadine does the opposite — it rescues the locomotion ensemble without normalizing pathway balance. In the dyskinetic state, forelimb dyskinesias correspond to a distinct ensemble — not the locomotion ensemble — and amantadine selectively suppresses the resting activity of that dyskinesia ensemble while leaving locomotion-coding cells alone. The big picture is that the classical pathway-balance model is the wrong lens for amantadine, and probably the wrong lens for anti-dyskinetic pharmacology in general — what matters is which action-coding ensembles fire when. Practically, this suggests two non-overlapping pharmacological strategies — pathway-balance correction for hypokinesia, and ensemble-specific suppression for dyskinesia — and explains why amantadine alone or layered onto L-DOPA does work that neither does on its own. For drug discovery the question becomes what receptor targets selectively modulate dyskinesia ensembles versus locomotion ensembles — amantadine is a weak NMDA antagonist plus a mild dopamine releaser, and neither pharmacology obviously predicts ensemble-specific action, so the upstream mechanism is now the open question.

Staying in the striatum but on the cholinergic side — a Surmeier-lab paper on how Parkinson's disrupts the cholinergic-interneuron control of intrastriatal GABAergic circuitry. The setup is that cholinergic interneurons sit at about one percent of striatal neurons but are pacemakers and key gain controllers; they have been studied mostly for their direct muscarinic effects on spiny projection neurons. This paper looks at the indirect arm — ChIs activate nicotinic acetylcholine receptors on local GABAergic interneurons, those GIs then deliver GABA-A currents onto SPNs in both direct and indirect pathways. In healthy mice, optogenetic ChI stimulation produced robust nAChR-driven GABA-A currents in SPNs, with simulations suggesting state-dependent control of SPN dendritic integration modulated by concurrent muscarinic signaling. In prodromal and overt parkinsonian models, that whole arm collapses — because the GABAergic interneurons themselves downregulate nicotinic AChRs. It's not a ChI failure. It's a downstream receptor adaptation that breaks the circuit. The clinical implication is that nicotinic-receptor agonists or positive allosteric modulators targeted to the relevant nAChR subunit on striatal GIs could restore the lost gain control even without rescuing dopaminergic neurons. It's also a possible explanation for the long-noted observation that some nicotinic pharmacology improves motor symptoms in PD — the protective effect may run through GI nAChRs, not directly through ChIs or SPNs. And because the disruption shows up in prodromal mice — before overt motor symptoms — these GI nAChRs are candidate early-biomarker and early-intervention substrates.

Now a paper that takes a developmental angle on Huntington's. Huntington's disease is autosomal dominant — a CAG expansion in HTT — but the clinical phenotype usually doesn't manifest until midlife, even though every cell in the patient has been carrying the expansion since conception. There's been mounting evidence of a neurodevelopmental component — postmortem and imaging studies suggesting altered striatal volumes and connectivity decades before motor onset — but actual human developing-HD tissue has been almost impossible to get. This preprint reports single-cell RNA sequencing on one HD-positive fetal striatal sample with an age- and sex-matched control. They confirmed the CAG-repeat expansion, identified two-thousand-and-thirty-two differentially expressed genes across nine cellular clusters, and ran enrichment against publicly available adult HD postmortem datasets. The signal is that key biological processes are disrupted across the developing HD striatum in a cluster-specific way, and there is meaningful overlap between fetal and adult HD transcriptional signatures. The take is straightforward — molecular HD pathology is present during early human striatal development. The therapeutic implication, if this replicates with additional samples, is that HTT-lowering and CAG-targeting approaches like tominersen or branaplam-style splicing modulators may need to be considered not only as adult disease-modification but as much-earlier interventions, including potentially prenatally in families with known expansions. The n-of-one caveat is real, and the field will need additional fetal samples and matched controls to firm this up, but the direction is consistent with the imaging literature and is a useful constraint on HD development timelines.

Onto a tour-de-force paper on exercise and tau. A multi-organ spatial metabolomic atlas of long-term exercise in wild-type mice — six organs, two-hundred-and-twenty-four metabolic features, coordinated inter-organ remodeling. The brain showed the most pronounced region-specific adaptation, which motivated the second arm — applying the same spatial-metabolomic pipeline to PS19 tauopathy mice. Exercise reduced more than seventy percent of observable tau pathology in PS19 hippocampus, and restored the mitochondrial-related brain metabolome. Then they integrated brain proteomics with the metabolomics and identified NADH dehydrogenase Complex I as the convergent node — the single axis where exercise effects in WT and the rescue effects in PS19 both lived. The kicker is the biology test. They expressed yeast Ndi1 — a single-subunit NADH dehydrogenase that can stand in for Complex I — in PS19 neurons via AAV, in mice that did not exercise. Ndi1 expression alone increased cerebral antioxidants, restored shuttle-linked metabolites including malate-aspartate components, and reduced tau pathology — recapitulating the anti-tau effects of exercise. Three things this matters for. First, the human-cohort literature linking exercise to delayed cognitive decline has a molecular substrate now, and it lives at neuronal Complex I rather than at peripheral metabolism. Second, the field has been searching for exercise mimetics for decades; this work nominates Complex I augmentation as a specific, drug-tractable axis. Third, the malate-aspartate shuttle and Complex I module being sufficient suggests that small-molecule mitochondrial cofactors — NAD precursors, MAS-component modulators — should be re-evaluated for tauopathies under the constraint that the effect must be cell-autonomous in neurons rather than systemic.

Pivoting to AD genetics — a paper from the Matthews group at Imperial on the common ABCA7 rs3752231 risk variant. ABCA7 has been one of the most reproducibly replicated late-onset AD risk loci, and rs3752231 is a common coding variant, but the mechanism by which it raises risk has been opaque. They combined quantitative neuropathology of 4G8-immunostained mid-temporal gyrus from ninety-nine donors spanning the Braak spectrum, with glial-enriched single-nucleus RNA sequencing from fifty-four of those donors. Carriers of the risk variant had higher overall amyloid-beta burden and larger plaques in late AD — but more interestingly, the shift was almost entirely from compact plaques toward diffuse plaques. That phenotype is consistent with impaired microglial plaque maturation; microglia compact diffuse plaques into the dense, neurotoxic-but-contained form, and ABCA7 carriers seem to do that less well. The transcriptional response to increasing amyloid burden was largely genotype-specific. Non-carriers showed canonical disease-associated microglial activation — complement, phagocytic, inflammatory pathways — alongside astrocyte responses consistent with preserved synaptic support. Carriers showed a distinguishable activation state with carrier-specific ligand-receptor signaling suggesting non-cell-autonomous suppression of microglial phagocytosis. The therapeutic nominations are concrete — microglial TREM2 activation and CD33 inhibition, both of which already have programs running, and astrocytic EAAT2 induction. The bigger point is the methodological one. ABCA7 is the kind of common variant that, in genome-wide association studies, looks like noise — small effect size, ambiguous direction. Pairing quantitative neuropathology with genotype-stratified glial single-nucleus transcriptomics in a hundred-donor cohort turns it into a tractable molecular phenotype that maps cleanly onto existing immune-modulating drug programs. That's a useful template for the dozens of other common AD risk variants whose mechanisms remain unresolved.

Onto a piece of chemical biology that goes the other direction — instead of inhibiting misfolding-related pathways, can you destroy the misfolded proteins directly. The compound is ADLumin-5, a self-photosensitizing chemiluminescence agent. The idea — molecularly produced light. ADLumin-5 emits light through chemiluminescence and that emission excites a co-located photosensitizer in the same molecule, generating reactive oxygen species that oxidize whatever the compound is bound to. They show in vitro photodegradation of amyloid-beta, tau, alpha-synuclein, and TDP-43, validated by LC-MS, MALDI-MS, and Western blot. For amyloid-beta they also show that the photo-oxidation reduces oligomer toxicity. The in vivo arm — 5xFAD mice, four months of treatment, longitudinal molecular imaging shows reduced amyloid-beta accumulation. The chemistry is the point. Conventional photodynamic therapy requires an external light source, which is a non-starter for brain targets that sit centimeters below the skull. Chemiluminescence-driven photosensitization means the light is generated where the compound is, so anatomy stops being the rate-limiter. Two open questions for the field. Selectivity — what makes ADLumin-5 preferentially bind misfolded over native forms of these proteins, and how stringent is that preference at therapeutic concentrations? And specificity of damage — ROS generated near a misfolded protein will oxidize nearby lipids and other proteins too, so the safety margin depends on how much native material is in the immediate environment. The fact that the same molecule images and treats — they call it a photo-theranostic — is a nice operational feature, but the mechanism is novel enough that the safety story will need work before it leaves rodents.

Now a translationally interesting circuit paper on feeding. Food intake is governed by two interacting drives — homeostatic hunger to meet caloric needs, and hedonic drive to consume palatable food beyond caloric need. The hedonic substrate has been hard to pin down. This Kravitz and Creed collaboration nominates ventral-pallidal GABAergic neurons as the population. The headline experiments — optogenetic stimulation of VP-GABA neurons drove robust consumption of high-fat diet and palatable liquids but not regular chow. Those neurons express few hunger-hormone receptors, are not activated by ghrelin or by fasting, and single-cell calcium imaging shows stronger engagement during long versus short feeding bouts — consistent with control over palatability-linked bout duration. Closed-loop optogenetics confirmed the bout-duration effect. Ablating VP-GABA neurons via taCasp3 reduced palatable-liquid intake and blocked high-fat-diet-induced obesity without impairing chow intake. The clinical context matters. GLP-1 and dual-incretin agonists like semaglutide and tirzepatide are reshaping obesity treatment by suppressing homeostatic hunger and gastric emptying. They work — but a fraction of patients respond modestly because their overeating is hedonically driven rather than homeostatically driven, and that fraction has been hard to identify and treat. A circuit identified here as preferentially hedonic, with a viable ablation phenotype that spares chow intake, is the kind of substrate that could rationalize a next generation of anti-obesity therapeutics targeting the hedonic axis specifically — and could explain a chunk of the GLP-1 nonresponse population. The translation path is non-trivial because VP is hard to target with small molecules, but identifying surface markers on VP-GABA neurons would open chemogenetic and biologic-targeting approaches.

On the AI side, an honest benchmark for single-cell foundation models — VCBench. The single-cell field has been collecting foundation models — Geneformer, scGPT, UCE, TranscriptFormer, Arc State — and positioning them as virtual cells. The benchmarking has been fragmented and largely single-task, which makes it easy to publish a model that wins on something narrow while failing on most everything else. VCBench synthesizes four independent virtual-cell frameworks into seven capability dimensions — perturbation response prediction, cross-species universality, gene regulatory network inference, modality integration, temporal dynamics, multi-scale integration, and in silico experimentation. Of those seven, five are operationally testable under current architectures and datasets; multi-scale integration and in silico experimentation are structurally untestable as end-to-end tasks. They pre-register linear and nearest-neighbor baselines against the five testable axes. The headline finding is uncomfortable for the field. On four of five testable dimensions — perturbation prediction, cross-species transfer, GRN inference, temporal ordering — linear and nearest-neighbor baselines match or beat every foundation model tested. The one exception is TranscriptFormer on cross-modal RNA-to-protein prediction — a fifty-three-percent Pearson improvement over the strongest baseline, the only model to reach Level 2 on the pre-registered Virtual Cell rubric, but with a documented contamination caveat and a spectral collapse that wrecks its temporal-ordering performance. They also flag that no foundation model in the comparison publishes a complete cell-level training manifest, which makes data contamination impossible for downstream users to detect. The pre-registered Contamination Reporting Schema they release is the methodological contribution that ought to outlast the specific benchmark. The interpretation is not that scaled-up models are useless for single-cell — it's that the current crop has not earned the virtual-cell framing, and the burden of proof has been on the wrong side of the table.

We close on a piece of computational chemistry with a clean methods story — SIMBA, a transformer-based model from the Bittremieux group that infers structural similarity directly from tandem mass spectra. The problem it addresses is real and persistent. In untargeted metabolomics, you observe an MS2 spectrum, you want to know the underlying small molecule, and the standard play is spectral library search. If the molecule is in the library, you're done. If it isn't — and most environmental, microbial, and natural-product metabolites aren't — you're stuck on so-called spectral similarity scores that map only weakly to actual structural similarity. SIMBA reframes the problem. Instead of computing spectral similarity and hoping it correlates with structure, it learns to predict two interpretable graph-based structural distances — maximum common edge subgraph and substructure edit distance — directly from spectrum pairs. The model retrieves structurally closer analogs than existing methods and the distances are interpretable as chemistry, not as opaque embedding distances. The use case is drug-discovery-adjacent analog discovery — you have a hit, you want to know what natural-product analogs and microbial congeners exist in your metabolomics data, and you want a structure-aware notion of nearness rather than the spectral-only one. This is the kind of small, focused methods paper that quietly changes how a community does its day-to-day work — and the interpretable-distance framing is the right move for a regulated, hypothesis-driven discovery workflow.
