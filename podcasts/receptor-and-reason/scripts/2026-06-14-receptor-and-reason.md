## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason for Sunday, June 14th, 2026. Eleven items today, with an unusually deep drug-discovery slant. We open with a mechanism-based therapy preprint for Rett syndrome that may be the most consequential rare-disease preprint of the month, then move through a string of receptor and transporter pharmacology items — citrate transporters, BMP signaling in Parkinson's, an MSH3 strategy for Huntington's, and a clean structural-pharmacology read on RXFP1 — into circuit-level work on cannabidiol, alcohol, and reward in the accumbens. We close with three computational items: a mechanistic propofol simulator, a sober head-to-head of AlphaFold three against physics-based docking, and an agentic-AI system that designs molecular dynamics pipelines.

The opener comes from Nick Tonks's group at Cold Spring Harbor. Tonks is the scientist who originally discovered PTP1B in 1988, and his lab has spent decades arguing that this phosphatase, best known as an insulin-signaling brake, is a tractable drug target. The new preprint extends that story into neurodevelopmental disease. In female mouse models of Rett syndrome, the group evaluated a selective allosteric small-molecule PTP1B inhibitor — note allosteric, not the active-site warheads that doomed earlier PTP1B drug programs — and shows brain distribution in the regions that matter for Rett, which is to say the cerebellum and brainstem nuclei controlling motor coordination and cardio-respiratory rhythm. Pharmacological dosing produces robust improvement on muscle weakness, motor and coordination deficits, and the cardiac and respiratory dysrhythmias that are the actual cause of Rett mortality. Crucially, genetic ablation of PTP1B in parallel produces concordant rescue that holds for over a year, which is most of a Rett mouse's life. Mechanistically the lab proposes that PTP1B inhibition disinhibits TRKB and insulin-leptin signaling, which is biologically sensible because MECP2 loss converges on neurotrophic deficits. The reason to take this seriously: Rett gene-therapy programs are progressing but face the usual AAV-payload constraints, and a brain-penetrant small molecule that addresses the same downstream signaling without genetic intervention is exactly the kind of complementary option the Rett community has been waiting for. Whether the durability seen here translates from inhibitor pharmacology to humans is the next question.

Staying on the rare-disease theme, item two is from Avner Schlessinger's group at Mount Sinai — a virtual-screening campaign against the citrate transporter NaCT, gene name SLC13A5. SLC13A5 epilepsy is a recessive neurodevelopmental condition that combines severe early-onset seizures with developmental delay; the protein moves citrate across membranes in liver and brain. Existing NaCT inhibitors are all substrate-mimetic, meaning they sit in the citrate-binding pocket and compete with the natural substrate — a chemotype that is intrinsically hard to make selective. The new paper does virtual docking of three and a half million ZINC compounds against a putative allosteric pocket at the NaCT dimer interface, takes the top fifty-four into a cell-based citrate-uptake assay, identifies three weak hits, and then through twenty-six analog optimizations gets to a six-compound series with low-double-digit micromolar potency. They confirm pocket relevance with deep-mutational-scanning data — the phenylalanine 362 mutant erases binding, exactly as the docking predicted. Twelve micromolar is not a drug, but it's a chemically distinct starting point that doesn't compete with citrate, and the dimer-interface pocket is now mapped well enough for medicinal-chemistry follow-on. For SLC13A5 epilepsy families this is the first promising allosteric handle on the target.

Third, a Parkinson's preprint from Claude Brodski's group at Ben-Gurion University that combines human genetics with mouse pharmacology in a way you rarely see in one paper. Step one: a polygenic risk score built from variants in the bone morphogenetic protein pathway is associated with PD risk at an odds ratio of one-point-two-one, replicates in proxy-case analyses at one-point-one-four, and survives sensitivity testing. Step two: genetic or pharmacological inhibition of BMP signaling in mice produces motor deficits and PD-like dopaminergic neuropathology, providing the functional bridge. Step three is the translational money shot — in an alpha-synuclein preformed-fibril mouse model, recombinant BMP5 and BMP7 administered alongside the fibrils protect dopaminergic neurons, and, crucially, BMP5/7 administered after motor symptoms appear is neurorestorative, improving both behavior and pathology. The neurorestorative claim in an alpha-synuclein fibril model is the part that should make you sit up. Most disease-modifying PD candidates of the last decade either failed to slow progression or worked only prophylactically. A growth-factor-pathway intervention that pulls back established pathology is exactly the kind of result that, if it replicates, reshapes the trial-design conversation away from prevention and toward rescue. The caveats are obvious — BMP biologics are pleiotropic, the mouse model is not human PD, and dose-finding for a CNS-delivered TGF-beta-family ligand is its own multi-year problem — but the conceptual case is strong.

That bridges to another disease-modifying neurodegeneration item, this one from Vanessa Wheeler's group at Mass General and Harvard Medical School. The somatic-expansion hypothesis of Huntington's disease has been ascending for several years now: the inherited CAG repeat keeps expanding in vulnerable neurons over the patient's lifetime, and the age of clinical onset correlates with how fast that expansion happens. MSH3, a mismatch-repair protein, is the genetically validated driver, and several companies — most notably LoQus23 — are advancing MSH3 modulators. The Wheeler preprint uses somatic CRISPR-Cas9 to knock out Msh3 in an HttQ-one-one-one knock-in line at three intervention ages — six, sixteen, and twenty-four weeks — that bracket increasing levels of pre-existing somatic expansion. All three timings slow further striatal CAG expansion, reduce nuclear huntingtin pathology, and rescue transcriptional dysregulation, with earlier intervention better than late. The bonus result that didn't get into the title: Msh3 knockout also suppresses the toxic exon-one HTT-one-a transcript, which is the spliced fragment thought to drive aggregate formation. The translational read is that an MSH3 therapeutic doesn't need to hit every neuron — which is good, because no CNS delivery vehicle does — and that even imperfect intervention timing can bend the trajectory. Whether the magnitude of the rescue is enough to translate to a meaningful clinical effect in a heterozygous adult human is the open question, but the preclinical case for MSH3 modulation is now considerably tighter.

Item five is a structural-pharmacology preprint from Andrew Kruse's lab at Harvard Med, with bigger implications than the title lets on. The receptor is RXFP1, the relaxin receptor, primarily a heart-failure and fibrosis target. The native ligand is the peptide hormone relaxin-2 — a serelaxin program ran for years and tanked in Phase 3 acute heart failure. The new drug-candidate of interest is A-Z-D 5-4-6-2, a small-molecule RXFP1 agonist from AstraZeneca that's in mid-stage trials. Kruse's group solved three cryo-EM structures — apo, relaxin-2-bound, and the AstraZeneca compound bound — and the divergent activation mechanism is striking. Relaxin-2 engages the ectodomain and induces a helical rearrangement of the linker that pushes signaling. The small molecule binds inside the seven-transmembrane bundle, doesn't touch the ectodomain at all, and stabilizes a uniquely active conformation that is specifically capable of recruiting beta-arrestin. So the protein and small-molecule agonists are not redundant — they engage RXFP1 at completely different sites and bias signaling differently. For the broader pharmacology audience this matters because RXFP1 and its sister relaxin receptors are members of a class whose activation logic has been hard to reconcile with the canonical class-A GPCR mechanisms, and for medicinal chemistry it matters because the transmembrane pocket is now structurally mapped well enough to rationally design next-generation small-molecule agonists with chosen signaling profiles. The wedge between an ectodomain-binding peptide and a TM-domain small molecule is the kind of result that legitimately reopens drug-design space.

Item six goes back to cannabinoids, from Andrea Hohmann's lab at Indiana University. The question is what mechanism cannabidiol actually uses to suppress chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathic pain — a major translational claim for CBD that has surprisingly thin mechanistic support. The model is paclitaxel-induced neuropathy in mice. Pharmacological dissection across four candidate receptors yields a clean answer: CB1 and CB2 antagonists don't block CBD's anti-allodynic effect, but PPAR-alpha and PPAR-gamma antagonists do. The cleaner experiment is genetic — NAPE-PLD knockouts lose CBD's analgesic effect entirely, both for the development phase and the maintenance phase of CIPN, while GPR55 knockouts retain it. NAPE-PLD is the enzyme that synthesizes anandamide and related N-acylethanolamines, so the proposed mechanism is that CBD doesn't directly bind PPARs; it shifts endogenous lipid signaling through NAPE-PLD to generate PPAR agonists in situ. This is a satisfying story because it explains why CBD's effects in pain models have been inconsistent across labs — if you're not measuring NAPE-PLD activity in the right tissue compartment, the effect won't appear. The clinical relevance is real: CIPN is one of the more common reasons cancer patients are dose-reduced or take an oncology drug holiday, and there is no approved disease-modifying therapy. A mechanistically coherent CBD positioning, focused on patients with intact NAPE-PLD activity, is exactly the kind of stratified-medicine argument that could rescue an indication where racemic CBD has so far underperformed.

Seventh, a striatal pharmacology item from Meredith Bauer and Stephen Boehm at Indiana University Indianapolis. The question is whether aversion-resistant drinking — which is the operational definition of compulsive alcohol use — recruits a specific glutamate-receptor program in the dorsomedial striatum. They use the quinine-adulterated alcohol paradigm to separate aversion-sensitive from aversion-resistant mice and combine western blot, ex vivo electrophysiology with the rectification-index readout, and pharmacological micro-injection. The story is internally coherent: in aversion-sensitive mice, dorsomedial-striatum GluA1 levels are negatively correlated with quinine-adulterated drinking, suggesting GluA1-containing AMPA receptors act as a brake. In aversion-resistant mice the relationship inverts — the GluA1-to-GluA2 ratio rises in both DMS and DLS, the rectification index in DMS spiny projection neurons jumps, and the only explanation is recruitment of calcium-permeable, GluA2-lacking AMPA receptors. Micro-injection of a CP-AMPAR antagonist into DMS increases drinking, confirming that DMS calcium-permeable AMPARs in this regime are anti-compulsion. The take is that the well-established DMS-to-DLS shift in alcohol use disorder isn't simply a transfer of behavioral control — it co-occurs with a specific synaptic remodel that places calcium-permeable receptors in the wrong place. Whether one could pharmacologically reverse the rectification phenotype with something like a derivative of IEM fourteen-sixty, the classic calcium-permeable AMPAR blocker is the obvious follow-up; CP-AMPAR antagonists have been kicked around for treatment-resistant alcoholism for fifteen years and this work gives the cleanest circuit rationale to date.

That pairs naturally with item eight, from Rosemary Bagot's group at McGill, in Neuropsychopharmacology. The question is whether the classic D1-versus-D2 medium-spiny-neuron opponent model — reward through D1, aversion through D2 — survives a mixed-valence conditioning task. Fiber photometry of D1 and D2 MSNs in nucleus accumbens medial shell, with cues paired to food reward, foot-shock, or no outcome. The headline finding is that both D1 and D2 MSNs show bidirectional valence responses to conditioned cues — they go up to reward-predicting cues, down to shock-predicting cues — but only in female mice. In males the picture is much more mixed. For unconditioned stimuli themselves, both populations activate to both reward and shock with response magnitudes scaling to stimulus intensity, which the authors interpret as salience encoding rather than valence. And D2 MSNs show enhanced responses to unsignaled outcomes, consistent with prediction-error signaling, while D1 MSNs do not. Three things to take from this. First, the strict D1-go-D2-no-go model of accumbens function is harder to defend with high-resolution photometry than it was with c-Fos. Second, the sex difference is large enough that pooling across sexes in NAc studies will obscure real biology, which has implications for how the addiction literature reads. Third, the dissociation between salience and valence within a single cell-type-defined population is the kind of finding that argues for circuit-specific rather than receptor-specific therapeutic targeting in disorders like anhedonia.

Item nine is a computational neuropharmacology paper from a Dartmouth-MIT collaboration including Anand Pathak, Earl Miller, and Richard Granger. The question is whether you can build a multiscale mechanistic brain model in which a single receptor — GABA-A — drives system-level anesthesia phenomena without fitting any parameters to anesthesia data. The model has explicit core-versus-matrix thalamocortical architecture, the distinction being that core thalamic neurons project topographically to cortical layer four while matrix neurons project diffusely to superficial layers. They turn the propofol knob — which is GABA-A positive allosteric modulation — and read out across scales. The simulation reproduces propofol effects on macaque auditory oddball, on anesthetized human functional connectivity, and on the selective attenuation of matrix-to-cortex projections that the clinical-imaging literature has reported. The novelty is what they call the simulation-first discovery — the model predicts a dose-dependent biomarker, elevated residual cortical activity between sensory stimuli, which they then go back into existing macaque data and find, but which had previously been overlooked. As a methodological move this is the right shape for the field: receptor-level modulation begets circuit-scale biomarker, validated by a held-out empirical dataset. The propofol-specific result will interest anesthesiologists, but the broader claim is that the same generative framework could be applied to ketamine on NMDA, to dexmedetomidine on alpha-two, or to muscarinic agonists in psychiatry — turning a receptor pharmacology into a predicted EEG or fMRI biomarker, prospectively.

Tenth, the AlphaFold3 ligand-discovery paper from Jiankun Lyu's group at Rockefeller, which is the most careful sober assessment we've had of whether AF3 actually works as a docking engine. Three experiments. First, retrospective enrichment over DUDE-Z decoys for forty-three drug targets, where AF3 beats DOCK3 — but they show this advantage is driven by ligand-only biases in computational decoy sets, which means it's an artifact of the benchmark, not a real superiority. Second, in three large experimental screens — sigma-2 receptor, D4 dopamine receptor, AmpC beta-lactamase, totalling over twenty-five hundred molecules — where DOCK3 actually achieves stronger overall enrichment, with AF3 contributing mainly to early enrichment, the top of the ranked list. Third, an out-of-sample pose-reproduction analysis on eight thousand complexes deposited after the AF3 training cutoff shows AF3's accuracy tracks training-set similarity, which means it is doing more memorization than principled molecular recognition. The prospective screen against sigma-2 is the closer: AF3 hit thirteen percent and identified a thirteen-nanomolar binder, but DOCK3 ran in parallel hit at twice the rate. The net take is the right one: AF3 is a complement to physics-based docking, particularly as a post-docking re-ranker, not a replacement. For everyone who has been told by a vendor that AF3 will replace their docking pipeline, this paper is the response.

Closing on the agentic-AI computational pharmacology item — MDForge, from Wang and colleagues on arXiv. The premise is that molecular dynamics pipeline design — choice of force field, water model, sampling protocol, free-energy estimator — currently requires a senior simulation expert and many iterations per system. MDForge wraps an LLM agent around the entire pipeline as open-ended code generation, with two innovations. First, instead of using predefined tools, the agent writes the code and gets graded by a multi-agent debate among synthetic physics experts that converts the sparse simulator feedback into dense verbal reward — basically using model-as-judge to densify the reward signal that real MD provides only at completion. Second, they evaluate on the three SAMPL host-guest binding free-energy benchmarks, which are the community-accepted hard-mode tests. MDForge designs pipelines competitive with human experts on the benchmarks, and the headline experimental result is that an unsupervised CB-seven host-guest screen using its pipeline predicted a novel binder that wet-lab NMR confirmed at picomolar affinity. That last point is the one that matters — agentic-AI tooling on top of MD has been mostly demos, and a prospective wet-lab confirmation is the bar for taking the methodology seriously. Note this is a borderline-case for a comp-pharm audience because the binding system is not a CNS receptor, but the methodology directly transfers to GPCR, kinase, and ion-channel campaigns, and it's an honest demonstration that agentic AI plus mechanistic simulation can beat human expertise on a defined optimization task.

That's our eleven for today. Two items I'd flag for closer follow-up: the PTP1B Rett-syndrome work, which if it holds will push allosteric phosphatase inhibitors into the rare-disease conversation in a way they have not been before, and the BMP-pathway PD work, where the neurorestorative claim, if it replicates, is the kind of finding the Parkinson's field has been waiting twenty years for.
