First up — and this is the lead because it's the kind of structural pharmacology you don't see every week — out of the Christopoulos and Thal groups at Monash, the cryo-EM structure of MK-97 bound to the M4 muscarinic acetylcholine receptor in complex with acetylcholine, at 2.7 angstroms. MK-97 is a second-generation M4 positive allosteric modulator, the lineage downstream of LY2033298 and the Merck program that produced the M4-selective compounds Karuna's xanomeline-trospium combination tried to validate the indirect way. The structure resolves the allosteric site at the extracellular vestibule and explains, residue by residue, why MK-97 closes the species-variability gap that has dogged this series — a leucine-to-tyrosine swap in the vestibule between rodent and human, and MK-97 reaches past it. They also map allosteric cooperativity with the orthosteric agonist and recapitulate the signaling profile in live cells. For a target this central to the new wave of non-D2 antipsychotics — schizophrenia is the obvious indication, but cognitive impairment in schizophrenia and Alzheimer's psychosis are both in play — having a high-resolution template for the second-generation chemistry is genuinely useful. It also gives medicinal chemists a starting point for biased PAM design, which is the next frontier after the M1-and-M4 selectivity story.

Item two stays in the transporter neighborhood but pivots from muscarinics to monoamines. New in Neuropsychopharmacology — and the title gives the thesis away — "It's not all DAT." A group using OCT3 knockout mice plus in vivo voltammetry shows that the organic cation transporter 3, which has been a wallflower next to DAT in the stimulant-pharmacology story for decades, is doing real work in mediating amphetamine's reinforcing effects and amphetamine-evoked dopamine release. Pharmacological inhibition of OCT3 selectively attenuates the reinforcement signal without flatly abolishing dopamine signaling — that's the molecule of interest here, because DAT-blocking strategies for cocaine and amphetamine use disorder keep crashing on the rocks of low therapeutic index. OCT3 has a low-affinity, high-capacity profile that may give you a wider window. The caveat is the usual one for OCT3 — it's expressed everywhere, including peripherally, and the selectivity case for CNS targeting is still aspirational. But the in vivo electrochemistry here is convincing.

Item three keeps the cocaine theme and brings in the kappa opioid system, which Alan, you've been tracking on the dynorphin side. From Wake Forest, a paper showing that kappa opioid receptor activation regulates cocaine's effects on nucleus accumbens dopamine specifically through phosphorylation of the dopamine transporter at threonine 53. So this is a phosphosite-resolved mechanism connecting KOR signaling to DAT trafficking — they show that blocking the Thr53 phosphorylation prevents the KOR-driven increase in DAT surface expression that normally follows cocaine, and that this phospho-event is necessary for the negative affective signature of cocaine withdrawal in their behavioral assay. The translational hook: KOR antagonists like aticaprant have been languishing in MDD trials, but the case for KOR antagonism in stimulant use disorder has always rested on the dynorphin-driven dysphoria model. A specific phosphosite handle on the DAT side of that is the kind of mechanism that lets you build a stratification biomarker into a trial.

Item four — and we're still in the allosteric modulator era apparently — is BMS-986122, a mu opioid receptor positive allosteric modulator, in rat models of neuropathic pain. The University of Michigan group asked whether BMS-986122 could boost the antinociceptive effect of low-dose opioids without bringing along the usual penalty package — constipation, reward, respiratory depression. In spared-nerve-injury and tibial-neuroma rats, BMS-986122 substantially potentiated low-dose morphine and oxycodone analgesia. The reward and respiratory readouts they have published earlier suggest the dissociation holds, but this specific paper doesn't re-litigate that; it's making the chronic neuropathic pain case. Worth flagging because the MOR-PAM concept has been a "twenty years away" project for the better part of twenty years, and BMS-986122 is the one that's actually generating clean preclinical pharmacology. Whether the rodent decoupling between analgesia and reward survives translation is the multibillion-dollar question. It hasn't yet.

Item five — and we're still in the opioid block — is the Duke paper on what they call a widespread internal brain state for fentanyl withdrawal. Multisite local field potential recordings across cortical and subcortical regions in rats, combined with interpretable machine learning, identify two distinct large-scale oscillatory signatures: a slowly-ramping high-beta and low-gamma network that tracks repeated fentanyl exposure, and a separate widespread network that emerges specifically during withdrawal and predicts withdrawal-driven drug-seeking on a trial-by-trial basis. Two things make this matter. One, it's network-level rather than region-level — the withdrawal signature is genuinely distributed, which is consistent with what clinicians describe and what point-source recordings have struggled to capture. Two, the interpretable-ML angle means the discriminative features are oscillatory bands rather than learned latents, which makes them in-principle translatable to scalp EEG. The translational distance is still big, but this is a candidate biomarker substrate for a withdrawal-targeting pharmacology trial, which is a gap in the OUD space.

Item six is the photopharmacology piece, and it's fun. Out of the Vrije Universiteit Brussel, a photoswitchable delta-9-THC derivative, called azo-THC-3, that transitions from an inactive trans configuration to an active cis configuration under violet light. The use case: temporal lobe epilepsy, where you want focal cannabinoid-receptor activation at the seizure focus without the cognitive and motor side effects of systemic CB1 agonism. They administer the inactive form systemically, implant an optic fiber in the epileptic focus, switch on the light during seizure activity, and suppress seizures with a target-engagement profile that follows the light schedule rather than the systemic pharmacokinetics. It's a tool-compound paper more than a near-term therapeutic, but the photopharmacology field has been promising precision drug delivery for two decades and this is the cleanest version I've seen for an FDA-approved-target indication.

Now to the agentic AI block. Item seven, out of Cedars-Sinai, is a paper titled "Robotic perturbation proteomics and AI agents enable scalable drug mechanism discovery." The platform pairs semi-automated sample prep and rapid LC-MS-MS proteomics with an agentic data analysis layer. The scale: 172 compounds screened in HepG2 cells, 1,232 proteomes, more than 8,700 proteins quantified, generated in about three weeks. The agentic AI handles the high-dimensional analysis — clustering compounds by proteomic signature, proposing mechanism-of-action hypotheses, and triaging which signatures warrant follow-up. The honest read: agentic systems for analyzing perturbation data are not novel — Cradle, the various AlphaFold-adjacent tools, and a dozen academic frameworks already do versions of this. What's interesting here is the integration with the wet-lab throughput on the front end. If you can hit 172 compounds in three weeks with a proteomic readout, the MoA-deconvolution agent has something genuinely useful to chew on instead of the usual ten-compound boutique dataset.

Item eight is the agentic-AI-meets-quantitative-systems-pharmacology piece, called Talk2QSP, out of Sanofi. The framing is one I haven't seen attacked head-on before: take the unstructured clinical-scenario descriptions in the QSP literature — the "patient with such-and-such baseline characteristics receives drug X at dose Y for Z weeks" prose blocks — and translate them into executable simulation scenarios. The agentic framework is human-in-the-loop, with specialist agents for literature parsing, scenario extraction, model intervention specification, and validation against the simulated trajectories. The reason this matters for computational pharmacology: QSP models are notoriously expensive to operationalize for any new scenario, and the bottleneck is the translation step from clinical description to model intervention, not the simulation engine itself. If this agent layer reaches even 70% accuracy at unattended scenario derivation, it changes the unit economics of QSP-driven dose selection and trial simulation. Sanofi running this in-house suggests they've validated against their internal models, which makes me read it more seriously than I would an academic prototype.

Item nine — and this is the circuit-level palate cleanser before we get to news — out of Korea University, an orexin-habenula circuit for stress resilience. Lateral hypothalamic orexin neurons projecting onto a population of aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase-positive D-neurons in the lateral habenula, signaling through OX2R. Activation of this pathway increases nucleus accumbens dopamine and promotes active coping during acute stress. The detail that makes it interesting is the self-limiting structure — optotagging revealed rapid stress-evoked recruitment followed by post-stress suppression, which they argue is the circuit-level basis for not getting locked into a chronic active-coping state. The pharmacological angle: OX2R-selective antagonists like seltorexant and suvorexant's selective derivatives are advancing for insomnia and depression, and the resilience-circuit story argues for a stress-context-sensitive rather than chronic blockade. Whether dosing dynamics in current programs respect that is an open question — the half-lives don't obviously match the suggested circuit kinetics.

Last item, and this one is news rather than science. The U.S. Department of Health and Human Services announced last week a federal initiative to reduce psychiatric "overprescribing," with explicit emphasis on antidepressant deprescribing protocols. The framing in the agency release is patient-safety-and-stewardship, but the policy contour matters for anyone running discovery programs in MDD adjacent indications — the federal posture is shifting toward a narrative that more pharmacotherapy is not better, which interacts with reimbursement and with how clinical-trial recruitment in TRD and MDD will be positioned. The juxtaposition with the FDA national priority vouchers issued in late April for Compass, Usona, and Transcend psychedelic programs is uncomfortable — one agency green-lighting accelerated review for new psychiatric pharmacotherapy, the other launching an overprescribing initiative aimed at the existing standard of care. The discourse around what psychiatric pharmacotherapy is for is becoming more contested at the policy level, not less, and the field's responses to date have been mostly defensive rather than reframing. Worth watching.
