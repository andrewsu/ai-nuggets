## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason. It's Thursday, July sixteenth, twenty twenty-six. Eleven items today. We open on de novo protein design applied to what has been considered one of the least tractable structural targets in neuroscience — the intrinsically disordered aggregation-prone domain of alpha-synuclein. That runs into two more Parkinson's items — a single-domain antibody that hits the same region from a different angle, and a nice mechanistic answer to why some substantia nigra dopamine neurons resist synucleinopathy while their neighbors die. Then two CNS gene therapy programs — an AAV Parkin replacement for recessive early-onset Parkinson's, and a CRISPR-activation approach to Dravet syndrome. Three neurodegeneration mechanism papers next — galectin-3 in tauopathy, oligodendrocyte proteostasis in Alzheimer's, and synaptic versus extrasynaptic NMDA receptors in multiple sclerosis. Then nicotinic gain control in macaque visual cortex, an fMRI dissection of cognitive restructuring in the depression-anxiety comorbidity, and closing on an agentic large language model that discovers ordinary differential equations from biological data.

Opening with the paper that could reset a lot of thinking about Parkinson's therapeutics — a joint preprint from Tuomas Knowles's group at Cambridge and Danny Sahtoe's group in Utrecht. This is a demonstration of de novo protein design applied to the non-amyloid beta component domain of alpha-synuclein, the hydrophobic stretch between residues sixty-one and ninety-five that drives fibrillization into Lewy bodies. The reason this region has been so hard to drug is that it's intrinsically disordered — it does not present a stable epitope, so nothing lands the way it would on a folded pocket. The Knowles-Sahtoe approach turns this apparent liability into a feature. They used deep-learning protein design to generate compact single-chain binders that specifically engage the extended beta-strand conformation the NAC domain adopts as it enters the aggregation pathway. Of twenty-one designs expressed, three engaged the target both in vitro and inside live cells. A single round of partial diffusion refined the strongest binder to a dissociation constant of about two nanomolar with no detectable cross-reactivity to tau, amylin, or amyloid beta — a specificity profile that even the best passive-immunotherapy antibodies for alpha-synuclein have struggled to reach. The critical mechanistic finding is that different binders engage distinct microscopic steps of the aggregation cascade. Combining seeded aggregation kinetics with the measured affinities for monomer, oligomer, and fibril, they can pinpoint which binder inhibits which step. The most potent one selectively captures on-pathway oligomers — the species most closely linked to neuronal toxicity — and suppresses fibril formation at substoichiometric ratios without engaging the bulk monomer pool. Substoichiometric potency against an intrinsically disordered target is what makes this a paradigm shift rather than a proof of concept. The traditional therapeutic strategy for alpha-synuclein has been passive immunotherapy — the Prothena, Roche, and Biogen antibody programs that have delivered disappointing Parkinson's trial results. De novo designed binders can be systematically optimized in ways that immunized-mouse antibodies cannot, and can be sized and shaped for whatever tissue penetration profile is needed. If the field is going to get a genuine disease-modifying therapy for synucleinopathies, the recipe is probably closer to this than to the antibody programs that have dominated the clinical pipeline.

Second, staying on alpha-synuclein and moving from designed proteins to natural single-domain antibodies. This is from a group that has been building a portfolio of anti-alpha-synuclein nanobodies for both imaging and therapy. They had previously reported diagnostic imaging with two of their nanobodies and acute clearance of alpha-synuclein in a mouse model, but the specific gap here was whether these nanobodies actually rescue neurodegeneration and motor deficits, not just clear the aggregate. They screened five single-domain antibodies in mouse neuronal cultures for their ability to lower phosphorylated serine-129 alpha-synuclein — the standard pathological marker — and then took them into a Drosophila synucleinopathy model for functional readouts. One nanobody, called 2H1 and previously unreported, binds strongly to the aggregation-prone region and produces the full package — lower phospho-serine-129 alpha-synuclein, prevention of dopaminergic neuron loss, alleviation of mitochondrial dysfunction, improved motor function, and prolonged fly survival. Proximity-dependent TurboID biotinylation shows the nanobody engages alpha-synuclein at sites involving synapse and vesicle trafficking machinery, giving a specific subcellular localization for the therapeutic interaction. Pairing this with the Knowles-Sahtoe designed binders is a useful moment for the field. Two independent approaches — one small natural nanobody, one de novo designed single-chain protein — converging on the same aggregation-prone stretch through different formats, and both showing therapeutic engagement of pathology, is a rare kind of triangulation for a target that has resisted conventional drug discovery for two decades.

Third, and finishing the alpha-synuclein arc at the cellular electrophysiology layer. This one uses the preformed fibril model of synucleinopathy — where small alpha-synuclein fibrils injected into the striatum seed the endogenous protein and propagate pathology retrogradely back into the substantia nigra pars compacta. The specific question is about the well-documented heterogeneity of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra — some subpopulations degenerate reliably in Parkinson's while others resist. Whether this differential vulnerability shows up in electrophysiology before frank cell death has been unclear. They find a bidirectional effect. In vulnerable substantia nigra dopamine neurons, alpha-synuclein seeding disrupts tonic firing patterns and specifically alters T-type calcium currents. In resilient neighbors, the same pathology increases excitability. That's a plausible functional-compensation-versus-stress dissociation, and it hands the field a specific ion-channel handle. T-type calcium modulators already exist in clinical use for absence epilepsy — ethosuximide sits in that space — and second-generation T-type-selective molecules are in development for chronic pain. The prospect of a T-type calcium modulator that selectively rescues vulnerable substantia nigra neurons early in the pathology cascade, before you get overt neuronal loss, is a testable idea with existing tool compounds.

Fourth, and moving to the translational gene-therapy layer of Parkinson's — this time on the recessive, monogenic form of the disease. Biallelic loss-of-function mutations in PRKN cause early-onset Parkinson's disease, typically before age fifty, and represent the best-characterized monogenic PD population. The Parkin protein, together with PINK1, marks damaged mitochondria for mitophagic degradation. Without functional Parkin, damaged mitochondria accumulate, oxidative stress builds in dopaminergic neurons, and cell loss follows on an accelerated schedule. The obvious therapeutic idea has been AAV-mediated PRKN replacement, but the capsid-and-promoter engineering is what has stalled it. This preprint validates phosphorylated ubiquitin serine-65 as a functional biomarker of Parkin-driven mitophagy — reduced in Parkin-knockout SH-SY5Y cells after mitochondrial stress, restored by AAV-Parkin overexpression, and also restored in patient-derived fibroblasts. In vivo, they demonstrate substantia nigra delivery in rats with AAV1 carrying an EF1-alpha promoter, then screen multiple promoters within a proprietary AAV capsid and identify EF1-alpha and Synapsin-1 as the most effective for transducing dopaminergic neurons at well-tolerated doses. What makes this worth flagging is that Parkin-deficient early-onset PD is exactly the monogenic scenario where an AAV gene therapy has a plausible path to a clinical trial with a defined endpoint — a well-defined patient population, a tractable delivery target in the substantia nigra, a mitophagy-engagement biomarker, and a translationally validated capsid-promoter combination. The field has been waiting for a rigorous preclinical package on AAV-Parkin, and this is one of the more complete ones I've seen.

Fifth, and staying with CNS gene therapy in a different disease. Dravet syndrome is a severe childhood epileptic encephalopathy caused by SCN1A haploinsufficiency. The affected sodium channel Nav one-point-one is expressed selectively in parvalbumin-positive inhibitory interneurons, and reduced Nav one-point-one in those interneurons destabilizes cortical excitatory-inhibitory balance. The preclinical program here — designated RT101 — is an AAV9-delivered CRISPR-activation system using a catalytically dead Cas9 fused to VP64, driven by the PV-selective E2 enhancer, to upregulate endogenous SCN1A from its native promoter. The design principle is worth understanding for a receptor-pharmacology audience because it side-steps the usual gene-replacement problem — SCN1A is too large to fit into an AAV capsid as a normal coding cassette. Instead of packaging the gene, they package a transcriptional activator that boosts the remaining functional SCN1A allele in the interneurons that need it. A saturating guide-RNA screen across the human SCN1A promoter identified a lead guide with robust and specific locus engagement. Human Dravet iPSC-derived GABAergic neurons show dose-dependent SCN1A upregulation. Intracerebroventricular delivery in Dravet model mice produces dose-dependent survival improvement, reduced hyperthermia-induced seizure susceptibility, and increased Nav one-point-one protein — with preserved PV-interneuron selectivity and minimal off-target expression. And in juvenile cynomolgus macaques, MRI-guided intracerebroventricular administration was well tolerated with broad cortical biodistribution and strong detargeting of peripheral tissues. The program is being advanced toward clinical evaluation. If it holds, this would be one of the first CRISPR-activation therapies for a CNS disease to reach humans.

Sixth, moving to tauopathy neuroinflammation. This one uses the P301S mouse model of tauopathy and asks whether Galectin-3 — a lectin secreted by activated microglia and elevated in Alzheimer's and frontotemporal dementia patient tissue — is causally driving pathology, or just serving as a downstream marker. The clean answer comes from crossing P301S onto a Galectin-3 knockout background. Ablating Gal-3 robustly attenuates hyperphosphorylated and pathological tau species across cortex, hippocampus, and piriform-entorhinal cortex; normalizes tau-kinase signaling; restores mitochondrial and vesicular trafficking programs; dampens microglial activation; and preserves white matter integrity. Proteomic and phosphoproteomic profiles of the Tau-Gal-3-knockout mice cluster with wild-type controls rather than with the tau-only mice — the phenotype is rescued at the systems level. The functional mechanism appears to be that Galectin-3 deficiency enhances microglial myelin phagocytosis and lysosomal degradation, so it acts as a gate on microglial clearance of myelin debris under inflammatory stress. And in human iPSC-derived neurons, extracellular Galectin-3 exacerbates tau hyperphosphorylation and aggregation after tau seeding, an effect reversed by small-molecule Gal-3 inhibitors. This is where the paper stops being a preclinical curiosity and becomes a translation story — Gal-3 inhibitors are already in phase 2 clinical trials for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis and non-alcoholic steatohepatitis. If this genetic result generalizes, the drug-repurposing pathway into frontotemporal dementia and Alzheimer's is unusually short. The caveat is that P301S is a tau-only model, so a co-pathology model with both amyloid and tau will be needed to seriously stage a Gal-3 program for AD.

Seventh, on the Alzheimer's-associated myelin loss that has emerged as a clinically significant but mechanistically underexamined feature of the disease. From a group building human iPSC-derived forebrain organoids with a specific engineering trick — a doxycycline-inducible cassette expressing three transcription factors, SOX10, OLIG2, and NKX6-2, that together drive the specification and full maturation of oligodendrocytes and compact myelin. That's not a trivial technical accomplishment on its own — existing organoid protocols have struggled to produce genuinely mature myelinating oligodendrocytes at scale. When they add pathogenic APP mutations, the organoids produce amyloid beta plaques and phosphorylated tau, along with reduced myelin basic protein and disrupted myelin ultrastructure. Then the spatial transcriptomics adds the mechanistic angle. Plaque density, not just plaque presence, reprograms oligodendrocyte transcriptomes in a graded way — the more amyloid, the stronger the transcriptional shift, coordinately inducing immune activation, calcium signaling, lipid remodeling, and proteasome subunit remodeling. And this program is conserved in human AD postmortem tissue, not just an organoid artifact. The specific take is that AD-associated myelin loss looks like an oligodendrocyte proteostasis failure — the myelin basic protein transcripts are still there but the cell's protein-handling machinery has been reconfigured into something incompatible with sustained MBP protein accumulation. That reframes how drug programs should think about white matter integrity in AD — mechanisms that support oligodendrocyte proteostatic capacity are likely better bets than mechanisms aimed only at driving OPC differentiation into new oligodendrocytes.

Eighth, on multiple sclerosis, from the group that last year described a novel MS neurodegeneration pathway they call Dendritic Syntaphilin Intrusion. The mechanism is that syntaphilin, an anchor protein normally restricted to axonal mitochondria, aberrantly translocates into dendrites and drives neurodegeneration downstream of glutamate excitotoxicity. Last year's paper made the case in a non-inflammatory MS model; this preprint asks whether the same pathway operates in inflammatory MS, and if so what regulates it. Working in primary hippocampal neuronal cultures under inflammatory cytokine exposure, they show cytokines sensitize neurons to DSI, and the balance between synaptic and extrasynaptic NMDA receptors is a switch. Blocking synaptic GluN2A-containing NMDA receptors increases DSI. Blocking extrasynaptic GluN2B-containing NMDA receptors reduces DSI. That's the classical synaptic-versus-extrasynaptic NMDA dichotomy — pro-survival synaptic signaling, pro-death extrasynaptic signaling — showing up in a specific neurodegeneration substrate. What matters for MS pharmacology is that GluN2B-selective antagonists exist as clinical tool compounds — ifenprodil, radiprodil, and follow-on molecules — and DSI gives them a plausible neuroprotective target in progressive MS specifically, where gray-matter neurodegeneration is the driver and disease-modifying therapies aimed at the inflammatory relapse phase run out of runway.

Ninth, moving to neuromodulator pharmacology in macaque primary visual cortex. Nicotinic acetylcholine receptors are heavily expressed on the thalamocortical axons arriving at layer 4C of V1, and activating them enhances local gain — that much has been well characterized. The specific gap this paper closes is quantitative — how far does that gain effect propagate through the cortical column, and does the circuit compensate to normalize it out. They recorded across the cortical depth in V1 while delivering nicotine locally to layer 4C during a perceptual task. What they see is widespread and heterogeneous gain changes across layers, some enhancement and some suppression, not explained by stimulus properties but predicted cleanly by a normalization model of divisive gain control. And critically, the circuit does not compensate — the injected gain changes are evident during behavioral performance. Two consequences for pharmacology. First, cognitive-enhancement programs targeting nicotinic receptors — nicotine partial agonists like varenicline, alpha-7-selective molecules like encenicline that had a hard clinical trial history — assumed a relatively uniform attention-boosting gain injection. The picture here is much messier and predicts patient-level heterogeneity in outcome, consistent with the mixed clinical record. Second, and more speculative, is the connection to the alpha-7 nicotinic dysfunction that's one of the more reproducible endophenotypes in schizophrenia — this kind of layer-resolved gain mapping is exactly what the schizophrenia field needs to figure out how alpha-7 signaling is contributing to prefrontal cognitive symptoms.

Tenth, on the neural basis of cognitive restructuring in the depression-anxiety comorbidity — a 7-Tesla fMRI study with seventy-three clinical participants with depressive or anxiety disorders and seventy healthy controls. The task was cognitive restructuring — repeating negative statements versus challenging them through Socratic questioning, which is the core operation of cognitive behavioral therapy. The behavioral finding is that clinical and control participants modify their negative beliefs equally well. The deficit is not in behavioral output. But at the neural level, depressive symptom severity and anxiety symptom severity produce opposite dorsolateral prefrontal cortex profiles — depression symptoms correlate with reduced dlPFC activation during challenging, while anxiety symptoms correlate with greater dlPFC activation. Dynamic causal modeling resolves this into two distinct circuit-level patterns. In depression, ventrolateral prefrontal cortex over-inhibits ventromedial prefrontal cortex. In anxiety, dlPFC exerts increased excitatory drive on amygdala, and vlPFC over-inhibits amygdala. This matters because depression and anxiety are frequently comorbid, respond to cognitive behavioral therapy at similar rates, and are often treated with the same first-line pharmacotherapy despite hitting distinct neural circuits. Symptom-profile-guided augmentation strategies become a much cleaner proposition when the underlying circuit differs — anxiety-dominant patients might respond better to amygdala-targeting adjuvants, and depression-dominant patients to prefrontal modulators. This isn't the paper that will change guidelines, but it's the kind of mechanistic dissection the RDoC-style biomarker-guided psychiatry program has been building toward.

Closing today on agentic AI applied to computational biology — an arXiv preprint on automatic ordinary-differential-equation discovery for biological systems using a large-language-model-powered agentic framework. The setup problem is that mechanistic modeling in biology has been rate-limited by the human bottleneck of proposing candidate ODE structures. A modeler with domain knowledge writes down a plausible system of equations, fits parameters, checks the fit, revises, and iterates. That workflow doesn't scale as biological systems grow more complex. The paper wires up a multi-agent LLM framework where specialized agents handle hypothesis generation, equation testing with numerical ODE solvers, statistical validation, and iterative refinement — the system proposes candidate structures from data, simulates each candidate, compares to observed dynamics, and revises. It's tested across synthetic and real biological ODE systems including population dynamics, biochemical reaction networks, gene regulatory circuits, and metabolic pathways. The reported result is recovery of ground-truth parameters from synthetic data, and comparable or improved performance versus expert-driven manual modeling with substantially less human time. The specific relevance for this show is that quantitative systems pharmacology models — the CNS QSP models we've featured multiple times recently — sit exactly in this category. They're large systems of ODEs describing receptor pharmacology, transporter dynamics, and downstream signaling; they take expert teams months to build and validate; and the bottleneck is structure discovery, not parameter fitting. If an agentic LLM system can propose valid model structures against biological data, the field could stop asking whether it can afford to build a QSP model for a given indication and start asking which candidate model structure best explains the available data. The obvious caveat is that LLM-based systems hallucinate biologically implausible structures and the burden of validation still falls on the modeler. But the direction — combining language-model pattern generation with mechanistic simulation constraints — is where computational pharmacology should be going.
