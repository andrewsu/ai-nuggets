## Script

This is Receptor and Reason for Wednesday, May twenty-seventh, twenty twenty-six. Ten items today, all fresh. We open on a cariprazine paper that bridges real-world clinical outcomes with hippocampal cellular electrophysiology, and we close on a spiking neural network comparison of disinhibition versus direct control in dopamine-driven reinforcement learning.

First, cariprazine modulates intrinsic excitability and network dynamics of hippocampal neurons in a cell-type dependent manner, from the Pircs lab at Semmelweis University in Budapest. Cariprazine is a dopamine D-two and D-three partial agonist with measurable D-three preference, approved for schizophrenia and bipolar mania, and clinically distinguished from older antipsychotics by relatively favorable extrapyramidal and metabolic tolerability. What the field has lacked is a clean cellular signature for what cariprazine does beyond receptor occupancy. The group did three things in one paper. First, they took Hungarian nationwide real-world prescription data and matched patients receiving cariprazine, aripiprazole, or haloperidol as index-drug monotherapy for schizophrenia, and found that haloperidol was associated with worse survival and higher cumulative incidence of first registered suicide attempt than either of the partial agonists. Second, in primary mouse hippocampal cultures on multielectrode arrays, acute cariprazine moderately and dose-dependently reduced spontaneous firing and prolonged burst intervals while leaving network synchrony intact. Third, the firing changes are cell-type dependent, with computational modeling tying the network signature to selective effects on a subset of pyramidal-like units. The reason this lands as the lead item is that it is increasingly clear that schizophrenia drug labels need cellular phenotypes the field can argue over. A drug that shifts burst structure rather than overall firing rate is the kind of signature that could rationalize cariprazine's profile in negative-symptom and cognitive-symptom domains, where pure D-two antagonism has historically underperformed.

Second, pancreatic mu opioid receptors regulate metabolism and ingestive behaviors, from the Castro lab at Washington University in Saint Louis. The mu opioid receptor is canonically a CNS target for analgesia, reward, and feeding behavior, but mu opioid receptors are also expressed in stomach, intestine, and pancreatic islets. The question is whether peripheral pancreatic mu opioid receptors contribute to the metabolic and ingestive phenotypes that conventional approaches attribute to brain mu opioid signaling. Global Oprm1 knockout males — but not females — show increased body mass and increased ad libitum food intake on continuous-access feeding devices, alongside enhanced glucose tolerance and insulin sensitivity on intraperitoneal challenge. The genetic dissection then narrows the source. Conditional deletion of mu opioid receptors in glucagon-promoter alpha cells produces no metabolic phenotype, which rules out alpha-cell mu opioid signaling as the relevant source and points to beta-cell or non-alpha-cell pancreatic mu opioid receptor populations. The translational angle the group is opening is that opioid agonist treatments — methadone and buprenorphine for opioid use disorder, opioid analgesics in chronic pain — have well-documented metabolic and weight effects that have always been ascribed to hypothalamic or mesolimbic mu opioid signaling, and the data here argue that some component runs through a peripheral pancreatic mu opioid receptor compartment. For a community thinking about peripherally restricted mu opioid ligands, that is a new lane.

Third, plus-trans-cannabidiol is a cannabinoid receptor two agonist, from the Connor lab at Macquarie University in Sydney. The phytocannabinoid cannabidiol has two chiral centres and thus four stereoisomers. The form that occurs naturally in Cannabis sativa and that dominates pharmacology — minus-trans-cannabidiol — is a weak inhibitor at cannabinoid receptors one and two, and most of its pharmacology runs through receptors other than the canonical cannabinoid pair. The plus-trans enantiomer has higher binding affinity at both cannabinoid receptors but its pharmacodynamics have been incompletely described. The group used a fluorescence-based assay of membrane potential in pituitary-tumor cells stably expressing either cannabinoid receptor one or cannabinoid receptor two. Plus-trans-cannabidiol produced a rapid, concentration-dependent hyperpolarization at cannabinoid receptor two with a half-maximal effect around two hundred and thirty nanomolar and a maximal effect about ninety percent of the response to the reference full agonist. The response was blocked by pertussis toxin pretreatment, which confirms Gi-coupling, and competitively inhibited by the cannabinoid receptor two antagonist AM-six-thirty with a Schild slope of one. At cannabinoid receptor one it behaves as a low-efficacy, low-potency agonist. For tool-compound users, this is the cleanest cannabinoid receptor two agonist in the cannabidiol-scaffold family, and the in silico modeling identifies a specific residue contact that the plus enantiomer makes and the minus enantiomer does not — which is a starting point for cannabinoid receptor two selective medicinal chemistry that does not collapse into the rest of the cannabidiol polypharmacology.

Fourth, an opioid-withholding human laboratory paradigm during opioid agonist treatment, with phase- and dose-dependent effects of cannabidiol — from the Sofuoglu group at Yale with collaborators at Penn, published in Neuropsychopharmacology. Even on stable methadone, patients with opioid use disorder and co-occurring chronic pain frequently report unrelieved pain, because opioid agonist treatment impairs endogenous pain modulation rather than restoring it. The group asks whether oral cannabidiol — the natural minus-trans enantiomer used clinically — acts as a non-opioid adjunct in this population. Twenty-three participants, eleven female, on methadone at an average dose of about eighty-five milligrams per day, received acute oral cannabidiol at four hundred, eight hundred, and twelve hundred milligrams in a randomized double-blind crossover. Each session had two phases, pre-treatment with methadone withheld, and post-treatment after methadone was given, and the outcomes were quantitative sensory testing for descending inhibition — conditioned pain modulation — and for central sensitization — temporal summation of pain — plus opioid craving and cognition. The headline is that cannabidiol's effects on pain modulation are phase- and dose-dependent, which is interesting because most clinical cannabidiol studies treat dose as monotonically additive and ignore where in the opioid-agonist cycle the patient is. For an audience designing chronic pain adjuncts in opioid use disorder, the methodological frame of dosing relative to opioid trough versus peak is the transferable advance.

Fifth, dualsteric and dual-acting modulation of muscarinic receptors by the antagonist K-H-five, out of the Jakubik group at the Czech Academy of Sciences. The muscarinic acetylcholine receptors have come back into central nervous system drug discovery hard with xanomeline and trospium combinations for schizophrenia, and the open methodological question is how to engineer muscarinic subtype selectivity at a target family where the orthosteric site is exquisitely conserved. K-H-five is a muscarinic antagonist whose functional potency in inositol-phosphate and G-protein binding assays exceeds its measured orthosteric binding affinity by a margin that classical competitive antagonism cannot explain. The group did a complete operational and structural workup. At human muscarinic M-one receptors expressed in Chinese-hamster cells, K-H-five antagonism is saturable and probe-dependent — both hallmarks of an allosteric interaction. But at muscarinic M-two receptors it shows dualsteric behavior — simultaneously engaging the orthosteric pocket and an extracellular allosteric vestibule — confirmed by molecular dynamics and site-directed mutagenesis at residues that line the vestibule. The point is that dualsteric and dual-acting modes are pharmacologically distinct from the more common bitopic ligands described in muscarinic drug discovery, and they predict different selectivity behavior across the M-one through M-five family. For programs trying to recapitulate xanomeline's M-one preference without the gastrointestinal side effects of broad muscarinic agonism, dualsteric antagonist scaffolds are a chemical lane that has been under-explored.

Sixth, psilocybin modulates social behaviour in male and female mice in a time-dependent manner, from Monash University in Australia, in Neuropsychopharmacology. Psilocybin is moving through pivotal trials for treatment-resistant depression and major depressive disorder, but most clinical work to date has been underpowered to examine sex as a biological variable, and most preclinical psilocybin work has used male mice exclusively. The group gave C57BL/6J mice a single one-point-five milligram per kilogram dose of psilocybin and assayed social behavior at multiple timepoints. The acute phase, within the first hour, produced enhanced huddling and hypothermia selectively in female mice — both characteristic five-HT-two-A-mediated effects but with a sex-specific magnitude. At four hours, in the post-acute window, novelty-seeking and social investigation were enhanced. The translational point is that the post-acute window is where the depression and anxiety clinical work has been pointing for a mechanism of action distinct from the acute psychedelic experience, and the fact that the pro-social signature has a different sex profile than the acute hypothermia signature means clinical trials of psilocybin in indications such as depression with social-withdrawal phenotypes and anorexia nervosa — where female prevalence dominates — need stratified analyses for both window and sex.

Seventh, resting-state functional connectivity of the default mode network as a predictor of escitalopram response in adolescents with depression, from Korea University Guro Hospital, also in Neuropsychopharmacology. Adolescent major depressive disorder is neurobiologically and pharmacodynamically distinct from adult major depressive disorder — selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor response rates are different, suicidality risk is age-stratified, and biomarker work in adults does not transfer cleanly. The group enrolled seventy medication-naive adolescents with major depressive disorder, ages twelve to seventeen, and ran an eight-week open-label escitalopram trial with the Children's Depression Rating Scale as outcome. Baseline resting-state functional MRI used seed-based connectivity from three default mode network nodes — ventromedial prefrontal cortex, dorsomedial prefrontal cortex, and posterior cingulate cortex. The finding the field will quote is that baseline default mode network connectivity patterns prospectively predict who responds to escitalopram, with effect sizes and seed specificity that differ from the adult literature. For computational psychiatry this is a usefully unglamorous result — a biomarker that survives in a real adolescent cohort, with a real clinical scale, in a population where pediatric selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor prescribing is contested and benefits from any rational stratification.

Eighth, a novel estrogen-replacement combination therapy including the investigational drug davunetide, out of the Gozes lab at Tel Aviv University. Davunetide — also called N-A-P — is the eight-amino-acid neuroprotective peptide derived from activity-dependent neuroprotective protein, which the same group has identified as a master regulator of cognition with prior clinical exposure in tauopathy trials. The hypothesis here is that estrogen and activity-dependent neuroprotective protein are dynamically coupled through the estrous cycle and steroidogenic signaling, and that davunetide could substitute for or augment classical hormone replacement therapy in the central nervous system without the systemic risks of estrogen. Ovariectomized three-month-old female mice received daily intranasal davunetide, estradiol, or vehicle, with cognitive and motor readouts. The combination arm — davunetide with low-dose estrogen — outperformed either monotherapy on cognitive and motor preservation. This is preclinical and not new chemistry, but it is exactly the kind of repositioning argument that matters for the Alzheimer-and-menopause overlap population, where estrogen withdrawal has been epidemiologically linked to accelerated cognitive decline and where conventional hormone replacement therapy has been constrained by cardiovascular and oncologic concerns.

Ninth, NanoCortex, a unified agentic system for nanopore sequencing analysis, from the Wanunu lab at Northeastern University. Nanopore sequencing has accumulated a fractured ecosystem of tools — basecallers, modification callers, isoform callers, downstream interpretation — and a typical biological question requires stitching half a dozen of them with non-trivial parameter choices. The group built NanoCortex as a multi-agent framework on top of Google's Gemini development kit, with separate agents responsible for task parsing, code generation, iterative code-level self-correction, and biological interpretation, plus retrieval against public sequence and annotation databases. End-to-end, a user submits a natural-language question and a raw nanopore signal file, and the system produces both an analysis pipeline — code that can then run offline — and a biological interpretation. The benchmarking shows substantially higher usability on complex analytical tasks than a general-purpose large language model alone, and the offline-runnability of the generated code addresses the audit and reproducibility concern that closed-API agentic systems raise. For the listener thinking about agentic AI in biomedical workflows where the underlying tool ecosystem is mature but fragmented, this is a representative architecture — and it makes the case that the agent's value is orchestration plus interpretation, not generation from first principles.

Tenth and closing, a spiking neural model of dopamine-driven reinforcement learning that compares disinhibition versus direct control, from the ETIS lab at Cergy Paris University. The midbrain dopaminergic system receives convergent inputs through two anatomically distinct motifs — direct excitatory and inhibitory projections from ventral tegmental afferents, and disinhibitory motifs through GABAergic intermediaries in the rostromedial tegmental nucleus and elsewhere. Both motifs exist in the brain, but their respective computational contributions to value learning have not been cleanly separated. The group built two fully spiking neural network models — one driven solely by direct inhibitory and excitatory projections, one driven solely by disinhibition — and put both into a three-armed bandit task as a test of value-based decision making. Both models reach the biologically realistic asynchronous-irregular firing regime, but the direct-control model is more sensitive to incoming-signal disruption and learns the bandit task less reliably than the disinhibition model. The implication is that disinhibition is not just a feature of the wiring diagram but a functional advantage — the gain control structure smooths out noise and is more robust to perturbation than direct integration. For computational psychopharmacology this matters because disinhibition motifs are exactly where benzodiazepines, GABAergic anesthetics, and several next-generation depression pharmacologies act, and the modeling suggests their effects on dopamine-mediated value learning may be larger than direct-pathway models predict.

That is the docket for May twenty-seventh. The week's middle gives us a cariprazine paper that grounds real-world clinical comparisons in cellular electrophysiology, and a disinhibition-versus-direct dopamine model that quietly suggests where the more interesting circuit-level pharmacology is hiding.
