## Script

This is Receptor and Reason for Saturday, May thirtieth, twenty twenty-six. Ten items today, all fresh from the last seven days. We open on a computational drug-repositioning argument that lysergic acid diethylamide opposes the transcriptional program of brain aging, and we close on a benchmarking critique of allosteric-site-prediction methods that argues conformational dynamics belong inside the predictor, not bolted on after.

First, lysergic acid diethylamide reverses aging- and neurodegeneration-associated brain transcriptional programs, from the Iorio group at Human Technopole. The conceptual move worth describing first. In computational drug discovery there is a principle called transcriptional signature reversal — if a disease is characterized by a coordinated shift in gene expression, then a compound that induces the opposite shift is a candidate therapeutic. The principle has been the backbone of LINCS and Connectivity Map work for over a decade, and it has produced repurposing leads in oncology and metabolic disease. Applying it to brain aging and dementia is the natural next move because both conditions are well-described by coordinated transcriptional programs involving synaptic dysfunction and altered neuron-glia interactions. The group compared transcriptional profiles induced by chronic LSD in rodent brain against age- and dementia-associated expression changes in human prefrontal cortex from public datasets. The result — LSD induces a gene-expression pattern strongly anti-correlated with both aging and neurodegeneration signatures. The reversal is specific. It is more pronounced than the reversal produced by other pharmacological perturbations they tested, and it reproduces across datasets and across species. They then ran a functional validation in primary cortical neurons, where amyloid-beta induces structural and molecular damage, and LSD attenuates both. This is not a clinical claim — chronic LSD as a therapeutic in dementia is not the message — but the message is that the neuroplastic signature engaged by serotonin two-A psychedelics has a direction-of-effect at the transcriptional level that opposes aging biology, which adds a mechanistic anchor to the broader case for psychedelics in age-associated cognitive decline.

Second, identifying and targeting abnormal mitochondrial localization associated with psychosis, from the Carpenter group at the Broad Institute imaging platform together with collaborators at McLean. The conceptual setup is that we need therapeutics with novel mechanisms in psychiatric conditions, and cell-based assays measuring patient-derived cellular phenotypes are a route into novel-mechanism discovery that does not require a pre-specified target. The group imaged skin fibroblasts from one hundred sixty-eight patients, stained for DNA, actin, and mitochondria. The phenotype that emerged on automated single-cell image analysis — mitochondria sit farther from the cell border in patients who have experienced psychosis, including subsets of bipolar, schizophrenia, and schizoaffective patients. Patients with major depression without psychosis show a trend in the opposite direction, not statistically significant. The phenotype reduces to a single quantitative metric, which is the part that matters for screening. They then queried existing public databases of fibroblasts treated with chemical or genetic perturbations and stained for mitochondria, and ranked compounds and genes by their effect on the psychosis-associated localization phenotype. The retrieved hits include compounds with known psychiatric activity, which is the kind of internal validation that licenses moving on to compounds without known psychiatric activity. The broader interest is that imaging-based, single-metric, transdiagnostic patient phenotypes are an underused entry point for psychiatric drug discovery, because they sidestep both the diagnostic-category heterogeneity problem and the target-validation problem at once.

Third, convergence of disease- and risk-associated circular RNAs in schizophrenia and bipolar disorder, from the Daskalakis group at McLean Hospital and Harvard Medical School working from the PsychENCODE consortium. Circular RNAs are a regulatory layer that has been largely missing from psychiatric transcriptomics — they are stable, often brain-enriched, and they can compete with messenger-RNA and bind microRNAs, so their absence from the consortium-level work has been a real gap. The group quantified circular-RNA expression in one thousand twenty-two postmortem cortex samples — five hundred eighteen controls, three hundred sixty-five schizophrenia, one hundred thirty-nine bipolar — and integrated those profiles with matched linear messenger-RNA and genotype data. They identified twenty-three differentially expressed circular RNAs in schizophrenia and three in bipolar disorder at a five-percent false-discovery rate. They then built genetically regulated circular-RNA expression models in the controls, applied those models to schizophrenia and bipolar genome-wide association results in a transcriptome-wide-association-study framework, and identified twenty-two and four trait-associated circular-RNA loci respectively. Pathway enrichment implicates neuronal and synaptic biology for both disorders, and brain-imaging associations in UK Biobank are predominantly anti-correlated with schizophrenia and bipolar circular-RNA associations but positively correlated with Alzheimer's-disease circular-RNA associations. One isoform of circular KLHL24 stands out across imaging associations. The takeaway is that circular RNAs are a non-redundant regulatory layer connecting genetic risk to synaptic biology and brain structure, and the transcriptome-wide-association-study framework now has a circular-RNA equivalent that prioritizes targets the linear messenger-RNA framework would miss.

Fourth, obsessive-compulsive disorder and abstract sequence-task contributions shift prefrontal cortical connectivity, from the Desrochers group at Brown University. The compulsive ritual structure of OCD — cleaning sequences, checking routines, ordering — has a sequential cognitive substrate that the field has not fully mapped. Prior work from the same lab identified a set of cortical regions activated by abstract sequence tasks, where the rule rather than the stimulus defines the structure, including rostrolateral prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate, dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, supplementary motor area, middle temporal gyrus, and a temporo-occipital region. Whether these regions form a coordinated circuit and whether that circuit is differently configured in OCD has been an open question. The group ran task-based functional and effective connectivity analyses on a previously published dataset. Healthy controls show less negative connectivity from anterior-cingulate-dorsolateral-prefrontal regions to the temporo-occipital region, stronger positive coupling between middle-temporal and temporo-occipital regions, and stronger coordination between rostrolateral and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex — a profile consistent with coordinated top-down prefrontal control. OCD shows increased connectivity from rostrolateral prefrontal cortex directly to middle-temporal regions, suggesting a more direct prefrontal influence on posterior cortex that bypasses the usual mid-circuit relay. Effective-connectivity analysis identifies anterior-cingulate-dorsolateral-prefrontal and middle-temporal regions as hubs, with task input entering via the temporo-occipital region and propagating forward through the middle temporal gyrus to rostrolateral prefrontal cortex. The pharmacological implication is upstream rather than direct — repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation and deep-brain stimulation programs for treatment-resistant OCD pick targets largely from the supplementary motor and anterior-cingulate cortex; an effective-connectivity-defined hub map suggests the rostrolateral-prefrontal-to-middle-temporal axis is the differentially configured edge, which is the kind of finding that should propagate into circuit-targeted intervention design.

Fifth, local control of dopamine release in nucleus accumbens gates opioid withdrawal aversion, from the Pritzker laboratory at Stanford. Opioid withdrawal has long been treated in neurobiology as a single syndrome, but clinically the physical symptoms — autonomic activation, somatic discomfort — and the affective symptoms — dysphoria, drug craving — predict relapse very differently, with the affective component carrying the larger share. The group identified an unusual population of mu-opioid-receptor-positive neurons in the nucleus accumbens expressing the transcription factor Tshz1, evolutionarily conserved across the mammalian lineage. Activity in these neurons is required specifically for withdrawal-aversion learning. Deleting mu-opioid receptors selectively from Tshz1 neurons abolishes the withdrawal-induced drop in nucleus-accumbens dopamine release and the associated affective aversion, while leaving the physical symptoms intact — a clean cellular dissociation of the two symptom domains. They then asked whether the Tshz1 population could be targeted pharmacologically without using opioid-receptor ligands, and identified metabotropic glutamate receptor eight, preferentially expressed in these cells. Pharmacological activation of metabotropic glutamate receptor eight reduces withdrawal aversion. The translational read is that group-three metabotropic glutamate receptors — long considered for stimulant use disorder and anxiety — have a defined nucleus-accumbens substrate for blocking the affective arm of opioid withdrawal without engaging the mu-opioid receptor itself, which is the right shape for a non-opioid adjunct to medication-assisted treatment.

Sixth, activation of metabotropic glutamate receptor two reverses persistent post-methamphetamine deficits in object-in-place recognition memory, from the Schwendt group at the University of Florida. Methamphetamine use disorder leaves persistent cognitive impairments after drug exposure stops, and those impairments are a real driver of relapse vulnerability. There are no approved treatments for this phenotype. The group ran rats through a stimulant self-administration paradigm — limited access for a week followed by extended access for two weeks, then a thirty-day forced-abstinence period — and tested object-in-place recognition memory, which is the rodent assay closest to human episodic-like memory and depends on intact prefrontal-perirhinal cortex circuitry. The extended-access animals show clear deficits that persist through the abstinence window. A single dose of LY-four-eight-seven-three-seven-nine, a positive allosteric modulator selective for metabotropic glutamate receptor two, administered prior to the recognition test, fully reverses the deficit. Total metabotropic glutamate receptor two-three protein levels are unchanged after methamphetamine exposure, but surface receptor expression increases in both the prelimbic and perirhinal cortices — a compensatory trafficking response insufficient to restore function without pharmacological boost. The relevance for stimulant-use-disorder pharmacology is that group-two metabotropic glutamate receptors have been considered for anxiety and schizophrenia for years with mixed clinical results, but the methamphetamine-induced cognitive-impairment phenotype is an indication where the compensatory upregulation makes the pharmacology setup well, and a positive allosteric modulator is a more selective handle than a direct agonist.

Seventh, restoring hippocampal sharp-wave ripples under glutamate-transporter dysfunction, from the Wu lab at Georgetown University Medical Center. Sharp-wave ripples are short bursts of high-frequency hippocampal network activity associated with memory consolidation and pattern replay, and they are degraded early in Alzheimer's pathophysiology. The proposed mechanism is that soluble amyloid-beta oligomers impair excitatory amino-acid transporters on astrocytes, which lowers glutamate clearance and pushes the hippocampal network out of the narrow excitation-inhibition window that supports ripple generation. The question this paper asks is whether an ion-channel-targeting intervention can mitigate that. In mouse hippocampal slices, TBOA — a selective glutamate-transporter inhibitor used here as a model of early-stage glutamatergic dysregulation — reduces sharp-wave-ripple amplitude and diminishes ripple-associated population calcium transients while increasing disorganized spontaneous single-neuron firing, the expected signature of network desynchronization. KCNQ-channel openers — ML-two-one-three and ICA-two-seven-two-four-three, both small-molecule activators of the slow-potassium-current channel family that determines neuronal firing threshold — partially restore ripple amplitude under TBOA. Blocking AMPA receptors, blocking NMDA receptors, blocking the HCN-mediated I-h current, or activating BK or GIRK channels do not produce the same restoration. The interest for Alzheimer's pharmacology is that KCNQ openers — which have an approved channel pharmacology in epilepsy and earlier interest in mood and tinnitus — occupy a unique position among ion-channel handles for stabilizing hippocampal network activity against amyloid-driven glutamatergic dysregulation, and the relevant phenotype is ripple-amplitude rescue rather than firing-rate normalization, which is a more network-level endpoint than the field typically uses.

Eighth, from APOE genetics to AI-designed drug candidates — an integrated pipeline for oral, brain-penetrant ACAT1 inhibitors in Alzheimer's disease, from CuraGen AI Labs. The motivation chain. Common allelic variation at APOE accounts for roughly seventy-two to ninety-three percent of Alzheimer's-disease risk depending on the population estimate, and the APOE-epsilon-four isoform impairs cholesterol transport in the brain. The downstream consequence is that microglia accumulate cholesteryl esters and lose phagocytic capacity for both amyloid clearance and autophagy-mediated tau degradation, linking a single upstream metabolic problem to both Alzheimer hallmark pathologies. ACAT1, also known as sterol O-acyltransferase one, is the predominant cholesterol-esterifying enzyme in brain, and its inhibition is predicted to reduce cholesteryl-ester formation, restore microglial amyloid clearance, and promote tau degradation through autophagy. Prior ACAT inhibitor programs were limited by isoform non-selectivity against ACAT2, by excessive lipophilicity, and by lack of central nervous system optimization. The new pipeline combines scaffold-constrained generative chemistry with multi-objective optimization across more than thirty absorption-distribution-metabolism-excretion-and-toxicity criteria simultaneously. From roughly six million generated molecules, staged filtering yields about seven thousand three hundred central-nervous-system-optimized candidates with predicted oral brain-penetrant profiles distinct from prior ACAT clinical compounds. Three nominated leads have predicted blood-brain-barrier probabilities above zero-point-nine-three, favorable physicochemical profiles, and stable ACAT1 binding poses. The paper is at the in-silico-design stage, with synthesis and in-vitro validation listed as next steps, so the right way to read it is as a target-validation argument plus a design exercise — but the multi-objective scaffold-constrained generative-chemistry approach is increasingly how brain-penetrant lead-generation actually gets done, and ACAT1 is a target where the genetic and mechanistic anchoring is strong enough that the design work is worth tracking.

Ninth, APOE-epsilon-four and INPP5D converge on membrane mechanics to regulate endocytosis in human astrocytes, from the Narayan group at the National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases. Endocytic dysfunction is one of the earliest cellular signatures of Alzheimer's disease, and most of the work to date has lived in neurons or microglia. Astrocytes — a third of the brain by cell count — have been comparatively underexplored on this axis. Using isogenic induced-pluripotent-stem-cell-derived astrocytes that differ only at the APOE locus, the group shows that the APOE-epsilon-four variant impairs clathrin-mediated membrane curvature, reduces clathrin-mediated endocytosis, and alters plasma-membrane lipid saturation and membrane tension. Compared to APOE-epsilon-three, APOE-epsilon-four cells accumulate flat clathrin structures that fail to mature into coated pits, with reduced early-endosome formation and reduced endocytic uptake — a mechanical defect at the membrane-curvature level rather than a pure trafficking defect. They then test INPP5D, a separate Alzheimer's risk gene, as a modifier. INPP5D overexpression restores early endocytosis in the APOE-epsilon-four astrocytes by promoting clathrin curvature and maturation through a mechanism distinct from membrane-tension regulation, and additionally reduces lipid-droplet accumulation and attenuates inflammatory signaling. Two implications worth pulling out. One — the APOE-epsilon-four phenotype reframes as a membrane-mechanics problem upstream of receptor-mediated trafficking, which expands the druggable surface beyond cholesterol-metabolism handles like the previous item. Two — INPP5D, previously thought of as a microglial inhibitory phosphatase, has astrocyte-level relevance, and the convergence with APOE-epsilon-four at endocytosis suggests astrocyte membrane biology is a node multiple Alzheimer's risk genes flow into.

Tenth and closing, benchmark bias and conformational dynamics in allosteric site prediction, from the Karami group at Inria and the University of Lorraine. Allosteric drug discovery has had a tooling problem. Most computational allosteric-site predictors operate on a single static protein structure and pass that structure through pocket-detection tools like fpocket, which were developed for orthosteric binding sites and are not built to capture the transient, dynamic pockets that allosteric sites typically are. The paper makes two contributions. First, a methodological critique. Standard benchmarks for allosteric-pocket prediction often apply fpocket to holo structures — that is, structures crystallized with the allosteric modulator bound — without removing the bound ligand from the pocket-detection input. The bound modulator deforms the protein into its pocket-open conformation, which is data leakage. They show that reported performance estimates on these benchmarks are inflated, and propose a properly preprocessed evaluation. Second, a method. AlloDyn integrates static pocket descriptors with dynamic features from both all-atom molecular-dynamics simulations of the apo-state protein and AlphaFlow-generated conformational ensembles. Dynamic feature augmentation significantly improves prediction performance over static-only baselines, and AlphaFlow ensembles deliver comparable performance to molecular-dynamics features at a fraction of the computational cost — which is the part that makes the approach scalable across the receptor and channel pharmacophore set. On a properly preprocessed benchmark, AlloDyn achieves the best balance of precision and recall and the highest F1 and Matthews correlation coefficient among evaluated methods. The takeaway is two-fold. Conformational dynamics belong inside the prediction model, not bolted on after. And anyone evaluating allosteric-pocket-prediction methods should check first whether the benchmark is leaking modulator-induced conformational state into the input — a reminder that pairs nicely with yesterday's attention-enabled PocketMiner paper, which sits in the same workflow space but on the cryptic-pocket-plus-coupling-prediction axis.

That is the docket for May thirtieth. The week ends on a transcriptional-signature-reversal argument for psychedelics in brain aging, a clean cellular dissociation of the affective and physical components of opioid withdrawal, two converging routes into computational Alzheimer's pharmacology that hit APOE biology from cholesterol-metabolism and membrane-mechanics angles respectively, and a critique of allosteric-pocket-prediction benchmarks that argues dynamics belong inside the predictor and that benchmark leakage has been inflating reported numbers.
