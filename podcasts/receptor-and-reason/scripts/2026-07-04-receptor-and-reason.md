## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. It's Saturday, July fourth. Ten items today, biased toward receptor pharmacology because bioRxiv had a good week for it — a 5-HT2A ligand comparison that argues for biased signaling, a heteromeric kainate receptor cryo-EM series with a clear subunit-selectivity takeaway, a subunit-specific NMDA receptor story in the dentate, a neuropeptide receptor pharmacology paper on avoidance extinction, and a nice stress-neurochemistry behavioral pharmacology piece. Then we swing through opioid overdose mechanism, tauopathy lncRNA biology, a repurposing story for rare mitochondrial disease, and close with two computational chemistry items on docking uncertainty and GPCR ligand-pocket prediction.

Let's start with the paper I think this audience will care about most this week. It's a systematic ligand comparison of two 5-HT2A antagonists on fear extinction in adult C57 mice, and it's a nice case study in why "the receptor" is not a monolith. The two compounds are MDL eleven-nine-thirty-nine, also known as glemanserin — which is a mixed 5-HT2A-slash-2C antagonist — and MDL one-hundred-nine-oh-seven, volinanserin, which is the selective 5-HT2A antagonist. Both were dosed acutely at behaviorally comparable levels — one milligram per kilogram for the mixed compound, ten micrograms per kilogram for the selective one — and both acutely enhanced freezing to the first CS presentation on extinction day one. So on acute expression of conditioned fear, the two ligands agree. Where they disagree is on the extinction phase itself. Repeated dosing of glemanserin significantly impaired extinction — more freezing across trials, more trials to criterion. Repeated dosing of volinanserin did not affect extinction at all. The obvious first suspect is 5-HT2C, and they killed that hypothesis directly by testing SB two-forty-two-oh-eighty-four, which is the selective 5-HT2C antagonist, at two doses. Neither affected fear expression or extinction. So we're left with two compounds that ostensibly hit the same receptor, both antagonists, both blocking acute fear expression, but only one impairing extinction. The authors' interpretation is that this is ligand-directed signaling — the two antagonists engage different downstream signaling pathways at 5-HT2A, and it's the pathway that matters for extinction plasticity, not the receptor occupancy per se. This has been debated for a long time on the agonist side, especially in the psychedelic 5-HT2A literature, and it's satisfying to see the same argument holding on the antagonist side. Small caveat, this preprint went up yesterday — the abstract carries the finding but the body's not rendered yet, so I want to see the intracellular readouts when they land. But if you work on 5-HT2A drug discovery, this reinforces that antagonist-versus-antagonist head-to-head in a behaviorally relevant assay is not optional.

Staying on ionotropic glutamate receptor pharmacology, the next paper is a cryo-EM series on the most abundant native kainate receptor heteromer, GluK2-slash-GluK5. What makes this useful is that heteromers behave differently from homomers, and the field has been working mostly with homomeric structures. This group solves the apo, glutamate-desensitized, and competitive antagonist states of the heteromer, using UBP three-ten as their tool antagonist, and importantly they engineer complementary affinity-altering mutations that let them swap which subunit UBP three-ten preferentially binds — so they can capture GluK5-selective and GluK2-selective inhibited states in the same tetramer. The mechanistic payoff is worth calling out. Antagonizing GluK5 — the high-affinity subunit that sits at the A and C positions of the tetramer — is dramatically more effective at closing the pore than antagonizing GluK2 at B and D. The structural reason turns out to be Met six-forty-eight on GluK5, which flips into a lid conformation across the pore apex when GluK5 ligand-binding domains rotate outward. That lid isn't reproduced when you antagonize GluK2. So there's a molecular rationale for pushing kainate-receptor drug discovery specifically toward GluK5-selective compounds. For anyone thinking about kainate receptors as a target in migraine, in depression, in epilepsy — this tells you which subunit selectivity buys you the closed-channel state you actually want.

Next, a paper that dropped a second revision this week on a subunit story I want to make sure you don't miss — GluN2D-containing NMDA receptors in dentate granule cells. GluN2D is the poor cousin of the NMDA receptor field. GluN2A and GluN2B get most of the pharmacology attention, and 2C and 2D are usually treated as backwaters expressed elsewhere. This group uses selective GluN2D antagonists and a conditional GluN2D knockout, and shows that in mature dentate granule cells GluN2D-containing receptors are tonically active, primarily extrasynaptic, and contribute to setting the firing threshold — so knocking them out or blocking them lowers action potential generation. They also show that a physiologically realistic stimulation pattern induces a strong, GluN2D-dependent LTP of NMDA-receptor-mediated transmission at the medial perforant path, which they attribute to lateral diffusion of GluN2D-containing receptors from extrasynaptic pools into the synapse. And conditional knockout impairs a hippocampus-dependent memory task. This matters for two reasons. One, several disease-modifying candidates being pushed at NMDA receptors — for depression, for autism, for schizophrenia — will hit or spare GluN2D depending on their subunit profile, and this paper gives you a functional readout to worry about. Two, the tonic extrasynaptic pool that mobilizes into synapses on demand is a very different pharmacology than pulsatile synaptic activation, and it argues for open-channel-block versus subunit-selective strategies being quite different bets in dentate circuits.

Now a nice neuropeptide-receptor circuit paper. Melanin-concentrating hormone receptor one — MCHR1 — signaling in the prefrontal cortex is proposed here as a regulator of active avoidance extinction. The tool compound is SNAP-nine-four-eight-four-seven, a selective MCHR1 antagonist. Systemic MCHR1 antagonism left acquisition of two-way active avoidance intact — so the animals still learned to escape a signaled shock. But once the tone was decoupled from shock, SNAP-treated animals extinguished the learned avoidance faster than controls. Prelimbic-mPFC-targeted infusion of the antagonist reproduced the effect, so the site of action is prefrontal. Fiber photometry from CaMKII-positive mPFC neurons then showed that MCHR1 antagonism enhanced excitatory prefrontal responses during successful avoidance trials and altered trial-history-dependent activity most on avoidance-after-avoidance trials. The interpretation is that endogenous MCHR1 tone in the prefrontal cortex helps persist an avoidance policy across trials — and blocking it releases the animal to update. MCHR1 has been through several rounds of failed obesity clinical development, and the R and D question worth putting on the table is whether it deserves a second look for psychiatric compulsivity and habit-persistence phenotypes — OCD, addiction, chronic anxiety — where the ability to extinguish a defensive policy is exactly the deficit.

Sticking with behavioral neurochemistry — a paper on how early-life stress, noradrenaline, and serotonin interact on probabilistic reversal learning in rats. The setup is a maternal separation model on postnatal days five through nineteen, then a serial probabilistic reversal learning task in adulthood, with three pharmacological probes — propranolol as a beta-blocker, citalopram as an SSRI, and exogenous corticosterone. The core finding is that early-life stress increases responsivity to noisy feedback — animals over-adjust to spurious punishment and reward, which reads as brittle flexibility rather than more flexibility. The pharmacology tells you the substrate is doubly modulatory — propranolol and citalopram each restore parts of that phenotype, and corticosterone recapitulates parts of the stressed profile in unstressed controls. What I like about this is that it dissects the classic clinical observation that adults with early-life adversity have altered feedback processing under uncertainty, and it does it in a way that maps onto both noradrenergic and serotonergic drug classes that are already in use for stress-related mood disorders. It won't rewrite anyone's clinical protocol, but for someone building a computational-psychiatry account of feedback-driven flexibility, this is a clean set of orthogonal manipulations to fit.

Now a piece of opioid-overdose mechanism that is going to matter for how we think about naloxone-resistance in the fentanyl era. Fentanyl is not just a respiratory depressant — it produces a phase of tonic diaphragmatic activation that essentially clamps the diaphragm and turns the ventilatory failure into something more like mechanical asphyxiation. This study, in urethane-anesthetized mice, resolves that into two distinct phases. Phase one is what you'd expect — profound reduction in breathing rate with the diaphragmatic rhythm still coordinated. Phase two is where the ventilation partially rebounds but the diaphragm goes tonic, loses inspiratory phase dominance in EMG, and — this is the key structural finding — the two hemi-diaphragms decorrelate from each other. Carotid body denervation eliminated the tonic component and the expiratory-phase EMG elevation, but did not restore bilateral coordination or ventilatory stability. And rhythmic brainstem slice recordings showed that the bilateral coordination of the pre-Bötzinger complex is disrupted intrinsically, not just as a downstream effect. Two implications. First, the rescue window is smaller than a naloxone-plus-time model assumes, because the muscle is not just paralyzed, it's actively fighting itself. Second, if you're thinking about pharmacological rescue compounds beyond naloxone — orexin agonists, thyrotropin-releasing hormone analogs — the target isn't just chemoreflex drive, it's brainstem rhythm coordination. That's a different pharmacology.

Pivoting to neurodegeneration. Long non-coding RNAs — lncRNAs — have been on the periphery of tauopathy research, and this paper drags one into the center. The group generates iPSC-derived neurons, astrocytes, and microglia carrying the MAPT intron-ten-plus-sixteen mutation associated with frontotemporal dementia. Across all three cell types and in matched patient brain tissue, two lncRNAs stand out — NORAD and MIR22HG — as consistently dysregulated. NORAD is also altered in Alzheimer's and Parkinson's brains, so it's not FTD-specific, it looks like a broader neurodegeneration signature. The mechanistic anchor is that NORAD is already known to sequester the pumilio RNA-binding proteins, PUM1 and PUM2. When you unbalance NORAD, pumilio-repressed transcripts get de-repressed, and their protein network converges on RNA regulation, cytoskeletal organization, proteostasis, and — most importantly for this paper — tau interaction networks. So the pitch is that lncRNA-pumilio dysregulation is upstream of the tau-propagation-associated proteome you would like to shut down. This is a v-one preprint so the body's not rendered yet, and I'd want to see the causal experiments in full text. But NORAD is druggable in principle — you can antisense it, you can compete with pumilio binding — and this is the kind of noncoding-first target hypothesis that the tauopathy field has been slow to explore.

Related in spirit — an update on a rare-disease repurposing platform. The idea, from the Rutkowski group, is hypoxia in a pill — you take a hemoglobin affinity enhancer, GBT six-oh-one, which is the second-generation cousin of voxelotor that's in sickle-cell development, and combine it with the HIF-two inhibitor PT twenty-three-ninety-nine to prevent the erythropoietic compensation. Effectively you deliver tissue hypoxia without breathing hypoxic gas. Their prior version got you partway there in the Ndufs-four Leigh syndrome mouse. This optimized combination hits therapeutic hypoxia at a dose that's now comparable to chronic eleven-percent oxygen breathing in efficacy, and it halts progression and reverses neurological symptoms in three preclinical models — the Ndufs-four Leigh model, a Friedreich's ataxia model, and a third neurodegeneration model. The interesting move here is repositioning — GBT six-oh-one is already deep in clinical development for sickle cell, and PT twenty-three-ninety-nine is in oncology development. If this animal package holds, an IND for Leigh syndrome or Friedreich's is a much shorter path than developing a new molecule. The obvious worry is chronic tissue hypoxia in humans is not a benign thing — you get pulmonary hypertension, you get right heart strain over years — but for a young child with Leigh syndrome and an untreatable disease, the risk-benefit conversation is very different.

Two computational items to close. First — ConfDock, a paper on molecular docking that finally does what should have been done years ago, which is honest uncertainty quantification. Standard docking gives you a pose and a score with no calibrated confidence, and the medicinal chemist ends up back-of-envelope guessing which predicted contacts to trust. ConfDock uses a conformal prediction framework — you pair a graph-neural-network-based quantile estimator over atomic positions with split conformal calibration, and you get atom-specific prediction intervals with distribution-free finite-sample coverage guarantees. Evaluated across two hundred and thirty-eight protein-ligand complexes and four different docking methods, they cut mean interval width by a bit more than half compared to a standard conformal baseline, without losing target coverage. The interesting result is where the GNN wins — it captures the structure-dependent variability, meaning that atoms in more constrained pockets get tighter intervals than atoms in solvent-exposed loops, which is exactly the calibration you want. If your downstream workflow depends on which contacts to prioritize for synthesis, this is a drop-in upgrade to any docking pipeline you're already running.

And finally — a paper on GPCR ligand pocket prediction using the Boltz-two co-folding framework, applied to the bitter taste receptor family. The bitter taste receptors, TAS2Rs, are unusual GPCRs — they're not clean orthosteric-only, several of them bind ligands in a non-classical intracellular pocket that recent cryo-EM has resolved. This group asks whether current AI co-folding can predict pocket choice — extracellular versus intracellular — for TAS2R-agonist pairs. On the fifteen experimentally solved agonist-TAS2R complexes, Boltz-two nails the correct pocket for twelve. And it correctly identifies intracellular binding cases that classical docking into predicted receptor models cannot. They then use aristolochic acid, which is intracellular at TAS2R fourteen and extracellular at TAS2R forty-three, as a test system, and do in-silico morphing to pin down transmembrane helices three and seven as the pocket-selectivity determinants. Extending to fifteen hundred agonist-receptor associations in the BitterDB, most TAS2Rs prefer the extracellular pocket, but several appear to be dual-pocket-competent. Two reasons this matters beyond taste biology. One, TAS2Rs are increasingly recognized as extra-oral targets — in airway smooth muscle, in gut chemosensing — so their pharmacology is not just a flavor-science question. Two, and more generally, if co-folding can predict pocket location without a structure, it becomes a viable early-triage step for any GPCR target where allosteric-versus-orthosteric bias is on the table. Boltz-two is not yet trustworthy enough to skip experiment, but as a hypothesis generator for pocket choice it's suddenly usable.
