# Receptor & Reason — 2026-06-04

## Script

Good morning, Alan. Ten items today, opening on a substantial mechanism story for hydroxynorketamine, then a human imaging biomarker that separates antidepressant responders by mechanism class, a unifying analysis of brain-wave propagation across psychedelics anesthesia and sleep, a multi-omic schizophrenia organoid paper that argues most of the biology lives in post-translational modifications, a cell-type-specific dissection of prefrontal cortex dynamics in fentanyl self-administration, three neurodegeneration items — melanin-concentrating hormone neurons linking tau to sleep, a metabolic-proteostatic axis for tau, and a structural biology paper on LRRK2 — a cerebrospinal-fluid proteomic biomarker study for Lewy body dementia, and closing on an infrastructure paper for reproducible large-language-model-driven bioinformatics pipelines.

First, and the most important antidepressant mechanism paper of the week — a paired pair of preprints out of the same group on June third arguing that the ketamine metabolite hydroxynorketamine works through opioid receptor heterodimers on astrocytes. Background: hydroxynorketamine, specifically the two-R-six-R stereoisomer, has been the most-discussed candidate for separating ketamine's antidepressant action from its N-methyl-D-aspartate-receptor-driven psychotomimetic and abuse-liability properties — because hydroxynorketamine appears to retain rapid antidepressant-like effects in rodents without blocking the N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor. The mechanism it works through has been contested. The opioid system has been on the list since the Schatzberg group's naltrexone work in humans showed that mu-opioid blockade attenuates ketamine's antidepressant effect, but the molecular target was not clear. These two preprints land on the same finding from complementary angles. Hydroxynorketamine selectively engages a heterodimer of the mu and delta opioid receptors expressed on astrocytes in the hippocampus. Pharmacological and genetic disruption of either the mu or the delta opioid receptor abolishes the antidepressant-like behavioural effects. PAINT-MINFLUX nanoscopy — a super-resolution single-molecule technique — directly visualizes increased mu-delta heterodimerization at the membrane after a single dose of hydroxynorketamine, and the heterodimer abundance had been reduced by chronic stress. Molecular dynamics simulations predict selective binding at two specific residues on the mu-receptor protomer — aspartate one-forty-seven and tyrosine one-forty-eight — and mutating those residues abolishes both the heterodimer-stabilizing effect and the in vivo behavioural rescue. The downstream signaling is unusual for opioids — Gs-coupled rather than Gi, raising cyclic AMP and CREB phosphorylation in astrocytes and shifting astrocytic calcium dynamics. This is a reasonably clean answer to a question that has been open for a decade. The translational implication is direct: a compound that selectively promotes assembly of an astrocytic mu-delta heterodimer through a defined binding mode is a new drug-discovery target distinct from anything in the current rapid-acting antidepressant pipeline. The caveats are also direct. The data are entirely in rodents, the dimer-promoting binding mode is unusual and will need biochemical validation independent of the modelling, and the field has been burned before by ketamine-mechanism papers that did not replicate. But the mechanism is testable, the residues are specified, and the next experiment is a small-molecule chemical screen for selective mu-delta heterodimer assembly enhancers.

Staying on antidepressant mechanism but pivoting to humans, from a study using high-resolution hippocampal-amygdala imaging during an emotional mnemonic discrimination task. The clinical problem is well known — antidepressant response is heterogeneous, and even among responders we do not know whether different medication classes work through different circuit-level mechanisms or through the same final common pathway. One hundred seventeen participants — individuals taking single-action antidepressants meaning selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, individuals taking multi-action antidepressants meaning serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors or norepinephrine-dopamine reuptake inhibitors or polypharmacy, and unmedicated controls — were scanned while doing pattern-separation-taxing emotional memory tasks. The headline result is that among non-responders, the relative balance of activity in hippocampal subfields differed by medication class: single-action antidepressant non-responders showed greater dentate-gyrus and CA-three activity than CA-one, while multi-action antidepressant non-responders showed the opposite. So failure of treatment is not class-independent — the circuit signature of an ineffective single-action drug is different from the circuit signature of an ineffective multi-action drug. Responders, regardless of class, showed strong coactivation between the dentate-gyrus-CA-three region and the basolateral amygdala during negative emotional memory discrimination, a pattern absent in non-responders and in controls. Effects were specific to anterior hippocampus and persisted beyond current depression severity, so this is medication-related modulation, not just symptom severity confounding. The actionable point is that hippocampal-amygdala imaging during an emotional memory task may be a mechanistically-specific selection biomarker — predicting which class of antidepressant a given person is likely to respond to before they take it — and that is something the field has been unsuccessfully looking for through symptom-based and genetic biomarkers for two decades.

Switching from medication to consciousness states more broadly. A spatiotemporal-dynamics paper that took resting-state functional magnetic resonance imaging data spanning non-rapid-eye-movement sleep, propofol anesthesia, and five psychedelic-or-dissociative states — lysergic acid diethylamide, dimethyltryptamine, psilocybin, nitrous oxide, and ketamine — and asked what brain-wave propagation looks like across all of these. They applied complex principal component analysis to characterize the spatial-temporal motifs of blood-oxygen-level-dependent signal propagation, and pulled out four dominant motifs of brain-wide wave propagation. The headline finding centers on a global synchronized wave that links unimodal sensory cortices to transmodal association cortex. In diminished states — sleep and anesthesia — that global wave slows, fragments, and becomes more spatially concentrated and individually variable. In psychedelic states the global wave does the opposite: it speeds up, becomes more spatially distributed, and becomes more uniform across individuals. So the same axis of variation distinguishes the two pharmacologically opposite kinds of altered consciousness in opposite directions. There is a methodological point here worth flagging. The literature on psychedelic neuroimaging has been dominated by static-connectivity analyses — entropy of activity, network modularity — and those analyses have shown that psychedelics increase global signal diversity, but they have not naturally produced quantities comparable to anesthesia. A propagation-dynamics framework that operates on the same spatiotemporal motif under both kinds of perturbation is exactly what is needed for a unified theory of pharmacologically-induced consciousness changes. The clinical-pharmacology implication is downstream but real — if propagation speed and spatial uniformity of the global wave are reliable correlates of pharmacological state, they become candidate target-engagement biomarkers for psychedelic and dissociative therapeutics that are not pinned to a particular receptor profile.

Next on schizophrenia, a paper from a group running a multi-omic comparison of dorsal forebrain organoids from seventeen individuals with idiopathic schizophrenia and seventeen matched controls. They profiled single-nucleus transcriptomics, quantitative proteomics, metabolomics, and deep post-translational modification analysis on the same organoid material. The interesting result is what is not there. Transcriptomic differences were relatively modest, with the strongest cell-type-specific changes confined to Cajal-Retzius neurons — the transient early cortical population that lays down the laminar architecture. Proteomic and especially post-translational modification analyses revealed substantially more disruption, spanning pathways for neuronal migration, neurite development, synaptic function, protein kinase signalling, extracellular matrix, and lipid metabolism. The earliest disease-associated changes emerged at the level of protein phosphorylation, consistent with altered neuronal maturation and neurite dynamics. At later developmental stages the schizophrenia organoids showed reduced synaptic protein abundance, fewer synaptic puncta, and dysregulated retinoic acid and YAP-one signalling. The headline interpretive claim — that most disease-associated alterations occur independently of transcript or protein abundance, meaning the biology lives in protein state — is plausible and matters because the dominant approach to schizophrenia genetics in human models has been transcriptomic. If most of the biology is at the post-translational level, transcriptomic and even bulk-proteomic studies are systematically missing the relevant signal. The next test worth seeing is whether the post-translational modification signature replicates across an independent organoid cohort, because a single seventeen-versus-seventeen comparison cannot characterize how much of the variance is between-individual and how much is disease-specific.

Continuing the cell-type-specific theme but pivoting to substance use, a paper using intermittent-access fentanyl self-administration in male and female rats with longitudinal fiber photometry from intratelencephalic neurons of the prelimbic cortex — meaning the prefrontal pyramidal neurons whose axons go to other cortical areas rather than down to the striatum. They tracked calcium activity across acquisition, escalation, extinction, and cue-induced reinstatement. The model produces distinct low-risk and high-risk addiction-severity phenotypes, with female rats showing a higher propensity for the high-risk classification — abundant fentanyl intake, strong cue responsiveness, and seeking during periods of drug unavailability. The fiber photometry recordings showed dynamic encoding of fentanyl-associated stimuli by the intratelencephalic population, with event-related calcium transients aligned to lever presses, infusions, and conditioned and discriminative cues across the whole paradigm. The methodological contribution is the cell-type and behavioural-phenotype dimension simultaneously — fentanyl self-administration with longitudinal recording from a specific genetically-defined projection neuron population in animals stratified by addiction-vulnerability phenotype. The translational handle is that prelimbic intratelencephalic dynamics shift their representation of drug-associated cues across the transition from controlled to compulsive use, and that population is now mapped well enough to be a target for cell-type-specific interventions in opioid use disorder.

Three neurodegeneration items in succession. First, melanin-concentrating hormone neurons in the lateral hypothalamus as a sleep-disruption node in tauopathy. Sleep disruption is one of the earliest and most reproducible non-cognitive features of Alzheimer's disease, but the circuit mechanism linking tau pathology to sleep-wake dysfunction has been weakly characterized. Using the PS-nineteen tauopathy mouse model with longitudinal electroencephalography and electromyography, the authors showed progressive impairments in sleep architecture and homeostasis paralleling tau accumulation. Histology revealed degeneration of both melanin-concentrating-hormone neurons and the neighbouring hypocretin population at late stages. But in vivo fiber photometry showed something more selective — functional impairment of melanin-concentrating-hormone neurons specifically, characterized by reduced activity during rapid-eye-movement sleep, while hypocretin neuron activity was preserved. Cell-autonomous expression of mutant tau in melanin-concentrating-hormone neurons alone recapitulated sleep disruption, establishing causality. And critically, optogenetic and chemogenetic activation of these neurons in aged PS-nineteen mice restored sleep — so the neurons are functionally rescuable even after they have been damaged. This identifies melanin-concentrating-hormone neurons as a specific tractable target for the sleep phenotype of tauopathy. Whether the same circuit applies to human Alzheimer's — where melanin-concentrating-hormone biology is less well characterized — is the obvious follow-up.

Second neurodegeneration item — and a metabolism story. A paper identifying the PFKFB3 enzyme — phosphofructokinase-2/fructose-2,6-bisphosphatase-3 — and its product fructose-2,6-bisphosphate as a central node in Alzheimer's pathogenesis. PFKFB3 is a glycolytic regulator, and fructose-2,6-bisphosphate is its product. The mechanistic claim is that pathological tau aggregates sequester PFKFB3, depleting fructose-2,6-bisphosphate, and that fructose-2,6-bisphosphate has multiple convergent protective functions beyond glycolysis. It directly activates polynucleotide kinase 3-phosphatase for DNA strand break repair, transcriptionally upregulates protein phosphatase 2A's catalytic subunit to dampen tau phosphorylation, stabilizes free PFKFB3 against sequestration, and directly inhibits tau aggregation. So the metabolite becomes a node coupling genome maintenance, proteostasis, and tau aggregation simultaneously. Exogenous supplementation with fructose-2,6-bisphosphate rescues pathological features across induced neuronal cell lines, primary neurons, organotypic hippocampal slice cultures, and a Drosophila Alzheimer's model. The translational reach is what makes this worth flagging. If the metabolite-supplementation rescue replicates in a mammalian Alzheimer's model with longer follow-up, this becomes a small-molecule supplementation strategy with multiple converging mechanisms — which is the kind of redundancy you want in a disease where single-target interventions have repeatedly failed. The caveat is that fructose-2,6-bisphosphate is normally controlled tightly by intracellular metabolism, and how you deliver and maintain pharmacological levels in human brain is a separate non-trivial problem.

Third Parkinson's-relevant neurodegeneration item — a structural biology paper resolving how the LRRK2 kinase switches between active and autoinhibited states. LRRK2 is the second most common genetic cause of autosomal-dominant Parkinson's disease, and increased LRRK2 kinase activity is also seen in idiopathic Parkinson's, making it one of the most actively pursued drug targets in the space — the Denali-Biogen LRRK2-inhibitor program is one example. The protein is a 286-kilodalton multidomain enzyme containing a Ras-like GTPase domain — called the ROC domain — and a kinase domain. Using cryo-electron microscopy with biochemical reconstitution and cell-based assays, this group shows that the ROC GTPase governs switching between autoinhibited and active states. GTP binding promotes activation; GDP binding enforces autoinhibition. And the two common Parkinson's-associated mutations — one in the kinase domain itself and the other in the ROC domain — activate LRRK2 through structurally distinct mechanisms. That genotype-specificity matters for therapeutic strategy. Most LRRK2 inhibitor programs have aimed at the kinase active site, but the data here argue that stabilizing the GDP-bound autoinhibited conformation may be an alternative inhibition strategy — and conversely, that pharmacological activators promoting the GTP-bound state could be valuable in tissues like lung where LRRK2 may play protective roles. The peripheral-tissue caveat is important — LRRK2 inhibitor programs have hit on-target tissue toxicity in lung and kidney, and a conformational-switching framework opens the conversation about tissue-selective LRRK2 modulators.

Penultimate item — a clinical biomarker paper for Lewy body dementia using multiplex cerebrospinal-fluid proteomics. Lewy body dementia, which encompasses Parkinson's-disease dementia and dementia with Lewy bodies, has lacked validated biofluid markers of its complex clinical and neuropathological heterogeneity — which has been a barrier to clinical trial design. The platform here is NULISA — the Nucleic-acid-Linked-Immuno-Sandwich Assay — a recent multiplex proteomic technique built around oligonucleotide-tagged antibody pairs that gives broad neurodegeneration-relevant protein coverage from small cerebrospinal-fluid volumes. They ran the platform on 852 baseline and longitudinal cerebrospinal-fluid samples from controls, Parkinson's, and dementia-with-Lewy-bodies subjects in the Parkinson's Disease Biomarker Program. Of the 124 markers, 54 were significantly altered in Parkinson's with cognitive impairment or dementia with Lewy bodies versus controls, and many functioned as informative classifiers in machine learning models. Fifteen proteins showed significant baseline or longitudinal changes tracking with cognitive progression. Stratifying 209 cases by underlying synuclein pathology — measured by the alpha-synuclein seed amplification assay — and amyloid-beta pathology — measured by NULISA phospho-tau-217 — let them separate the disease-driving from co-pathology-driven signatures. Notably, select synaptic markers moved in opposite directions in synucleinopathy versus amyloid-beta pathology, which means a single synaptic biomarker interpreted naively in a co-pathology case is potentially misleading. They replicated the neuropathological signatures in 917 independent cases from the Alzheimer's Disease Neuroimaging Initiative. The take-home is that multiplex proteomic panels stratified by underlying pathology are now feasible and replicable for the Lewy body dementia spectrum, and that the field can start designing clinical trials that enrich for specific pathological substrates rather than treating Lewy body dementia as homogeneous.

Closing on the agentic-AI thread. A bioinformatics paper introducing Autopipe, a platform for converting natural-language-prompt-driven bioinformatics analyses into reproducible shareable workflows. The motivation is a familiar problem if you have tried to use a chat-based large-language-model agent to drive a bioinformatics pipeline: the conversation is non-deterministic, the execution environment is heterogeneous, the result is hard to re-run, and it cannot easily be moved to a remote high-performance-computing cluster. Autopipe sits on top of the Model Context Protocol, the emerging standard for letting a language model orchestrate external tools, and produces containerized re-executable pipelines from those conversations. It includes a desktop application with an embedded protocol server for pipeline management and remote execution, an online registry for pipeline and plugin discovery, a web-based results viewer, and a command-line tool for plugin customization. The pragmatic point is that this is infrastructure not algorithm — the agent that runs the conversation is plug-replaceable, and the value is in giving researchers without server-administration experience a path from a prompt-driven analysis to a containerized auditable pipeline that survives the next model version. It does not solve the bioinformatics-agent quality problem — that is the territory of benchmarks like the one we covered yesterday — but it does solve the more boring and arguably more important reproducibility problem, and if it gets adoption, it makes the agent benchmark scores actually mean something operationally.
