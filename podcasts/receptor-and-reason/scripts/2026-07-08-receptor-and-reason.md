## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. It's Wednesday, July eighth. Ten items today. The through-line is circuits and pharmacology: a lead story on how motor cortical stimulation achieves durable analgesia through endogenous opioid peptides and NMDA plasticity in the brainstem, a hypothalamic circuit that calibrates the HPA axis to stressor intensity, computational signatures of psychiatric subtype, and two mPFC organization papers that converge on how the prefrontal cortex uses context and prediction error to drive flexible behavior. Around those, a psychedelic visual processing paper, a glia homeostatic mechanism, a BBB delivery structure, and we close on the expanded SEA-AD multiregional atlas.

Lead item — from the Scherrer and Schnitzer groups at Stanford, on motor cortical stimulation as a route to lasting analgesia via the brain's own opioid peptides. The clinical context first. Motor cortex stimulation, delivered transcranially or via implanted electrodes, has been known since the 1990s to relieve chronic neuropathic pain. Responses can last weeks after a single session. Nobody understood why. This paper builds a mechanism from scratch. They created a miniaturized, millimeter-scale transcranial magnetic stimulation device for mice — small enough to target only the motor cortex and validate with histology and electrophysiology. In mice with neuropathic pain from chronic constriction injury, a single TMS session reduced reflexive and affective pain behaviors for one to two weeks. Working out the circuit, they show that the relevant output is layer-5 pyramidal neurons of motor cortex with axonal projections to the rostral ventromedial medulla, the brainstem's descending pain control hub. TMS shifts the balance of RVM activity — the classic ON-cells and OFF-cells that gate dorsal horn pain gating — toward a state that suppresses pain. Then the pharmacology. Blocking NMDA receptors abolished the lasting effect. Blocking mu-opioid receptors in the RVM also abolished it. The sequence appears to be: motor cortical input drives NMDA-dependent synaptic plasticity in the RVM, and that plasticity is expression-gated by endogenous opioid peptides acting through mu-opioid receptors. The key translational experiment — injecting an enkephalinase inhibitor, which prolongs the lifetime of endogenously released enkephalins, into the RVM during TMS treatment amplified both the magnitude and the duration of analgesia. And a retrospective analysis of human clinical data supported the same idea: co-administration of exogenous opioids during motor cortex stimulation appeared to augment the analgesic response. The significance here runs in two directions. First, this is a mechanistic validation of enkephalinase inhibition in the RVM as a pharmacological target, something the field has discussed since Porecca's work in the 2000s but never had a circuit-level treatment rationale for. Second, it argues that neurostimulation and opioid-system pharmacology are genuinely synergistic at the circuit level — not additive noise, but a mechanistic interaction where the drug extends the plasticity window opened by the stimulation. That is a concrete hypothesis for a combination trial. The patent application from Stanford is already filed.

Second item, sticking with hypothalamic stress pharmacology — a paper out of a Chinese group on how the arcuate nucleus gates the scalability of HPA-axis responses to stressors of different intensities. Background: PVH CRH neurons are the primary regulators of the HPA axis; they receive dense GABAergic input that holds them in check. Where does that GABA come from? This paper asks the question in a circuit-specific way using retrograde tracing and shows that the arcuate nucleus provides the most robust source of GABAergic input to PVH CRH neurons — not AgRP neurons or TH-expressing neurons, but a different, molecularly distinct non-AgRP, non-TH Arc GABA population. Then the stressor-intensity experiment. Mild stressors — a loud sound, a puff of air, water spray — produce time-locked activation of PVH CRH neurons but no change in GABA release onto them and no change in the activity of their Arc GABA inputs. High-intensity stressors — tail restraint, physical restraint — produce the same PVH CRH activation but now also a robust reduction in both GABA release and Arc GABA neuron activity. The anticorrelation between Arc GABA activity and PVH CRH activity during high-intensity stress means the circuit is scaling up the HPA response by releasing it from inhibition, not just passively allowing the excitatory drive to win. Selective silencing of PVH CRH-projecting Arc GABA neurons phenocopied the high-intensity stressor response; selective activation suppressed it. The clinical translation: this is a defined non-AgRP Arc circuit whose activity is selectively reduced under the most pathological stress conditions — the type that produces PTSD and anxiety disorders — and that can be pharmacologically targeted. The Arc is reachable by peripherally-administered compounds that penetrate the median eminence and arcuate, including neuropeptide analogs and the GLP-1 agonist class, which has established Arc GABA effects. Whether this specific population is engaged by any existing drug is not tested, but the circuit exists and it controls a dial that goes directly to adrenal output.

Third — a computational psychiatry paper from a group in the Netherlands that makes a clean double dissociation between two types of learning failure and two psychiatric dimensions. The setup: adaptive behavior requires distinguishing two sources of outcome variance — volatility, which is genuine change in the environmental state, and stochasticity, which is moment-to-moment noise. They demand opposite adjustments to learning rate: volatility calls for faster updating; stochasticity calls for slower, because fast updating in a noisy environment chases phantom signals. The inference problem is hard because both inflate experienced variance, so individual differences in how the brain decomposes them are both likely and consequential. The paper identifies three computational phenotypes: intact learners; stochasticity-blind learners who over-update because they treat noise as meaningful change; and volatility-blind learners who under-update because they treat real environmental change as noise. Across two large online samples and three learning tasks they find a double dissociation: stochasticity-blind learners score higher on internalizing dimensions, meaning anxiety and depression; volatility-blind learners score higher on externalizing dimensions, meaning behavioral addiction and compulsivity. This is not just a curiosity. It means the two most common clusters of psychiatric symptoms map onto distinct computational failures at the learning-under-uncertainty level — and those failures predict opposite adjustments to learning rates, opposite behavioral strategies, and presumably opposite neurochemical targets. The internalizing-to-noise-sensitivity link connects naturally to serotonin, where the classic 5-HTTLPR literature has long argued for heightened threat-signal salience. The externalizing-to-volatility-blindness link fits the dopamine literature on aberrant salience and compulsive updating to reward. Whether these phenotypes respond to current drugs differently is an open question that this paper nominates as worth testing.

Fourth — from the Tank and Brody groups at Princeton, on how hippocampal memory traces configure prefrontal computation in real time. They tagged hippocampal engrams in a context-dependent task-switching paradigm in mice and then optogenetically reactivated those engrams while simultaneously recording hundreds of neurons in medial prefrontal cortex. The key result: reactivating a hippocampal engram for a given context reinstated that context's representation in mPFC within hundreds of milliseconds, and animals applied engram-consistent decision rules in the subsequent choice. The reinstated mPFC state was not a vague bias signal — it was a specific population-level reconfiguration from which you could decode which context the HPC was instructing. Other aspects of endogenous mPFC dynamics, like ramping and preparation signals, were left intact. The conceptual contribution is that hippocampal engrams are circuit configurators, not just memory stores — they do not just trigger responses, they set a state in downstream cortical circuits that then runs the computations needed for flexible context-dependent behavior. For a pharmacology audience, the relevance is that mPFC flexibility is a core impairment in most psychiatric conditions, and this work identifies the HPC-to-mPFC rapid-reconfiguration axis as a mechanistic substrate. Drugs that impair hippocampal memory retrieval — NMDA antagonists, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics — should by this mechanism specifically disrupt mPFC flexibility by preventing engram-driven context reinstatement, and that is a testable prediction in humans.

Fifth, staying in prefrontal cortex — a paper from the Kuner lab at Heidelberg using longitudinal miniscope calcium imaging to resolve a long-standing contradiction about infralimbic cortex function. The IL has been described, depending on the study, as both a facilitator of reward-seeking and a terminator of it. Lesion and optogenetic-inhibition studies give opposite results depending on task phase. This paper's proposal is a dual-ensemble model: there is an action-outcome predictor ensemble that sharpens its activity as contingencies stabilize, and a prediction correction ensemble that activates specifically in response to reward omission at extinction onset. As learning progresses, population-level entropy decreases in the predictor ensemble, reflecting a refined internal model of what the action produces. When the outcome stops matching the prediction, the correction ensemble fires. This two-ensemble architecture is sufficient to recapitulate the contradictory IL manipulation results: you can pharmacologically disrupt the predictor ensemble and get one behavioral outcome, and disrupt the correction ensemble and get the opposite. For the addiction and anxiety fields — where extinction-based exposure therapies live or die on infralimbic function — this is a better mechanistic model than the simple facilitator-inhibitor dichotomy. It also predicts that the success of extinction-based interventions should depend on which ensemble you can preferentially engage, which is a parameter that varies with how error signals are delivered.

Sixth — social motivation and the subthalamic nucleus, from a group in Marseille. The STN is traditionally a motor-circuit structure, the target of deep brain stimulation for Parkinson's. It has a side-effect literature in DBS patients that includes flattened emotional processing and altered social behavior, but the causal specificity of those effects has never been resolved at the circuit level. This paper uses ArchT3.0 optogenetic inhibition of the STN in rats performing a social operant task — pressing a lever to gain thirty seconds of access to a peer — under both low-effort fixed-ratio and high-effort progressive-ratio schedules. Two results. First, in males, STN inhibition specifically blocked the familiarity-driven reduction in social interest: control rats normally worked less to interact with a familiar cagemate than with a stranger, and this hierarchy and familiarity effect disappeared when the STN was inhibited. Second, under progressive ratio, STN inhibition reduced motivation for social interaction regardless of familiarity or social rank — so the STN is doing something at the effortful-social-motivation level that is independent of the novelty signal. Female rats did not show the familiarity or dominance effects in the first place, which is a genuine sex difference that the paper documents but doesn't explain. The clinical implication: the STN is not just a motor gate, it is part of a social value circuit that modulates the rewarding properties of familiar social interaction in males. That's relevant for DBS side effects in Parkinson's patients, and it's relevant for psychiatric conditions like autism and schizophrenia where social motivation is specifically impaired.

Seventh — psychedelics and visual processing, from the Niell lab at Oregon, in freely moving mice. The serotonergic psychedelic they use is DOI, a 5-HT2A and 5-HT2C agonist that is reliable and long-lasting in rodents. They record from primary visual cortex in animals free to move around an arena while simultaneously tracking eye and head position. DOI does two things. First, it increases the frequency of active sensing behaviors — gaze shifts, exploratory head movements — so animals on DOI are attending more actively to visual input, not less. Second, it reduces the visually-evoked response in V1 generated by those gaze shifts. The effect is laminar-specific — suppression and facilitation of different subpopulations, not uniform silencing. Critically, DOI did not affect visually-evoked responses to flashed sparse noise in the same animals during head-fixed sessions. That dissociation between behavior-coupled and externally-driven visual responses, controlled only by whether the stimulus was predictable or self-generated, points to a selectivity-filter account: DOI may be disrupting the brain's mechanism for predicting and attenuating self-generated sensory signals — the corollary-discharge suppression that normally reduces the impact of self-generated visual input. Losing that filter means every gaze shift now arrives with unexpectedly full sensory weight. That is mechanistically coherent with the visual distortions that psychedelics produce in humans — not hallucination from nowhere, but over-significance assigned to every act of looking. For 5-HT2A pharmacology, this is a useful mechanistic frame: agonists and antagonists at 5-HT2A in visual cortex may modulate the gain on self-generated versus externally-driven visual signals differently.

Eighth — a short item on a new glia homeostatic mechanism from Long-Jun Wu's group at Mayo. They ask how glial cells detect and respond to neuronal hypoactivity — the circuit-gain-lowered state that occurs during deep anesthesia, slow-wave sleep, or pathological silencing. The answer involves a three-way interaction. Cortical hypoactivity, detected via reduced purinergic tone, triggers ATP release from astrocytes through pannexin-1 hemichannels. These hemichannels are structurally enriched near perisomatic parvalbumin-positive GABAergic boutons. The released ATP acts on microglial P2Y12 receptors, driving microglial process movement to form bulbous endings that physically contact perisomatic synapses. When pannexin-1 in astrocytes or P2Y12 in microglia was knocked out, bulbous endings did not form, and the normal rebound increase in neuronal activity during emergence from anesthesia was abolished. The same pattern — astrocytic ATP release and bulbous-ending formation — was observed during chemogenetic neuronal silencing and during natural slow-wave sleep. So the pathway is astrocyte-sensed hypoactivity to microglial-mediated synaptic shielding to circuit rebound. Pannexin-1 and P2Y12 are both druggable — probenecid inhibits pannexin-1, and clopidogrel inhibits P2Y12 — so this is a pharmacologically tractable homeostatic circuit. The interest for psychiatry is that states of chronic cortical hypoactivity, which appear in depression and post-traumatic syndromes, might involve a breakdown of exactly this rebound mechanism.

Ninth — a structural pharmacology item on BBB delivery, from a collaboration between Cambridge and AstraZeneca. The transferrin receptor has become the default target for transcytosis-based brain-delivery of antibody payloads — the mechanism behind several CNS biologics programs including an approved anti-amyloid shuttle approach. The model shuttle antibody is 8D3. This paper solves the cryo-EM structure of mouse transferrin receptor one bound to 8D3 and asks why 8D3 achieves the brain penetration it does without getting degraded in endosomes. The structural answer — 8D3 is geometrically positioned to form small-scale inter-receptor cross-links, specifically in pairs, and those paired cross-links generate avidity without clustering enough receptors to trigger redistribution or degradation. That is the sweet spot: enough receptor engagement to drive transcytosis, not enough to trigger lysosomal routing. The second result is on pH-dependent binding variants. 8D3 variants with pH-sensitive binding — tighter at physiological pH, weaker at endosomal pH — grade brain penetration quantitatively in vivo, consistent with the idea that pH sensitivity at the endosome facilitates cargo release before lysosomal trafficking. The design rules from this structure — pair-level cross-linking for avidity, pH-tuned affinity for release — are generalizable to any transferrin-receptor-targeting shuttle design, and this matters because the field has multiple CNS-biologic programs where the delivery problem is the bottleneck.

Closing item — the expanded Seattle Alzheimer's Disease Brain Cell Atlas from Ed Lein's group at Allen Institute. They profile approximately seven million nuclei from eighty-four donors across ten neo- and allocortical regions spanning the canonical pathological staging arc of Alzheimer's, with single-nucleus RNA-seq, ATAC-seq, and multiome, plus quantitative neuropathology and whole-genome sequencing. They map everything to a reference taxonomy of 207 cell types. The major findings. About thirty percent of cell types shift in abundance with disease, and those that do move consistently across brain regions — this is not region-specific noise, it is a coordinated cellular program. The earliest losses are specific inhibitory interneuron subclasses: Sst, Lamp5, Vip, Sncg, and Pvalb populations are depleted in preclinical donors with minimal pathology, alongside early emergence of AD-associated microglia. L2/3 and selected deep-layer excitatory types are lost later. There is a regionally specialized finding that is particularly interesting: V1 primary visual cortex contains a specialized layer-4 excitatory neuron type that was thought to be resilient to AD and is in fact lost late in disease. Working backward with a multi-agent AI workflow that built literature-grounded hypotheses from differential expression, the team nominates hyperexcitability mediated by high NMDA receptor expression as the convergent vulnerability phenotype for those V1 neurons. A parallel analysis of the pan-cortical Sst interneurons — which are lost earliest — also converges on hyperexcitability through partly distinct pathways, and those vulnerable Sst neurons are enriched for expression of AD GWAS-prioritized genes. So vulnerability maps onto the genetic architecture of AD and onto a shared physiological phenotype of hyperexcitability. The pharmacological implication — NMDA-modulating strategies may specifically address the most vulnerable cell populations, in both the inhibitory interneuron arm and the specialized excitatory arm, and the cell-type-resolved transcriptomic data at SEA-AD.org is a public target nomination resource. That's a strong closer.
