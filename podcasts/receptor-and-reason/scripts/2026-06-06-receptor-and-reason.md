## Script

Alan, this is Receptor and Reason for Saturday, June 6th, 2026. Ten items today, leading with a druggable Parkinson lipid-LRRK2 axis, working through alpha-synuclein and amyloid-beta mechanism papers, into cholinergic and astrocyte neurochemistry, through three substance-use translational items, and closing with computational pharmacology — including a 5-HT1B triptan derivative design and an agentic spatial-omics platform.

Opening with what I think is the most interesting item of the day. From Chiara Zurzolo's group at the Institut Pasteur, with Michela Deleidi as a key co-corresponding, the identification of N-acylphosphatidylethanolamines — NAPEs — as endogenous lipid regulators of LRRK2 kinase activity in Parkinson's disease. Some quick framing — LRRK2 is one of the most prominent genetic drivers of familial Parkinson's, and its gain-of-function kinase variants, especially G2019S, have made small-molecule LRRK2 kinase inhibitors one of the most heavily prosecuted target classes in PD drug discovery. NAPEs, on the other hand, are atypical glycerophospholipids that accumulate under cellular stress, and they're best known as precursors of fatty-acid ethanolamides — the family that includes the endocannabinoid anandamide. Until now the assumption has been that the NAPE-precursor role was the main story; signaling roles of NAPEs themselves were largely ignored. The team shows that increasing NAPE levels — either by boosting NAPE synthesis or by inhibiting NAPE-phospholipase D, the enzyme that degrades them — suppresses LRRK2 kinase activity, restores lysosomal function, and accelerates clearance of alpha-synuclein aggregates. The reciprocal manipulation, lowering NAPEs, increases LRRK2 activation and lysosomal dysfunction. Critically, they take this into induced pluripotent stem cell-derived dopaminergic neurons carrying the LRRK2 G2019S variant — the disease-relevant cell context — and show pharmacological inhibition of NAPE-PLD restores lysosomal activity and clears preformed alpha-synuclein fibrils. Why this matters — direct LRRK2 kinase inhibitors have run into peripheral safety issues at doses needed for CNS engagement, particularly in lung. A NAPE-PLD inhibitor would act upstream through an endogenous lipid signal, potentially providing a wider therapeutic window. There is a long history of disappointing endocannabinoid-system drug development in neurodegeneration — the FAAH inhibitor era — so I'd want to see whether NAPE-PLD inhibition actually translates without engaging the same off-target pharmacology. But as a mechanistic finding, this is the kind of upstream-of-LRRK2 result that motivates a new chemical biology campaign and reframes a lipid pathway most people had filed under endocannabinoid metabolism only.

Staying with dopaminergic neuron biology — from the Sulzer and Bartolini labs at Columbia, a mechanism for how alpha-synuclein regulates dopamine release at presynaptic terminals. Alpha-synuclein has been linked to synaptic regulation for two decades but the molecular basis has been frustratingly elusive — most studies have anchored to synaptic vesicle binding without producing a clean handle on what alpha-synuclein actually does at active boutons. This paper proposes a microtubule-nucleation mechanism. Using live imaging of cultured dopaminergic neurons, the team visualizes dynamic microtubules at individual presynaptic boutons and shows that neuronal activity triggers local gamma-tubulin-dependent microtubule nucleation, which is required for synaptic vesicle transport between boutons and for sustained dopamine release during high activity. They then show alpha-synuclein binds directly to gamma-tubulin and to the alpha-beta tubulin heterodimer, and acts as a positive regulator of nucleation. The phosphorylation hook is the cleanest part — activity-evoked phosphorylation of alpha-synuclein at serine 129, which is the modification that accumulates in synucleinopathies and is the standard pathological marker, occurs in the alpha-beta tubulin binding region and is both necessary and sufficient for microtubule initiation. The implication — serine 129 phosphorylation isn't just a passive pathology marker, it's an activity-coupled regulator of dopamine release, and chronic dysregulation could plausibly drive dopaminergic synaptic dysfunction by perturbing the on-demand vesicle redistribution needed during high firing rates. This reframes how we should think about serine 129 in synuclein biology and gives a more concrete biochemical handle on activity-dependent dysfunction in early PD.

A second neurodegenerative mechanism paper, this time on Alzheimer's — from the Selkoe lab at Brigham and Women's, on direct binding between amyloid-beta aggregates and the U1 spliceosomal ribonucleoprotein. Some context — there's a well-established but mechanistically murky observation that U1 small nuclear RNP is insolubilized and dysfunctional in Alzheimer brain, with downstream missplicing of disease-relevant transcripts. The prevailing assumption has been that this is a tau-driven phenomenon, since the cytoplasmic U1 mislocalization partly co-localizes with tau neurofibrillary tangles. But there have been clues that didn't fit — proteomics consistently shows that insoluble U1 RNA-binding proteins correlate better with amyloid-beta than with tau. This paper resolves the discrepancy. While studying amyloid-beta aggregates that diffuse out of AD brain tissue, the team found that some are bound to small RNA, and immunoprecipitation plus deep sequencing identified U1 small nuclear RNA specifically. Double immunoelectron microscopy then showed that amyloid-beta fibrils, more than tau fibrils, are decorated with U1-70k protein, and immunofluorescence revealed labeling of a subset of amyloid plaques with anti-U1-70k antibodies, with co-localizing U1 snRNA. So a subset of AD amyloid-beta aggregates carries the U1 snRNP at the edges of plaques. This unites amyloid-beta with the family of RNA-binding aggregating proteins — TDP-43, FUS, the C9orf72 dipeptides — whose aggregation biology is intimately tied to RNA binding. The translational implication is incremental but real — splicing dysfunction may be an amyloid-beta-driven mechanism, not purely a tau-driven one, which would shift the case for splicing-modifying interventions in early AD where amyloid pathology dominates.

Shifting from aggregation to neurochemistry — from Dan Polley's group at Mass Eye and Ear and Harvard Medical School, optic fiber recordings of a genetically encoded acetylcholine sensor in auditory, visual, and prefrontal cortex of awake head-fixed mice. The motivation is methodological — the classical view of basal forebrain cholinergic projections has been of a slow, spatially diffuse arousal signal, but recent high-resolution sensor work has revealed rapid local cholinergic modulation. The problem is that sensory events that recruit cortical acetylcholine also change arousal and evoke movements, so it's been hard to separate stimulus-locked acetylcholine from arousal-locked acetylcholine. This paper does that separation. Across all three cortical areas, acetylcholine tracks spontaneous arousal fluctuations indexed by pupil dilation and orofacial movements. Sensory stimuli also evoke rapid acetylcholine transients, with sounds producing larger and more widespread responses than visual stimuli. The key result is a regional dissociation extracted by multivariate modeling — sound-evoked acetylcholine release in prefrontal cortex is largely explained by arousal and movement, whereas in auditory cortex acetylcholine release retains a stronger relationship to stimulus features themselves. So the cholinergic system multiplexes — area-specific sensory processing layered onto a shared arousal signal. For drug discovery this matters because cholinergic targets in psychiatric and neurological indications — donepezil for AD, xanomeline-trospium in schizophrenia, varenicline in nicotine and cognition contexts — are being evaluated against behavioral readouts that mix arousal and stimulus processing. Cortical-area-specific cholinergic dissociation should be incorporated when interpreting which of those functions a cholinergic intervention is engaging.

Carrying acetylcholine forward into substance use — from Ritchie Huda's lab at Rutgers, dorsal striatal circuit mechanisms for astrocyte modulation of alcohol responses. The dorsolateral striatum coordinates the alcohol-induced stimulant response, which is itself predictive of future alcohol use disorder, but the non-neuronal contribution has been understudied. The team used ex vivo two-photon calcium imaging, in vivo fiber photometry of both astrocyte and neuronal calcium signals, and astrocyte-specific manipulations in mice. Intraperitoneal ethanol at 0.5 to 2 grams per kilogram produced the expected locomotor stimulation while dose-dependently decreasing astrocyte calcium activity, with minimal direct effect on direct- or indirect-pathway neuronal activity. To establish causality, they expressed CalEX, an astrocyte-specific calcium-extruding pump, to mimic the reduction in astrocyte calcium — and that manipulation alone facilitated ethanol stimulation. So dorsolateral striatal astrocyte calcium suppression contributes causally to the stimulant response. They then asked whether the cholinergic system modulates this astrocyte effect. Fiber photometry with the GRAB-acetylcholine sensor showed that acute ethanol also inhibited acetylcholine release in dorsolateral striatum. But — chemogenetic stimulation of striatal cholinergic interneurons to artificially restore acetylcholine release did not rescue astrocyte calcium activity under ethanol. So acetylcholine release and astrocyte calcium are both suppressed by ethanol but they are not in a simple causal upstream-downstream relationship in this circuit. The non-null astrocyte causality is the interesting piece — astrocyte calcium signaling is now a tractable target for AUD pharmacology, and the calcium-extrusion-via-CalEX manipulation suggests astrocyte-selective Gi-coupled DREADD or chemogenetic approaches could be productive starting points.

Sticking with substance-use neuropharmacology — from Max Joffe's lab at Pittsburgh, validation of an oral xylazine self-administration model in mice. Xylazine is the alpha-2 adrenergic agonist veterinary sedative that has become a pervasive adulterant in illicit opioid supply over the past several years, with serious clinical sequelae including necrotic skin ulcers and a withdrawal syndrome that doesn't respond to standard opioid pharmacotherapy. The long-term cognitive and motivational consequences are essentially unknown. The team developed a four-hour drinking-session model with xylazine in the only available drinking water at concentrations from 0.01 to 1 milligram per milliliter. Oral bioavailability and brain penetrance were confirmed by mass spectrometry. The behavioral phenotype — male and female mice both decreased fluid intake at high concentrations, with males consuming less than females at intermediate doses, and females showing decreased locomotion after sessions. Brain xylazine concentrations after a session exceeded the affinity constants and EC50 values of several known xylazine targets — meaning the doses achieved are pharmacologically meaningful, not just nominal. In a three-bottle choice setup with xylazine water, fentanyl water, and plain water, mice consumed less xylazine than either fentanyl or plain water — so xylazine on its own isn't reinforcing in the standard sense. That's an important negative result. It supports the clinical narrative that xylazine isn't being chased for its own effects — it's a contaminant — which means the right experimental setup for asking about long-term xylazine consequences is polysubstance, and this model is built for exactly that. A solid translational tool paper that the field needed.

A third substance-use item — from Elizabeth West's lab at Rowan, behavioral flexibility and gut microbiome composition as joint predictors of oxycodone self-administration in rats. The framing is the still-relevant question of why some individuals taking prescribed opioids transition to misuse while others don't. The team established an oral oxycodone self-administration model in Long-Evans rats and split animals into high-intake and low-intake phenotypes that emerge spontaneously. They then assessed behavioral flexibility using a contingency-degradation task, and characterized the gut microbiome. Two findings — behavioral flexibility correlated with oxycodone intake, identifying it as a potential pre-existing behavioral biomarker of vulnerability, and oxycodone exposure itself altered gut microbiome composition, with microbiome features associated with both behavioral flexibility and drug-taking. The framework being argued for is multifactorial — individual differences in intake arise from the interaction of pre-existing behavioral traits with biological state including gut microbiome. I'd treat this as hypothesis-generating rather than a clinically actionable biomarker right now — the contingency-degradation task is a research instrument and gut microbiome features are notoriously hard to translate across species. But the framing is the right one — the field has been searching for single biomarkers of opioid use disorder vulnerability for years without much luck, and a multivariate behavioral-plus-microbiome readout has more degrees of freedom to actually map onto the heterogeneity that exists in humans.

Crossing into computational pharmacology now — from the Siegel lab at UC Davis, a computational design study of two new 5-HT1B serotonin receptor agonists derived from naratriptan. Migraine pharmacology already has the triptan class targeting 5-HT1B and 5-HT1D — but the existing compounds have well-known limitations around vasoconstriction risk, contraindications in cardiovascular disease, and inconsistent response across patients. The newer CGRP-targeted agents have shifted some of the conversation but triptan pharmacology is still the workhorse for acute treatment. The team used structure-based modeling to design two new ligands derived from naratriptan and report improved docking scores at the 5-HT1B binding pocket compared to the parent compound. This is a very early-stage paper — docking scores alone don't predict ligand binding affinity, let alone selectivity over 5-HT1D and 5-HT2B, agonist efficacy, or the CNS pharmacokinetic profile triptan optimization requires. They explicitly acknowledge that the next steps are synthesis feasibility and laboratory characterization. I'm including it because it's a clean example of the kind of computational pharmacology our audience is interested in — anchored on a clinically used scaffold, against a structurally well-characterized GPCR with known crystal structures, with an explicit design rationale. The honest assessment of what is and isn't known at this stage is also worth highlighting in a literature that increasingly overclaims for AI-driven small molecule design.

Continuing with computational pharmacology, more applied — from QuantisMol, an evaluation of a pretrained graph neural network for predicting P-glycoprotein substrate status on the Therapeutics Data Commons benchmark. Some quick framing — P-glycoprotein is the major efflux transporter at the blood-brain barrier and at the intestinal lumen, and it's a critical determinant of CNS drug exposure. Substrate-status prediction is a workhorse ADMET task that every drug discovery program runs early. The team uses a Graph Isomorphism Network — GIN — pretrained on roughly two million molecules with a self-supervised attribute-masking strategy, then fine-tuned with a small MLP classification head on the Pgp-Broccatelli benchmark. They report AUROC 0.937 across five independent runs, ranking second on the TDC leaderboard as of May 2026, and outperform an XGBoost baseline on Morgan fingerprints at 0.912. The takeaway is incremental but useful — pretrained graph-based representations with transfer learning do beat traditional fingerprint baselines on small-dataset ADMET tasks, and the gap is consistent rather than marginal. The Therapeutics Data Commons benchmark suite is becoming the de facto standard for these comparisons, which is the right development — ADMET prediction has been dominated by anecdotal model claims for too long, and a leaderboard with shared splits forces actual comparison. Not a paradigm-shift result, but a clean confirmatory data point.

Closing on agentic AI in computational biology — from Mayo Clinic, a tool called ChatSpatial for orchestrating spatial transcriptomics analysis. Spatial transcriptomics is now in the same uncomfortable methodological situation that single-cell RNA-seq was in three years ago — dozens of computational methods, incompatible Python and R toolchains, and most of the researcher time goes into infrastructure rather than biology. The team's design choice is the interesting one. Instead of letting an LLM generate free-form code — the failure mode where the model invents nonexistent function signatures, drops parameters, or stitches incompatible workflows — they have the LLM select from pre-validated tool schemas, with domain expertise embedded in the schema descriptions themselves so parameter inference is context-aware. It's built on the Model Context Protocol — MCP — which is the open standard for connecting LLM agents to external tools. The platform unifies more than 60 methods across 15 analytical categories spanning both Python and R. The validation matters — they replicate two published studies recovering subclonal heterogeneity in ovarian cancer and tumor microenvironment organization in oral squamous cell carcinoma, and run cross-platform validation across seven different LLM backbones to demonstrate that the schema-enforced orchestration produces near-deterministic reproducibility at the workflow level. That last point is the one to emphasize for our audience — the schema-enforcement-not-code-generation approach is a credible answer to the reproducibility problem that has been the fundamental blocker for LLM-driven bioinformatics workflows. The reason agentic-AI work in biology hasn't replaced human bioinformaticians yet isn't that the LLMs aren't smart enough, it's that they aren't reproducible enough across runs and across models. Pre-validated tool schemas, swappable LLM backbones, deterministic workflows — that's the architecture the field needs to converge on if these systems are going to be trusted in real analytical pipelines.

Closing thoughts. The two reusable themes today were the upstream-of-the-classical-target findings — NAPEs upstream of LRRK2, amyloid-beta upstream of U1 snRNP splicing dysfunction — and the multifactorial-substrate-of-vulnerability work in substance use. Both patterns point to the same direction of travel — biology is more interconnected than the simple receptor-by-receptor or pathology-by-pathology framing of the last drug-discovery cycle, and the most useful new findings are often the ones that connect two domains that the field has been treating as separate. That's it for today.
