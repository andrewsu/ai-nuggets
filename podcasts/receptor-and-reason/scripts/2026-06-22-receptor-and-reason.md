## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason for Monday, June 22nd, 2026. Ten items today — a tight cluster of neurodegeneration and CNS drug discovery in the front half, an honest negative result on microglia and alcohol use disorder in the middle, then a turn into AI agents in pharmacology and protein and RNA drug discovery, with a hepatic pharmacokinetics piece to close.

We open with the most chemically unusual paper of the week. A group at Mass General has posted a preprint on ADLumin-5, a self-photosensitizing chemiluminescence compound. The framing is straight at the heart of the misfolded-protein problem in neurodegeneration. They show that ADLumin-5 emits light intramolecularly — molecularly produced light, in their phrase — and that the photons it generates drive photo-oxidation and photodegradation of beta-amyloid, tau, alpha-synuclein, and TDP-43 in vitro, validated by LC-MS, MALDI-MS, and Western. With beta-amyloid as the test case, oxidized and degraded protein loses toxicity, and four months of dosing in 5xFAD mice lowers amyloid by in-vivo molecular imaging. Then the dual-use twist: because ADLumin-5 is both the photosensitizer and the imaging agent, it is described as a photo-theranostic. Bioxiv hasn't rendered the full body yet for this preprint — it landed in the last twenty-four hours — so I'm taking this off the abstract, and the mechanistic details around tissue depth, photon yield, and selectivity are what I'd want to see in the body. But the modality is interesting. Light-driven protein degradation outside the lab has historically been blocked by the need for external illumination, which doesn't reach deep brain. A molecule that lights itself up sidesteps that problem entirely, and if the selectivity for amyloid conformers holds, it sits in a different therapeutic class from monoclonal antibodies and from small-molecule aggregation inhibitors.

Staying on amyloid but on the diagnostic side. A group in Chieti has run a structural-bioinformatics analysis of the amyloid PET tracers — Pittsburgh Compound-B, flutemetamol, and flutafuranol — against brain-expressed sulfotransferases. SULTs are sulfo-transfer enzymes, and there's been a longstanding observation that PiB binds SULT1A1 off-target. The Chieti work extends this to the two clinically used fluorinated derivatives. They mine GTEx and Human Protein Atlas to identify SULT1A1, SULT1A3, and SULT4A1 as the brain-expressed isoforms, then run molecular docking and molecular dynamics on all three tracers against all three enzymes. The differential matters. PiB and flutemetamol sit stably in SULT1A1's catalytic pocket, but PiB drifts out of SULT4A1. Flutafuranol binds weakly to SULT1A1 but forms stable complexes with SULT1A3 and SULT4A1. The kicker is that all three SULTs are expressed in the cerebellum — which is the reference region used to normalize amyloid PET signals. So the off-target binding isn't a contamination in the disease-region signal — it's potentially in the denominator. That changes how a clinician should interpret subtle tracer-to-tracer disagreement in flutemetamol-versus-flutafuranol scans, and it makes the case that SULT-binding profiles should be a routine part of how the next generation of amyloid tracers gets evaluated. It also adds context to the persistent finding that some PET-positive subjects don't have postmortem amyloid loads that match — intracellular SULT binding could account for some of that variance.

A complementary piece on a clinical-stage amyloid antibody. A MODEL-AD consortium paper from the Dage group treats male and female 5xFAD mice with murine chimeric aducanumab — chAdu — weekly from eight months out to seventeen weeks of treatment, side by side with murine IgG2a-kappa isotype and saline controls. The MODEL-AD framing is deliberately translational: they're trying to define the molecular state that persists after amyloid lowering, because that's the real efficacy gap aducanumab is up against in clinic. Three results that the abstract carries cleanly. First, sex-dependent PK and PD — chAdu raises plasma Aβ42 over Aβ40 and lowers brain amyloid in a sex-dependent way, but produces only mild behavioral changes, no actual cognitive improvement. Second, the IgG isotype control, not just chAdu, produces similar brain amyloid reductions, which is to say nonspecific IgG effects are doing real biological work — this is consistent with the Fc-receptor-mediated microglial activation literature, but seeing it cleanly in this design is useful. Third, treatment-emergent anti-drug antibodies in ten percent of treated mice, with measurable loss of exposure and efficacy. And the multi-omics back end identifies disease-associated transcripts and proteins that simply don't move with chAdu treatment even when amyloid does — the persistent signature that any next-generation AD therapeutic will need to address. For an audience watching the post-aducanumab landscape, this paper is the kind of preclinical readout that you'd want anchoring lead-optimization decisions on combination approaches.

From clinical-stage AD to TBI. A team at Rubicon Biotechnology, with Walter Reed and Stanford, has posted a preclinical package on Fv-HSP72 — a humanized 3E10 single-chain Fv antibody fragment fused to human heat shock protein 72 with a cleavable linker. 3E10 binds extracellular DNA at sites of tissue injury and shuttles cargo through ENT2 into stressed cells. HSP72 is the cytoprotective chaperone. The fusion is engineered with one of two linkers — a swivel linker or a cathepsin-B-cleavable linker — so that HSP72 is released by cathepsin B, which is upregulated in injured tissue. They blast male Sprague-Dawley rats with a twenty-psi advanced-blast-simulator pulse and dose a single intravenous injection of one of three Fv-HSP72 variants at fifteen minutes post-injury. The clinical-candidate identification path is laid out: three biomarker classes — 3-nitrotyrosine for oxidative stress, phosphorylated tau at T181, T231, and S396 for neurodegeneration, and GFAP plus S100A10 and C3d for glial state — distinguish the variants. RBB010 is dropped because it doesn't lower tau-S396 or GFAP; RBB012-CTB, the cathepsin-B-cleavable variant, is selected. Then the behavioral piece: a single 30 mg/kg dose of RBB012-CTB at 15 minutes post-blast preserves novel-object discrimination at two days and at eight days post-injury, with sham-equivalent performance. The biomarker work corroborates — pTau and GFAP suppression sustained through day nine, and astrogliosis biased toward the neuroprotective S100A10-positive A2 state rather than the C3d-positive A1 state. PK is the part you'd most want to see: plasma clearance is fast, sub-LLOQ by four hours, but brain retention is preserved out to four hours and significantly higher in TBI rats than naïve rats at twelve hours — the drug stays where the BBB is leaky and clears where it isn't. That is the clean version of the targeted-cargo argument for ENT2-mediated uptake. As a CNS-cargo platform this is interesting because the same engineering — DNA-binding-Fv plus cleavable chaperone payload — extends to a lot of acute neurodegenerative settings where transient BBB compromise is the entry pathway.

Adjacent to the amyloid-clearance side of AD, an updated preprint from a group studying INPP5D — the gene encoding SHIP1 — in human iPSC-derived microglia. INPP5D is a top-tier AD-risk locus, and the prevailing model has been that SHIP1 suppresses microglial inflammation. This paper sharpens the mechanism considerably. SHIP1 localizes to plasma membrane and endolysosomal compartments and binds the CapZ family, which is required for endosome maturation. CRISPR reduction of SHIP1 impairs endosome maturation and lysosomal function, lipid droplets accumulate, and cathepsin B leaks out of lysosomes into cytosol, which activates the NLRP3 inflammasome. So the inflammation phenotype is downstream of a lysosomal failure, not a primary signaling defect. CITE-seq on SHIP1-deficient microglia shows a shift from an immune-responsive state toward a DAM-like phagocytic state — increased uptake of synaptic material and apoptotic neurons through TREM2. And the surprising clinical pivot: amyloid-beta uptake itself is unchanged in SHIP1-deficient microglia, but degradation fails, so amyloid accumulates intracellularly. That reframes INPP5D as a lysosomal-function gene, and it predicts that the therapeutic intervention for INPP5D-associated AD risk is on lysosomal acidification and proteolytic capacity, not on the broader inflammatory cascade. It's also another data point in the slowly growing argument that AD risk genes — APOE, TREM2, INPP5D — converge on microglial lipid and lysosomal handling rather than on inflammation per se.

A negative result, which I want to flag explicitly because the field needs more of them in print. A group looking at microglial MyD88 in alcohol use disorder. Background: whole-body MyD88 loss had been shown to increase voluntary alcohol drinking, and microglial MyD88 was the natural hypothesis. They knock MyD88 out specifically in microglia in mice and run a one-bottle voluntary binge-drinking paradigm. The clean answer: microglia-specific MyD88 deletion has minimal effect on voluntary alcohol intake or anxiety-like behavior, and alcohol exposure doesn't modify MyD88-dependent changes in parvalbumin interneurons or perineuronal nets — even though alcohol does perturb microglial morphology in the male prefrontal cortex independent of genotype. So the whole-body MyD88 phenotype is not microglial. But here is the twist that keeps the paper from being a flat null. When they add an early-life endotoxin challenge, both MyD88-deficient and wild-type males increase adult drinking — and so do saline-injected MyD88-deficient males. The implication is that microglial MyD88 may be doing something only in the presence of an early-life perturbation, and that something is interacting with stress sensitivity rather than with alcohol per se. Methodologically the lesson is that a cell-specific knockout in a single-context behavioral paradigm is the wrong test for an immune signaling node that probably integrates developmental history. For AUD genetics this is useful because it argues against microglial MyD88 as a baseline drug target, but keeps it on the table as a context-dependent modifier.

Onto the AI-in-pharmacology stack. A new arXiv preprint from a Therapeutics Initiative group introduces TxBench-PP — TherapeuticsBench Preclinical Pharmacology — a verifiable benchmark of one hundred evaluations covering mechanism-of-action reasoning, pharmacodynamic readouts, compound-target engagement, causal target validation, developability, safety, and translational efficacy. The design point is the one this field has needed. Agents receive realistic workflow snapshots — actual assay readouts, not encyclopedia trivia — and have to inspect files in a coding environment and return structured answers that are graded deterministically. The structure forces the agent to reason from data the way a real preclinical program does, rather than from memorized literature. They ran sixteen model-and-harness configurations across eleven models and forty-eight hundred trajectories, and no system reliably recovered preclinical pharmacology decisions. The strongest configuration was Claude Opus 4.8 with the Pi harness at 59.3 percent endpoint pass rate, GPT-5.5 with Pi at 55.3 percent. The interpretation is two things at once. First, a frontier model in a thoughtful harness can do most of the easy preclinical reasoning — that's not nothing. Second, fifty-nine percent on what is at the program-decision level a binary-ish task is well below where you would want to be before letting an agent make program calls. The benchmark itself is more important than the numbers — it's the first one I've seen that's structured to verify whether an agent can actually walk through assay data the way a working pharmacologist does. Anyone building agents for drug discovery should be calibrating against this rather than against text-based pharma QA.

A structural drug-discovery method. SteerAF, from a group whose central insight is that the AlphaFold2 distogram is informative about alternative protein conformations and not just the dominant one. End-to-end structure predictors collapse to the training-distribution mode, and recovering alternative conformations matters enormously for drug discovery — many targets are druggable in a state that isn't the one AlphaFold gives you out of the box. SteerAF runs an inference-time optimization over the distogram derived from deep MSAs, using sparse block-gradient-ascent perturbations to steer toward alternative conformations. Across four benchmarks they match or exceed existing methods, and crucially, the sparse MSA features the method touches correlate strongly with experimentally characterized functional residues — about fifty percent precision at recovering them in the tested proteins. So the steering knob isn't a black-box latent — it's interpretable in terms of which positions in the alignment encode the alternate state. They also show it works as a decoy-selection tool in the absence of experimental structures, and as a seed-structure generator for molecular dynamics. The natural use case is allosteric and cryptic-site drug discovery on targets where the cryo-EM or NMR ensemble doesn't yet exist — protein language models can plausibly bridge that gap if the steering is principled.

Staying on the structure-based drug-discovery side, but pivoting to RNA. SMARTPocket, from a U.S. group, is a geometric deep learning framework for predicting ligandable small-molecule binding pockets on RNA directly from three-dimensional structure. The clever move is transfer learning from over a hundred and ten thousand protein-binding-interface structures, which gets around the data scarcity problem — there are very few experimentally elucidated RNA-ligand complexes by comparison. RNA is represented as a full-atom point cloud, and the model outputs predicted pocket locations. It outperforms existing RNA pocket predictors and generalist biomolecular models across four single-chain benchmarks and three broader curated benchmarks, generalizes to apo RNA when conformational change is modest, finds cryptic ligandable pockets, and recapitulates experimentally validated sites in the SARS-CoV-2 frameshifting element and an evolved RNA aptamer. SMARTPocket-guided docking then improves near-native pose recovery and runs faster than blind docking. The deeper point is that RNA-targeted drug discovery has been gated by the assumption that you cannot transfer pocket-prediction methods from proteins to RNA because the chemistry and geometry are too different — this paper is one of several recent challenges to that assumption, and the protein-to-RNA transfer learning is the operational lesson. The body of the preprint wasn't rendered yet, so this is off the abstract — the question I'd want answered is how the cryptic-pocket discovery rate compares to molecular-dynamics-based pocket-detection on the same RNA targets.

Closing on pharmacokinetics. A clean mechanistic piece from a Seattle group on cortisol and hepatic drug transport in pregnancy. The context: pregnancy reshapes hepatic drug metabolism in known ways, but the corresponding question on hepatic drug transport has been largely unexplored. They use the differentiated HepaRG hepatocyte line and identify cortisol as the principal pregnancy-related hormone driving induction of three uptake transporters — sodium-taurocholate-cotransporting polypeptide NTCP, organic anion transporter 2 OAT2, and organic cation transporter 1 OCT1. The mechanism is teased apart by CRISPR knockdown: the glucocorticoid receptor is the primary mediator, and HNF4-alpha and HNF1-alpha are selective downstream contributors — HNF4-alpha knockdown selectively attenuates OAT2 and OCT1 induction, HNF1-alpha knockdown enhances NTCP induction but attenuates OCT1. PXR and CAR are not the drivers, despite both being induced in this context. The clinical relevance is direct: antenatal corticosteroids — dexamethasone or betamethasone — are routinely administered to pregnant women to prevent neonatal respiratory distress, and this paper says you should expect substantial changes in hepatic drug uptake at the same time. For any psychotropic or anticonvulsant or analgesic given concurrently with antenatal steroids, the disposition is potentially shifted in a way that PBPK models that ignore GR-driven transporter induction will miss. This is the kind of preclinical mechanistic piece that should feed directly into pregnancy-specific dosing guidance.
