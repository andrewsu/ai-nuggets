## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. It's Tuesday, July seventh. Ten items today. The through-line is receptor and channel pharmacology at the tissue level — a spinal kappa story that changes how we should think about non-addictive analgesia, a schizophrenia-multiple-sclerosis genetic bridge that nominates a new lactate-sensing GPCR checkpoint, a CB2 receptor phage-display peptide antagonist series, and a fresh allosteric pocket on Kv1.3 with a geometry-driven selectivity mechanism. Around that, one glutamate imaging paper in trauma-exposed adolescents, a chromatin-architecture paper on POGZ that reframes an autism gene as a heterochromatin-boundary keeper, and a large CSF proteomic aging clock that predicts neurological decline. We close on three agentic-AI items — BoltzMol-1 as an operational virtual-screening pipeline, PRECEDE as a precedent-grounded LLM co-scientist for side-effect-aware drug redesign, and EEG-SpikeAgent as an LLM-driven feature synthesis loop for clinical EEG.

Lead item — the kappa opioid receptor story from Tayler Sheahan's group at the Medical College of Wisconsin, on spinal KOR neurons as an acute-pain gate. The context here is that KOR has been the field's designated non-addictive alternative to mu — nalfurafine is approved in Japan for uremic pruritus, difelikefalin is peripherally-restricted and works on the CKD pruritus indication, and various biased and G-protein-selective KOR agonists have been chased for years to try to separate analgesia from the dysphoria that killed most centrally-penetrant efforts. Yesterday we covered a paper putting the dysphoria on the anterior paraventricular thalamus. Today's paper puts the analgesia on the spinal cord — specifically on excitatory KOR-expressing neurons in the dorsal horn. They use chemogenetics both ways — pharmacologically inhibiting KOR-expressing spinal neurons blocks nocifensive responses to noxious stimuli, and chemogenetic activation of those same neurons drives nocifensive behavior in the absence of an actual painful stimulus. So this is a KOR-defined population that is both necessary and sufficient for acute pain signaling. Molecular characterization shows these neurons are excitatory and co-express pain-promoting neuropeptides, most notably tachykinin precursor Tac1 — the substance P precursor — and, crucially, the pattern holds in human spinal cord tissue, not just mouse. The mechanistic model is that spinal KOR agonism reduces release of pain-promoting neuropeptides from a translationally-relevant excitatory population. Why this matters — it's a concrete site of action rationale for peripherally-restricted or spinal-selective KOR agonists to work on pain without touching the aPVT circuit that produces dysphoria and stress aversion. It also aligns with what the intrathecal-KOR-agonist literature has hinted at for decades — that a lot of the useful analgesic tone from KOR is at the spinal level, and a lot of the psychiatric baggage is central. If you can preferentially deliver — chemically, or via limited BBB penetration — you should be able to separate the two. Going off the preprint here since it was posted yesterday and bioRxiv hasn't rendered the body yet, but the abstract-level claim structure is well-defined and the mouse-and-human coverage is exactly what you'd want.

Staying with receptor-level neuroimmune pharmacology, but shifting to psychiatry — a paper cross-referencing the genomic architectures of schizophrenia and multiple sclerosis and finding a shared structural fracture localized to the hydroxycarboxylic-acid-receptor tandem array on chromosome 12. This is the HCAR1-HCAR2-HCAR3 cluster. Two things you should carry with you. First — the tandem is not just adjacent GPCRs, it's a functionally-coupled unit where HCAR2 is the immune cooling switch that dimethyl fumarate agonizes to get its efficacy in MS, and HCAR1 is a lactate sensor that has been entirely unexplored pharmacologically. What this paper claims is that the shared schizophrenia-MS risk allele acts specifically on HCAR1 — the un-drugged one — via a peripheral-macrophage-specific enhancer failure that renders activated macrophages lactate-blind. They cannot sense their own glycolytic exhaust to engage the cAMP-suppressing negative feedback loop that halts immune proliferation. Second — the risk locus is unequivocally not associated with Crohn's, lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, or psoriasis, and it drives disease susceptibility for schizophrenia and MS without predicting MS severity. That's an unusual signature. It says the locus is doing something at the initiation-of-neuroimmune-engagement step that is shared between a demyelinating disease and a psychotic disorder, and not doing anything at the general-inflammation level. The clinical read — if the biology holds up, HCAR1 becomes the same kind of switch HCAR2 is for MS, but potentially for both indications simultaneously, and specifically for psychiatric symptoms in schizophrenia that align with the macrophage-mediated neuroinflammation hypothesis. HCAR1 agonists exist as research tools but none has been developed clinically. This paper is an argument that they should be. The evidence tier is genomic association plus eQTL-in-purified-immune-lineages — no drug or animal model yet — so it's a hypothesis-generating result, not a validation, but the target selection logic is exactly the kind of shared-architecture genetics work that has generated the best psychiatric drug candidates of the last five years.

Third — a paper on cognitive control and trauma exposure in adolescents that uses functional MR spectroscopy to measure dynamic changes in dorsal ACC glutamate during an inhibitory control task with negative emotional stimuli. Fifty adolescents split by trauma exposure history. The mechanistic hypothesis is that childhood trauma degrades glutamatergic excitatory tone during effortful cognitive control specifically when negative emotional distractors are present — meaning the top-down control system fails to recruit the same neurotransmitter pool when it needs it most. Two things worth attention here. First, dynamic proton MRS is starting to work well enough as a within-subject readout that you can look at neurotransmitter concentration changes on the order of task blocks, not just resting-state levels. That's the technical development. Second, the trauma-by-emotional-stimulus interaction on dACC glutamate is exactly the kind of intermediate phenotype that could stratify anxiety-disorder trials in adolescents. If glutamatergic tone during emotional-control tasks is a mediator between trauma exposure and clinical anxiety, then modulators that raise excitatory tone during control — the ketamine literature is the obvious analogy, but also LY354740 or older group-II mGluR-modulator programs — would have a natural biomarker readout. This is a modest-sample imaging study, not a treatment trial, but it fits into the growing MRS-as-target-engagement-biomarker use case that the field needs for psychiatric drug development.

Now to a chromatin architecture paper that has direct psychiatric-drug-discovery implications, even though it's mechanistic. POGZ is one of the top loss-of-function genes in autism spectrum disorder — de novo protein-truncating variants in POGZ are associated with White-Sutton syndrome and with ASD in the SPARK and SSC exome data. Nobody knew what POGZ was actually doing at the chromatin level in the developing brain. This paper — from a group using embryonic mouse cortex, immunoprecipitation-mass spectrometry, ChIP-seq, and Micro-C — identifies POGZ's principal in vivo interactors as G9a and GLP, the H3K9 methyltransferases. So POGZ sits at the heart of the H3K9-methylation machinery. Losing POGZ produces bidirectional megabase-scale redistribution of H3K9me3 — regions that should be euchromatin gain the repressive mark, get repositioned to the nuclear lamina, strengthen their B-compartment scores, and lose TAD boundary insulation. Nascent transcription of neuronal genes within those newly-repressed domains collapses. So POGZ is a boundary-keeper that protects neurodevelopmental gene domains from encroachment by the heterochromatin machinery. The clinical implication — this is one of the cleanest mechanistic explanations we now have for why a specific ASD gene disrupts brain development at the transcription-of-neuronal-programs step. It also predicts that G9a-GLP inhibitors — which exist as tool compounds like UNC-0642 and are being developed for oncology and sickle cell — might have neurodevelopmental application in POGZ-haploinsufficient syndromes by restoring the balance between euchromatin and heterochromatin. That's a specific pediatric-neurodevelopmental hypothesis that a targeted trial could actually test.

Now shifting from single-gene mechanism to a large biomarker paper — a CSF proteomic clock for brain aging out of a group that assembled more than ten thousand CSF proteomes across multiple cohorts and proteomic platforms. The result — a 249-protein CSF aging clock that predicts chronological age accurately, generalizes across independent datasets, and shows accelerated aging in essentially every neurological disease they tested. Brain-age acceleration correlates with blood-brain barrier dysfunction and predicts longitudinal cognitive decline, neuroimaging progression, and conversion to dementia. Two things to carry. First, a simplified 30-protein panel retains most of the prognostic performance, which brings this into the range where a clinical assay is feasible. Second, the biology behind the clock resolves into two opposing programs — a pro-aging activation of immune, vascular, extracellular matrix, and coagulation pathways, marked by CHI3L1, CD14, von Willebrand factor, LRG1, and LTBP2, and an anti-aging collapse of neuronal, synaptic, axonal, and structural-maintenance programs, marked by NPTX2, collagen 1A2, nidogen 1, cadherin 8, and proenkephalin PENK. So brain aging is not one process, it's two opposing programs, and the CSF sees both. The therapeutic read — this gives target-engagement handles for the two arms separately. Anti-BBB-inflammation programs — think anti-CHI3L1, or GLP-1 agonist repurposing for AD — should specifically move the pro-aging arm. Synaptic-restoration programs — think NPTX2-directed or BDNF-adjacent — should move the anti-aging arm. And the composite clock gives you a single-number readout you can use as a Phase 2 endpoint. This is real infrastructure for CNS neurodegeneration drug development, not just a biomarker paper.

Now a receptor engineering item. A group at Trinity College Dublin, working on Crohn's disease and cannabinoid biology, used phage display selection with libraries of stabilized constrained peptides against conformation-selected states of CB2R on human T cells. Out of fifty thousand enriched sequences, they express and functionally characterize ten candidate protein antagonists, all with IC50 values between five and ten nanomolar on beta-arrestin recruitment and ERK phosphorylation. Their lead candidate, called SLKC-09 at 5.4 nanomolar, inhibits gut homing of CD4 and CD8 naive, effector, and memory T cells in a mouse ileitis model. Why this is interesting for a CNS-pharmacology audience even though it's an IBD paper — this is one of the first demonstrations of biologic antagonism of a cannabinoid GPCR with low-nanomolar potency, using a modality that is neither a small molecule nor a full antibody. Constrained peptides against GPCRs are an underexploited space — the CGRP-monoclonal-antibody class demonstrated that biologic receptor blockade is a real modality for CNS-relevant GPCRs, and constrained peptides offer smaller size, cheaper manufacturing, and potentially better tissue penetration. If this modality generalizes — which it should, because the phage-display machinery is generic and the CB2R work here doesn't rely on anything CB2R-specific — then you should expect to see peptide antagonists appearing against other CNS-relevant GPCRs like the orexin receptors, kappa opioid, and the trace amine-associated receptor family within the next year or two.

Sticking with the theme of newly-discovered druggable pockets on ion channels — a group in Hunan reports a novel allosteric inhibitor of Kv1.3 that binds an extracellular pocket that hasn't been characterized before. Kv1.3 is the validated autoimmune drug target — it drives effector-memory T-cell activation, and Kv1.3 blockers have been in development for MS and psoriasis for years, with limited success because the disclosed inhibitors all converge on a narrow set of conserved pore or turret sites and either lack selectivity across Kv1 paralogs or fail in the clinic. This paper describes a compound called MPiN that engages a distinct extracellular allosteric pocket framed by the PP1 and PP2 turret loops, the pore helix, and the outer S5 and S6 helices, and acts through a bidirectional coupling that simultaneously constricts the selectivity filter and pushes the voltage sensor toward activation. The most interesting result is on selectivity — despite structural conservation of this pocket across Kv1 subtypes, selectivity is dictated by two peripheral residues, G427 and H451, that don't contact the ligand at all but define the pocket geometry. So this is a geometry-driven, non-contact selectivity mechanism, which is a general principle that should apply to structure-based design in other ion channel families where paralog selectivity has been hard to achieve. MPiN itself shows in vivo efficacy in a mouse psoriasis model. For a CNS drug developer, this matters because Kv1 channels — Kv1.1, Kv1.2, Kv1.4 — are neuronal, and Kv1.1 loss of function is the mechanism behind Isaac's syndrome and some episodic ataxias. Achievable paralog selectivity within Kv1 is a prerequisite for CNS-safe use of Kv-channel modulators. This paper is an argument that non-contact geometric selectivity is a real design lever.

Onto the AI drug-discovery block. First — BoltzMol-1, from Boltz Public Benefit Corporation. This is an optimized version of the Boltz-2 co-folding model, packaged into a hit-discovery pipeline explicitly adapted for prospective virtual screening rather than retrospective benchmarking. Two things make this a useful item to know about. First, they run it on ten targets, most of which have no representation in the underlying affinity training data, and they find functional actives or binders on six out of ten, with modest experimental budgets of twenty-eight to ninety-six compounds per target. That's a hit rate that is genuinely competitive with high-throughput screening in the difficult-target regime, at a fraction of the cost and library size. Second, they layer in an ADMET model triage for kinetic solubility, logD, and Caco-2 permeability before compounds go out for synthesis or purchase — meaning developability triage is happening at the ranking stage, not as a post-hit filter. This is a small but real workflow improvement — it saves the cost of chasing binders that will never make it through the physicochemical funnel. For CNS drug developers this is particularly relevant because BBB-penetrant hits have narrow physchem windows and screening those in earlier is high-leverage. The caveats — six out of ten is a good number but not a spectacular one, and the "functional actives or binders" bucket includes weak binders in some of the counts. Take it as evidence that co-folding-model-based virtual screening is now workable for prospective use on out-of-distribution targets, not that it has replaced HTS.

Next — PRECEDE, a precedent-guided co-scientist for side-effect-aware drug redesign, out on arXiv this week. The framing is that redesign of an existing drug to remove a specific adverse effect while preserving therapeutic function is not just a molecular generation problem — it's an evidence-grounded reasoning problem over drug-side-effect associations, biomedical knowledge graphs, and precedent cases of safety-driven optimization from the medicinal chemistry literature. The system uses an LLM orchestrator coordinating structured retrieval over those three evidence sources, with explicit human-review checkpoints. The point of interest is not that this outperforms a benchmark — you should discount their headline numbers because side-effect-redesign benchmarks are extremely gameable — but that the workflow embeds precedent retrieval as first-class evidence. The right mental model here is that a medicinal chemist redesigning a drug does not do it by de novo generative sampling; they do it by remembering that "compound X had the same off-target liability and was fixed by adding a methyl at position four", and applying that precedent. PRECEDE encodes that inductive move. Whether the current implementation is good enough is debatable; the workflow shape is where the field is going.

Closing item — EEG-SpikeAgent, from a group targeting interictal epileptiform discharge detection in scalp EEG. The core idea is agentic closed-loop program synthesis — an LLM proposes deterministic signal-processing feature modules one at a time, the code executes on EEG, features feed a gradient-boosted classifier, run-level metrics get summarized back to the LLM, and it refines. On a public 29-channel dataset with about twenty-five thousand four-second epochs, the auto-generated features achieve an area under the ROC curve of 0.935. That's within striking distance of end-to-end deep-learning models on the same problem, and the entire feature set is expressed as auditable, inspectable code that a clinician or reviewer can inspect. The reason to care — clinical EEG deployment has been blocked less by model performance and more by explainability, especially in the pediatric epilepsy setting where a false-negative epileptiform discharge changes a diagnostic call. An agentic approach that produces human-readable feature code rather than a monolithic learned filter bank is a plausible path to clinical acceptance for automated EEG interpretation. It also generalizes — the same closed-loop pattern should work for any biosignal domain where the field wants inspectable models more than it wants raw performance.
