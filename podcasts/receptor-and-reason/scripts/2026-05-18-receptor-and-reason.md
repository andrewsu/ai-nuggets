Lead item, and the cleanest piece of psychiatric genetics this week — Spatial Transcriptomics Implicates the Thalamus and Cortex in Autism and Schizophrenia. They generated their own spatial transcriptomics dataset across the adult mouse brain sagittal midsection, then asked a specific question — for the two big gene lists in neuropsychiatric genetics, autism risk genes from exome sequencing and schizophrenia risk genes from GWAS, where in the brain are those genes most enriched. The answer is a method point and a biology point. Method first — they introduce a statistic called Gene Fraction Enrichment Score, GFES, designed to control for the fact that some brain regions just have more neurons than others, which has been a long-standing confound in this literature. Layer five of cortex looks special partly because it has dense excitatory pyramidal cells; if you don't correct for neuronal density you're partly measuring cell-count differences, not pathway differences. The biology — autism risk genes from exome sequencing are most enriched in the thalamus first, cortex second. Schizophrenia GWAS genes are enriched in thalamus, hippocampus, and cortex, in that order. The thalamus has been creeping into both literatures for a few years now — thalamocortical loops in ASD, thalamic dysconnectivity in early psychosis — but most large-scale gene-expression analyses have either pooled the thalamus or treated it as a single structure. This paper says the signal is genuinely there at the regional level, and it survives the neuron-density correction. The implication for drug discovery is non-trivial — thalamic-projecting and thalamus-resident neuron populations are pharmacologically distinct from cortical pyramidal cells, with different receptor expression profiles for glutamatergic and serotonergic targets, and the field has been mostly building drugs around cortical-receptor assumptions. Worth pulling up if you're thinking about cell-type-specific targeting for either indication.

Item two — staying with glutamate receptor pharmacology but pivoting from psychiatric genetics to cancer neuroscience. Glutamatergic Neuron-Tumor Synapses Shape Human Glioblastoma Cell States Through Radial Glia Plasticity. The neuron-tumor synapse field has been emerging for about five years now, the observation being that glioblastoma cells form bona fide synaptic contacts with surrounding neurons, that those synapses are predominantly glutamatergic and AMPA-receptor-driven, and that the resulting calcium transients drive tumor proliferation and invasion. What this paper adds is a human-organoid tumor transplantation model they call so-HOTT, which preserves patient tumor heterogeneity, recapitulates excitatory neuron-tumor synapses, and lets you clonally lineage-trace tumor cells after you perturb the synapses. The substantive finding — both genetic knockdown and pharmacological inhibition of AMPA and kainate receptors shift glioblastoma cell composition away from neuronal-like fates and toward progenitor-proximal astrocytic and mesenchymal states. The mechanism runs through attenuated calcium signaling and reduced plasticity of a radial-glia-like progenitor population. For a neuropsychopharmacology audience this matters for two reasons. First, perampanel, the AMPA-receptor antagonist used clinically for refractory epilepsy, has been quietly accruing case reports as adjunctive therapy for glioblastoma, and this paper provides the cleanest mechanistic frame I've seen for why that might actually work — and why the answer is more nuanced than "AMPA blockade kills tumor cells." Second, kainate receptor pharmacology in general has been the unloved cousin of AMPA and NMDA in CNS drug discovery; there are very few selective kainate ligands in the clinic. If kainate signaling is a meaningful driver of cell-state plasticity in GBM, that's a more aggressive case for actually developing the chemistry.

Item three, on cerebellar contributions to higher cognition — Cerebellar Perturbation Impairs Human Working Memory and Degrades Spatial Tuning Throughout Cortex. The cerebellum has been creeping into the working-memory literature on neuroimaging grounds — task-positive cerebellar activations during working-memory tasks have shown up in fMRI studies for over a decade. The persistent question has been causality. Are those cerebellar signals doing the cognitive work, or are they downstream readouts of cortical activity that happen to be visible in fMRI. The setup here is the right one to answer that — combined functional MRI and transcranial magnetic stimulation to the cerebellum, with spatial working-memory tasks. Cerebellar TMS perturbation broadly degraded cortical spatial tuning, not just locally, and impaired spatial working-memory recall. Crucially, the magnitude of the impairment matched what they see when they perturb canonical frontoparietal working-memory areas with the same paradigm. So the cerebellum is doing the cognitive work, not just reflecting it. Why this matters pharmacologically — the cerebellum has its own distinctive receptor pharmacology, dominated by GABAergic Purkinje output and glutamatergic mossy fiber and climbing fiber inputs, and its cell-type composition is dramatically different from the cortical pyramidal-cell-centric models that most cognitive drug discovery has been built around. If cerebellar function is causally necessary for working memory, drugs that affect cerebellar transmission — including agents at metabotropic glutamate receptors, which are densely expressed in cerebellar cortex — should be reconsidered for their cognitive profile rather than dismissed as motor-side-effect risks.

Item four, a sobering systematic review with a real methods bite — Decades of Parkinson's Disease Neuropathology Yield a Sparse and Underpowered Map of Neuronal Vulnerability. They preregistered a multilevel meta-analysis of one hundred sixty-six case-control postmortem studies published between 1963 and 2025, mapping neuronal loss across eighty-five brain regions, thirty-eight cell types, and one hundred forty-five region-cell populations. The headline number is harsh. Only four of one hundred forty-five populations are adequately powered. Eighty-two percent of Allen Brain Atlas regions have never been quantified in Parkinson's disease at the cell-counting level. An additional eighteen populations would reach adequate power with five or fewer additional studies, which is an actionable shortlist for any group with a postmortem brain bank and a stereology setup. The substantive finding among the powered comparisons is the one that should change how people think about PD therapeutic targets — noradrenergic neurons of the locus coeruleus degenerate to a similar extent as the substantia nigra dopaminergic neurons, with both populations losing more than sixty percent of neurons. Cholinergic neurons of the basal nucleus and pedunculopontine tegmental nucleus, plus the ventral tegmental area's dopaminergic neurons, show significant but less severe loss. The substantia nigra is iconic for PD, but the disease is operating in parallel on noradrenergic, cholinergic, and other dopaminergic populations at comparable magnitude. This has been creeping into the literature — Heiko Braak's staging put non-nigral involvement on the map twenty years ago — but the meta-analysis is the first quantitative receipt. For drug development this means the prion-like alpha-synuclein hypothesis isn't enough on its own; therapeutics need to engage noradrenergic and cholinergic vulnerability simultaneously, and the non-motor symptoms — autonomic, cognitive, sleep — that drive most of the disease burden are probably more tractable through those non-nigral cell types than through dopamine replacement.

Item five, the most striking neurotechnology of the week and a direct follow-on from the Parkinson's point — Near-Infrared-II Chemo-Optogenetics for Deep-Brain Stimulation. Optogenetics has been transformative for circuit dissection but has had a hard time getting to deep-brain depths in intact tissue because visible light penetrates only millimeters. Near-infrared-II light, the eleven-hundred-nanometer window, penetrates much deeper, but you can't directly drive opsins with it. The construct here, called HaloNeu, fuses a circularly-permuted HaloTag to TRPV1, the heat-sensitive ion channel that's the target of capsaicin, and then conjugates near-infrared-II photothermal nanotransducers covalently. The nanotransducers absorb at ten sixty-four nanometers — a wavelength clinically familiar from laser medicine — and convert the light into local heat, which opens TRPV1. The depth numbers are real. They get non-invasive neuromodulation at one centimeter at sixty milliwatts per square centimeter, and up to five centimeters under the standard skin-exposure safe limit of one watt per square centimeter. The construct is stable enough for greater than two months of in vivo modulation, and they show sustained activation of ventral tegmental area circuits and effective alleviation of Parkinsonian motor symptoms in mouse models. Two reasons this matters. First, the human ventral tegmental area sits roughly six to seven centimeters from the scalp surface depending on entry angle, which puts it inside the five-centimeter window for the safe-limit power they report — at least in principle for an animal-scale rodent VTA, and worth modeling for primate depth. Second, this is non-invasive — no implanted electrode, no chronic infection risk, no hardware revision surgery. The molecular engineering and biocompatibility hurdles for getting nanotransducer-tagged opsin-channel hybrids into a human are non-trivial, but the basic physics says the depth is achievable and the modulation is stable. This is the closest thing to a real challenger to electrode-based DBS I've seen at the bench level.

Item six, opioid pharmacology with a clinical question that's been under-resolved — Acute Buprenorphine Exposure Depresses Neonatal Respiratory Chemoreflexes in the Presence or Absence of Naloxone. Buprenorphine has become a workhorse for medication-assisted treatment of opioid use disorder during pregnancy, and it's used to manage neonatal opioid withdrawal syndrome. Its safety profile in adults rests on a partial-agonist ceiling at the mu opioid receptor that's supposed to cap respiratory depression. The neonatal respiratory control system is not the adult system. Here, they did dual-chamber head-out plethysmography in awake post-natal day four-to-five Sprague-Dawley rat pups, measuring ventilation and metabolic rate during eupnea and during a hypoxic-hypercapnic challenge — the standard test of chemoreceptor-driven ventilatory drive. The result is unambiguous and concerning. Acute buprenorphine produced frank hypoventilation — reduced breathing frequency and tidal volume, reduced oxygen consumption and CO2 production. The mu opioid receptor antagonist naloxone, the standard rescue agent, did not fully reverse it. Even naloxone methiodide, the peripherally-restricted form designed to engage only peripheral mu opioid receptors, did not normalize the response. The implication is that buprenorphine's neonatal respiratory effects are partly central and partly mediated by sites that aren't standard mu opioid pharmacology. This is the kind of result that should inform two real clinical conversations. First, how aggressively to use buprenorphine for neonatal opioid withdrawal syndrome when the alternative is morphine or methadone with its own depressive risks. Second, the practical limits of naloxone as rescue in neonates exposed to buprenorphine in utero. The clinical answers aren't going to come from a P4 rat pup study, but the mechanistic question of why naloxone doesn't rescue is genuinely interesting and worth the next experimental round.

Item seven, pivoting to the agentic-AI methods side — Evaluating Open LLMs for Agentic Analysis Orchestration in a Typical Biomedical Lab. This is exactly the kind of practical benchmarking that the field needs more of. The premise — frontier models are expensive on a per-call basis, and most routine biomedical data analysis is high-volume repetitive execution that doesn't actually need frontier intelligence, just reliable tool calling and code execution. The question is whether a free, locally-runnable open-weight model can take over the implementer role while a frontier model authors the plan. They used Claude Opus to author plans of increasing detail for per-sample variant calling, and ran six 2026-release open-weight implementer models against those plans on desktop GPUs. The headline result — qwen3.6:27b reproduced frontier accuracy on every plan and matched Opus cell-for-cell on a thirty-six-cell error-injection matrix. A sub-two-thousand-dollar Jetson or Apple Mac Mini was sufficient to run it. Two things to like about this paper. First, the methodology is reproducible — they share plans, harness, scoring code, and per-cell artifacts on GitHub. Second, they're honest about the half-life of the result. Open-weight models evolve on the order of months, and the specific implementer they recommend will be superseded; what they provide is a framework for re-evaluation, not a tablet of stone. The takeaway for any computational pharmacology group that's running an agentic workflow — the cost model where the frontier model is the planner and a small local model is the executor is the right architecture. The plan-execute split is what makes this work, and most current agentic-AI deployments in biomedicine are still running frontier on every call out of habit.

Item eight, a benchmark with a counterintuitive headline — Do Larger Models Really Win in Drug Discovery? A Benchmark Assessment of Model Scaling in AI-Driven Molecular Property and Activity Prediction. They tested across twenty-six endpoints spanning ADMET, toxicity, safety liabilities, and bioactivity, with one hundred fifty-six fold-mean comparisons under random, Murcko-scaffold, and structure-separated splits — the splits matter because each approximates a different real use case. Random split is retrospective evaluation on a closed library. Scaffold split is hit-to-lead expansion. Structure-separated is library expansion onto truly novel chemotypes. The result — classical machine learning models, specifically random forests on ECFP4 fingerprints and ExtraTrees on RDKit descriptors, won one hundred sixteen of the one hundred fifty-six fold-mean comparisons. Graph neural networks, including GIN and the recently-popular Ligandformer, won twenty-five. Pretrained molecular sequence models like MoLFormer and ChemBERTa2 won twelve. Large language model-based structure-activity-relationship baselines won three. Classical ML dominates random-split interpolation, where the test compounds look like the training compounds, and loses some of that advantage as splits get harder. Graph neural networks claw back some performance on the structure-separated splits, which is the hardest test. Pretrained transformers do not, on this benchmark, justify their parameter count. The headline is being read in the field as a corrective to the scale-centered narrative that dominates much of computational pharmacology marketing, and I think that read is approximately correct, but the more useful framing is that pretraining costs and per-prediction costs are not free, and a random forest that runs on a laptop and beats a hundred-million-parameter foundation model on most ADMET endpoints is the right tool for that job until somebody demonstrates otherwise.

Item nine, a sharp methodological observation on the same broad family — Deep Learning Models for Chemical Perturbation Prediction Do Not Yet Utilise Drug Molecular Features. The L1000 chemical perturbation prediction problem is to predict the cell-line transcriptional response to a given small molecule based on the molecule's structure and the cell line's baseline state. Several recent deep learning models have been published with dedicated drug molecular encoders — graph neural networks, transformer encoders on SMILES — sitting alongside the cell-line input. What this short paper does is run the cleanest ablation I've seen on this question. They retrained seven such models from scratch, but with the drug input zeroed out or with the drug labels shuffled. Under cold-drug evaluation, where you're testing on compounds that weren't in the training set, the ablation caused negligible performance changes. A multilayer perceptron that uses only cell-line basal expression — no drug information at all — matched all of the architected drug-encoder models. The conclusion is unambiguous. Current architectures are not actually using drug molecular features for generalization to unseen compounds. They're predicting cell-line behavior, and the drug encoder is essentially decorative. This is the kind of result that should make anyone working on chemical perturbation prediction stop and look at their ablation matrix before claiming generalization. It's also a setup for the field to actually solve the harder problem, because the harder problem — predicting how a specific compound modulates a specific cell — is genuinely interesting, and the answer is not going to come from gluing a graph encoder onto a cell-line model and reporting a marginal improvement on a random split.

Closing on the constructive counterpoint to those last two — ToolMol: Evolutionary Agentic Framework for Multi-Objective Drug Discovery, on arXiv this week, out of UC San Diego. The premise is that LLM-based de novo molecule generation has been hobbled by two recurring failure modes. First, the syntactic limits of SMILES and similar string representations mean LLMs generate invalid molecules at high rates. Second, even valid generations are often low-quality drug candidates that fail on synthesizability, ADMET, or selectivity. The architecture they propose is a multi-objective genetic algorithm where the mutation operator is an agentic LLM with access to a curated toolbox of RDKit-backed cheminformatics functions — bond modifications, ring transformations, scaffold hops, the standard medicinal-chemistry vocabulary. The agentic operator plans modifications, calls the tools to actually execute them, and iterates. The result on three protein targets is greater than ten percent stronger predicted binding affinity than existing methods, plus a thirty-five-percent improvement on the gold-standard absolute binding free energy benchmark. The interesting analytical observation in the paper is from the chain-of-thought traces — tool calling lets the model faithfully execute its planned modifications, whereas without tools the model would describe a planned mutation but then produce a chemically inconsistent output. The combination of LLM chemical prior with explicit tool execution is what makes it work. So between Doctor Larger Models Really Win, which says raw scale isn't enough, the chemical perturbation paper, which says drug encoders need to actually use drug information, and ToolMol, which says tool-augmented LLMs with classical optimization scaffolding can produce real wins on hard chemistry — there's a coherent story emerging this week about where agentic AI in drug discovery is genuinely useful, and it's roughly that the LLM is most valuable as a planner and tool orchestrator on top of mature cheminformatics, not as a monolithic predictor. That's the frame I'd carry forward. Back tomorrow.
