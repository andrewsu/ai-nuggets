Good morning, Alan. Ten items today, opening with two adjacent pieces of analgesia mechanism — a new pH-sensing principle for opioid receptors and a substantially new mechanism for acetaminophen — followed by a hydrogen-persulfide pain donor with a novel cytoskeletal route to potassium-channel restoration. Then the May pipeline roundup, a TMS-for-depression result from UCSD, a thalamocortical GABA finding on PTSD, an addiction-circuit dissection, a JAK-pharmacology result on persistent astrocyte inflammation, a Parkinson's gut-microbiome index, and closing on an authority-aware drug-information agent worth taking seriously.

First, from the Isom lab at the University of Miami, a bioRxiv paper arguing that all three opioid receptors — mu, delta, and kappa — behave as proton-gated coincidence detectors. They used a humanized yeast platform that lets you control extracellular pH without confounders from the cell interior, and showed that agonist efficacy at each opioid receptor falls steadily as pH drops from physiological levels toward what you actually see in inflamed tissue. Structure-based modeling pointed to one conserved aspartate, D149 — D-three-thirty-two in the standard GPCR numbering — sitting just below the ligand-binding pocket. Protonating that aspartate disrupts the salt bridge that anchors opioid agonists, and the receptor stops signaling. Two implications worth chewing on. First, a lot of clinical opioid pharmacology happens in tissue that is inflamed and therefore acidic — pain, infection, ischemia — and this paper says the receptor is, by design, partially deactivated in exactly that environment. Second, it offers a clean mechanistic explanation for why opioid analgesia is often less effective in inflamed tissue than in naive tissue. Tool-compound studies will need to revisit how they measure efficacy at the opioid receptors at neutral pH versus the acidic conditions of the targeted tissue.

Staying on analgesia. The Hohmann lab at Indiana University posted an updated bioRxiv paper proposing a substantially new mechanism for acetaminophen, the most widely used over-the-counter analgesic in the world. They report that acetaminophen's suppression of mechanical hypersensitivity in mouse post-surgical and inflammatory pain models is blocked by inhibitors of diacylglycerol lipase and monoacylglycerol lipase — the two enzymes that, between them, control endocannabinoid tone — and by global, but not peripherally restricted, CB1 antagonists. So the analgesia appears to require central endocannabinoid signaling. They also see acetaminophen pushing corticosterone more than two-fold and dropping prostaglandins more than five-fold across the brain and the inflamed paw, plus suppressing close to forty percent of detected signaling lipids in a targeted screen. The takeaway: the textbook weak-COX-inhibitor explanation is incomplete. If acetaminophen is leveraging the endocannabinoid system through diacylglycerol lipase and monoacylglycerol lipase and then signaling centrally through CB1, that opens a different design space for next-generation non-opioid analgesics, and it raises real questions about co-administration with cannabinoid drugs, which is an increasingly common clinical scenario.

Continuing on analgesia but switching from receptors to channels. A bioRxiv paper from a group at Sichuan University introduces HPD1, an esterase-responsive donor of hydrogen persulfide — the sulfane species written H-two-S-two that has been emerging as an endogenous signaling molecule with antioxidant and redox-regulating properties. HPD1 reduced mechanical and cold allodynia at fourteen milligrams per kilogram in mouse chronic constriction injury and paclitaxel-induced neuropathic pain models, with negligible toxicity even at twice that dose. The mechanism is novel and worth slowing down on. HPD1 suppresses paclitaxel-induced hyperexcitability in dorsal root ganglion neurons by specifically potentiating A-type potassium currents. Mechanistically, it activates LIM-kinase-one, which inactivates cofilin, stabilizes F-actin, and promotes interaction between the actin-binding protein Filamin A and the Kv4.2 channel that carries the A-type current. Pharmacological inhibition of LIM-kinase-one dampens both the channel restoration and the analgesic effect. The reason to care: A-type potassium currents are dialed down in chronic pain states and have been hard to drug directly. Restoring them by stabilizing the cytoskeletal scaffold that anchors the channel is a different lane than direct channel agonism, and the hydrogen-persulfide chemistry gives you a tractable donor template to work with.

On the pipeline side, Psychiatric Times published its May-in-review of psychiatric drug development. Three items from that roundup are worth flagging. First, a first-in-class SIRT6 inhibitor presented at the American Society of Clinical Psychopharmacology meeting. SIRT6 is a histone-deacylase sirtuin, and this would be the first explicitly epigenetic-mechanism antidepressant in the psychiatric pipeline. Early data showed robust efficacy in women but not in men, which is either a hard-to-explain artifact or, more interestingly, a real signal that the SIRT6 axis interacts with sex-dependent chromatin biology in a way that matters clinically. Second, Synendos has begun phase 2 testing of SYT-510, a selective endocannabinoid reuptake inhibitor, in generalized anxiety disorder. That is a mechanistically distinct way to elevate endogenous anandamide and 2-AG tone, and it pairs interestingly with the acetaminophen result we just discussed — two different lanes converging on raising endocannabinoid tone for therapeutic effect. Third, a network meta-analysis ranking lumateperone as the top adjunct atypical antipsychotic for major depressive disorder across four efficacy metrics with relatively low weight gain. Sponsor-funded, so weight the framing accordingly, but the comparative-efficacy presentation is what clinicians have been asking for.

Next, from the Ramanathan lab at UC San Diego, a bioRxiv paper that landed at version three this week. It isolates the inter-train interval as the key parameter governing whether intermittent theta-burst stimulation actually drives plasticity. The current FDA-cleared antidepressant TBS protocol uses short inter-train intervals on the order of four to ten seconds. Using calcium imaging, optogenetics, and behavior, they show that these short intervals strongly recruit GABAergic — and especially parvalbumin — interneurons, which then clamps down the resulting cortical excitability and spine plasticity. Extending the inter-train interval to twenty seconds — they call this extended theta burst, or eTBS — releases the GABA brake, drives much larger spine and synaptic plasticity, and produces rapid and durable antidepressant-like effects after a single session. Optogenetically activating the GABA neurons during eTBS abolishes both the plasticity and the antidepressant effect, which is a clean causal link. The actionable point: a parameter current TMS protocols treat as fixed may be the lever that explains why intermittent theta-burst response rates have plateaued where they have.

Next, a circuit-level mechanism paper on the GABA-PTSD connection. PTSD patients have dysfunctional GABA-A receptors in midline thalamic regions, and sleep spindles — those rhythmic non-REM EEG signatures — have a characteristic waxing-and-waning shape that becomes abnormal in PTSD, but the mechanism has not been clear. A group at Warwick used localized CRISPR to knock down GABA-A receptors specifically in thalamocortical neurons in mice, and tested behavior. Sleep spindles in the knock-down animals were shorter and abnormally shaped, losing their characteristic envelope. Behaviorally, the knock-down mice failed to recover from learned fearful reactions in a contextual fear conditioning paradigm — controls habituated to a previously fear-conditioned context across subsequent unshocked visits, but the GABA-A-deficient mice never habituated. The implication is that thalamocortical GABA-A receptor function is mechanistically upstream of both sleep spindle shape and trauma-memory extinction. For drug development, this nudges interest back toward GABA-A subtype-selective positive allosteric modulators that act at thalamic rather than cortical GABA-A receptors — historically a hard problem, but a clear circuit-level target nonetheless.

Switching to addiction circuitry. From the Flagel lab at the University of Michigan, a chemogenetic dissection of the lateral hypothalamus to paraventricular thalamus pathway in cue-motivated behavior. In a Pavlovian conditioned approach paradigm, rats split into three behavioral phenotypes when reward is paired with a cue: sign-trackers, who approach and interact with the cue itself; goal-trackers, who approach the reward delivery location; and intermediate responders, who do both. Using a dual-vector approach to express inhibitory chemogenetic receptors selectively in LH-to-PVT projection neurons, they showed that inhibiting that pathway selectively attenuates goal-tracking without affecting sign-tracking, and that the effect is driven primarily by the intermediate-responder rats. Why this matters: sign-tracking versus goal-tracking captures a real and stable individual-difference axis in addiction vulnerability — sign-trackers show stronger cue-induced reinstatement of drug-seeking. The intermediate responder is often treated as the noisy middle, but this work argues they are actually the most behaviorally flexible subgroup, and they are the ones whose behavior reorganizes when you take the LH-PVT motivation input offline. That argues for clinical attention to individual-difference subgrouping when designing circuit-targeted addiction therapies.

Adjacent on the neuroinflammation theme. A bioRxiv paper looking at whether transient inflammatory stimulation locks human astrocytes into a persistent reactive state after the stimulus is removed. The group exposed human astrocytes to a defined inflammatory cytokine-and-complement cocktail and then ran a time-course washout. Most of the expected inflammatory response genes — CXCL10 and the NF-kappa-B-associated genes NFKBIA, TNFAIP3, and RELB — returned toward baseline after stimulus removal. But C3 expression stayed elevated, indicating that the astrocytes had transitioned into a persistent C3-high post-inflammatory state. Pharmacological JAK inhibition reduced the persistent C3 expression back to near-baseline. The take-home: there appears to be a JAK-dependent maintenance loop that keeps astrocytes locked in a reactive C3-high state even after the upstream inflammatory drive is gone — and approved JAK inhibitors are an existing class with a plausible repurposing handle. C3 is a central complement-cascade actor in synaptic pruning, neurodegeneration, and psychiatric inflammation hypotheses, so a persistent C3-high astrocyte state is exactly what you would not want sitting in the background after a neuroinflammatory event. Worth tracking as it moves into in vivo testing.

A methods and biomarker paper on the gut-microbiome side of Parkinson's. Curli fibres are extracellular bacterial amyloids produced by several Enterobacteriaceae and related taxa, and they are the most credible microbiological bridge between gut microbial ecology and alpha-synuclein-centered gut-brain hypotheses for Parkinson's. The group built a mathematically explicit index called Curli Carrier Burden, which estimates the aggregate evidence-weighted abundance of curated curli-producing bacterial taxa in processed metagenomic profiles. They evaluated it across five major Parkinson's gut metagenomic cohorts — including Wallen 2022, Integrated-US, Mao Central China, Romano non-Wallen, and DuruIC 2024 — and report it as a reproducible sample-level microbial trait variable enabling cohort-wise comparison of amyloidogenic bacterial signals. Why this matters: Parkinson's microbiome studies have repeatedly reported disease-associated taxa shifts that do not replicate cleanly across cohorts, partly because each group hand-picks a different taxon set. A transparent, trait-level index focused on a specific biologically coherent exposure — bacterial amyloid production — is the right shape for comparative meta-analysis. And it operationalizes a falsifiable arm of the gut-to-brain alpha-synuclein hypothesis that has been largely correlational up to now.

Closing on the agentic-AI thread. A new arXiv paper introduces DrugClaw and DrugAudit. DrugClaw is a multi-agent retrieval-augmented system that grounds drug-information answers in primary regulatory and peer-reviewed sources, organized via what the authors call a reflection-driven state-machine workflow that routes queries through a registry of drug and pharmacovigilance skills. DrugAudit is the companion benchmark: thirty-seven hundred drug-information questions, scored with a dual-judge LLM-as-judge protocol that achieved an inter-rater kappa of zero point eight-eight, measuring primary-source match, semantic snippet overlap, and citation faithfulness. DrugClaw reports a primary-source rate above ninety-one percent and a faithfulness score around eighty-nine percent, beating competitors by roughly six to ten percentage points. The durable contribution here is the authority-aware framing. Most existing biomedical RAG benchmarks score whether the answer is correct, not whether it is grounded in the right kind of source — and in regulated drug information, where a citation to a forum post is meaningfully worse than a citation to a drug label, that distinction is exactly what clinicians and regulators care about. A useful template for benchmark design in any high-stakes pharmacology question-answering setting.
