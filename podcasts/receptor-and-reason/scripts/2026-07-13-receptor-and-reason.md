## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason. It's Monday, July thirteenth, twenty twenty-six. Ten items today, heavy on serotonergic mechanism and computational psychiatry, with a couple of agentic-AI-in-pharmacology picks to close.

Opening with what I think is the strongest neuropsychopharmacology preprint of the week. The Sohal lab at UCSF asks a question that has been sitting awkwardly under the psychedelic-plasticity conversation for a while — psychedelics improve cognitive flexibility in some patients and don't in others, so what actually determines whether psilocybin is going to work? Their answer, which is the interesting move, is mechanism-specific. Using two different optogenetic ways to induce cognitive inflexibility in mice on a rule-shifting task, they show psilocybin rescues one and doesn't touch the other. The one it rescues: aberrant burst stimulation of TH-positive VTA projections to medial prefrontal cortex. The one it doesn't: inhibition of callosal parvalbumin projections that disrupts interhemispheric gamma synchrony. Both look like the same behavioral deficit at first pass, but they arise from different circuit failures and only one responds to the drug. The mechanism they land on for the responsive case is beautiful. The pathological signal is abnormally elevated activity in mPFC to mediodorsal thalamus projection neurons during the ten-to-ninety-second post-error exploration window — exactly when the animal should be updating its strategy. Psilocybin attenuates this both acutely and twenty-four hours later, tracking behavioral rescue. Whole-cell recordings in Layer 5 subcortically-projecting neurons then show two lasting changes. First, potentiated synaptic input from MD thalamus — increased EPSC and EPSP amplitude, decreased paired-pulse ratio consistent with higher release probability. Second, and this is the specific one, the D2 receptor and NMDA receptor-dependent afterdepolarization that normally sustains prolonged activity in these neurons is essentially abolished after psilocybin. So the story is: dopaminergic burst input to mPFC triggers a D2R-NMDA afterdepolarization that sustains hyperactivity in mPFC-to-MD neurons during the post-error window, that hyperactivity blocks strategy update, and psilocybin breaks the afterdepolarization mechanism. Two consequences worth flagging. First, this predicts that psilocybin should work better in patient populations whose cognitive flexibility deficit is driven by aberrant mesocortical dopamine — which is a specific and testable prediction across schizophrenia, addiction, and Parkinson's, not just depression. Second, it identifies the D2R-NMDA afterdepolarization as a pharmacological locus in its own right — you could imagine getting the same functional effect without the psychedelic subjective experience. That's a big claim and the paper doesn't test it directly, but the target it points at is now specified in a way it wasn't before.

Next, sticking with serotonergic mechanism but going up to human EMA. This is from a lab at Yale collaborating with Rutledge's group in London. The setup is a double-blind placebo-controlled study of citalopram, twenty milligrams for eight days, in sixty-six healthy participants, with eleven days of ecological momentary assessment on their phones — up to eight pre- and post-activity surveys per day covering momentary mood plus predicted and consummatory reward for real-life activities like getting lunch with a friend or going for a walk. From those pre- and post-activity ratings they compute real-world reward prediction errors, and then ask how citalopram modulates the coupling between RPEs and mood. Three findings. Citalopram amplified the impact of reward outcome on positive affect throughout the study, and gradually amplified the impact of RPEs on positive affect over time, with the effect stronger for positive than negative RPEs. Citalopram selectively attenuated the impact of negative affect on reward expectation — the negative affective bias correction that lab studies have shown with faces now shown to operate on real-world reward. And RPE-mood coupling during dosing predicted mood in the washout period, which is the mediation you'd want to see if this is the mechanism driving eventual symptom change. Standard depression questionnaires — the PHQ-9, the GAD-7 — showed no change in eight days, as expected. But the EMA signal was there from early in dosing. If this replicates in a depressed cohort, the practical implication is that you get an early-adherence readout of whether an SSRI is engaging its target computational mechanism, which is a completely different signal than waiting for six weeks of symptom scale movement.

Third, a bioRxiv paper on sex differences in serotonergic circuitry that pairs interestingly with the citalopram result. Anxiety disorders are diagnosed about twice as often in women, and the neurobiological basis has been thin. This study — the Amilhon group in Montreal — identifies median raphe region 5-HT neurons projecting to ventral hippocampus as a sex-specific anxiety substrate in mice. Retrograde-labeled 5-HT to ventral hippocampus neurons in females showed heightened intrinsic excitability, elevated in vivo recruitment during aversive contexts on the elevated plus maze, and — the specific finding — delayed disengagement from aversive environments. When females retreated from open into closed arms, 5-HT to vHP activity dropped less than in males, meaning the aversive signal persisted longer after the actual aversive stimulus was gone. Optogenetic activation of this pathway with ChETA increased anxiety-like behavior and risk assessment in females but not in males, and disrupted the normal habituation-related steepening of the theta-velocity slope in hippocampus selectively in females. The framing here is worth taking seriously — female-biased vulnerability to anxiety may not be a scaled version of male circuit dysfunction, it may be a distinct disengagement failure in a specific 5-HT projection. And that hooks directly to the SSRI finding: if the sex-specific circuit lives in a slow-timescale serotonin disengagement failure, drugs that flatten the aversive-to-safe transition are exactly where you'd expect asymmetric benefit.

Item four, from the Liston lab at Cornell, is a spatially-resolved transcriptomic study of stress-induced motivational impairment. This one was posted July eleventh so I'm going off the abstract — bioRxiv hasn't rendered the full body HTML yet. Chronic non-discriminatory social defeat stress in mice, combined with a head-restrained effortful reinforcement task where anticipatory and consummatory behavior can be tracked separately. As expected, chronic stress impaired effortful reward seeking, but with substantial between-animal variability. Spatial transcriptomics of anterior cingulate cortex then divides susceptible from resilient. Resilience is marked by enhanced inhibitory neuropeptide signaling from interneurons onto excitatory pyramidal cells and preserved astrocytic contact signaling; susceptibility looks like ACC hyperexcitability driven by loss of that neuropeptide inhibition and loss of astrocyte support. The framing that susceptibility is a targetable hyperexcitability of ACC pyramidal neurons — rather than a global dysfunction — is the actionable piece here. Neuropeptides get a specific mention but the abstract doesn't name them; that's the piece I'd want out of the full text once it renders. The lab has been building toward pharmacological interventions on cortical hyperexcitability for a while; this fits.

Item five, staying in computational learning theory but shifting to a different piece of striatum. Mahajan and Seymour at Oxford have a theory paper — abstract-only for us since the body isn't rendered yet — on the tail of the striatum, the piece of dorsal striatum that has been controversial in the reinforcement-learning-dopamine literature. Some groups argue tail-projecting dopamine neurons encode aversive prediction errors and drive threat-related retreat behavior. Other groups argue they encode action prediction errors and support habit formation. These sound incompatible but this paper argues they aren't. They build an entropy-regularized reinforcement-learning model where the tail-of-striatum dopamine signal updates both aversive values and a default-policy term. Threat belief gates aversive value initialization — so novel objects that might be threatening produce the aversive-retreat signal — while the default policy term generates action prediction errors that decline as behaviors become habitual. Both signals coexist inside the same temporal-difference error, and the model qualitatively reproduces the empirical patterns from studies on both sides of the debate. Take with the appropriate caution for an abstract-only read, but it's a nice reminder that "aversive" versus "action" prediction errors aren't necessarily separate computations — they can be two components of the same regularized learning signal with different empirical shadows.

Sixth, an operant alcohol paper from UNC-Chapel Hill on the presynaptic glutamate side. Thirty-five days of operant sweetened-ethanol self-administration in male mice, paired to a behaviorally matched sucrose control cohort. After the final session, they punched nucleus accumbens, amygdala, and prefrontal cortex and blotted for group II metabotropic glutamate receptors — mGlu2 and mGlu3. Ethanol selectively reduced mGlu2/3 protein in nucleus accumbens, with no change in amygdala or PFC, and both the monomer and dimer bands moved together, consistent with a coordinated downregulation of the presynaptic autoreceptor. Then the pharmacology dissociation. In an independent binge-drinking cohort, the orthosteric mGlu2/3 agonist LY379268 dose-dependently reduced binge-like ethanol intake. The mGlu2-selective positive allosteric modulator LY487379 was ineffective. The clean interpretation is that mGlu3, not mGlu2, is doing the work here — voluntary ethanol drives loss of presynaptic mGlu2/3 restraint on glutamate release in nucleus accumbens, and restoring group II signaling via an agonist that hits both suppresses binge drinking, while selectively boosting mGlu2 sensitivity does not. This is a lot of the mechanistic detail that the mGlu2/3 alcohol-use-disorder field has been circling for years, tightened up into a clean assay-to-behavior arc. Two caveats. It's male-only, which given today's earlier serotonin sex-differences item is worth noting. And LY379268 is a tool compound with known cross-species tolerance issues, so mGlu3-selective ligands remain the practical near-term direction.

Item seven, from the Jagust lab at Berkeley, on how dopamine buffers the preclinical Alzheimer's default mode network. Task-based fMRI plus amyloid, tau, and dopamine PET in cognitively normal older adults. Amyloid burden impairs a learning task independent of tau — that's the baseline finding — but the impairment is recovered in individuals with higher dorsolateral striatal dopamine synthesis capacity. Effective connectivity modeling during error-related feedback shows amyloid produces disinhibition of the default mode network — the DMN doesn't shut down when it should during feedback — and higher striatal dopamine rebalances the effective connectivity between DMN and the frontostriatal network to oppose that disinhibition. Also abstract-only for now. The take, which is worth holding as a hypothesis, is that dopamine-dependent frontostriatal network rebalancing is a candidate cognitive-resilience mechanism in preclinical AD. Two implications if that holds. First, striatal dopamine synthesis capacity becomes a measurable biomarker of cognitive resilience distinct from tau or amyloid burden. Second, and this is where a pharmacology audience should sit up, dopamine-precursor or dopamine-releasing interventions in the preclinical amyloid-positive window become a plausible cognitive-protection strategy independent of anti-amyloid therapies. That's not a claim the paper makes, but the mechanism it proposes points there.

Item eight bridges neurodegeneration and agentic AI. The SEA-AD DREAM Challenge is a community-benchmarking competition — that DREAM is Dialogue for Reverse Engineering Assessments and Methods — asking teams to predict Alzheimer's disease neuropathological staging and quantitative amyloid and tau burden from single-nucleus RNA sequencing data. The dataset is the Seattle Alzheimer's Disease Brain Cell Atlas — eighty-four deeply phenotyped post-mortem donors — and the challenge included a first-of-its-kind AI Agent Track alongside the human-team track. Two headline findings. On categorical staging — Braak, Thal, CERAD, and overall AD neuropathological change — the top team hit a quadratic weighted kappa of one point zero, which sounds miraculous until you read further and see that this was largely donor-metadata leakage: the same donors appeared across training, validation, and test splits with different anonymized IDs, and top models identified them and got the constant per-donor label for free. The organizers ran a metadata-shuffling perturbation and confirmed the leakage. The organizer-side transcriptomics-only model, scVIP, without access to metadata, achieved kappa around zero point six five — still very good, and the honest measure of what single-nucleus expression actually encodes about staging. Quantitative pathology prediction — the continuous 6E10 amyloid and AT8 phospho-tau burdens — proved much harder and less explained by shortcuts, with the best concordance correlation around zero point four eight. Two lessons that generalize beyond AD. First, the model-to-data privacy-preserving framework is worth stealing for any competition using deeply phenotyped human samples where metadata leakage is a live risk. Second, on the agentic side, the AI Agent Track's inclusion is more precedent than result — it establishes that DREAM will run agent tracks alongside human tracks — but the actual comparison is early and the paper doesn't yet let us conclude much about autonomous versus human-guided model development in this domain.

Item nine, from a group at Johns Hopkins that has been building tooling for LLM-assisted QSP calibration. MAPLE — Model-Aware Parameterization from Literature Evidence. If you were listening yesterday when I covered the Ensemble QSP multi-agent architecture, this paper is the calibration-data counterpart. The problem MAPLE addresses is that manual literature curation for QSP model calibration is slow and inconsistent, and naive LLM extraction hallucinates values and fabricates citations at rates that are unacceptable for quantitative modeling. Their approach is to make structured YAML schemas the collaboration interface between the LLM and the modeler. Two schemas: SubmodelTarget for isolated experiments constraining individual parameters, CalibrationTarget for clinical and in vivo endpoints constraining the full model through observables. Both separate data extraction from modeling decisions. Targeted validators catch characteristic failure modes — value-in-snippet matching catches hallucinated numbers, DOI resolution against CrossRef catches fabricated citations, code execution against mock data catches malformed forward models. None of the eighteen batch extractions passed on the first pass — every one required at least one automated retry. Then the honest finding: even after the automated pipeline, the modeler changed the forward-model type in sixty-five percent of files, adjusted prior distributions in forty-six percent, and revised source relevance in every single file. So MAPLE doesn't automate away modeler expertise. What it does do is make the collaboration surface auditable — every extracted value has to appear verbatim in a source snippet with a validated DOI, so downstream users can check the extraction without re-reading methods sections. This is the pragmatic direction I keep expecting agentic-AI-in-pharmacology to settle into — not autonomous end-to-end model builds, but validator-heavy hybrid workflows where the LLM does the reading and the human owns the judgment. Yesterday's Ensemble QSP was the modeling half of the same picture; this is the calibration-data half.

Closing with a light-but-important item from arXiv on evaluation of LLMs in clinical psychiatric encounters. The paper introduces MentalHospital, a virtual environment for evaluating LLMs on complete SOAP-workflow psychiatric encounters — that is, subjective interview, objective mental status exam, assessment, and plan. It's built on one thousand one hundred ninety-three de-identified psychiatric electronic health record cases spanning seventy-six disorders across all major ICD-11 categories. They pair it with MentalEval, a five-evaluator scoring rubric that agrees with expert clinicians at quadratic weighted kappa zero point nine four, which is a genuinely tight number for this kind of rubric, and they get a mean clinician-rated fidelity of three point eight-eight out of five across twenty-two clinician reviewers. The headline result is that even the strongest LLM they evaluate trails clinicians by about thirty-seven percentage points on objective psychiatric competence. The bottleneck they identify is the mental status assessment step — LLMs are relatively strong on the interview and treatment-planning ends but weak on the structured objective component. The reason this is an important benchmark rather than another LLM-medical-QA paper is that most existing benchmarks in this space test isolated tasks — diagnosis given a vignette, or medication choice given a diagnosis — and they don't force the model to run a full encounter with an unfolding evaluation. This one does. It puts a lower bound on how much of the psychiatric workflow can plausibly be automated with current models, and it names the specific failure mode — mental status assessment — that would need to be fixed for that gap to close. That's useful whether you want LLMs on the assessment side or you want to keep them out of it.
