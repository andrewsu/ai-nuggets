# Receptor & Reason — 2026-06-27

## Script

Good morning. It's Saturday, June 27th, 2026 — Receptor and Reason. Ten items today. We open on a clean human-pharmacology dissociation between cholinergic and noradrenergic modulation of belief updating, and close on a methods cautionary tale about case-control EEG studies in psychiatry.

Leading off — a within-subject crossover from the Jocham group in Düsseldorf. Sixty-two healthy participants did a perceptual decision-making task three times: once on biperiden, a muscarinic acetylcholine receptor antagonist that crosses the blood-brain barrier and is used clinically for parkinsonism; once on propranolol, the beta-adrenoceptor antagonist; and once on placebo. The task forced participants to combine bottom-up sensory evidence with top-down learned expectations, with the reliability of each source varying independently — a clean Bayesian-style design that separates "how you weight evidence in the moment" from "how you update the prior over time." The result is satisfyingly dissociated. Neither drug changed how much choices were governed by the strength of sensory evidence — so neither is acting on the gain of the moment-to-moment evidence pathway. What both drugs *did* do is speed up the updating of prior beliefs in response to new evidence. Propranolol's effect was specific to reward outcomes — so noradrenaline appears to gate prior updating in a valence-specific way. Biperiden sped updating after both reward and non-reward outcomes, with the side effect of producing less stable beliefs over time. The clean reading is that muscarinic acetylcholine sets the overall learning rate on priors, while noradrenaline gates the reward-specific channel of the same updating process. This is the kind of dissociation that maps neatly onto computational psychiatry's interest in failures of belief stability — relevant to psychosis, anxiety, and the more general literature on aberrant precision.

Second — an Alzheimer's drug-discovery story out of the Rauch lab at Sanford-Burnham, with Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation funding. LRP1 is the endocytic receptor that mediates uptake and trans-cellular propagation of tau in the brain — a high-value target if you can interfere with the binding without breaking the rest of LRP1's biology. They build a quantitative platform around LRP1's ligand-binding domain four, the BD4 region that does the tau interaction, and run three orthogonal binding assays — fluorescence polarization, split luciferase complementation, and time-resolved FRET — to characterize the interaction in the nanomolar range and to show that tau, RAP, and a known peptide ligand all compete for overlapping interfaces. Then they put the platform into high-throughput screening, pull out a set of small-molecule hits, and show that selected compounds reduce tau uptake in a cellular assay, phenocopying competitive blockade. So this isn't just a screening paper — it's a tractable interface, three orthogonal assays that agree on affinity, and chemical matter that works in cells. For tauopathy drug discovery this is the kind of upstream-of-aggregation handle that lateral programs have been hunting for. Watch for the medicinal chemistry follow-up.

Third — staying in Alzheimer's, but on the neuroimmune side. The Akassoglou group at Gladstone has a long-running story that fibrin — leaked across a compromised blood-brain barrier — activates microglia via its inflammatory domain on CD11b, and that knocking out that signaling without touching fibrin's clotting function is sufficient to rescue neuroinflammation in MS and AD models. This is the AD follow-up. They cross the *Fgg γ390-396A* knock-in, which selectively eliminates the inflammatory epitope, into 5XFAD mice and run the full stack. Behaviorally, in a cost-benefit conflict task, 5XFAD mice show the high-risk decision-making phenotype that's been described as an early AD-like executive dysfunction; the fibrin knock-in brings choices back to wild-type levels. In vivo two-photon imaging plus correlative light-electron microscopy shows that 5XFAD microglia retract their processes away from active neurons near amyloid plaques, while fibrin-blocked 5XFAD microglia restore those direct microglia-neuron somatic contacts. The single-nucleus RNA-seq, on 165,000 nuclei across four genotypes, pulls out specific ligand-receptor pairs that get rescued — microglial SLC1A3-mediated glutamate sensing, CD39-adenosine signaling, GAS6-Tyro3 — pathways that mechanistically tie excitability regulation to the cells that are supposed to be doing it. And in 57 AD patients' CSF profiled with SomaScan, fibrinogen levels correlate positively with complement, coagulation, inflammation, and synaptic-marker proteins, providing the translational bridge. The story is now fairly complete: fibrin is a vascular-derived driver of the microglial state that disrupts neuronal network function in AD, and you don't have to be an anticoagulant to block it.

Fourth — staying with reactive microglia, this time as a non-invasive biomarker rather than a target. A group has developed a molecular MRI contrast probe targeting CSF1R, the colony-stimulating-factor-1 receptor that's expressed almost exclusively on microglia in the brain. The ligand binds the extracellular immunoglobulin domain of CSF1R, the probe is taken up actively by both murine and human microglial lines, and in vivo imaging of the A53T alpha-synuclein mouse model of Parkinson's separates PD model from wild-type controls using radiomics on the MR images, with ex-vivo immunohistochemistry confirming the microglial localization. The motivation is straightforward — reactive microgliosis is one of the earliest detectable events in PD pathogenesis, years before motor symptoms, and the field has needed an imaging modality with the spatial resolution of MR and the molecular specificity of PET. PET tracers for TSPO, the conventional microglial-activation target, have well-known specificity and pharmacogenetic problems. A CSF1R-targeted MR contrast agent is, on paper, a much cleaner solution. The translation will turn on dosing, kinetics, and how well the rodent radiomic signature transfers to humans — but as a preclinical proof-of-concept it's a useful direction.

Fifth — switching to channelopathy pharmacology. SCN2A and SCN8A gain-of-function mutations are textbook causes of pharmacoresistant developmental and epileptic encephalopathies, and the field has been short on rapid screening models for variant-specific drug response. A new zebrafish platform from a group developing pediatric-epilepsy pipelines uses mRNA overexpression of patient-derived variants — SCN2A R1882Q and R853Q, plus SCN8A R1872Q — in three-day-post-fertilization larvae. All three variants drive measurable sporadic seizure behavior in this short-term assay, and three anti-seizure medications knock the activity down: topiramate, GS967, and PF-04856264. The latter two are persistent-sodium-current-selective blockers that are exactly the kind of mechanism you'd want for a gain-of-function sodium channelopathy — the assay is correctly distinguishing the right pharmacology. The use case is clear: variant-specific screening, fast turnaround, before committing to a much slower mammalian model. Anyone running a precision-epilepsy program should look at this as a front-end triage tool.

Sixth — a structural-plasticity item with an underappreciated psychopharmacology angle. The axon initial segment — the high-density patch of voltage-gated sodium channels where action potentials initiate — physically shortens or lengthens to tune neuronal excitability, but the molecular machinery has been unclear. A group has built an in vivo CRISPR/Cas9 knockout platform in the chicken nucleus magnocellularis using an all-in-one triple-guide vector, and they screen fourteen microtubule-associated candidates. Two come out as required for developmental AIS shortening: GSK3β and Tau. Overexpressing a constitutively active GSK3β is sufficient to drive AIS shortening on its own, and microtubule stabilization abolishes the effect — so the mechanism is GSK3β-dependent microtubule remodeling at the AIS. The psychopharmacology hook is GSK3β itself. GSK3β is one of the most established direct molecular targets of lithium, the lasting effects of lithium on neuronal excitability remain partly mysterious, and Tau — which here cooperates with GSK3β in a structural plasticity context — is also a substrate of GSK3β. This isn't a mood-disorder paper, but it's a clean mechanistic answer to the question "what does GSK3β actually do to a neuron's intrinsic excitability machinery?", which the lithium literature has wanted for thirty years.

Seventh — circuit-level dopamine pharmacology. Reward-predictive cues need to drive both *learning* and *decision making*, and the literature has converged on VTA dopamine as the teaching signal, but the projection-specific story has been less clean. A new paper isolates VTA dopamine projections to the basolateral amygdala using a stack of fiber photometry, cell-type and pathway-specific optogenetic inhibition, Pavlovian conditioning, and decision-making probes. The data is consistent across techniques. Cue-evoked VTA-to-BLA dopamine signals encode the value of the predicted reward — not just its presence. That signal is required for cues to bias action selection, and it's required for action selection to track the *current* value of the reward, which is the operational test for goal-directed control. And separately, the same cue-evoked signal constrains new learning — block it and animals overlearn from cue-paired outcomes. The interpretive headline is that BLA dopamine isn't doing "reward" in the generic VTA sense; it's specifically a value signal that supports flexible, goal-directed decision making. Clean enough to be a textbook diagram in a couple of years.

Eighth — moving to GPCR drug-discovery methods. Class B1 GPCRs — the CGRP receptor, PTH1, glucagon, GLP-1 receptor, all of them — depend on their native lipid environment to maintain signaling-competent active conformations, and conventional detergents disrupt that environment. The field has moved toward amphipathic copolymers that pull receptors directly out of the membrane into native-lipid nanodiscs, but it hasn't been clear which copolymer chemistry actually preserves active-state pharmacology. A group has done the controlled comparison among three: SMA 2000, DIBMA-12, and the electroneutral sulfo-DIBMA. All three preserve extracellular ligand binding affinity — the easy part. But only sulfo-DIBMA preserves intracellular function: mini-Gαs recruitment at the CGRP receptor, and G-protein-dependent allosteric modulation with prolonged residence time at PTH1, are intact only in sulfo-DIBMA nanodiscs. The mechanistic claim is that polymer charge and backbone chemistry, not extraction yield, determine whether the conformational landscape required for receptor activation survives solubilization. For anyone doing biophysics or structural work on class-B1 GPCRs — or biased-agonist programs that require functional readouts on purified receptor — this is the new default polymer.

Ninth — sliding into the agentic AI tier with a paper that hits the audience squarely. A group has audited large language models for what they call framing-sensitive behavioral instability in mental health interactions. Semantically equivalent concerns get rephrased into multiple contextual framings — the same underlying ask, different surface. Across several instruction-tuned model families, framing systematically changes the interpretive response. They go further than the behavioral observation: layer-wise probes show that framing-related information remains decodable through transformer depth, with consistent above-chance held-out probe performance even against strong lexical baselines. Activation-steering experiments suggest these framing-associated representational directions are partially causal — you can move outputs around by steering on them. The implication for clinical deployment is the important part. If you're standing up an LLM for mental-health-adjacent triage or safety-information dialogue, behavioral stability under semantically-equivalent rephrasing isn't a nice-to-have — it's a deployment criterion, and current models, including aligned chat models, don't have it. Add it to the eval set before any rollout.

Tenth and last — a methods paper that should make every psychiatry-adjacent EEG researcher uncomfortable. A multisite resting-state EEG dataset of 2,874 children and adolescents, ages five to eighteen, spanning ADHD, autism spectrum disorder, anxiety, learning disorders, and controls. They extract a comprehensive spectral, temporal, and complexity feature set and run repeated case-control comparisons with random subsampling across a wide range of sample sizes. The result is the result you didn't want but probably should have expected. Small-sample comparisons produce inflated, highly variable effect sizes. Large-sample comparisons converge on a uniformly *small* but robust effect. Power rises steeply with sample size; false-positive rates are roughly stable. This is essentially the EEG version of the brain-wide-association study fight from a few years ago, and the take-home is the same: case-control biomarker discovery in psychiatry needs sample sizes the field has not been routinely using, and historical small-N findings should be assumed to be a mix of true small effects and inflated noise until shown otherwise. If you're publishing a new EEG biomarker in 2026, the burden is on you to demonstrate that it's not a sample-size artifact.

That's ten. The story of the day is the biperiden-versus-propranolol dissociation — clean, classical pharmacology used to carve apart the cholinergic and noradrenergic contributions to belief updating, in humans. The drug-discovery items to watch are the tau-LRP1 chemical hits and the sulfo-DIBMA platform for active-state class-B1 GPCRs. And before you write your next case-control EEG paper, look at the sample-size paper. Have a good Saturday.
