## Script

Alan, this is Receptor and Reason for Sunday, June 7th, 2026. Ten items today, leading with a clinical pharmacology systematic review on beta-blockers in cocaine intoxication that quietly refutes a long-running ED dogma, then through psychiatric circuit and cognition work — Shank3 nucleus accumbens encoding of social avoidance, depression and facial-affect representation, sleep slow-oscillation propagation, an adaptive Bayesian TMS localizer — into two Parkinsonism structural-biology mechanism papers, two agentic AI tools for bioinformatics, and closing with a curiosity item on exercise-induced sweat as an antidepressant signal.

Leading with what I think is the most clinically consequential item today. From a Middlesex University group, a PRISMA-compliant systematic review of beta-blocker use in emergency department presentations following recent cocaine use. The clinical context — for almost three decades the standard teaching has been to avoid beta-blockers in cocaine intoxication, on the theoretical grounds that blocking the beta-receptor leaves alpha-adrenergic stimulation unopposed, leading to a hypertensive crisis and worsened coronary vasospasm. That doctrine traces back to a small case series in the late 1980s and has been baked into ACLS-adjacent algorithms and tox-textbook chapters ever since. The team identified four retrospective ED cohorts totalling 1140 patients, with 503 receiving at least one beta-blocker dose during their cocaine-related presentation. Across the four cohorts, beta-blocker use was not associated with increased in-hospital mortality or malignant arrhythmias. Myocardial infarction signal was heterogeneous and definition-sensitive. Hemodynamics, importantly, showed no hypertensive surge — if anything, modest systolic blood pressure reductions. Risk of bias was moderate and GRADE certainty very low to low. The honest read is that the strong contraindication does not survive a structured look at the actual human data, though the authors appropriately caveat that severe intoxication and myocardial depression phenotypes were underrepresented. For our audience this matters in two ways — first, in collaborative care with emergency medicine and toxicology around stimulant-use-disorder patients, this is an evidence base worth being aware of when the team is debating beta-blocker administration. Second, mechanistically, the unopposed-alpha hypothesis was always pharmacologically thin given the kinetics of cocaine plasma levels in the typical ED window, and this is yet another example of a piece of received clinical pharmacology lore that the empirical record doesn't support.

Second item, into psychiatric neuroscience. From the Picciotto and Jiang labs at Yale, in a Shank3 deletion-4-to-22 mouse model of autism, characterization of how nucleus accumbens neurons encode social information and what goes wrong. Some framing — Shank3 encodes a postsynaptic scaffold protein at glutamatergic synapses, and Shank3 mutations are one of the more penetrant genetic risk factors for autism with prominent social deficits. The Shank3 mouse has been around for years and the social-preference phenotype is robust, but the mechanistic story has generally been framed as a loss of social motivation — that mutant animals are simply less drawn to social stimuli. This paper argues that framing is wrong, and the difference is consequential for drug-discovery thinking. Using genetic capture of social-interaction-activated ensembles plus chemogenetics, optogenetics, and one-photon calcium imaging, they show that in wild-type mice the NAc social ensemble encodes appetitive social cues — they drive social investigation. In the Shank3 mutant, the same anatomically defined ensemble now actively encodes social avoidance. NAc neurons are hyperactive, and they show hypermodulatory responses specifically to social novelty. The clean intervention is suppressing the activity of the social ensemble during social novelty exposure, which prevents future social avoidance and restores social investigation. The reframing here matters — if the deficit is active aversion rather than loss of motivation, then drugs that promote social reward through dopaminergic or oxytocinergic mechanisms are a worse pharmacological fit than agents that dampen NAc hyperactivity during social engagement. That's an inversion of the working pharmacology assumption for ASD social symptoms and worth tracking as people design new programs against this indication.

Third, a methodology paper on depression and facial-affect perception, from Peter Bex's group at Northeastern. Standard paradigms for testing emotion recognition in depression present pre-selected faces and ask whether subjects label them correctly — which conflates the cognitive process of building an internal emotional representation with its endpoint product. The team replaced that with a genetic-algorithm face-synthesis task in a 199-dimensional 3D face-shape space. Fifty-seven undergraduates, seventeen at risk per Beck Depression Inventory-Two scores, iteratively evolved photorealistic faces across seven generations to match their own internal representations of thirteen emotions. They then extracted five evolutionary metrics — convergence speed, velocity, stability, range, and endpoint peak intensity. The key result — at-risk participants converged on embarrassment representations roughly twice as fast as low-risk participants, an effect size of point eight eight that survives permutation validation. Anger representations showed greater evolutionary instability in the at-risk group. Critically, the endpoint face intensities did not differ between groups for any emotion. So the perceptual product looks identical, but the cognitive process of generating it differs. The methodological point is the interesting one — it suggests rigid self-conscious-emotion schemas and unstable anger templates are an underexplored axis of depression-related cognitive bias that endpoint-recognition paradigms have systematically missed. Sample size is modest and the subclinical at-risk population caps the generalization, but as a paradigm contribution this is a credible way to extend the cognitive-schema theory of depression into operationalizable measures.

Fourth, sleep neurophysiology with implications for closed-loop neuromodulation. From the University of Chicago, simultaneous scalp EEG plus stereo-EEG from eleven thalamic nuclei across twenty-four full-night recordings in six epilepsy patients. The question — slow oscillations of NREM sleep vary in cortical propagation extent, from widespread Global events to spatially restricted Frontal events, and these propagation classes are thought to support different memory consolidation functions. Whether individual thalamic nuclei are differentially engaged across propagation types had not been directly testable in humans before. All well-sampled nuclei showed temporal co-occurrence with prefrontal slow oscillations but occupied distinct phase positions in the cortical cycle. The pulvinar — specifically the medial pulvinar — showed the strongest phase locking across subjects, preferring the ascending post-trough phase. Frontal slow oscillations were associated with significant phase locking in pulvinar, whereas Global slow oscillations preferentially synchronized the centromedian nucleus near the cortical down-state trough. The predictive finding is that pre-onset pulvinar activity — suppressed alpha, sigma, and beta power, plus delta-phase cross-frequency coupling — predicts whether a slow oscillation will be Global or Frontal before it begins. Two implications for our audience. First, this contradicts the workhorse picture of the thalamus as a functionally uniform structure during NREM. Second, it gives a real target for closed-loop neurostimulation programs — pulvinar pre-onset signatures could in principle trigger external stimulation calibrated to a specific propagation mode. The sample is six patients and replication in larger cohorts is needed, but the within-subject statistics are clean.

Fifth and adjacent in methodology — from the Ilmoniemi group at Aalto University, an adaptive Bayesian protocol for transcranial magnetic stimulation localization of motor cortical representations. The clinical use case is presurgical planning, where neurosurgeons need accurate non-invasive motor mapping. Existing localization either ignores the spatial information in the TMS-induced electric field or uses fixed acquisition schemes that don't update from earlier responses. The team's method represents the cortical origin of motor responses as a spatial probability distribution, updated after each stimulus, with the next stimulus optimized in real time to maximize expected localization improvement given the prior responses. Validated in eight subjects with multi-locus TMS as the adaptive arm and single-coil TMS with randomized placement as the non-adaptive reference. The adaptive protocol at least halved the number of stimuli required for stable localization — converging in about sixty stimuli on average — with ninety-five-percent highest-density regions often below ten square millimeters by one hundred fifty stimuli. The relevance for receptor-and-reason listeners is the broader trend — Bayesian optimization frameworks are starting to make TMS-based assays — both for motor mapping presurgically and for neuropsychiatric biomarker work — substantially more efficient and reproducible. The active-learning principle here generalizes to any TMS protocol where stimulus placement isn't pre-committed.

Sixth, into Parkinsonism structural biology. From the Schulman lab at the Max Planck Institute of Biochemistry, with the Harper lab at Harvard, cryo-EM structures defining how the two Parkinsonism-associated proteins FBXO7 and PI31 regulate the twenty-S core particle of the proteasome. The clinical motivation — FBXO7 and PI31 variants are linked to rare familial Parkinsonian syndromes, implicating proteasome dysregulation in a subset of neurodegeneration, but the mechanistic basis was unclear. Both proteins can associate independently with the proteasome. FBXO7's carboxy-terminal domain engages multiple subunits inside the twenty-S core and blocks the beta-five peptidase activity. PI31, separately, engages all three catalytic sites in the twenty-S core, including a previously uncharacterized structural basis for beta-one inhibition. The disease-associated variants disrupt both proteasome inhibition itself and assembly of the SKP1–FBXO7–PI31 complex. Why this matters — there has been a recurring temptation in PD drug discovery to think of proteasome activity as a clean tuning knob for alpha-synuclein clearance, but the molecular logic of how the proteasome is regulated by these adapter proteins has been a black box. With the structural basis now in hand for two independent inhibitory engagements at distinct catalytic sites, there's a more principled foundation for thinking about whether pharmacologically dialing up or down specific catalytic subunits is achievable in CNS, and which variants would change the calculus. This is the kind of paper that quietly seeds the next round of small-molecule design programs.

Seventh, staying in Parkinson pathology — from the Stahlberg group at EPFL, correlative light and electron microscopy of phosphorylated alpha-synuclein inclusions in postmortem substantia nigra of Parkinson's disease brain. The method, in brief — they target serine-129-phosphorylated alpha-synuclein-positive inclusions with fluorescence first, then do EM on the exact same structures to characterize the ultrastructure of bona-fide pathological inclusions rather than mistaking incidental cellular features for Lewy pathology. The findings are clean and ultrastructurally specific. Somatic phospho-serine-129 inclusions in nigral dopaminergic neurons are consistently fibrillar. Membranous-type inclusions, by contrast, are restricted to neuritic processes. Within both populations there's marked ultrastructural heterogeneity, ranging from predominantly membranous to mixed membranous-fibrillar. This connects to a debate from the Lashuel work several years ago — the surprising observation that Lewy bodies contained more membranous material than pure protein fibrils. This paper shows that the resolution is partly compartmental — soma is fibrillar, neurites are membranous — and that there is a genuine continuum within neurites between mostly-membranous and mixed forms. For receptor-level intervention thinking, the distinction matters because membrane-engulfing pathology and fibril-driven pathology have different chemical-biology handles — small molecules that disaggregate fibrils versus agents that prevent membrane sequestration are different programs, and the prevalence of each will dictate which clinical phenotypes will respond.

Eighth, into agentic AI for bioinformatics — from a group at Auburn University with collaborators, PAG-Agent, a virtual research assistant for pathway-level analysis. The pain point — standard pathway-analysis workflows produce long lists of statistically significant pathways that are hard to interpret, validate against experimental context, or connect to a literature base. PAG-Agent integrates pathway-level statistical analysis, context-aware biological interpretation, literature-supported reasoning, and scientific writing within one workflow. It accepts bulk and single-cell transcriptomic data through click-based and chat-based interactions. The differentiator from generic enrichment tools is that it incorporates experimental conditions and research objectives to prioritize biologically relevant pathways, not just statistically ranked ones. Case-study validation on three Alzheimer's transcriptomic datasets showed consistent identification of neurodegeneration-related pathways across multiple analysis methods. In a citation-retrieval benchmark, PAG-Agent outperformed six competing LLMs across five common literature-support scenarios. That last point is the one to take away — the dominant failure mode of LLM-augmented bioinformatics tools has been confidently hallucinated citations to nonexistent papers, and the gating problem for adoption is whether the literature support is trustworthy. A purpose-built pathway-analysis agent with a measurable citation-fidelity advantage over generic LLMs is the right architectural direction.

Ninth, on agentic AI infrastructure — BioManus, an MCP-native graph-planning agent for biomedical workflows, on arXiv from a group at the King Abdullah University of Science and Technology and collaborators. Some framing — Model Context Protocol, MCP, is the open standard that lets large language model agents call structured external tools, and over the past six months MCP-based architectures have been showing up across spatial transcriptomics, ChatSpatial last week being the example. BioManus addresses a structural problem with these systems — current biomedical agents face two coupled bottlenecks. Bioinformatics tools are extremely heterogeneous in interfaces and execution environments. And agent planning relies on flat prompt-retrieved tool descriptions, which means context windows blow up as the tool inventory grows, producing tool confusion and unstable planning. The team's contribution is twofold. First, a BioinfoMCP Compiler that converts heterogeneous bioinformatics software into standardized MCP servers, creating a large executable MCP ecosystem. Second, organizing this ecosystem as a typed heterogeneous graph over tools, operations, datatypes, and workflow stages — at inference time, retrieving compact task-specific subgraphs and synthesizing operation-level workflow scaffolds. The headline efficiency claim is that planning context compresses on the order of total-tool-count divided by workflow-horizon-times-average-candidates-per-operation, which decouples planning complexity from raw tool inventory size. Experiments on BioAgentBench and LAB-Bench show improvements in execution accuracy, workflow validity, and context efficiency over advanced biomedical agent baselines. The architectural argument is the durable contribution — scalable biomedical reasoning needs structured executable capability graphs, not larger flat tool-retrieval prompts. Pairs neatly with PAG-Agent in framing the same conclusion from different ends — that domain-specific structure imposed on the tool layer is what makes these systems actually usable.

Tenth and the curiosity close. From a group at Wuhan Sports University, a preprint titled "exercise-induced sweat suppresses depression-like behaviors in mice." Their starting point is a transcriptome-based bioinformatics pipeline showing that the gene signature of depression is anti-correlated with the gene signature of exercise-induced sweat — meaning the transcriptional changes that exercise-sweat exposure produces look approximately opposite to those in depression. To follow that prediction, they used a lipopolysaccharide-induced mouse depression model and compared topical or systemic exercise-induced sweat to fluoxetine. The behavioral readouts — increased sucrose preference, reduced tail-suspension immobility, increased elevated-plus-maze exploration — moved in the expected direction, with the authors claiming high-dose exercise-induced sweat matched or exceeded fluoxetine. I'm including this because it's such an unusual claim that the audience deserves to see it surfaced, and because the gene-signature-anti-correlation framing is a legitimate emerging discovery pattern in pharmacology — Connectivity Map and CMap-derived approaches have produced several real hits this way. But I'd treat the central finding skeptically. The active component isn't identified, the mode of delivery isn't fully characterized, and the LPS depression model has well-known specificity issues — anti-inflammatory interventions of essentially any kind tend to produce signal in that paradigm because LPS-induced depression-like behavior is largely an inflammatory phenotype rather than a depression-specific one. The interesting question this raises is whether the exercise benefit signal in psychiatric disorders has a sweat-borne component at all — and the cleaner version of the experiment would be in a non-inflammatory depression model. File this as hypothesis-generating, not as a therapeutic claim.

Closing thoughts. Three patterns held across today's items. First, several papers — the beta-blocker review, the Shank3 NAc encoding, the Lewy ultrastructure — are inversions of long-running working assumptions, where the field's default mental model turns out to be either oversimplified or affirmatively wrong. Second, the agentic-AI tooling for bioinformatics is now consistently building structured-capability-graph approaches with measurable benchmarks rather than freeform-LLM-codegen architectures, and that's a real architectural convergence worth tracking. Third, the methodology papers — the GA face-synthesis task, the adaptive Bayesian TMS localizer, the CLEM workflow on Lewy pathology — all use computational pipelines to extract signal that classical paradigms have systematically smoothed over. That's a coherent theme. That's it for today.
