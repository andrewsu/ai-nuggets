# Receptor & Reason — 2026-06-30

## Script

Good morning. It's Tuesday, June 30th, 2026 — Receptor and Reason. Ten items today. We open on a clean mouse model dissection of why APOE4 carriers get ARIA on anti-amyloid antibodies, work through three Alzheimer's mechanism-and-discovery papers, then into a structural story on AMPA receptor gating in LTP, two synaptic-condensate papers in ADHD and interneuron biology, an antipsychotic-versus-schizophrenia transcriptome meta-analysis, a fear-circuit gene discovery in PTSD, a methodologically important caution on a 5-HT4 tool compound that's actually an AChE inhibitor, and close on an evaluation of clinical reasoning in frontier language models.

Leading off — anti-amyloid immunotherapy drives APOE4-specific increases in glial reactivity, perivascular immune activation, and ARIA-like events. This one is the clinically important paper of the week. Anti-amyloid antibodies are the first disease-modifying class for Alzheimer's, and their adoption has been slowed by amyloid-related imaging abnormalities — ARIA-E for edema, ARIA-H for microhemorrhages — that disproportionately strike APOE4 carriers. Roughly seventy percent of the Alzheimer's population carries at least one E4 allele, so the safety question is structural to the indication. The Johnson lab at Kentucky used the EFAD mouse — humanized APOE on a 5xFAD amyloidosis background — to compare anti-amyloid therapy across the three APOE isoforms head to head. Nine-month-old E2, E3, and E4 FAD mice got twelve weeks of weekly chimeric aducanumab or IgG control. E4 mice cleared plaques like the others, but on MRI and histology they accumulated cerebral microhemorrhages, with microglia and astrocyte reactivity concentrated specifically in the perivascular compartment. Single-cell and spatial transcriptomics on the neurovascular unit showed a loss of vascular plasticity and a coordinated upregulation of inflammatory and immune signaling that the E2 and E3 mice did not show. The conclusion the authors land on, and it's the right one, is that the E4 cerebrovasculature is uniquely susceptible to antibody-mediated vascular damage, and that immunological targets in the perivascular compartment — not just antibody dose or schedule — are where ARIA risk mitigation has to live. For anyone following lecanemab and donanemab in the real world, this paper specifies the cell types you'd watch in next-generation immunomodulatory co-therapy.

Second — staying in Alzheimer's vasculature, mTOR drives cerebrovascular dysfunction and blood-brain-barrier breakdown in a model of Alzheimer's with cerebral amyloid angiopathy. Cerebral amyloid angiopathy — vascular A-beta deposition — is the proximal cause of intracerebral hemorrhage and ischemic stroke in advanced AD, and it shows up in normal aging too. The mechanistic claim here is that mTOR is upstream of the whole cascade. In Tg2576 mice with sex-balanced design, mTOR activation drives accumulation of fibrillar vascular A-beta, which impairs endothelium-dependent cerebrovascular reactivity. mTOR-dependent breakdown of tight junctions remodels the BBB and increases cerebral microhemorrhages. mTOR also drives neurovascular uncoupling via nNOS dysfunction and impaired non-NOS-dependent components of neurovascular coupling. Rapamycin reversed cognitive deficits in contextual memory, and that rescue tracked with reduced fibrillar A-beta in vessels, restored vasodilation, fewer microhemorrhages, and restored neurovascular coupling. The pharmacology lesson worth carrying around is that mTOR inhibition is now connected mechanistically to both ARIA-relevant vascular pathology — note the linkage to the prior paper — and to the cognitive-impairment phenotype, in the same model. Whether rapamycin or rapalogs survive the CNS pharmacokinetic, immunosuppressive, and metabolic profile concerns in older adults is the open question, but the target biology is now well-defined.

Third — the CHIP-LMO7-BAG5 complex controls tau clearance and yields repurposed and newly designed therapeutic candidates for Alzheimer's disease. Huda Zoghbi's group ran a cross-species genetic screen and identified LMO7 as a regulator of tau levels in vivo. The mechanism is elegant: LMO7 bridges the E3 ligase CHIP and its inhibitory cochaperone BAG5, holding CHIP in a state that can't efficiently ubiquitinate tau. Knocking down LMO7 in adult tauopathy mice unleashes CHIP, drops total and phospho-tau, attenuates gliosis, and rescues memory deficits. The drug-discovery payoff is a textbook example of the AlphaFold-plus-virtual-screen pipeline executed end to end. They used AlphaFold-derived structural models of the complex and AI-driven virtual screening to nominate FDA-approved drugs that disrupt the LMO7-BAG5 interaction. Two emerged: telmisartan — the angiotensin II receptor blocker that's been at the edge of CNS repurposing for years — and an optimized mycophenolic acid derivative they call MPA-2. Oral telmisartan or MPA-2, or viral delivery of a CHIP-derived competing peptide, reduced pathogenic tau in tauopathy mice without overt toxicity. The implication is that CHIP-LMO7-BAG5 is a druggable node, and it's relevant not just for Alzheimer's tauopathy but for STUB1-associated ataxias where CHIP itself is the variant. Telmisartan being repurposable here is a real result given its safety profile and CNS penetration.

Fourth — a structural biology pivot into long-term potentiation: cryo-EM of hippocampal AMPA receptors reveals how PRRT1, also known as SynDIG4, gates GluA1's role in LTP. The selective involvement of GluA1-type AMPA receptors in LTP has been a black box because both GluA1 and GluA2 subunits live in the same dendrites, and the basis for the GluA1 selectivity wasn't mechanistically clear. The cryo-EM structures show that PRRT1 associates preferentially with GluA1 via its membrane-embedded CD225 domain, and the key trick is that PRRT1 sequesters the GluA1 carboxy-terminal tail through a previously unappreciated lipid modification at cysteine 825. Once sequestered, the GluA1 regulatory motifs that have to be available for LTP — including the CaMKII binding site that gets phosphorylated at serine 831 — are physically inaccessible. The functional confirmation closes the loop. Overexpressing PRRT1 impairs LTP. Mutating cysteine 825 to serine, which abolishes the lipid modification and frees the tail, rescues LTP. The model is that PRRT1-SynDIG4 controls the size of the GluA1 pool that's mobilizable for trafficking into the synapse during LTP induction — and the lipid modification at Cys825 is the switch. For pharmacology, this is the cleanest structural picture yet of where to intervene if you want to bidirectionally tune the LTP-mobilizable AMPA pool, which is the substrate every cognitive-enhancer program is implicitly targeting.

Fifth — bridging directly to synaptic biology on the postsynaptic side, CaMKII alpha biomolecular condensation is required for structural LTP, and a mutation in its phase-separation interface causes an ADHD-like phenotype. CaMKII has been studied for decades as an enzyme, but recent work has shown it also undergoes liquid-liquid phase separation with substrate proteins, including GluN2B-containing NMDA receptors. The physiological significance of that condensate-forming behavior has been speculative. This group generated CaMKII alpha knock-in mice carrying an I205K point mutation in the hydrophobic pocket that's essential for phase separation. The mice show complete loss of structural LTP despite normal dendritic spine morphology, marked hyperactivity, and profound deficits in aversive memory formation. Two things make this clinically translatable. Atomoxetine — an FDA-approved selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor used for ADHD — ameliorated the hyperactivity phenotype, so the circuit consequence is in a treatable space. And the authors identified a human patient with an I205N variant presenting with ADHD and mild intellectual disability, mirroring the mouse phenotype. Additional neurodevelopmental-disorder variants in the same hydrophobic pocket also disrupted phase separation in vitro, and molecular dynamics simulations showed that these variants destabilize the CaMKII-GluN2B condensate through distinct mechanisms. So CaMKII phase separation is established as causally upstream of spine plasticity, hyperactivity, and memory — and it's a defined molecular handle for a subset of ADHD and intellectual disability variants.

Sixth — sticking with phase separation, Btbd11 regulates glutamatergic synapse organization in GABAergic inhibitory interneurons through condensate formation. Glutamatergic input onto inhibitory interneurons has distinct basal transmission and plasticity properties from glutamatergic input onto principal cells, and impaired interneuron excitation is implicated in schizophrenia and anxiety. The molecular basis for those interneuron-specific synaptic properties has been thin. Btbd11 is interneuron-specific, and this paper shows it forms a phase-separated condensate with PSD-95 and TARP-gamma-2, stabilizing TARP-gamma-2 and GluA1 within glutamatergic interneuron synapses. Super-resolution imaging shows Btbd11 forms nanoscale clusters that correlate with PSD-95 nanostructure. Btbd11 deletion shrinks the postsynaptic density, reorganizes PSD-95 nanoclusters, and drastically reduces glutamatergic input onto interneurons. The reason to care, beyond the synaptic biology, is that interneuron-specific PSD architecture is now experimentally accessible as a therapeutic target distinct from principal-neuron synapses. Most schizophrenia hypotheses run through interneuron hypofunction; until now the molecular machinery has been studied as if interneurons used the same scaffold proteins the same way. Btbd11 says they don't.

Seventh — and complementary to that mechanistic picture of interneuron biology, a meta-analysis of the converging effects of antipsychotic classes on the frontal cortex transcriptome in rodents and non-human primates. The Tseng-Akil-Watson group pulled five rodent transcriptomic datasets from the Gemma database, eight drug-versus-control comparisons covering both first- and second-generation antipsychotics, sixty-eight animals total, and ran a random-effects meta-analysis with FDR correction. Sixty-three genes were differentially expressed across antipsychotic exposures, and the enrichment landed on three pathway clusters: oligodendrocyte development and myelination, cellular stress responses, and cardiovascular function. The exploratory analysis adding non-human primate data brought the DEG count to 117 and confirmed the rodent signal. Here's the part that should make every schizophrenia post-mortem transcriptomics paper nervous: when they compared the antipsychotic-induced signature against post-mortem schizophrenia signatures, some of the gene-expression patterns that have been attributed to schizophrenia biology overlapped with the antipsychotic-induced signature. The natural inference is that a fraction of what we've been calling schizophrenia-related expression changes may actually reflect chronic antipsychotic exposure. For comparing patient cohorts to controls, treatment status has to be modeled, not just adjusted for — and for interpreting a published schizophrenia DEG list, the antipsychotic confound has to be on the table. The myelination and oligodendrocyte signal is also worth noting because it converges with multiple independent lines suggesting antipsychotics affect oligodendrocyte biology, which is a candidate handle for some of the structural imaging findings in long-treated patients.

Eighth — fine mapping of PTSD GWAS reveals a role for amygdala FOXP2 in regulation of fear and threat responses. FOXP2 is best known as a transcription factor linked to speech and language, but the recent large PTSD GWAS pulled it out as a top putative risk gene, and the eQTL fine-mapping pointed to amygdala-specific expression as the relevant tissue. In vertebrates, FOXP2 mRNA is most densely expressed in the intercalated cells of the amygdala — ITCs — which sit between the basolateral and central amygdala and provide critical inhibitory gating of central-amygdala fear output. Knocking down FOXP2 in mouse ITC neurons reduced freezing during and after auditory fear conditioning — less fear expression, not more. Patch-clamp from individual ITC neurons showed FOXP2 knockdown increased intrinsic excitability and action potential frequency, with reduced potassium channel conductance, consistent with more inhibition onto central amygdala and reduced downstream fear. Bulk RNA-seq from mouse amygdala after the knockdown confirmed decreased potassium channel transcription and showed broader regulation of CRH, Wnt, and neurokinin signaling pathways. The translational check is striking: medial amygdala post-mortem tissue from human PTSD cases with low FOXP2 expression also showed decreased potassium channel transcription, and the mouse downstream gene set was significantly enriched for orthologs of tier-one PTSD GWAS risk genes. So FOXP2 in ITCs acts as a regulatory hub coordinating expression of multiple top PTSD risk genes — and the relationship to fear is the opposite of what a naive risk-gene-equals-more-fear model would predict. Reduced FOXP2 means more ITC excitability, more inhibition of central amygdala, less freezing. The directionality matters for any drug program targeting this axis.

Ninth — a methodological caution worth knowing about before you cite a 5-HT4 receptor experiment. RS67333 is a tool compound widely used as a partial 5-HT4 agonist in neuroscience and pharmacology. This paper, from a Wightman-adjacent striatal-release group, used fast-scan cyclic voltammetry and the GRAB-ACh-3.0 acetylcholine sensor in mouse striatal slices to test whether RS67333 modulates dopamine and acetylcholine release through 5-HT4 receptors. RS67333 modulated electrically evoked dopamine release in dorsolateral striatum and nucleus accumbens core, and the effect was abolished by a nicotinic antagonist. In parallel, RS67333 prolonged extracellular acetylcholine lifetime and directly inhibited striatal acetylcholinesterase activity. BIMU8, a structurally different 5-HT4 ligand that does not inhibit AChE, had no effect on striatal acetylcholine or dopamine. The cleanest reading is that RS67333's striatal effects in this paradigm are not 5-HT4-mediated at all — they're driven by acetylcholinesterase inhibition. That has direct consequences for two literatures. Anyone interpreting a striatal experiment that used RS67333 as a selective 5-HT4 probe needs to revisit the conclusions. And the alternative reading is more interesting: RS67333 may be useful as a CNS-penetrant AChE inhibitor pharmacotype for acetylcholine-dopamine disorders — a scaffold-level handle that is distinct from donepezil and rivastigmine. Either way, "selective tool compound" is an empirical claim, not a label.

Tenth — closing on agentic AI in clinical reasoning. A team at UCSF asks whether modern language models reaching sixty to seventy percent diagnostic accuracy on hard clinical case benchmarks are doing stable, clinically-grounded reasoning, or pattern-matching to surface features. They built clinical reasoning graphs — structured representations extracted from a model's diagnostic trace using a domain ontology with five node types and seven edge types — and analyzed seven hundred fifty traces from five frontier language models across fifty New England Journal of Medicine Clinicopathological Conference cases, under three different prompt conditions. The finding is that the reasoning graphs for correct diagnoses and incorrect diagnoses are essentially indistinguishable — graph-similarity 0.488 for correct pairs versus 0.484 for incorrect pairs, and within-cluster similarity matched between-cluster similarity. Across multiple-testing correction, no comparison survived. Asking the models to do explicit discriminating-feature analysis with structured reflection prompting did move things — a thirty-three percent increase on that specific subtask. The honest summary, and the title of the paper, is competence without consistency. The accuracy is real, but the reasoning underneath isn't stable across clinically similar cases. For anyone deploying a model as a diagnostic aide in psychiatric or neurological assessment, that's the operational warning to attach to the accuracy number — the same model giving the same case-shape can route through completely different reasoning paths, and post-hoc graph extraction is a tractable way to surface that variance.

That's ten. The piece I'd come back to first is the APOE4 ARIA paper — it specifies cell types and signaling pathways that turn ARIA risk from a black-box safety signal into a tractable target, and that's where the next generation of anti-amyloid co-therapy lives. The CaMKII alpha condensation result is the most molecularly satisfying single finding of the day — phase separation moving from a structural curiosity to a defined therapeutic substrate. And the RS67333-equals-AChE-inhibitor result is the methodological land mine to be aware of if you use 5-HT4 tool compounds in any striatal or cortical pharmacology work. Have a good Tuesday.
