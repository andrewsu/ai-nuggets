## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. It's Saturday, July eleventh. Ten items today, and the through-line is affective control and neuronal vulnerability — how you construct, diagnose, and rescue them at every scale from an RL agent to a chimeric brain. We open with a computational-psychiatry paper that manipulates seven distinct disorders in a reinforcement-learning agent as tunable knobs and reads out a transdiagnostic geometry. Then a clozapine mechanism-of-action paper that finally names a druggable process in schizophrenic cortical organoids. Then a patient-derived tau chimera that produces bona fide adult Alzheimer's tau tangles in human neurons living inside a mouse brain, and shows the human neurons are intrinsically more vulnerable than their mouse neighbors. Then human single-neuron recordings in the medial temporal lobe on delay discounting and impulsivity. Then striatal dopamine locking onto motif boundaries in zebra finch song, a natural skill learned without cues or rewards. Then two astrocyte papers — one on metabolic anaplerosis controlling seizure susceptibility, one on locus coeruleus tone maintaining astrocyte arbors in the hippocampus. Then a Rett syndrome paper that pins down why the trofinetide peptide actually works. Then a disease-aware small-molecule generative model, and we close with a piece of textbook GPCR–ion channel pharmacology on the nociceptin receptor and Cav1.2.

Lead item — A Transdiagnostic Space of Disorder-Like Phenotypes in Reinforcement Learning Agents, out this week on arXiv. This paper takes computational psychiatry seriously as an agentic-AI question and treats it with unusual discipline. Prior work in this vein induces one or two disorders in a reinforcement-learning agent by hand-tuned reward shaping, labels the resulting behavior post hoc, and reports single runs. That is nowhere near a benchmark. Here, the authors recast disorder modeling as dose-controllable manipulation of cognitive appraisal signals in an appraisal-guided PPO agent. Seven disorders — anxiety, mania, obsessive-compulsive checking, depression, impulsivity, addiction, and post-traumatic stress — each map to a single knob grounded in an accepted computational-psychiatry account, and each is measured by a preregistered assay tied to a recognized paradigm. Across more than a thousand runs — ten seeds, four controls, ninety-five-percent confidence intervals — every disorder shows a graded, monotone dose-response that no control reproduces. That is the induced result. The three unexpected results are the interesting ones. First, the disorders self-organize into a two-dimensional affective space in which mania mirrors anxiety — a structural finding the reward function did not encode. Second, removing the knob remits the reward-distortion disorders — mania, checking, addiction — but does not remit the avoidance disorders — anxiety, PTSD — which instead recover under a graded-exposure curriculum, a computational analog of exposure therapy. Third, two simultaneous knobs interact non-additively, which yields testable comorbidity predictions. And to answer the natural skepticism about whether this is a grid-world artifact, three of the knobs — depression, addiction, anxiety — transfer cleanly to a three-dimensional pixel environment with a standard convolutional agent and no appraisal critic. Cross-assay dissociation holds across both domains. This is not a psychiatric evaluation of a large language model — it is a physically instantiated model of disorder itself. For anyone thinking about computational-psychiatry benchmarks, mechanistic disorder ontology, or the affective-control failure modes that will actually matter as agents get deployed into human-facing roles, this framework is worth understanding. It is the most disciplined version of induce-then-treat that has appeared in this space.

Second — a paper you should not miss if you work anywhere near schizophrenia drug discovery. Cortical brain organoids derived from a large cohort of schizophrenia patients and healthy controls, exposed to clozapine over an unusually long twenty-four-week window, with comprehensive multi-omic profiling. Clozapine remains the only pharmacological option for treatment-resistant schizophrenia, and its cellular mechanism of action has never been convincingly pinned down. The result: a schizophrenia-specific, metabolism-independent alternative-splicing program that clozapine reverses at non-toxic doses. The dominant events are exon skipping and intron retention, concentrated in glutamatergic neurons. Two other pieces raise the confidence. The splicing program was recapitulated in primary human brain tissue, so it is not a pure organoid artifact. And it captures splicing-mediated genetic signals of schizophrenia risk, meaning the same events are enriched at loci with common-variant support. The take: clozapine's therapeutic window may be largely explained by its splicing-modulation activity in glutamatergic neurons, and the specific spliceoform-level targets are now nameable, which is the first step toward asking whether a cleaner spliceoform-modulator could deliver the efficacy without the myocarditis, agranulocytosis, and metabolic burden that keep clozapine restricted to last-resort use.

Third — patient-derived tau-seeded human neuronal chimeras. The chimera field has been circling a specific hole: adult mice express essentially only 4R tau while adult humans express a roughly one-to-one ratio of 3R to 4R tau, and mouse tauopathy models rely on frontotemporal-dementia mutations that are absent from Alzheimer's disease itself. This paper closes both gaps. Human pluripotent stem-cell-derived neural progenitors get transplanted into neonatal immunodeficient mouse brains. The human neurons mature in place, reach adult tau isoform composition — all six isoforms, that one-to-one 3R-to-4R ratio — and then receive intracerebral injection of pathological tau seeds extracted from postmortem AD brain. What follows is remarkable. The human neurons develop mature Alzheimer's tau pathology: paired helical filaments, straight filaments, neurofibrillary tangles, neuropil threads. Pathology spreads across anatomically connected regions. Plasma phospho-tau-two-seventeen rises. Memory deficits emerge. And critically, when the same seeds are given to wild-type mice without the human graft, no tangles develop — so the seed itself is not sufficient, the human neuronal substrate is required. Inside the chimera you can score pathology per neuron and compare humans to mice sharing the same tissue: roughly three-quarters of AT8-positive cells are human despite humans being only a fraction of total neurons. Single-nucleus RNA-seq maps this to higher basal expression of tau-uptake genes and widespread synaptic suppression in human neurons after seeding, while mouse neurons stay transcriptomically resilient. Adding a familial-Alzheimer's PSEN2 mutation on top exacerbates the pathology. If you are running a tau-directed therapeutic and your preclinical readouts are on P301L mice, this is the model to add to your package. It also names a mechanistic hook — tau-uptake genes as a species-specific vulnerability set — for anyone thinking about targets upstream of tangle formation.

Fourth — human medial temporal lobe neurons linking future reward coding with intertemporal choice and impulsivity. Delay discounting has been mapped extensively at the behavioral and fMRI level, but the single-neuron picture in humans has been essentially absent. This work exploits the rare access afforded by intracranial recordings in refractory-epilepsy patients undergoing pre-surgical workup. During intertemporal-choice tasks, decision-predictive neurons appear in the amygdala and hippocampus. Delay-encoding neurons appear in the entorhinal cortex and hippocampus, with hippocampal population activity carrying prospective temporal information — meaning the neurons represent the upcoming delay, not just the current state. The impulsivity finding is the clinically relevant one. Impulsive individuals show diminished prospective temporal coding, and their neurons predict decisions only in the very brief window before the choice is reported, rather than well in advance. Put another way, impulsivity is not just weighting an immediate reward more heavily — it is a compressed prospective time horizon at the level of hippocampal population dynamics. This has direct implications for how you interpret behavioral discount rates in psychiatric populations and for how you might target impulsivity pharmacologically or through neuromodulation. If you are building computational-psychiatry accounts of ADHD, addiction, or manic phases of bipolar disorder, this is now the single-unit evidence you should cite for the prospective-time hypothesis.

Fifth — striatal dopamine at learned sequence boundaries in birdsong. Reward-prediction-error dopamine is standard textbook; sequence-boundary dopamine is not. Zebra finch song is learned during development and produced in adulthood without external instruction or immediate reward, which makes it a clean natural-skill preparation. Using the dopamine sensor dLight-one-point-three-b in Area X — the striatal nucleus of the song basal ganglia — with fiber photometry in singing birds, the authors show a stereotyped dip-then-peak pattern in dopamine locked to song motif onset, with a smaller rebound near motif offset. The developmental piece is where the paper gets sharp. Early in learning, the peak sits near motif offset. As song matures, the peak backward-shifts to motif onset, exactly the shape a temporal-difference-learning account predicts as the reward-associated signal migrates to the earliest predictor of reward — except here there is no external reward at all. Temporally targeted optogenetic inhibition of dopamine at motif onset — leaving the rest of the trajectory intact — produces gradual and severe deterioration of adult song, so the boundary signal is not just correlated with the behavior, it is required for maintenance. The concept to take with you: dopamine at natural sequence boundaries is doing self-referential temporal-difference bookkeeping on internally scored performance, with no external reward in the loop. That is a genuinely useful frame for thinking about tic disorders, action-sequence deficits in Parkinson's disease, and any skill-learning phenotype where the animal is grading its own performance.

Sixth — mitochondrial pyruvate import in astrocytes as a non-redundant anaplerotic gate for seizure resistance. Textbook astrocyte metabolism has emphasized glycolysis and lactate export to neurons — the astrocyte-neuron lactate shuttle. This paper does something surprising: adult astrocyte-specific deletion of MPC2, an obligatory subunit of the mitochondrial pyruvate carrier, in mice. If the shuttle were the main story you would expect a bioenergetic phenotype. Instead the mice show motor deficits, neuronal hyperexcitability, and seizure-associated lethality. Metabolic profiling shows pyruvate gets diverted toward alanine as an unsuccessful compensatory bypass. TCA-cycle flux is impaired, and — the critical readout — neurotransmitter-related metabolite pools are unbalanced: glutamate, glutamine, and GABA. The conclusion is a real reframing. Astrocytic mitochondrial pyruvate import isn't primarily bioenergetic; it's an anaplerotic supply line that keeps the excitation-inhibition balance intact by maintaining neurotransmitter pool sizes. This puts astrocyte TCA-cycle metabolism directly into the epilepsy target space, and it makes MPC and related anaplerotic entry points worth screening as anti-epileptic targets — a space we've largely left to neuronal ion channels and receptors.

Seventh — a short pharmacology paper that lands cleanly on the locus coeruleus. Adult male mice received DSP-4 — a selective LC neurotoxin, used as a tool compound for decades to model LC degeneration — with or without concurrent isoproterenol as a broad beta-adrenergic-receptor agonist. Twenty-two days after the last DSP-4 injection, dopamine-beta-hydroxylase fiber coverage in the dentate gyrus molecular layer was down by more than eighty-three percent, confirming the lesion. Sholl analysis of GFAP-stained astrocytes showed significant reductions in branching complexity in the DSP-4 group, concentrated at five to fifteen microns from the soma. Peak intersection count dropped. The surprise: isoproterenol failed to rescue morphology, even though it did increase SOX9-positive astrocyte density independently. Two implications. First, LC-derived noradrenergic tone is required for maintenance of astrocyte arbor complexity in the hippocampus, so LC neurodegeneration — which we know precedes cognitive decline in Alzheimer's and shows up in depression — will drive astrocyte remodeling as a downstream consequence that has not been foregrounded. Second, beta-adrenergic agonism alone is not sufficient to rescue that structure, which is a real caution against a naive isoproterenol-family strategy. It probably means the maintenance signal requires alpha-adrenergic engagement, or a co-transmitter, or an action on non-astrocyte cells that then feeds back. The paper is small, but the mechanism it flags is worth internalizing.

Eighth — a Rett syndrome paper that finally gives a molecular mechanism to the therapeutic activity of trofinetide, the FDA-approved IGF1 tripeptide mimetic. Using an indirect astrocyte-neuron co-culture — Rett astrocytes on one side, wild-type neurons on the other, no direct contact — the authors show that Rett astrocytes suppress excitatory synapse formation in the wild-type neurons non-cell-autonomously, and that treating those astrocytes with an IGF1-derived peptide reverses the suppression. Proteomics of Rett astrocytes and their conditioned media identifies elevated IGF-binding-protein-two as the key aberrant secretion. The peptide drives proteasomal degradation of that binding protein, increases IGF1 bioavailability, restores mitochondrial function in the astrocytes, and enhances PI-three-K/Akt signaling downstream in neurons. The clinical connection is direct: trofinetide is an IGF1 amino-terminal tripeptide analog, and this work argues its efficacy in Rett is proximally due to knocking down astrocyte-secreted IGFBP2, not to acting on neurons directly. For an FDA-approved drug in a rare pediatric neurodevelopmental disorder, actually knowing where the mechanism sits — non-neuronal, on the binding protein rather than the IGF1 axis itself — is unusually clean and reframes the biomarker work you would want to do to titrate the drug.

Ninth — DrugGen 2, a disease-aware generative language model for drug discovery. Most target-conditioned molecular generators condition on target protein sequence alone. This one adds disease ontology as a parallel conditioning input, on the premise that the same target behaves differently across disease contexts and the therapeutic outcome you actually care about is disease-specific. The architecture is a fine-tuned GPT-two on approved drugs annotated with their disease and target labels, further refined with reinforcement learning via group relative policy optimization on rewards for chemical validity, novelty, diversity, and predicted binding affinity. The evaluation case study is diabetic nephropathy across five relevant targets. Compared to two prior generators — DrugGPT and the original DrugGen — DrugGen-two produces more unique molecules, molecules more structurally similar to approved drugs on average, and better predicted binding affinities across all five targets. Reported top candidate docking scores are stronger than the reference approved drug enalapril for angiotensin-converting enzyme. Two important caveats before you get excited. The affinities are predicted, not measured — this is still docking, not a wet assay. And a well-tuned generator that produces molecules similar to approved drugs is often just interpolating within the training distribution, which is safe but not novel-target territory. That said, disease-context conditioning is a genuinely underused signal in generative drug design and this paper is a proof of concept worth reading. The natural extension for the CNS pharmacology audience is disease-ontology conditioning for psychiatric and neurological indications, where target-only generation has been especially unrewarding.

Closing on a piece of clean channel–GPCR pharmacology. The ORL-1 receptor — the nociceptin opioid receptor — is Gi/o-coupled, is expressed heavily in the hippocampus and periphery, and has an established interaction with the N-type voltage-gated calcium channel Cav-two-point-two. Whether it interacts with the L-type channel Cav-one-point-two has been genuinely unclear. In tsA-two-oh-one cells co-expressing the full Cav-one-point-two complex — alpha-one-c, alpha-two-delta-one, beta-one — plus ORL-1, co-immunoprecipitation shows ORL-1 independently binds both the alpha-one-c and alpha-two-delta-one subunits. Electrophysiology shows co-expression with ORL-1 reduces Cav-one-point-two peak current density without altering its biophysical properties — no shift in voltage dependence, no change in activation kinetics. Acute nociceptin at one micromolar does not further alter the current, which is important — this is a constitutive, agonist-independent effect on channel behavior. Confocal imaging shows ORL-1 significantly decreases Cav-one-point-two plasma-membrane expression by disrupting forward trafficking, not by promoting endocytosis. So ORL-1 sequesters or mis-routes the channel before it reaches the surface. Yesterday we were talking about the nociceptin receptor in the context of progressive multiple sclerosis and remyelination. This is a completely different face of the same receptor — the calcium-signaling face — and it is a reminder that ORL-1 antagonism has a compound-selective, tissue-selective consequence profile that will matter to whichever indication the field pushes it toward next. If a program is aiming at nociceptin-receptor modulation in the hippocampus for cognition or depression, an intrinsic effect on Cav-one-point-two surface density is now on the list of things to control for.
