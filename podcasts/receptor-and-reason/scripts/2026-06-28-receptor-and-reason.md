# Receptor & Reason — 2026-06-28

## Script

Good morning. It's Sunday, June 28th, 2026 — Receptor and Reason. Ten items today. We open on a head-to-head perturbation-modeling readout of psilocybin versus escitalopram in major depression, work our way through opioid-withdrawal circuitry, dyskinesia, methamphetamine, and serotonergic control of anesthesia emergence, then turn to two computational-pharmacology methods papers and close with a couple of agentic-AI items, ending on a multi-agent system for brain-signal interpretation.

Leading off — a methodological reframing of how to compare antidepressants from the Carhart-Harris, Deco, and Kringelbach groups, using data from the Imperial psilocybin-versus-escitalopram MDD trial. The premise: don't just measure functional connectivity at rest; perturb the connectivity model and see how it reorganizes. They build whole-brain models fit to each individual's pre- and post-treatment resting fMRI, extracting what they call generative effective connectivity. Then they push the model with systematic and local virtual perturbations across a range of intensities, re-optimize each one, and quantify the network reconfiguration index — how far the brain's effective wiring moves under stress. The headline result is a clean divergence. After psilocybin, the reconfiguration index goes up — the post-treatment brain is more reorganizable, more flexible under perturbation. After escitalopram, it goes down — the post-treatment brain settles into a more stabilized regime. Both are arguably therapeutic, but they're not the same kind of therapeutic. The conceptual payoff is that "what does an antidepressant do" stops being a single endpoint and becomes a question about whether you want to *destabilize* a stuck attractor or *fortify* an alternative one. For the psychedelic-versus-SSRI debate, this is the kind of physiology-grounded difference that the field has been groping toward, and the perturbation modeling looks like a portable methodology — applicable to any treatment with pre-post imaging.

Second — staying with affective neuroscience but moving from depression to opioid withdrawal, from Jun Wang's group with NIAAA funding. The question is how negative affect during withdrawal suppresses dopamine — the hypodopaminergic state that drives relapse. Their answer threads a specific circuit. Naloxone-precipitated fentanyl withdrawal selectively activates corticotropin-releasing factor neurons in the central amygdala and the bed nucleus of the stria terminalis. Those CRF neurons make monosynaptic excitatory connections directly onto dorsostriatal medium spiny neurons — and specifically onto striosomal MSNs, the patch compartment that's positioned to suppress dopamine release. CRF, acting on CRFR1, strengthens glutamatergic drive onto those striosomal cells in a postsynaptic-receptor-dependent way. In vivo dopamine photometry shows that withdrawal amplifies striosome-mediated suppression of striatal dopamine release, and CRFR1 antagonism prevents that amplification. So you have a complete pathway: extended amygdala stress signal, striosomal gain control, dopamine suppression, negative affect. The actionable piece is CRFR1 — pharma has cycled CRFR1 antagonists multiple times for stress disorders without a strong clinical hit, but a striosome-specific opioid-withdrawal indication with this circuit map is a sharper place to retry the chemistry.

Third — circuit-level pharmacology of levodopa-induced dyskinesia, asking what local striatal lateral inhibition is *for*. Striatal medium spiny neurons make GABAergic synaptic connections onto each other, but those lateral connections have been dismissed as too weak to matter — low connection rates, small unitary currents. This paper uses optogenetics in the dorsolateral striatum to show that those connections matter a great deal. The asymmetry is real: D2-MSN to D1-MSN lateral inhibition is the strongest of the four MSN-MSN connection types. In a 6-OHDA mouse model of Parkinson's, D2-to-D1 lateral inhibition is weakened — consistent with homeostatic compensation for the hypoactive D1-MSNs of the dopamine-depleted state. Chronic levodopa treatment restores those lateral connections to baseline strength. And — the causal test — chemogenetically suppressing D2-originating lateral inhibition lowers the threshold for the animal to develop dyskinetic involuntary movements when given levodopa. The interpretive frame is that lateral inhibition is doing action selection: D1-MSNs facilitate the intended motor program, D2-MSNs suppress competing programs, and lateral inhibition is the local microcircuit that lets one ensemble win cleanly. Dyskinesia, in that frame, is what happens when the suppression machinery breaks. For LID pharmacology — historically a story about D1 supersensitivity and downstream cortical-thalamic remodeling — this adds a *local* circuit handle that hasn't been on the standard target list.

Fourth — methamphetamine and developmental neuropeptide expression, from the Harkany lab. The setup is a high-temporal-resolution method for monitoring methamphetamine-induced hyperactivity in infant mice, distinguishing it from the biphasic response of adults and linking it to Fos activation in cortical areas executing goal-directed escape behavior. The substantive finding is in the neuropeptides. Methamphetamine differentially alters somatostatin, cholecystokinin, and galanin expression in corticolimbic areas, and in an age-specific pattern. These are co-released inhibitory neuropeptides — the slow modulators that ride alongside fast GABAergic transmission, and the framing the authors offer is that altering their expression could compromise the circuit-protective buffering against stimulant-induced hyperexcitability during critical developmental windows. The seizure-susceptibility and long-term neuronal maladaptation literature for early-life methamphetamine exposure has been somewhat orphaned from molecular mechanisms; the neuropeptide axis is a candidate that connects exposure to durable circuit vulnerability. Worth tracking.

Fifth — serotonergic control of emergence from sevoflurane anesthesia. The clinical motivation is delayed emergence, a non-trivial complication in patients with preoperative anxiety and depression. The paper, in a mouse model, runs a coherent pharmacology-plus-circuits package. Exogenous 5-HTP shortens both induction and recovery time. Chemogenetic activation of serotonergic neurons in the central amygdala or the medial prefrontal cortex — using TPH2-promoter-targeted hM3Dq DREADDs — accelerates emergence; inhibition slows it. Optogenetic activation of the CeA-to-mPFC serotonergic projection mimics the chemogenetic effect. In anxiety-and-depression-like mouse models, where emergence is normally prolonged, both pharmacological and circuit-level engagement of this 5-HT pathway restores recovery toward baseline. The clinical translation is obvious in principle — serotonergic preconditioning, or targeted 5-HT support during emergence, in anxious or depressed surgical patients — and there's a partial readout already from the literature on perioperative ketamine. The mechanism here is more specific than ketamine's broad anti-depressive engagement: a defined serotonergic limbic-prefrontal arousal pathway. Watch for the human pharmacology follow-up.

Sixth — into computational pharmacology. A nicely conceived structure-based drug-design story at the platelet P2Y1 receptor, from King's College London. The therapeutic premise is platelet biology, but the methods are squarely about biased agonism at a GPCR, and the principle generalizes. Platelet P2Y1 signals through both Gαq — which drives PLC and aggregation, the hemostatic function you don't want to block — and Gα12/13, which drives Rho-GTPase-dependent migration and inflammation, the function you do want to block. The team uses molecular docking against the human P2Y1 crystal structure to identify residues — Val194, Lys196, Thr205 — that endogenous biased nucleotides engage but that conventional antagonists like MRS2500 don't. They rationally design a nucleotide analogue, KMR-82-13, predicted to engage exactly that pocket region. The compound selectively inhibits platelet chemotaxis without touching aggregation, both in vitro and in vivo against LPS-induced lung inflammation, while preserving tail-bleeding time — a sharp dissociation from the non-selective MRS2500. They then migrate the binding mode onto a non-nucleotide scaffold, KSN-159-27, validated by 200-nanosecond molecular dynamics, and demonstrate that it functions as a pathway-selective inverse agonist at P2Y1. The substantive lesson for CNS pharmacology is the workflow itself: identify residues that distinguish biased endogenous ligands from cognate ligands, design compounds to engage exactly that set, transfer the engagement pattern across scaffolds. The same logic is what biased-agonism programs at the 5-HT2A receptor for next-generation psychedelics, and at the kappa-opioid receptor for analgesia without dysphoria, have been chasing — this is a worked example of the rational-design loop.

Seventh — a theoretical methods paper that should sharpen how anyone in pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic modeling thinks about target engagement. Drug-target residence time, as Copeland operationalized it, equals one over k-off — the average dwell time of a single binding event before dissociation. The paper argues that this misses the actual pharmacological observable, which is *cumulative* target occupancy over the dosing interval, integrated across rebinding events and drug elimination. They return to Paul Ehrlich's 1913 principle that drugs act only when bound, and formalize Ehrlich occupancy time as the time-integral of fractional occupancy. For a closed equilibrium system, relative Ehrlich occupancy time equals b-zero over K-d plus b-zero — exactly the equilibrium-bound fraction, with K-d as the relevant parameter, not k-off. For induced-fit mechanisms, conformational changes reduce the effective K-d by the ratio of forward to reverse isomerization rates, and the framework recovers kinetic trapping naturally. The key result is for open systems with first-order drug elimination: they derive analytic bounds showing that Ehrlich occupancy time is jointly governed by binding affinity *and* elimination rate, with rebinding extending occupancy beyond what a Copeland-style single-event analysis would predict. The practical consequence is that high-affinity, fast-cleared compounds underperform what their K-d alone would suggest; and the framework collapses back to Copeland's definition exactly when rebinding is absent. For CNS programs where receptor reserve and rebinding can dominate — kappa opioids, 5-HT2A, dopamine D2 — this is a more principled way to set the target-engagement endpoint for med-chem prioritization.

Eighth — agentic AI in drug-metabolite discovery, from the Dorrestein, Wang, and Northen groups around the GNPS metabolomics infrastructure. The substantive problem is that the vast majority of chemical signals in public metabolomics repositories — including human drug-metabolism studies — remain structurally unannotated. The pitch is that LLMs are probabilistic structure generators that can hypothesize molecules outside their training data, and you can constrain hallucination by wrapping them in domain tools. Their system, an agentic AI built on the GNPS2 platform, takes LC-MS/MS spectra and integrates spectral alignment, molecular formula inference, rule-based structural enumeration, machine-learning-based spectrum prediction, and natural-language hypotheses from a human expert into dynamically generated analytical workflows. The demonstration is what makes this credible. The agent predicted structures for previously unknown human drug metabolites, and they experimentally confirmed three classes: a phosphorylated hydroxyzine metabolite, an acetaminophen-p-coumaric-acid ester, and two new oxidative ibuprofen-carnitine conjugates. The acetaminophen-p-coumaric-acid finding is the kind of unexpected dietary-drug crossreactive metabolite that historically required combinatorial chemistry and a bored postdoc to find. The general principle — LLM proposes, domain tool validates, expert constrains — is a working template for agentic systems in any structural-bioinformatics domain. CNS drug metabolism, where active metabolites and CNS-penetrant transformations matter clinically, is a natural next application.

Ninth — a comp-pharm methods paper that opioid pharmacologists should put on their desk. Public bioactivity databases — ChEMBL, BindingDB, PubChem, IUPHAR's Guide to Pharmacology — are heterogeneous not just in measurement units but in *pharmacology*. The same compound and target carry mixed agonist, antagonist, and inhibitor records, measured through binding displacement, cAMP, beta-arrestin, or GTP-gamma-S readouts that quantify different biological events. QSAR models trained on the unstratified pool can post high R-squared while predicting nothing coherent. This paper applies an OECD-anchored stratification framework to the four opioid receptors — mu, delta, kappa, and the nociceptin receptor — reconciling 115,232 raw records into 50,977 curated measurements across 19,585 compounds, with explicit pharmacology-class and assay-readout strata. Receptor-level binding tasks yield strong locked external benchmarks — KOR pKi at R-squared 0.79, DOR pKi at 0.77. Pharmacology-resolved functional endpoints, which the pooled baseline would obscure, hit R-squared 0.86 for MOR-antagonist functional inhibition and up to 0.81 for agonist potencies. The substantive finding is that a fully pooled model can match a stratified model on coherent endpoints, *or* it can reach a deceptively high R-squared on a functional-IC50 endpoint by silently training on binding-displacement records — predicting affinity while pretending to predict function. SHAP attribution confirms that binding and functional potency encode partially distinct structure-activity signals. The piece that stays with you: the dataset contract, not model architecture, defines what a QSAR claim can mean. Anyone running biased-agonist programs at opioid receptors — or 5-HT2A, or any GPCR with pathway-selective ambitions — needs a curation layer that respects pharmacology, not just units.

Tenth and last — closing on agentic AI for neuroscience. A group has built BrainAgent, a multi-agent LLM framework for autonomous brain-signal understanding, targeting EEG-based brain-computer interface tasks. The architecture decomposes signal interpretation into specialized agents that handle preprocessing, feature extraction, task identification, and downstream classification, with an orchestrating LLM driving the workflow. The selling point is generalization across heterogeneous EEG paradigms — motor imagery, P300, emotion classification — without the per-task hand-tuning that conventional BCI pipelines need. The system is competitive with paradigm-specific traditional ML approaches across the benchmarks they report, while being substantially more flexible at the workflow level. The honest caveat is that BCI benchmarks reward systems that can pattern-match on stereotyped recording paradigms, and the genuinely hard problem — robust generalization across subjects, sessions, and hardware — isn't fully solved by an agentic wrapper. But the architectural direction is the right one for clinical BCI deployment, where workflows need to be assembled on the fly for individual patients without a CS engineer in the loop. As a proof of concept for LLM agents in neurophysiology, it's worth knowing about.

That's ten. The piece I'd come back to first is the psilocybin-versus-escitalopram perturbation modeling — the methodology is portable, the destabilize-versus-stabilize framing is conceptually clarifying, and it puts the psychedelic-versus-SSRI debate on physiology rather than affect. The comp-pharm methods items are the Ehrlich-occupancy-time framework and the opioid-receptor stratification paper — both should change how you set up your next QSAR or PKPD project. Have a good Sunday.
