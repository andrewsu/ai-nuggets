## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. It's Sunday, July fifth. Twelve items today. Big themes are neuromodulator-and-astrocyte biology of learning, an interesting projection-specific opioid mechanism, a couple of tauopathy microglia stories, and two computational picks — a distillation approach to ultra-large virtual screening and a light closer on how LLM internals map onto cognitive-neuroscience-style parcellation.

Let's open with a chemogenetic locus coeruleus study out of the Winstanley lab. The question is whether the noradrenergic LC causally shapes the acquisition of decisional strategies under uncertainty, and whether that contribution differs by sex. Using tyrosine-hydroxylase-Cre rats — thirty-five females out of sixty-nine — they chemogenetically inhibited catecholaminergic LC neurons throughout acquisition of the cued rat gambling task. That's a probabilistic decision-making paradigm with salient audiovisual reward-paired cues that also lets you read out motor impulsivity in the same session. Two findings worth carrying with you. First, LC inhibition accelerated the development of risky choice strategies early in training, in both sexes — impaired adoption of the advantageous option, increased preference for risky options. The trial-by-trial breakdown is the interesting part — LC inhibition promoted switches in choice strategy after safe wins, and reduced switches away from risky options after both wins and losses. So the LC seems to normally provide a signal to break the cycle of returning to uncertain outcomes; take that away and animals get stuck chasing the risky option. Second, LC inhibition selectively enhanced motor impulsivity in females, and only early in training. The clinical read is that LC-noradrenergic pharmacology — atomoxetine, guanfacine — for impulse-control disorders is not one drug for one deficit. Choice impulsivity and motor impulsivity appear to have partially separable LC substrates, and the motor arm may be more sensitive in females. That's a specific hypothesis for pediatric ADHD and gambling-disorder trials to test with sex-stratified endpoints.

Staying on neuromodulator-and-astrocyte biology, next is a really provocative paper on cortical astrocytes extending phasic norepinephrine signals — this is a v-two update out of the Sur lab at MIT. The setup is a go-no-go task with graded stimulus evidence — mice press a lever at a go tone for water, refrain at a no-go tone to avoid an air-puff. Phasic LC firing after a false alarm — an unexpected, aversive outcome — improves discrimination on the next trial several seconds later. The puzzle: phasic LC bursts last tens of milliseconds. How does that persist long enough to shape the next trial? The answer here is that cortical astrocytes act on alpha-1a adrenergic receptors, respond to phasic NE with sustained calcium elevations lasting seconds — longer than neuronal responses and spanning the inter-trial interval — and that calcium elevation is causally required for the behavioral gain. Chemogenetic silencing of LC-NE reduces astrocyte calcium and eliminates the next-trial improvement. Chemogenetic blockade of astrocyte calcium alone eliminates it. And the astrocyte-to-neuron signal turns out to be purinergic — pathway-specific astrocyte gene expression changes with training, and blocking neuronal adenosine A1 receptors also prevents the post-reinforcement behavioral gain. Astrocytes are not just following neuronal drive here — they're the substrate for turning a milliseconds-long prediction error into a seconds-long trace that alters prefrontal cortex encoding on the next trial. For anyone thinking about noradrenergic drug action in learning-and-memory contexts — attention deficit, PTSD, dementia — this argues you should stop modeling NE effects as a pure neuronal story. The astrocyte alpha-1a receptor and the neuronal A1 adenosine receptor are the actual downstream levers.

Turning to depression cell biology. A multi-omics paper from the Turecki and Nagy groups at McGill combines spatial transcriptomics with matched single-nucleus RNA-seq and single-nucleus ATAC-seq to map astrocyte subtypes in dorsolateral prefrontal cortex from MDD post-mortem tissue. The finding that survives across all three modalities and localizes spatially is astrocyte dysfunction in the deep cortical layers, converging on the PSAP-slash-GPR37L1 signaling axis. GPR37L1 is a GPCR expressed on astrocytes; prosaposin, PSAP, is one of its endogenous ligands. In MDD dlPFC deep layers, this axis is coherently downregulated across transcript, chromatin, and spatial context. Two reasons this matters. First, GPCRs are drug-tractable, and GPR37L1 has only recently attracted medicinal-chemistry interest — this paper puts it on the depression target map with a proper multi-omics anchor rather than one modality alone. Second, the astrocyte-focused story is now stacking up — this depression paper, the norepinephrine-astrocyte learning story we just discussed, plus recent work on astrocyte reactivity in Alzheimer's disease all point to astrocyte GPCR signaling as an underappreciated psychopharm surface. The caveat is that this is a v-one preprint, and the mechanism from GPR37L1 dysfunction to synaptic phenotype and back up to behavior is not tested here. But the spatially-resolved deep-layer localization is a much cleaner target-nomination than the older bulk-tissue astrocyte-in-MDD literature.

Related on the projection-target-specific side, next is a very clean opioid mechanism paper on the VTA. The question: do the same fentanyl exposure and same VTA neurons project differently for reward context, or is it just a cell-type story that could be told by transmitter identity alone? The answer here is that projection target matters independently of cell type. They RNA-sequenced VTA neurons that project to nucleus accumbens versus prefrontal cortex, and fentanyl induces discordant — meaning opposite-direction — transcriptional adaptations depending on projection target. Fiber photometry then shows different timescales of activation between VTA-to-NAc and VTA-to-PFC populations during conditioned place preference. And chemogenetic manipulation of each pathway supports distinct behavioral roles — the VTA-to-NAc arm carries the acute rewarding activation, the VTA-to-PFC arm carries the more delayed contextual-association updating. Two implications for pharmacology. First, the VTA is not a single node you can hit with a broad-spectrum modulator and expect coherent effects on opioid-associated behavior — dopaminergic D3 antagonists, orexin antagonists, kappa opioid receptor agonists all sit in this circuit differently depending on which arm you engage. Second, therapeutic interest in reversing fentanyl-context associations for relapse prevention should focus on the mesocortical arm, since that's where the persistent context-updating happens. This is a nice model paper for how projection-specific transcriptomics plus circuit manipulation should be the standard for opioid-behavior mechanism work.

Now a really interesting learning-and-memory paper from the Stuber and Palmiter labs. Conditioned flavor avoidance is the phenomenon where an animal eats a novel food, feels ill some minutes to hours later, and learns to avoid the food. There's an obvious computational puzzle — how does the brain bridge the temporal gap between the taste and the malaise? This paper points at a cholinergic eligibility trace from parabrachial calca-positive neurons projecting to central amygdala. Two-photon calcium imaging in brain slices shows ACh from these parabrachial afferents widely activates central amygdala neurons and enhances glutamatergic responsivity — critically, on the timescale of minutes, not milliseconds. That's the "eligibility trace" — a slow modulator that keeps synapses primed for potentiation long after the initial sensory event. CRISPR-Cas-9 knockdown of the cholinergic release machinery and optogenetic silencing both block conditioned flavor avoidance, and large-scale neuronal recordings show that loss of ACh signaling to CeA blocks the plasticity signatures normally seen after learning. The general implication is bigger than flavor avoidance. There's a longstanding computational argument that biological learning requires eligibility traces to solve the temporal credit-assignment problem, and here's a concrete molecular mechanism — a neuromodulator GPCR-mediated enhancement of glutamatergic responsivity, on the right timescale, in the right circuit. If you work on muscarinic pharmacology, this is a specific hypothesis about which cholinergic circuits get engaged when an animal has to bind temporally distant events — with obvious implications for how M1 and M4 agonists in current schizophrenia trials might be affecting associative learning.

Now a receptor-pharmacology paper on developmental programming. The question is whether oxytocin receptor dysfunction during the early postnatal window is sufficient — by itself, without any actual stress — to program adult pain hypersensitivity and cognitive deficits typically attributed to maternal separation. They gave neonatal rats daily injections of dOVT — a selective peptide OTR antagonist — from postnatal day two through twelve, matching the maternal-separation window. In adulthood, the dOVT animals showed mechanical and cold thermal hypersensitivity indistinguishable from maternal-separation animals, though hot thermal sensitivity was unaffected — implicating specifically the polymodal-nociceptor and Aβ pathways rather than heat-sensitive channels. Anxiety-like behaviors, notably, were not reproduced by the dOVT treatment, so the anxiety phenotype apparently needs more than OTR blockade during that window. The cognitive story was sex-specific — males in the maternal-separation group and females in the dOVT-only group showed impaired object location recognition. Molecular readouts in spinal cord tissue showed downregulation of GAD65, BDNF, and CD11b in the dOVT animals, and elevated NKCC1-to-KCC2 ratio in both sexes, indicating compromised chloride homeostasis — the same mechanism that's been proposed to underlie inflammatory and neuropathic pain hypersensitivity in adults. The pharmacology takeaway is uncomfortable — OTR agonists like carbetocin have been considered for various neurodevelopmental indications, but this argues that the receptor system is under tight developmental-window control, and both directions of perturbation could program lasting phenotypes. Going off the abstract here, the preprint went up yesterday — I want to see the receptor autoradiography in the full body when it lands.

Now two related tauopathy microglia stories that go well together. First is a new humanized model called HuMiNAX out of the Li Gan lab. The idea is a triple xenograft — human iPSC-derived neurons, astrocytes, and microglia all grafted into adult mouse brain — so you can watch human microglia interact with human neurons under tau seeding. Key finding is that tau seeding causes aggregation only in mutation-carrying human neural grafts, and that progranulin overexpression in the human microglia dampens tau-associated inflammation, preserves neurons, and restores neuronal gene expression and RNA splicing. So progranulin therapy — which is in clinical development for FTD-GRN patients — is here mechanistically anchored to microglia-mediated neuronal resilience in the tauopathy context, not just in progranulin-haploinsufficiency contexts. The other pharmacologically interesting result — CRISPR-interference knockdown of a human-specific long noncoding RNA, HNRNPK-AS1, also protects neurons. That's a druggable-by-antisense target for tau, and adds to the noncoding-first tauopathy target hypothesis we covered yesterday on the NORAD paper. Complementing this — the second tauopathy microglia paper, out of the Bowles and Mancuso groups, tests iPSC-microglia carrying the MAPT S305N mutation across two independent differentiation protocols. Under both protocols the mutant microglia show hypoactivity — impaired phagocytosis, reduced cytokine release, diminished regulation of synaptic function. So the disease-linked microglial phenotype in tauopathy is not the classic pro-inflammatory activation the field has focused on — it's an early hypoactive state, a failure to respond, that then contributes to neuronal vulnerability. The two papers together argue for a therapeutic strategy that boosts microglial function early — which is the opposite of the anti-inflammatory strategies currently in Alzheimer's-adjacent trials.

Now a nice combined CRISPR-and-drug-repositioning paper on Alzheimer's-linked myeloid migration. The genetic story on Alzheimer's disease has kept implicating microglial genes — TREM2, ABI3, PLCG2 — that all touch actin dynamics and cell migration in myeloid lineages. But the field has struggled to nominate druggable functional regions within those pathways. This group applied dense CRISPR-tile mutagenesis to the WAVE regulatory complex — the actin-remodeling machinery downstream of RAC1 — in human THP-1 myeloid cells and read out CCL2-directed migration. Two components, NCKAP1L and CYFIP1, come out with residue-level functional maps that pin down discrete migration-associated hotspots, including one on NCKAP1L that they specifically nominate for pharmacological targeting because human single-nucleus atlases show NCKAP1L is microglia-enriched across diverse microglial states. Then — and this is the fun part — they used machine-learning-guided compound prediction against the nominated pocket, and experimentally validated two hits: montelukast, the CysLT1 antagonist that's actually a widely prescribed asthma drug, comes out as a negative modulator of chemokine-directed myeloid migration, while piperacetazine — an old dopamine D2 antagonist antipsychotic, mostly historical — comes out as a positive modulator. Whether either becomes a real Alzheimer's asset is very uncertain, but the workflow is worth learning: CRISPR-tile the functional protein, ML-guided repurposing library screen, wet-lab validate. This is a lot cheaper than de novo medicinal chemistry against a novel target and, for a target class like microglial migration where you actually don't want too selective a modulator, potentially better.

Turning to two computational-pharmacology items. FastBindRank is a distillation approach to ultra-large virtual screening. Boltz-2 — the structure-based co-folding model — is a very strong scorer, but it's not cheap enough to run over hundreds of millions of compounds. The idea here is to train a lightweight sequence-based surrogate on just one percent of the one-hundred-twenty-two-million-compound PubChem library, using Boltz-2 predictions as the training signal. Applied to HDAC11 as the target, FastBindRank enriches high-confidence binders substantially over the background chemical space. Under a fixed computational budget the framework hits a seventy-four-fold increase in hit rate and a thirty-fold increase in discovery yield over direct subset-based screening — and they validated two novel active compounds experimentally. There's an interesting technical point that the lightweight sequence-based model still captures structural patterns associated with predicted binding, meaning distillation doesn't just fit the score, it partially transfers the pocket-selective preferences. For anyone building screening pipelines against psychiatric or CNS targets, this argues distillation of an expensive structure-aware model into a cheap sequence model is now a viable general recipe — you don't need to pick between fidelity and library scale.

Now a structural chemistry paper that will interest anyone building serotonin biosensors. There's a widely used forty-four-nucleotide DNA aptamer, Apt-44, that binds serotonin with about thirty-nanomolar affinity — used in field-effect transistor sensors, fluorescent probes, and other real-time detection platforms. Nobody knew the recognition mode. This group solved the high-resolution NMR structure of a shortened variant, both in free and serotonin-bound states. The architecture turns out to be a two-layer chair-type antiparallel G-quadruplex core paired with a Watson-Crick duplex — a G-quadruplex-duplex hybrid. Serotonin binds at the junction between the two elements, stabilized by stacking, electrostatic attraction, hydrogen bonding, and hydrophobic contacts. The sensing mechanism worth carrying away — the aptamer is not preorganized in the parent forty-four-nucleotide form; the third loop is long enough to introduce conformational dynamics into the G-quadruplex scaffold, and serotonin binding triggers a discrete conformational switch. That's exactly what a good sensor wants — a large binding-induced structural change to transduce. This gives medicinal chemistry a hard target for rational aptamer engineering — you could imagine biasing the loop dynamics with base modifications, or extending the platform to related monoamines by mutating the junction residues that contact the indole ring. If you work on serotonin dynamics in vivo — psychedelics research, SSRI mechanism — this is the enabling structural biology that lets these aptamers become properly quantitative tools rather than qualitative reporters.

Closing with a light computational piece I found genuinely fun. NeuroCogMap out of an arXiv preprint proposes a cognitive-neuroscience-inspired framework for organizing the internal features of large language models into functional parcels — analogous to how you'd parcellate cortex by resting-state fMRI. They then link those parcels to interpretable cognitive functions and to a cognitive hierarchy. Three claims to sift through. First, the parcels are stable and semantically coherent across models — meaning this isn't just an accident of one training run, there's a cross-model organization. Second, major LLM failure modes — hallucination, bias, refusal failure, sycophancy — correspond to distinct disruptions in the representational and behavioral-control parcels, which could give you a mechanism-guided detection or targeted-intervention handle. Third — and this is the part that connects it back to us — the parcellation improves prediction of human cortical responses during naturalistic language comprehension, with the strongest correspondence in higher-order association cortex. Two takes. It's an ambitious framing and the "cortical parcellation for LLMs" analogy will be argued about — the alignment between artificial and biological cortex is more decorative than causal at this point. But the mechanism-guided intervention angle for LLM safety failures is a fresh direction that borrows the right stuff from systems neuroscience — namely, that failures often map to circuit-level disruptions rather than to bad individual weights. Worth reading if you're thinking about the interface between mechanistic interpretability and cognitive science.
