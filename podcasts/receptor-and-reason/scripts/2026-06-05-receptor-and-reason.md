Opening with what I think is the most interesting item of the day. From the Fenno lab at UT Austin, a Cre-dependent AAV-SaCas9 strategy to knock down the Clock gene selectively in ventral tegmental area dopamine neurons in mouse. The motivation is bipolar disorder — Clock has been one of the strongest circadian-clock candidate genes implicated in mood dysregulation for two decades, but the field has mostly worked with global or whole-region knockouts, which makes the mood-relevant cell-type contribution impossible to disentangle from the well-known peripheral and sleep-wake roles. They built the editing tool in two stages — a rapid in vitro guide RNA screening pipeline that predicts in vivo editing efficiency, then a single-AAV in vivo delivery of the editing machinery with TH-Cre driver lines to restrict editing to VTA dopamine neurons. They confirm robust titer-dependent reduction of Clock expression by three orthogonal readouts — targeted sequencing, in situ, and immunohistochemistry — and run a behavioral battery, EEG-EMG sleep architecture, and slice electrophysiology, with changes across all three. Two things I find load-bearing here. First, it's a clean methodological template for going after any psychiatric risk gene in a cell-type-specific way without breeding a flox line — and the field has hundreds of candidate genes from GWAS and exome work that have never had cell-type causality tested because the breeding cost was prohibitive. Second, by isolating Clock disruption to mesolimbic dopaminergic cells, the work makes a specific testable claim about which arm of Clock biology matters for bipolar-relevant phenotypes — distinct from suprachiasmatic, peripheral, or non-dopaminergic VTA contributions. The abstract is light on the actual behavioral and electrophysiological details, so I read this primarily as a method-and-model paper — the next step will be using this exact toolkit to test, for example, lithium response in the cell-type-specific knockdown, or to layer in cell-type-specific manipulation of other clock genes like Per2 or Bmal1 for triangulation.

Sticking with psychiatric circuit work — from the Karlsgodt lab at UCLA, a transdiagnostic early-psychosis study using the Human Connectome Project Early Psychosis sample, 124 patients, that asks which neurotransmitter systems map onto which symptom dimensions. The motivation is the practical clinical fact that roughly half of first-episode patients don't respond to first-line antipsychotics, and negative symptoms and cognitive impairment are essentially treatment-refractory in the existing pharmacopeia. The approach is partial least squares correlation to extract whole-brain resting-state functional connectivity signatures and anatomical signatures for five symptom dimensions — positive, negative, general psychopathology, mania, and cognition — and then spatial correlation of those signatures against twenty-one PET-derived receptor and transporter maps from the neuromaps toolbox covering nine neurotransmitter systems. The most actionable result for drug discovery — the negative-symptom resting-state signature correlates with the norepinephrine transporter map and the vesicular acetylcholine transporter map, and the cognition signature also correlates with vesicular acetylcholine transporter. That's a direct argument for prioritizing noradrenergic and cholinergic mechanisms for negative symptoms and cognitive impairment in early psychosis, exactly the symptom domains where D2-centric antipsychotics fail. The caveats — 124 is a meaningful sample for PLS correlation but the per-receptor-system effect sizes are modest, and the anatomical signatures, unlike the resting-state ones, were susceptible to confounding by antipsychotic dose and substance use. Worth pairing with the muscarinic-cholinergic mechanism behind xanomeline-trospium — this is the transdiagnostic cross-symptom mapping that justifies why that mechanism class probably has real ground in negative and cognitive symptoms specifically.

Adjacent stress-circuit work — from Chakravarty's group at McGill, a whole-brain structural MRI study of stress susceptibility in male and female mice. The setup deserves a beat: females require about a week of chronic variable stress to develop comparable depression-like and anxiety-like behavioral changes, while males require three to four weeks. Most prior work runs both sexes on the male protocol, which means females are likely overstressed relative to phenotype-matching. This study titrates stress duration to phenotype-match each sex, then runs whole-brain MRI plus connectomic analysis plus spatial gene-expression overlay. The findings — both sexes show neuroanatomical changes in nucleus accumbens and hippocampus, but in opposite directions in some regions. In females, the signature couples to both depression-like and anxiety-like behavior together; in males there are two orthogonal signatures, one for anxiety-like behavior and one for social preference. The connectomic underpinning is cortical-hub-dominant in females, subcortical-hub-dominant in males. And the spatial gene-expression overlay points to cholinergic-signaling-associated genes in females. The why-this-matters — sex-specific direction-of-change is the cleanest mechanistic argument I've seen for why mixed-sex preclinical antidepressant work has been so noisy. If males and females are driving opposite-sign adaptations in the same regions, a drug screen that pools across sexes will under-detect effective compounds for at least one sex.

Carrying the prefrontal-stress thread forward — from the Barbier lab at Linköping, the role of the histone methyltransferase PRDM2 in stress-induced alcohol reinstatement. The starting observation is from postmortem human tissue — PRDM2 expression is reduced in the prefrontal cortex of both men and women with alcohol use disorder versus controls. To get at causality, they used viral-mediated PRDM2 knockdown in the dorsomedial prefrontal cortex of male and female rats trained on alcohol self-administration, then tested stress-induced reinstatement after extinction. PRDM2 knockdown increased reinstatement in both sexes without altering pain sensitivity or being influenced by estrous cycle. The clean follow-up — they then restricted PRDM2 knockdown to dmPFC neurons projecting to nucleus accumbens, using a projection-targeting viral strategy, and the projection-specific knockdown was sufficient to increase stress-induced reinstatement in a shock-intensity-dependent way. So the human postmortem signal is replicated as causality in a circuit-specific manner — reduced PRDM2 in the dmPFC-NAc projection is sufficient to increase stress-driven relapse. What I take from this — PRDM2 as a histone methyltransferase is not directly druggable, but the projection-specific causality is the kind of result that can motivate a behavioral or chemogenetic intervention strategy that bypasses the epigenetic upstream and acts on the dmPFC-NAc pathway directly. The sex-equivalent result is the clinically meaningful one — this is a mechanism that doesn't fall apart between male and female AUD, which is unusual for stress-relapse work.

Staying in substance-use territory but on a very different question — from Unterwald's group at Temple, the impact of chronic cocaine on Long-COVID-like outcomes from SARS-CoV-2 spike protein in the rat. The acute-COVID side is well established — substance use exacerbates acute outcomes — but the substance-use-by-Long-COVID interaction has been a gap. They gave rats intermittent chronic cocaine, then a single intravenous spike protein injection, and assessed at two months. The spike-plus-cocaine combination produced persistent reduction in weight gain, lowered withdrawal thresholds suggesting increased pain sensitivity, and shifted the hippocampal IL-6 to IL-10 ratio toward a pro-inflammatory state. Paw withdrawal thresholds positively correlated with IL-10 levels in hippocampus and prefrontal cortex — the pain-and-inflammation linkage is doing real work in this model. The model has limitations — spike protein alone, not infection — but it makes a specific prediction worth following clinically: people with co-occurring cocaine use disorder and Long-COVID may carry an excess pain and inflammatory burden that the clinical literature isn't yet stratifying for. The neuroinflammatory pain mechanism is a more tractable therapeutic handle than chasing the spike protein directly.

Shifting from psychiatric to neurodegenerative drug discovery — from Rachel Harding's lab at Toronto, a panel of macrocyclic peptides targeting Huntingtin Associated Protein 40, or HAP40. Brief framing — Huntington's disease has a clearly defined genetic cause, polyglutamine expansion in the HTT protein, but the molecular function of HTT itself remains incompletely understood, partly because HTT has been a difficult protein to study directly. HAP40 forms a stable complex with HTT and is emerging as a key interaction partner in the HTT structure-function paradigm, but until now there have been no tools that engage endogenous HAP40 directly. The team used the Random nonstandard Peptide Integrated Discovery platform — a display technology developed by Hiroaki Suga's group that allows selection of macrocyclic peptides from libraries containing non-canonical amino acids — and recovered nanomolar-affinity binders against distinct HAP40 epitopes. They confirmed binding biophysically by surface plasmon resonance, fluorescence polarization, and hydrogen-deuterium exchange mass spectrometry, then showed the macrocycles engage endogenous HAP40 in cellular lysates and enable selective isolation of HAP40-containing complexes by macrocycle precipitation. This is a tool paper, not a therapeutic claim. But the broader pattern matters — for a long list of neurodegenerative-disease-relevant protein-protein interactions, the bottleneck has been not target identification but a lack of selective ligands for endogenous protein. RaPID-selected macrocycles, including some now advancing toward clinic, are increasingly the answer.

Continuing on neurodegeneration ultrastructure — from Amanda Lewis's group at EPFL, a correlative light and electron microscopy study of Lewy pathology across cortical regions and substantia nigra in confirmed Parkinson's and dementia-with-Lewy-bodies donors. The aim is to settle whether the ultrastructure of alpha-synuclein inclusions differs between PD and DLB. The headline result is unexpected — Lewy body ultrastructure alone does not distinguish PD from DLB. In substantia nigra of DLB donors they see the full range of classical nigral Lewy body morphologies previously described in PD; cortical inclusions show diverse maturation stages from low-density fibrils through highly compact fibrillar inclusions, but the morphological repertoire is shared. They also identify a previously-unreported population of electron-dense degenerating alpha-synuclein-positive cortical neurons. The disease-distinguishing signal, when they go looking, is in the mitochondria — quantifying over ten thousand mitochondria, PD shows increased mitochondrial density and enlargement in substantia nigra, while DLB shows increased density only in entorhinal cortex, and mitochondrial enlargement is exclusive to PD. The mechanistic implication is that disease identity at the ultrastructural level is more about neuronal vulnerability and regional mitochondrial homeostasis than the morphology of the aggregates themselves — a useful reframing for the strain-and-template-conformer line of synucleinopathy thinking.

Continuing in Parkinson's, on a clinically pressing phenotype — from Aaron Kucinski's group at Michigan, an optogenetic dissection of falls in a dual-disruption rat model of Parkinson's. The motivation is the clinical observation that PD patients with cortical acetylcholine deficits, on top of nigrostriatal dopamine loss, have substantially higher fall rates than PD patients with intact cortical cholinergic tone. The model layers transient optogenetic inhibition of basal forebrain cortical cholinergic projections onto rats with dorsomedial striatal dopamine lesions, then tests them on straight and zig-zag balance beams. The result is the expected interaction — combined cholinergic inhibition plus dopamine depletion produces substantially more falls than either disruption alone, and the effect is largest on the more challenging zig-zag task. This formalizes in a causal preclinical model what has been a correlational clinical signal for a while — falls in PD are not simply a dopamine-loss phenomenon; they're a dual-system failure. The therapeutic angle is that cholinergic augmentation strategies for falls in PD — for instance, the donepezil and varenicline literature in PD-fall reduction — now have a clearer mechanism to anchor against. The limitation is that optogenetic projection inhibition is acute and complete, while clinical cholinergic deficits are partial and chronic, so the magnitude of the interaction is probably an upper bound on what cholinergic augmentation can buy back.

One more pharmacology pick — from Fan Fan's group at Medical College of Georgia, chronic inhibition of soluble epoxide hydrolase rescues cognitive deficits in a diabetic-related dementia rat model. The compound is TPPU, a soluble-epoxide-hydrolase inhibitor that increases bioactive epoxy fatty acids by blocking their hydrolysis — same tool compound used in pain and inflammation studies, and the same group has previously shown TPPU rescues spatial memory in both Alzheimer's and diabetic-dementia models. This paper extends to cortical recognition memory and more thorough neurovascular and cellular phenotyping. Chronic TPPU at 1 milligram per kilogram per day for nine weeks reduces brain sEH expression, restores cortical and hippocampal recognition memory, improves cerebral perfusion and normalizes whisker-evoked functional hyperemia — an effect they link to upregulation of the inward-rectifier potassium channel Kir2.1 in cerebral capillaries. Downstream effects are broad — restored tight-junction proteins at the blood-brain barrier, reduced capillary rarefaction, attenuated astrocyte and microglial activation, restored synaptic protein expression PSD-95 and SY38, and reduced pro-inflammatory chemokines. The breadth is a strength and a weakness — these are all known downstream consequences of sEH inhibition through epoxy-eicosanoid-mediated vascular and immune effects, so the mechanism story is internally consistent, but which arm actually drives the cognitive rescue is unresolved. GWAS links to sEH polymorphisms in AD and ADRD make this more than a preclinical curiosity — sEH inhibitors are at various stages of clinical development, and the diabetes-ADRD intersection is a natural enrichment population for an early trial.

Pivoting to the agentic-AI side, two evaluation papers from arXiv that bear directly on how LLMs handle pharmacology-and-neurology content. First, a paper titled "What's in a Name? Morphological Shortcuts by LLMs in Pharmacology." The premise is an obvious-in-retrospect observation — drug names aren't random. Beta-blocker generics end in olol, ACE-inhibitor generics end in pril, statins end in statin, monoclonal antibodies end in mab, kinase inhibitors end in nib. These suffixes are systematically assigned by the WHO international-nonproprietary-name committee to encode pharmacological class. The authors ask — when an LLM reasons about a drug, is it actually using the drug name, or just the suffix? They build fictitious drug names by gluing real affixes onto invented stems — their example is "wugcillin" — and show LLMs will confidently generate plausible-looking clinical content about these fictitious drugs purely from the affix. They build a framework to attribute a model's drug semantics to the affix, the stem, or the full name, run it across 653 real drugs, and find that models often induce meaning primarily from the affix without explicitly indicating this reliance, and sometimes wrongly conflate properties across affix-sharing drugs. Activation patching localizes the behavior to early-mid layers. The clinical-safety implication is non-trivial — a model deployed as a clinical-decision-support agent that confidently reasons about a drug from its suffix alone will generalize within-class behavior to drugs where the within-class assumption is wrong, and the within-class assumption is wrong often enough in pharmacology that it can't be the default mode.

Closing with a smaller item, complementary to the last — a head-to-head study of ten board-certified headache specialists versus a retrieval-augmented agentic AI system on summarizing the recent headache literature. The setup is direct — they built a RAG-based agentic framework using three current LLMs (Claude Sonnet, GPT-4o, and Llama 3.1), had a headache specialist write thirteen clinical questions, used three for prompt optimization and ten for the evaluation, then for each of those ten questions had a headache specialist write an expert summary and the three LLMs each produce a summary. The other nine specialists blind-rated all four summaries on correctness, completeness, conciseness, and clinical utility, and tried to guess which was which. The bottom line — expert summaries were preferred, but the specialists frequently couldn't reliably tell whether a summary was written by a human or an LLM. The interesting bit, beyond the headline, is what the authors call out as expert-valued features beyond the standard rubric metrics — things that signal practitioner judgment and that the LLM summaries underweighted. Bringing it back to your work — the win condition for RAG-based clinical literature agents isn't beating the median specialist on a rubric; it's matching specialists on the features that actually shape clinical decisions and that the standard correctness-completeness-conciseness rubric doesn't capture. That's a useful framing for any agentic tool aimed at literature triage for an expert practitioner.
