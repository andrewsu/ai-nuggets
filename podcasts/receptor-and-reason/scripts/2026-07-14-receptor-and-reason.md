## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason. It's Tuesday, July fourteenth, twenty twenty-six. Eleven items today. Heavy dopamine day — four dopamine papers running head-to-head — then serotonergic sex differences, two opioid picks, an APOE-cerebrovascular item, and closing with computational pharmacology and two agentic-AI-in-medicine picks.

Opening with the most conceptually interesting neuropsychopharm paper of the week, and one that changes how a pharmacologist should read the receptor-subtype-level literature. From Michael Elowitz's lab at Caltech. The puzzle is one that everyone working on dopamine drugs has papered over — most neurons express only D1-like or only D2-like receptors, so the sign of the cAMP response is set by the receptor family, and pharmacology just tunes it. But some neurons co-express opposing subtypes, and the question of how a single cell resolves simultaneous stimulatory and inhibitory dopamine input has been mostly hand-waved. This paper builds a multiplexed single-cell assay that reads out surface receptor abundance and dynamic cAMP output in the same cell, and then uses mathematical modeling to ask where the integration actually happens. Their answer, which is the load-bearing claim, is that the integration is not at the receptor. It's at the adenylyl cyclase. Different AC isoforms have different biochemical responses to concurrent Gs and Gi activation — some cancel, some suppress one signal, some amplify — and it's the AC-isoform expression profile of the cell that determines what a D1-plus-D2 co-activation actually does to cAMP. Brain transcriptome analysis then shows that neurons which co-express opposing dopamine receptors preferentially express AC isoforms predicted to sustain signaling during multi-receptor activation. Two consequences that are worth carrying with you. First, ligand bias at a single receptor is only half the pharmacology — the cell-specific AC repertoire is the other half, and receptor-family drug design that ignores it may be reading the wrong integration function. Second, this is a testable prescription for which cell types in striatum, prefrontal cortex, and hippocampus are actually integrating opposing dopamine inputs and which are just seeing one sign. A caveat — this is a preprint posted yesterday, so the full body isn't rendered yet and the take is from the abstract. But the framing alone is enough to file this against the D1-versus-D2 subtype-drug-design conversation.

Second, staying on dopamine and picking up the D2 side. A group at KIST in Korea used RNAscope-guided GeoMx spatial transcriptomics to compare D1 and D2 neuronal compartments in the dorsal striatum after chronic restraint stress. Both populations responded transcriptionally, with comparable numbers of differentially expressed genes. But the D2 response was the interesting one. Under matched threshold criteria, D2-downregulated genes cleanly organized into synapse-centered functional categories — glutamatergic synapse, postsynaptic organization, dendritic spine. D1 genes did not show the equivalent coherent pattern. And the pathway analysis pulls out AMPA receptor trafficking and EPHB-mediated signaling as the specific programs attenuated in stressed D2 neurons. The take is that chronic stress produces a D2-specific synaptic-trafficking downshift in dorsal striatum, which is compatible with the phenomenology — reduced flexibility, blunted motivational drive — but names a molecular substrate that hasn't been cleanly resolved before. Combining this with today's lead, you have a mechanistic picture where D2 neurons that co-express opposing receptors and integrate at the AC level are also the ones whose synaptic trafficking machinery is preferentially degraded under stress. That's a lot of hypothesis-generation from two spatially-resolved measurements, but the two papers together look like the beginning of a targetable circuit-plus-molecular substrate for stress-related motivational impairment.

Third dopamine item, a revised preprint that just landed this week as a new v4 from a 2024 original. Optogenetically-identified VTA dopaminergic neurons recorded in mice running successive operant sensory discrimination tasks. The classical prediction-error story is that phasic firing tracks unexpected reward at first and then shifts to the reward-predictive cue with learning; expected omission produces a dip. This paper puts pressure on that story by introducing a delay between the nose-poke choice and the trial outcome signal. During sub-criterion performance — animals still struggling — the neurons increased firing after correct choices but before the outcome signal. In other words, the dopamine neurons predicted whether the choice would be rewarded even when the animal itself was performing at chance. Then, when the reward was actually delivered, the same neurons fired again as though the outcome had been unexpected — but when the trial was punished, they were inhibited as though the reward had been expected. Those two things are only compatible if you allow the neurons to represent multiple parallel value estimates simultaneously — one about the choice quality and one about the trial outcome — and reconcile them. Their framing is that these neurons function as a critic signal for the immediately preceding choice, distinct from the classical reward prediction error, and consistent with an actor-critic architecture where multiple Bayesian belief representations have to be reconciled before criterion performance emerges. This is a nice paper to read against the recent tail-of-striatum reconciliation paper from Oxford I covered yesterday — both are pushing toward the view that "the" dopamine prediction error was always a compound of computationally distinct components that only look unified in the averaged aggregate.

Fourth, a briefer note connecting to that computational learning theme. From Alexxai Kravitz's group at Washington University, published on bioRxiv this week and abstract-only for the moment. NMDA receptors in dorsomedial striatum, they show, are required for learning from previously rewarded actions. And blocking striatal NMDARs almost fully abolished in-vivo striatal calcium dynamics but not action potential firing. Two things to notice. The dissociation between calcium and spiking means the standard fiber-photometry striatal calcium signal is largely reporting NMDAR-driven calcium influx, not spike output — a methodological caveat that reshapes how you interpret ten years of striatal fiber-photometry papers. And functionally, striatal NMDAR calcium looks like the substrate for updating action policy on the back of past reward. It's a slim abstract and I'd want the full body to see the behavioral specificity, but paired with the dopamine-critic story above, it fits a picture where the dopamine signal defines the target of learning and NMDAR-gated striatal calcium implements the update.

Fifth, from Johannes Ramaekers' group at Maastricht, on sex as a biological variable in psychedelic pharmacology. Two pooled double-blind placebo-controlled studies in healthy volunteers, seventy-two participants total, comparing psilocybin fifteen milligrams, 2C-B twenty milligrams, and LSD fifty micrograms across sexes. The finding that matters — after adjusting for age, female participants reported significantly higher subjective intensity, reduced vigilance, and greater impaired control and cognition across all three drugs, with medium-to-large effects. Empathy measures were unchanged. And, critically, peak plasma concentration and area under the curve were the same. The mechanism therefore is not pharmacokinetic — it's pharmacodynamic. Combined with yesterday's Amilhon-lab bioRxiv item on female-biased hyper-excitability in median raphe-to-ventral hippocampus serotonin neurons, a coherent picture is forming — the female serotonin system operates in a different dynamic regime, and psychedelics land on it differently. The immediate practical implication for the trials — dosing, informed-consent language, and safety monitoring should treat sex as a first-class variable, not a covariate. The wider point is that if the intensity difference is real and pharmacodynamic, "one dose fits all" is going to overshoot in a large fraction of female participants and undershoot in a large fraction of male participants, and effect-size claims from mixed-sex psychedelic trials should be re-read with that in mind.

Sixth, on mu-opioid modulation of the A11 dopaminergic nucleus. This one deserves a moment of orientation — A11 is the hypothalamic dopamine nucleus that provides the only known descending dopaminergic input to the spinal cord and modulates pain and motor systems. Its cellular electrophysiology had not been characterized in detail until now. The paper does whole-cell and cell-attached patch-clamp on fluorescence-identified A11 dopamine neurons and finds most of them are spontaneous pacemakers around five hertz, driven by a subthreshold net inward current. Mu-opioid receptor activation reduces that inward current via potassium-channel activation and also reduces GABAergic synaptic input onto the same cells. Cell-attached recording — chosen specifically to preserve the natural chloride gradient — showed that the net effect of mu-opioid activation is to slow spontaneous firing of A11 dopamine neurons. That's a mechanistically clean assay on a nucleus whose pain-modulation role has been inferred but whose intrinsic pharmacology was blank. Two implications. First, opioid-induced changes in spinal dopamine tone via A11 slowdown provide a novel candidate substrate for opioid analgesia and, potentially, for the motor-side effects of chronic opioid exposure. Second, the pacemaker-plus-inward-current biophysics of A11 makes it a druggable node — inward-rectifier potassium channels, T-type calcium channels, HCN channels — that a small-molecule effort could plausibly target without engaging the mesolimbic dopamine reward pathway that most CNS opioid work is preoccupied with.

Seventh, and continuing the opioid thread but at the behavioral-model layer. A group from the University of Pittsburgh published a voluntary two-bottle drinking-in-the-dark model for oral opioid intake in mice, tested with both oxycodone and fentanyl across four cohorts, both sexes, and varying session duration and concentration. The important results. Oxycodone at up to one milligram per milliliter produced clean voluntary intake but minimal naloxone-precipitated withdrawal. Fentanyl at ten to one hundred micrograms per milliliter produced dose- and session-duration-dependent naloxone-precipitated withdrawal — four-hour sessions produced stronger withdrawal than two-hour sessions at matched concentration. Escalating high-concentration fentanyl revealed a sex difference — females showed greater intake and stronger withdrawal at the highest doses. Persistent affective disturbances in the week after withdrawal were minimal across all cohorts. This is a scalable model without catheters or taste adulteration that produces graded physical dependence, which is a genuinely useful platform for preclinical opioid work — the field has been carrying the surgical-catheter tax on IV self-administration for decades. The lack of persistent affective disturbances is worth flagging, both as an honest limitation of the model and as a specific prediction — if voluntary oral fentanyl doesn't reliably produce post-withdrawal negative affect, then any medication candidate whose primary mechanism is anti-negative-affect will be hard to test in this paradigm and should be tested elsewhere.

Eighth, an APOE-focused Alzheimer's item that I think is important precisely because it looks at a variable most APOE work skips. Homozygous APOE3 and APOE4 mice at six months and twenty-four months across a full cerebrovascular and behavioral battery. The interaction term is where the story is. Age-alone effects were seen for cognition, endothelial function, and arterial stiffness across genotypes. But brain volume loss, neuroinflammation, and cerebral artery vasoconstriction to endothelin-1 were driven by the age-by-APOE4 interaction — old APOE4 mice showed exaggerated vasoconstriction to ET-1 concomitant with altered ET-1 receptor and endothelin-converting enzyme expression. The clean take is that APOE4 doesn't just accelerate a common trajectory — it opens a specific vascular-inflammatory failure mode in old age that isn't present in APOE3. And endothelin-1 signaling is now named as a mechanistic hinge. That matters because ET-1 antagonists are approved for pulmonary arterial hypertension and have decades of clinical experience — bosentan, macitentan, ambrisentan — and the paper implicitly suggests them as a repurposing lane for late-onset AD in APOE4 carriers. Whether that stratification would translate is a real question, but the mechanism is testable enough to justify asking.

Ninth item, shifting to computational pharmacology. This is a systems pharmacology preprint from Gunnar Cedersund's group in Sweden demonstrating what they call M4 drug discovery — a framework for integrating pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic data across multiple time scales, multiple physiological levels, mechanistic ODE models, and multiple species. They exemplify it with sixteen new and six prior human cell studies of the GLP-1 receptor agonist exenatide, plus six rat studies, and successfully predict a thirty-week human treatment outcome — chi-squared p greater than point zero five for the model-vs-data fit. Why this matters beyond the specific GLP-1 receptor case: the field has been building QSP models for a decade, but the actual integration of static human cell assays with microphysiological co-culture systems, with animal PK-PD, into a single fitted mechanistic model that can predict a human clinical outcome has been rare. And they explicitly show which experimental system contributes which piece — cell data gives potency, microphysiological co-cultures give organ-organ crosstalk, animal data provides the appetite effects that cell assays cannot capture, and the multi-species scaling piece gives clinical pharmacokinetics. It's a template that generalizes off GLP-1 immediately. The CNS-drug version — pair human iPSC-derived neuronal cultures with an appropriate BBB-integrated microphysiological system, add rodent behavioral pharmacology, and fit a shared mechanistic model — is a natural next step and would attack the translational gap that has kept CNS pharmacology depending on animal-behavior anchoring longer than any other therapeutic area.

Tenth item, and directly relevant to the diligence work on LLM-in-medicine claims. A benchmark of thirty-two frontier LLMs on hematologic-oncology clinical reasoning under uncertainty, out of TU Munich and Charité. The setup is important — the models don't get a fully-specified case up front. They have to proactively request clinical data across three sequential rounds before committing to a diagnosis and treatment plan. Two headline findings. The best model achieved only sixty-eight percent overall accuracy, which is well below what static medical-QA benchmarks would suggest. And, more interestingly, the strongest predictor of diagnostic accuracy was information utilization — the fraction of available data the model actually requested — but utilization collapsed from fifty-seven percent early on to twenty-six percent in the final round, so models systematically stopped seeking critical molecular and cytogenetic data right when treatment selection needed it. Their reasoning-trace rubric scored ninety-one percent above threshold, but reasoning quality decorrelated from accuracy — the traces are locally coherent while landing on globally wrong conclusions. The failure modes they name are the same cognitive biases that characterize novice clinicians in dual-process models — search satisficing, anchoring, premature closure. The important framing for a pharmacology audience is that the bottleneck for LLMs in clinical practice — and by extension, in agentic drug-development workflows that have to gather data across trials, labs, and literature — is not medical knowledge. It's the meta-decision of what to look for. That's a much more actionable target for evaluation and remediation than the endless we-need-better-knowledge argument.

Closing with a shorter agentic-AI-in-biomedicine item, and this one is a concrete-application companion to the benchmarking paper we just discussed. EcoXAI is a containerized multi-agent system that structures biomedical analysis through explicit pipeline execution stages against a knowledge graph, with the specific goal of avoiding the hallucination and workflow-fragmentation failure modes of monolithic LLM interfaces. As a proof of concept they run it on drug repurposing for Alzheimer's disease — one hundred and three drug candidates evaluated, seventy-nine flagged as exceeding a randomized baseline. The most prominent surviving candidate is the CCR5 antagonist maraviroc, and they note independent literature support for CCR5 involvement in Alzheimer's neuroinflammation. This is worth watching not because the maraviroc hypothesis is novel — CCR5-in-AD has been in circulation for a few years — but because it's the kind of concrete, reproducible, knowledge-graph-grounded output that agentic biomedical systems have to start producing at scale before the field can move past benchmark papers and into deployed workflows. Modular execution stages plus knowledge-graph grounding is the pragmatic recipe that keeps showing up across today's episode — yesterday's MAPLE for QSP calibration, today's EcoXAI for hypothesis generation, and the underlying pattern is validator-heavy hybrid workflows rather than autonomous end-to-end model runs.
