## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. Ten items today, with a center of gravity on dopamine — psychedelic-driven dopamine, addiction dopamine, computational dopamine, and the Parkinsonian dopamine that radiates into oscillatory pathology far beyond the substantia nigra. We also have a fresh Lancet Psychiatry pharmacoepidemiology piece on antidepressants in pregnancy, a clinical update on a deuterated psilocin in late-stage development, a tRNA-fragment angle on Alzheimer's tau, and a process-level benchmark for agentic LLMs in biomedical research. Let's go.

Lead item, and the most mechanistically dense piece of psychedelic pharmacology I've seen this week — Appetite for Change: How Psilocybin Reshapes Food Reward Learning Through Striatal Dopamine Function, out of the Foldi group at Monash. The serotonin 2A receptor has been the canonical pharmacological substrate for psilocybin's pro-cognitive effects, with most of the field's mechanistic work running through serotonin receptor subtype antagonism. The downstream dopaminergic question — what striatal dopamine actually does when psilocybin is on board — has been under-resolved. This group did the right experiment. Female rats received psilocybin at 1.5 milligrams per kilogram, then ran probabilistic reversal learning under operant and touchscreen paradigms, with in vivo fiber photometry recording nucleus accumbens dopamine transients across seven days. Three results. First, psilocybin broadly amplified accumbens dopamine transients time-locked to both expected and unexpected outcomes — not just to prediction errors in the canonical sense, but to outcome occurrence generally. Second, computational modeling of trial-by-trial choice behavior identified psilocybin-specific increases in learning rate alongside reductions in prior value weighting, consistent with strengthened feedback-driven updating — the pro-cognitive signature, formalized. Third, and this is the translational hook, calorie restriction selectively attenuated the psilocybin-enhanced reversal learning, and prior activity-based anorexia exposure abolished the discrimination accuracy benefit and trended toward worsened reversal learning, likely reflecting activity-based-anorexia-induced reductions in cortical 5-HT2A availability. Why this matters — psilocybin is being seriously developed for anorexia nervosa, and there's a real concern that the nutritional and receptor-density state of the patient population for whom you most want the drug to work is precisely the state in which the cognitive flexibility benefits are attenuated. This is the first direct evidence that psilocybin modulates striatal dopamine prediction error in a behaving animal, and the first formal demonstration that nutritional state moderates its pro-cognitive effects. Both findings should shape how the anorexia trial designs handle nutritional rehabilitation timing relative to dosing.

Item two — staying with dopamine but pivoting to a different addiction theme and a cell type the field has been slow to take seriously. Astrocytic D1 Dopamine-Signaling Regulates Synaptic Remodeling and Cocaine Seeking, from the Dong group at Pittsburgh. D1 dopamine receptors in the nucleus accumbens shell are an essential substrate for the persistent circuit changes that embed drug-seeking memory. D1 is expressed on both neurons and astrocytes, and the astrocyte contribution has been mostly speculative. They did a clean dissociation. In accumbens shell slices, D1 agonists drive astrocytic calcium activity that's attenuated by astrocyte-specific D1 knockdown — establishing that the receptor is functional on the astrocyte. Then, in the behavior — selective knockdown of astrocytic D1 in the accumbens shell prior to cocaine self-administration inhibited the cocaine-induced generation of silent synapses, which is the textbook substrate for accumbens circuit remodeling during drug experience. Behaviorally, astrocytic D1 knockdown expedited extinction of cocaine seeking and reduced cue-induced reinstatement. The framing is important. The field has spent a decade developing neuron-targeted approaches to manipulate accumbens dopaminergic plasticity for addiction, and they've mostly worked at the bench and failed to translate. The astrocyte is doing meaningful pharmacological work on the same receptor, in the same nucleus, and is potentially a different target — astrocyte-selective viral delivery has matured enough in the last few years to be tractable, and astrocytic D1 may be the more druggable handle for relapse prevention than the neuronal pool the field has been chasing.

Item three, opioid pharmacology with a genetically diverse cohort design that's exactly what the field has needed — Addiction-Like Severity Predicts Prolonged Oxycodone Withdrawal-Induced Allodynia in Genetically Diverse Rats, from the George lab at UC San Diego. Heterogeneous stock rats, which carry the genetic diversity of eight founder strains and approximate human genetic variation at the level relevant for opioid response, underwent long-access twelve-hour-per-day intravenous oxycodone self-administration. They were stratified by an Addiction Index assembled from escalation of oxycodone intake, motivation under progressive ratio, tolerance to analgesic effects, and acute withdrawal mechanical pain sensitivity. Mechanical allodynia was measured at six timepoints across three weeks of abstinence. Headline — withdrawal-induced allodynia is significant and prolonged, lasting up to three weeks, and the High Addiction Index rats show greater intensity and longer duration of pain sensitivity than the Low Addiction Index rats. The relationship survives even when allodynia itself is excluded from the Addiction Index, so it's not circular. Self-administration behaviors — escalation and motivation, specifically — predict subsequent withdrawal allodynia severity in linear regression. This is the cleanest individual-difference framing of opioid-induced hyperalgesia I've seen. Clinically it matters because protracted withdrawal pain is a known driver of relapse, and a candidate biomarker that's prospective rather than reactive — a stratification done during the active-use period to predict who will have the worst three-week pain trajectory — is exactly what clinical buprenorphine and methadone induction protocols could use. The mechanistic next step is the obvious one and not yet done — figure out what's different about the central pain circuitry in the High Addiction Index animals.

Item four, an ADHD pharmacology paper from Cordoba, currently in its third bioRxiv revision and worth pulling up now that the v3 is out — Working Memory Deficits and Altered Prefrontal-Hippocampal Activation in p35 Knockout Mice with ADHD-Like Phenotypes: Influence of Sex and Acute Monoaminergic Treatments, from the Paglini group. The p35 knockout mouse is a model of cyclin-dependent kinase 5 dysregulation with ADHD-like phenotypes, and the p35-Cdk5 pathway has been implicated in ADHD pathophysiology through its effects on neuronal connectivity and dopamine regulation. They asked the practical pharmacological question. Spontaneous alternation in the Y-maze, c-Fos as a marker of task-related activation in prefrontal and hippocampal regions, and acute treatment with methylphenidate, fluoxetine, or the combination. At baseline, p35 knockouts show reduced spontaneous alternation, with intact recognition memory — a working-memory-selective deficit, which is the right phenotype to ask the monoaminergic question against. The c-Fos analysis shows reduced prefrontal and increased hippocampal task-related activation in the knockouts, with sex effects. The pharmacology — acute methylphenidate or fluoxetine, separately or in combination, produces region- and sex-dependent normalization of the activation pattern. The interesting clinical handle here is the combination — methylphenidate plus fluoxetine is a common real-world prescription pattern when ADHD is comorbid with depression, and the mechanistic interaction between the dopaminergic and serotonergic arms of that regimen has been understudied in the preclinical literature. A working-memory model with measurable prefrontal-hippocampal correlates is a reasonable place to start asking what the combination is actually doing, and the sex-dependence of the response should make it into the clinical conversation about dosing.

Item five, on the clinical psychedelic landscape this week — Federal Backing of Psychedelic Research and Developments in HPL-003 for Depression, from Psychiatric Times. Two threads worth pulling on. First, the federal-research-funding thread — explicit support has been emerging for psychedelic-assisted treatment research at the institute level, with implications for which sponsors can credibly take depression and post-traumatic-stress trials into definitive Phase 3 without being entirely dependent on private capital. Second, and the more concrete piece, HPL-003 — Helus Pharma's deuterated psilocin analog. The pharmacological move is the right one. Psilocybin itself is a prodrug; the body has to dephosphorylate it to the active psilocin, and the rate-limiting metabolism contributes to between-patient pharmacokinetic variability. Going to a deuterated analog of the active metabolite — replacing hydrogens at metabolically labile positions with deuterium — slows downstream metabolism, narrows the pharmacokinetic distribution, and gives a more predictable in-clinic experience with a more manageable session duration. Helus has reported statistically significant adjunctive benefit in Phase 1/2a in inadequate responders, with no requirement for antidepressant discontinuation. That last point is important — most psychedelic depression trials to date have required SSRI washouts, which is a real implementation barrier given how slowly serotonergic antidepressants wash out and how much patients deteriorate during the washout. If HPL-003 can be added on top of stable SSRI therapy without compromising the psychedelic response, that's a meaningful second- or third-line positioning story if Phase 3 holds.

Item six, the pharmacoepidemiology pick of the week — the Lancet Psychiatry systematic review and meta-analysis of antidepressant use in pregnancy and risk of autism and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder in children. Thirty-seven prior studies, more than six hundred thousand pregnancies with antidepressant exposure, against approximately twenty-five million unexposed pregnancies. The naive analysis shows a thirty-five percent increase in ADHD risk and a sixty-nine percent increase in autism without intellectual disability — the kind of effect size that's been driving prescribing anxiety in obstetrics for two decades. The methodological work the paper does is the right kind. After adjusting for maternal mental health, family history, and genetic confounding, the associations become significantly weaker or statistically insignificant. The negative-control logic is the most convincing piece. Paternal antidepressant use around the time of conception, which cannot be a direct in-utero exposure mechanism, is associated with a forty-six percent increase in ADHD risk and a twenty-eight percent increase in autism risk in offspring. Maternal antidepressant use before conception shows the same pattern. The straightforward interpretation is that the apparent association in unadjusted analyses reflects parental mental-health and genetic load that is shared with offspring neurodevelopmental risk, rather than a direct teratogenic effect of the drugs. The clinical translation matters and is going to matter in patient conversations. The most common reason women discontinue serotonin reuptake inhibitors in pregnancy is fear of neurodevelopmental harm, and the discontinuation itself carries non-trivial maternal relapse risk and downstream offspring harm. This meta-analysis doesn't license casual prescribing, but it does shift the risk-benefit conversation back toward treating depression in pregnancy.

Item seven, and back to fundamental dopamine biology with a clean computational result — Error-Driven Representation Learning in the Mesolimbic System, from a Harvard group. Classical reinforcement learning models in neuroscience have assumed a fixed state representation, and asked how reward prediction errors carried by phasic dopamine update the mapping from that fixed representation to value. Modern machine learning has known for years that you can get much more powerful predictive systems by using the same errors to update the representations themselves — backpropagating prediction errors not just to weights but to learned features. Whether the brain does this has been an open question with mixed evidence. They asked it cleanly. Simultaneous recordings from striatal projection neurons in the olfactory tubercle — which they treat as carrying putative state features — and dopamine neurons in the ventral tegmental area. Trial-by-trial changes in striatal activity are more consistent with dopamine-driven representation learning than with several alternative updating schemes, including pure value updating with a frozen representation. This is one of those convergence results that's quietly important. The architecture that's worked in machine learning — let your errors reshape what the system represents, not just what it predicts — appears to be implemented in the mesolimbic system. For pharmacology this matters because dopaminergic drugs aren't just modulating value learning; if the representation itself is what dopamine is updating, then the substrate that antipsychotics and dopaminergic agents are intervening on is a much more flexible target than the textbook prediction-error frame suggests.

Item eight, and the computational complement to item one's dopamine story — Dopamine Depletion Drives Whole-Brain Oscillatory Disruptions via Cortico-Subcortical Resonance: A Multiscale Model of Parkinson's Disease in Mice, from a Politecnico di Milano group. Parkinson's disease has a hallmark — exaggerated beta-band synchrony in the 10-to-30 hertz range — that's measurable not just in basal ganglia but in motor cortex, thalamus, and cerebellum. The puzzle has been how focal subcortical dopamine depletion in the substantia nigra produces brain-wide oscillatory pathology. They built a connectome-constrained multiscale model embedding biophysically detailed spiking networks of basal ganglia and cerebellum within whole-brain corticothalamic dynamics, grounded in the Allen Mouse Brain Connectivity Atlas. Two results that matter. First, confining dopamine depletion exclusively to subcortical circuits is sufficient to produce widespread beta hypersynchrony, with heterogeneous theta and gamma dysregulation alongside. The brain-wide pathology does not require widespread dopamine loss — it propagates from focal subcortical depletion through network coupling. Second, and this is the actionable mechanistic statement, virtual loop ablations show that cortical and cerebellar beta amplification strictly requires intact cortico-basal-ganglia-thalamic feedback; severing this loop confines beta to subcortical generators. So the cortical and cerebellar beta signatures aren't independent oscillators in pathology — they're resonance phenomena in closed large-scale loops. For drug development this matters because therapies that target the closed-loop dynamics — basal ganglia output stage, thalamic gating — rather than the cortical oscillation itself, are the leverage points implied by the mechanism. It also gives a theoretical foundation for adaptive deep brain stimulation paradigms that use cortical beta as a control signal — the cortical signal is informative because it reflects the loop, not because it's the disease.

Item nine, an Alzheimer's mechanism paper with a small RNA twist — tRNA-Derived Fragments Elevated in Alzheimer's Disease Facilitate Tau Aggregation, from the Krichevsky lab at Brigham and Women's and Harvard. Tau aggregation in tauopathies requires co-factors, and small RNAs have been proposed as candidates, but the specific endogenous transcripts that promote tau pathology have remained vague. They identify stress-induced transfer-RNA-derived fragments — short RNA species generated under cellular stress, abbreviated tRFs — as the most dysregulated small RNA class in human Alzheimer's brains, in PS19 mutant tau transgenic mice, and in human neuronal tauopathy models. Two specific fragments — 5-prime GluCTC and 5-prime GlyGCC — show the strongest accumulation. The functional experiments are clean. The 5-prime GluCTC fragment induces serine 396 phosphorylation, oligomerization of tau, and impaired neurite growth in cellular models. The fragment is enriched in pathological tau precipitates and co-localizes with oligomeric tau in PS19 mouse brains. Inhibiting 5-prime GluCTC mitigates tau pathology. And the propagation hook — the fragments are secreted by stressed neurons and taken up by recipient cells, implicating them in cell-to-cell spread of pathology. Why this is interesting beyond the cellular mechanism — antisense and RNA-targeting therapeutics for tau aggregation have so far focused on tau messenger RNA itself, which has been a hard target. A non-coding RNA co-factor that's specifically dysregulated in disease, secreted from stressed neurons, and necessary for aggregation is exactly the kind of target that the antisense oligonucleotide and RNA-based small molecule toolboxes are well-suited to engage. Worth watching whether anyone takes 5-prime GluCTC into a chemical-probe or therapeutic-development campaign.

Closing on the agentic-AI side of the slate — BiomniBench: Process-Level Evaluation of LLM Agents for Real-World Biomedical Research, from the Phylo group. LLM agents are doing real biomedical research now, and the field has been evaluating them with outcome-only benchmarks — does the agent produce the right final answer. Outcome-only scoring fails in two ways. First, a correct final answer can come from memorization, reward hacking, or wrong reasoning that lands on the right number by coincidence. Second, valid alternative analyses get scored wrong simply because they differ from the reference solution. The BiomniBench framework scores the entire agent trajectory against expert-designed task-specific rubrics. The first release, BiomniBench-DA, has one hundred data-analysis tasks across seventeen task types, five disease areas, and a general biology category — each grounded in a published paper from journals like Nature, Cell, and Science, and co-developed with either an original author or an independent domain expert. Three findings worth carrying. Frontier and open-weight models cluster within a few percentage points of each other, with substantial headroom on the rubrics for all models. The agent harness — the scaffolding around the model — shifts scores by more than the gap between successive model generations, which is a striking inversion of where the field has been investing optimization effort. And agents reliably ground their claims in real sources, but consistently fall short on method selection, biological interpretation, and scientific reasoning. That last point is the diagnostic value of process-level scoring — outcome benchmarks have been missing exactly the failure modes that matter most for routine biomedical use. This is the kind of evaluation infrastructure the field actually needs, and the dimension-level diagnostics it provides should reshape how groups choose harnesses and how they target their reasoning interventions.
