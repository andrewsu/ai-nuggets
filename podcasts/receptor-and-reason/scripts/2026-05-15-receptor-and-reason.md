## Script

Good morning, Alan. This is Receptor and Reason for Friday, May 15, 2026 — your daily roundup of neuropsychopharmacology, computational pharmacology, and agentic AI in biology. Ten items today. Two threads pull through the day. The first is dopamine pharmacology yet again — opioids reshaping tonic versus phasic release, nitrous oxide working through a glutamate-to-dopamine circuit, and an adolescent-dopamine knockout that reproduces a schizophrenia phenotype. The second is the psychiatric pipeline, with two head-to-head readouts that just landed and a unusual TMS-plus-drug combination trial that the FDA just waved through.

First up — and this is the strongest opioid mechanism paper to land this week — out of Neuropsychopharmacology yesterday, a study showing that acute fentanyl drives opposing changes in tonic and phasic dopamine release, and that this dissociation predicts impaired Pavlovian reward learning. The framing that the field has carried for the last decade is that opioids and stimulants converge on the same final common pathway — nucleus accumbens dopamine release — and the differences between them are quantitative rather than qualitative. This paper goes after that framing directly. Using fast-scan voltammetry and a fiber photometry approach in rats, they show fentanyl produces a sustained elevation of tonic dopamine in the accumbens, but suppresses the phasic dopamine transients that are time-locked to reward-predictive cues. In a Pavlovian conditioned-approach task, the magnitude of phasic suppression predicts the magnitude of behavioral impairment — animals stop tracking the cue. This matters because tonic and phasic dopamine encode different computational quantities — roughly, motivational vigor versus reward prediction error — and opioids appear to selectively scramble the prediction-error channel while preserving or amplifying the vigor channel. That gives you a candidate computational signature for opioid-induced incentive disruption that is distinct from stimulant signatures. Worth tracking for both the basic-science framing and the human-imaging translation.

Item two is the cleanest receptor-pharmacology paper this week and stays in the opioid-and-memory neighborhood. From bioRxiv neuroscience Wednesday, the role of mu and delta opioid receptors in tianeptine-induced memory enhancement. Quick anchor on tianeptine — it's marketed as an antidepressant in Europe and Asia, classically labeled as an atypical SSRI, but the modern understanding is that it doesn't meaningfully touch the serotonin transporter at therapeutic doses. The antidepressant effect requires mu-opioid receptor activation, which has been replicated by several groups. What hadn't been done is the same dissection for tianeptine's cognitive-enhancing side, which is clinically the more interesting effect because pro-cognitive antidepressants are essentially a missing category. This study uses mu-knockout and delta-knockout mice in a cross-maze spatial memory task with hippocampal local-field-potential recording. The memory enhancement and the increase in hippocampal beta-band oscillations both require the mu receptor — knockout abolishes them. The delta knockout does not. Tianeptine's locomotor effect, by contrast, depends on both receptors. Clean dissection. It also reframes how to read the renewed interest in mu agonism for depression — the field has been worried about pushing mu agonism into clinical use because of the obvious abuse-liability concern, but tianeptine has a forty-year clinical record at low doses, and an effect on cognition that is mu-mediated and separable from analgesia is precisely the kind of pharmacological asymmetry that motivates the search for safer biased mu agonists.

Item three. From Molecular Psychiatry yesterday, a paper titled "How muscle talks to brain — apelin protein mediates exercise-induced antidepressant effects." The exercise-antidepressant literature has been chasing a peptide mediator for two decades. Several candidates — BDNF, irisin, lactate via HCAR1 — have each captured part of the effect. This study makes the case for apelin, which is a short peptide hormone released from contracting skeletal muscle, with central receptors enriched in regions relevant to mood. In a chronic-restraint-stress model, exercise rescues the depressive-like phenotype, and exercise raises plasma apelin. Apelin receptor antagonism in the brain blocks the exercise rescue. Peripheral or central apelin administration alone partially reproduces the rescue. The receptor pharmacology then converges on hippocampal neurogenesis and signaling through AKT and mTOR. The implication, if it survives independent replication, is that apelin or an apelin-receptor agonist could serve as an exercise mimetic for patients who can't engage in physical activity — a population that overlaps heavily with severe depression. There is already an apelin receptor agonist in cardiometabolic clinical trials, so the chemistry exists. The honest caveats are the standard ones for a mediator paper — exercise effects are multidimensional, no single peptide will recapitulate them, and the most one can ask is whether apelin is necessary and partially sufficient. By this paper's tests, it is.

Item four pivots to a fast-acting depression mechanism with a clearer pharmacological pedigree. Also from Molecular Psychiatry Wednesday, nitrous oxide controls the deep-cerebellar-nuclei to VTA dopamine circuit by enhancing AMPA receptor function during rewarding behavior. Nitrous oxide has been creeping back into the rapid-acting antidepressant conversation alongside ketamine and the psychedelics — there's a small but reasonably positive treatment-resistant-depression literature, and the mechanism has been hand-waved as NMDA antagonism, by analogy to ketamine. This paper goes after a different node. Using slice electrophysiology and in vivo recordings, they show nitrous oxide enhances AMPA receptor function specifically at deep-cerebellar-nuclei terminals projecting onto VTA dopamine neurons — recall this is the same projection that the depression circuit paper from earlier this week implicated through a different route, via VGLUT2 trafficking. Nitrous oxide acutely increases the VTA dopamine response to reward in vivo. AMPA receptor blockade in the VTA prevents the antidepressant-like behavioral effect. So the picture you get is nitrous oxide acting as an AMPA-receptor potentiator at a specific cerebellum-to-VTA synapse, with downstream dopamine release, with behavioral consequences in a depression model. That's structurally similar to the ketamine story — rapid AMPA upregulation as the proximal mechanism — but at a different anatomical site. AMPA-receptor positive modulators are an active drug-discovery space, with osavampator from Biogen the most advanced clinical candidate, and circuit-level dissections like this matter for site-specific dosing arguments down the road.

Item five stays in dopamine and moves to schizophrenia. Also from Molecular Psychiatry Wednesday, an adolescent-restricted Grin2a knockout in dopamine neurons. Anchor — Grin2a encodes the GluN2A subunit of the NMDA receptor, and GRIN2A loss-of-function variants are among the more robust schizophrenia genetic findings to come out of the recent rare-variant exome studies. The functional consequences in dopamine neurons specifically have not been resolved. This group deletes Grin2a from VTA dopamine neurons during a defined adolescent window. The adult phenotype includes disrupted reward-prediction-error signaling in dopamine neurons measured by fiber photometry, alterations in cue-reward association learning, and a set of behavioral abnormalities the authors map to schizophrenia-relevant constructs. The temporal specificity matters — adult-onset Grin2a deletion in dopamine neurons does not produce the same phenotype. So you get a model that locks together three things that have been argued for separately in schizophrenia — adolescent vulnerability windows, NMDA hypofunction, and dopamine prediction-error disruption — into a single causal chain. Translationally, this is upstream of antipsychotic pharmacology by quite a few steps, but it makes the case that prediction-error-restoring rather than dopamine-blockade interventions could be the more etiologically targeted approach. The xanomeline plus trospium combination, marketed as Cobenfy, is the closest currently approved drug to that framing.

Item six is the week's most useful piece of psychiatric-pipeline reporting. The first network meta-analysis of all FDA-approved adjunctive treatments for major depressive disorder was presented at the Neuroscience Education Institute Spring Congress in early May and published in press release on Tuesday. The comparator set was lumateperone, aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, cariprazine, and quetiapine extended-release. Across ten registrational trials, lumateperone — sold as Caplyta — ranked first on all four efficacy outcomes: Montgomery-Åsberg depression scale change from baseline with a mean difference of about 4.7 points versus placebo plus antidepressant, response and remission odds ratios in the 2.2 to 2.3 range, and Clinical Global Impression severity change of 0.6 points. On safety, lumateperone was the only agent showing no statistically significant weight gain versus placebo plus antidepressant, and the only one with akathisia rates comparable to placebo. The interpretive caveat is the standard one for industry-sponsored indirect comparisons — this is a Johnson and Johnson analysis, the trial-selection rules were defined by the sponsor, and network meta-analysis as a method is more sensitive to those choices than people often assume. That said, the akathisia and weight signals match what individual lumateperone trials have reported, and clinicians have already been treating lumateperone as the gentler add-on in this class. The data give that practice a more defensible quantitative footing.

Item seven extends the psychiatric pipeline thread to a less conventional design. The FDA cleared on May 7 a Phase 2/3 trial of NRX-101 — that's a fixed-dose combination of D-cycloserine, a partial NMDA agonist at the glycine site, with lurasidone — in combination with robotic-enabled transcranial magnetic stimulation for treatment-resistant depression with suicidality. The trial is three-armed — drug plus active TMS, drug plus sham TMS, and presumably a control arm — at 240 patients, led by NRx Pharmaceuticals. The combination logic is mechanistic — D-cycloserine has been shown across several small studies to potentiate the antidepressant effect of TMS by promoting NMDA-receptor-dependent plasticity at stimulated cortical targets, and lurasidone provides receptor coverage that the company argues mitigates the suicidality-related agitation risk that has bedeviled rapid-acting glutamatergic interventions. The novelty is less in the molecular pharmacology — D-cycloserine plus TMS has been published — and more in the regulatory framing of TMS plus drug as a unified intervention requiring its own approval pathway. If it works at scale, it changes the design space for everything else in the rapid-acting-depression pipeline.

Item eight is the most methodologically novel piece of preclinical work this week. From bioRxiv neuroscience yesterday, ultrasound-mediated focal serotonin delivery into perigenual anterior cingulate cortex of awake behaving macaques. Brief anchor — perigenual ACC is the prefrontal-limbic region most implicated in negative affective bias and in the targeting of subcallosal-cingulate deep brain stimulation for treatment-resistant depression. The serotonin question in this region has been impossible to study causally because there are no spatially focal pharmacological tools for primate cortex. This group uses focused ultrasound to transiently open the blood-brain barrier over the pgACC, then administers serotonin systemically — only the targeted region gets exposure. Multimodal readout combines computational behavior modeling, resting-state functional MRI, magnetic resonance spectroscopy, and pupillometry. The behavioral effect of pgACC serotonin elevation is specific — it reduces occupancy of high-motivation states and weakens the energizing effect of rich reward environments, without altering sensitivity to immediate offer value. The neuroimaging effect is altered frontal functional connectivity and reduced local glutamatergic metabolite signal. This is the closest a primate study has come to a causal regional pharmacology experiment in the cortex of an awake animal, and the behavioral readout aligns with longstanding human serotonergic-depression hypotheses about effort and energization. If the technique generalizes, it's a route to causal pharmacology in the primate brain that doesn't require chronic implants. The translational ceiling is high — focused ultrasound for blood-brain-barrier opening is already in human trials for chemotherapy delivery — and a serotonin-into-pgACC paradigm for treatment-resistant depression is now imaginable rather than science-fiction.

Item nine. From the same issue of Neuropsychopharmacology last week, computational phenotyping of effort-based decision-making in type-2 diabetes patients on and off semaglutide. The premise is that the persistent question about GLP-1 receptor agonists and mood — whether semaglutide reduces appetite and weight by reducing reward valuation broadly, with mood and motivation as collateral — is best addressed not with depression scales but with effort-based decision-making tasks, which are sensitive to motivational changes that scales miss. They use a computational task that decomposes choice into reward sensitivity, effort cost, and decision noise, fit a hierarchical Bayesian model, and compare T2D patients on and off semaglutide. The on-drug effort cost is elevated — patients require larger reward offers to take physical effort — while reward sensitivity itself is preserved. That's the opposite of what you'd want to see if semaglutide were producing the persistent treatment-emergent anhedonia signal that the FDA flagged in 2024 — that would predict reduced reward sensitivity. Instead, the signature looks like a selective increase in subjective effort cost. The interpretation is that the GLP-1 metabolic effect — reduced energy availability, lowered ghrelin tone — translates into a computationally specific motivational change rather than a generalized hedonic suppression. The piece also illustrates the right way to use computational psychiatry methods on a drug class — task choice and model design matched to the pharmacological hypothesis, not to a generic depression rubric.

Last item, the lightest. From bioRxiv pharmacology and toxicology Wednesday, a virtual-screening method called Simpatico that produces an embedding for each atom in a protein pocket and each atom in a ligand, rather than collapsing protein and ligand each to a single embedding vector. The motivating problem — DrugCLIP and its successors, the dominant embedding-retrieval virtual-screening paradigm, force protein and ligand into single vectors and then look up nearest neighbors. That collapses the per-atom binding information into a single number and loses partial-binding compatibility — the case where a ligand's pharmacophore is right but only at a subpocket. Simpatico keeps the atom-level resolution, uses a CLIP-style contrastive training objective so atoms that interact in known complexes are co-located, and demonstrates higher virtual screening enrichment on the standard benchmarks at speeds comparable to existing embedding-retrieval methods. The relevance for the audience is twofold. First, it's a useful resolution to a real bottleneck in billion-compound virtual screening that has been the dominant industrial use case for AI in drug discovery this year. Second, the modeling instinct — keep the structural granularity that the biology demands rather than compressing for engineering convenience — is the same instinct that the better small-molecule generative models are converging on. Worth watching whether it gets adopted in the agentic-AI drug-discovery loops that are pushing virtual screening as the front-end retrieval step.

And the focused-ultrasound serotonin delivery into primate pgACC is the methodological piece I'd most want to see replicated. See you tomorrow.
