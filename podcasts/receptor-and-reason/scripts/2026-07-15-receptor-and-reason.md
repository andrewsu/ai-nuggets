## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason. It's Wednesday, July fifteenth, twenty twenty-six. Eleven items today. Structural GPCR pharmacology opens, then a peptide-receptor angle in progressive MS, cholinergic circuits in fear extinction, sex-specific epigenetics of the pain-depression comorbidity, a striatal astrocyte-neuromodulator paper leading into two more Parkinson items, then non-invasive brain stimulation for sleep-restricted cognition, and closing with three computational-pharmacology and agentic-AI-in-biomedicine picks.

Opening with what may be the most consequential structural neuropharmacology paper of the week — a revised preprint from Bryan Roth's lab at UNC Chapel Hill. mGluR2 has been the target of one of the most persistent failure lanes in neuropsychiatric drug development. Pomaglumetad, the poster-child mGluR2/3 agonist for schizophrenia, and multiple mGluR2 positive allosteric modulators have failed to reproduce their preclinical effects in humans despite years of structure-guided medicinal chemistry. The Roth group's diagnosis is that everything the field knows about mGluR2's molecular structure was learned from recombinant HEK-cell systems with engineered mutations and nanobody stabilizers. What the receptor actually looks like in the brain has never been visualized. Here they built a knock-in mouse where CRISPR insertion places an mCherry tag in the flexible C-terminus of endogenous mGluR2 without disturbing pharmacology, developed a rapid immunoaffinity purification method for brain-derived receptor complexes, and solved cryo-EM structures of eleven distinct endogenous receptor assemblies spanning active and inactive states, homomeric and heterodimeric, G-protein-coupled and uncoupled. Two findings change how the field should think about drug design here. First, mGluR2/3 heterodimers were detected only in active-state complexes — the heterodimer isn't a static receptor species, it's an activation-associated ensemble, which recontextualizes selectivity claims made against recombinant heterodimers. Second, endogenous ternary complexes contain both mGluR2 homodimers and mGluR2/3 heterodimers coupled to a single G-alpha-oA heterotrimer, and these brain-derived structures differ significantly from the recombinant reconstructions. If the failed clinical mGluR2 program was based on the wrong structural target, the medicinal chemistry was structurally miscalibrated by design. The upside is that the endogenous cryo-EM data now provide a real blueprint for the next generation of mGluR2 modulators — and specifically for state-selective nanobodies of the kind that recently rescued NMDA hypofunction in schizophrenia models.

Second, staying on peptide-GPCR pharmacology and moving to progressive multiple sclerosis. From Carmen Sato-Bigbee's group at Virginia Commonwealth. They previously showed that the endogenous opioid-family peptide nociceptin — orphanin FQ, the endogenous ligand of the NOP receptor, which is neither mu, delta, nor kappa opioid — blocks premature oligodendrocyte differentiation in the developing brain. That function fits with nociceptin's normal developmental expression pattern, which drops sharply as myelination begins. Here they find that cerebrospinal-fluid nociceptin is dramatically elevated in progressive MS patients — but not in relapsing-remitting patients or controls. They reproduce the phenomenology by inducing experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis in older mice at an age equivalent to the human transition-to-progressive-MS risk window, and those mice develop severe, persistent clinical deficits with elevated brain nociceptin. The interventional result is what turns this into a pharmacology story. NOP-receptor antagonism produces regression of clinical scoring, higher oligodendrocyte-to-progenitor ratios, increased myelination, and reduced reactive astrocytes. Human astrocytes upregulate nociceptin secretion under proinflammatory cytokine exposure, positioning the peptide as a soluble mediator of microglia-astrocyte crosstalk. The specific take here is that the progressive-MS remyelination-failure phenotype may be mechanistically driven by aberrant reactivation of a developmental brake — with NOP antagonism as a testable pharmacological lever. NOP-antagonist tool molecules already exist and have been in clinical exploration for depression and cocaine use disorder, so if this circuit holds up, the translation clock is shorter than for most demyelinating-disease targets.

Third, on cholinergic control of fear extinction. From Vincent Laurent's group at UNSW Sydney, with Stephen Maren as coauthor. The infralimbic cortex is essential for extinction learning and receives dense cholinergic input from the horizontal limb of the diagonal band of Broca. The specific role acetylcholine plays there has been unresolved. The group used optogenetics in ChAT-Cre transgenic rats to selectively silence the diagonal-band-to-infralimbic cholinergic projection during either fear conditioning or extinction. Silencing during conditioning was inert. Silencing during extinction produced two behavioral effects that are exactly what you'd want for exposure-based therapy — enhanced extinction retrieval, and complete prevention of fear renewal in both ABA and AAB paradigms. The anatomical specificity is convincing — silencing the neighboring diagonal-band-to-prelimbic projection had no effect. Mechanistically, cholinergic terminals in infralimbic preferentially excite superficial-layer interneurons, consistent with feed-forward inhibition of infralimbic pyramidal cells. And the pharmacological punchline — nicotinic, but not muscarinic, blockade in infralimbic during extinction fully reproduced the effects of silencing the pathway. That's a specific, testable proposal for the anxiety-disorders clinic. A brief nicotinic antagonist given during human exposure therapy sessions might increase extinction durability against contextual relapse. The most-cited failure mode of exposure therapy is renewal — patients extinguish in the clinic and relapse in the world. If this circuit generalizes, targeting the transient nicotinic signal during exposure is a much more surgical intervention than boosting whole-brain acetylcholine with a cholinesterase inhibitor.

Fourth, on the epigenetic biology of chronic-pain-induced depression, with sex as a first-class variable. From Pierre-Eric Lutz's group at INCI CNRS in Strasbourg. Neuropathic pain from the sciatic nerve cuff model produces depressive-like behaviors in mice, matching the human comorbidity where forty to sixty percent of chronic-pain patients develop anxiety or depression. In the anterior cingulate — a shared substrate for pain and affective processing — the group profiled genome-wide DNA methylation, three histone modifications, and gene expression, and integrated them through co-expression network analysis. The finding that reframes how the field should read this literature: both sexes show extensive epigenomic remodeling, but at largely distinct genomic loci. The differentially modified regions in males and females barely overlap. However, the regulatory features of those regions — transcription-factor binding profiles, chromatin annotations, positional distribution — are similar across sexes. And the affected loci converge on partly overlapping gene sets and biological pathways, with synapse-related modules dominating in both. This is the sex-specific-but-functionally-convergent architecture that has been proposed but not cleanly demonstrated for pain-depression comorbidity. The therapeutic implication is directly actionable — a treatment aimed at the shared downstream pathway, say synapse-modulating drugs, should work in both sexes, but any drug aimed at the upstream epigenetic modifier will need sex-specific targeting to hit its intended substrate. With HDAC and DNMT inhibitors already in clinical development for other CNS indications, this becomes a fairly concrete prescription for how to segment the trial arms up front rather than post-hoc.

Fifth, and shifting to Parkinson's. From Rafiq Huda's group at Rutgers. The starting question is that striatal dopamine and acetylcholine have been at the center of the Parkinson's neurotransmitter story for decades, but the role of astrocytes in the dopamine-acetylcholine axis has been mostly unexamined. They combined fiber photometry recordings of dopamine release, cholinergic-interneuron acetylcholine release, and astrocyte calcium activity in the dorsolateral striatum during voluntary locomotion. They see a two-timescale relationship — on fast timescales dopamine and acetylcholine release anticorrelate, as expected, but over continuous locomotion, as astrocyte calcium builds up, dopamine and acetylcholine positively covary with the astrocyte signal. Muscarinic or D1/D2 blockade reduces locomotion-evoked astrocyte calcium; closed-loop optogenetic silencing of striatal cholinergic interneurons during locomotion reduces astrocyte calcium as well, establishing acetylcholine as a causal driver. In the 6-OHDA Parkinsonian mouse, acetylcholine release during locomotion was severely compromised alongside the expected dopamine loss, and astrocyte calcium activity dropped as a consequence. This is what turns the paper from a nice circuit demonstration into a druggable hypothesis. Prior work from the same group had shown that chemogenetic facilitation of astrocyte cholinergic signaling improves both calcium activity and motor deficits in the same PD model. So striatal astrocytes emerge as an underexplored PD therapeutic target — with a specific access point through the muscarinic system that has been thought of as a neuronal target but may actually be doing much of its work through astrocytes.

Sixth, staying on Parkinson's but at the behavioral-pharmacology layer. This is a third revised version of a preprint from Aaron Wong's group at University of Delaware. The theoretical setup is that bradykinesia could reflect any of three distinct effort-reward-trade-off failures — reduced reward sensitivity, increased effort perception, or a biased mapping between effort and reward — and each maps onto a different treatment recipe. They test each with thirty-seven Parkinson's patients on and off dopamine medication and thirty-nine controls, using a gambling task for reward sensitivity, a force-matching task for effort perception, and the effort-reward slope for the mapping. The primary result is a strong, well-controlled null on reward sensitivity — Parkinson's patients did not differ from controls in psychometric-function fits, and Bayes factors support the null hypothesis. Same story for the effort-reward slope. The positive finding is on effort perception, using both a sensorimotor force-matching task and subjective ratings — Parkinson's patients produced lower match forces and rated the same physical exertion as more effortful than controls. Fatigue was ruled out with per-trial regressions. What breaks the classical dopamine-and-reward story is that dopamine medication did not shift effort perception meaningfully — the effect was resilient to whether the patient was ON or OFF their L-DOPA. That has a clean pharmacological implication. Dopamine replacement is the wrong lever for effort-perception-driven bradykinesia, and the field should be looking upstream — at sensorimotor cortex, cerebellum, or the effort-signaling contributions from cortico-basal-ganglia loops that don't feed through midbrain dopamine.

Seventh, moving to a C. elegans Parkinson's model with a molecular-biochemistry twist, from the Caldwell lab at University of Alabama. The paradox they attack is that dopaminergic neurodegeneration in C. elegans alpha-synuclein models is temporally age-dependent but is fully uncoupled from the organism's aging trajectory — worms can lose dopaminergic neurons on the normal schedule while their overall lifespan is unaltered. Their hypothesis is that the interaction between dopamine and alpha-synuclein is the molecular hinge that separates the two. Overexpressing tyrosine hydroxylase to raise endogenous dopamine exacerbated A53T-driven dopaminergic degeneration, and knocking out the worm TH ortholog to deplete dopamine rescued the neurons. Mutating a specific dopamine-interaction motif within alpha-synuclein made the neurodegeneration insensitive to any dopamine manipulation, structurally implicating the dopamine-alpha-synuclein adduct as the toxic trigger. The other half of the story is what makes this a systems-level result. The same interaction acts as a pleiotropic trigger driving two compartmentalized responses — localized dopaminergic degeneration via oxidative stress, and organism-wide TFEB-dependent proteostatic remodeling that actually extends lifespan. The therapeutic prescription — a small-molecule strategy that boosts autophagy without exacerbating dopamine-driven oxidative stress could preserve the systemic-longevity signal while protecting the vulnerable neurons. That's a two-vector pharmacology prescription that most current PD drug programs are not organized to test.

Eighth, on a translational cognitive-enhancement paper from Walter Reed Army Institute of Research. Slow-oscillatory transcranial direct-current stimulation applied during a two-hour restricted-sleep opportunity, followed by forty-six hours of sleep deprivation. Twenty-six healthy adults, stimulation delivered for one hour at zero-point-seven-five hertz starting sixty minutes after sleep onset. Two performance measures. The Psychomotor Vigilance Test showed the stimulation group had significantly better vigilance during the subsequent sleep-deprivation window compared to sham. The paired-word learning task is where the memory-consolidation angle shows up — the sham group recalled fewer word pairs at twenty and one-twenty minutes post-wake than at pre-sleep baseline, while the stimulation group actually improved at one-twenty minutes relative to pre-sleep. Taken together — sleep-restriction protection for vigilance and net gain in overnight memory consolidation from about one hour of stimulation during a very short sleep opportunity. It's a small sample and the tDCS field has a well-documented replication problem, so the caveats are real. But the setup — brief slow-oscillation stimulation as a pharmacology-adjacent intervention for sleep-restricted cognition — has direct relevance for shift work, military operational contexts, and potentially for residency training. And the proposed mechanism — enhancing the endogenous UP-DOWN state alternation of NREM sleep that mediates synaptic homeostasis — is at least conceptually consistent with the modafinil-plus-caffeine cognitive-protection literature but attacks a different physiological substrate.

Ninth, and moving to computational pharmacology. From a group at TCS Research in Hyderabad. They present a hybrid framework where a mechanistic quantitative systems pharmacology model of prostate cancer immunotherapy provides the physical dynamics, reinforcement learning learns adaptive population-level dosing policies, and Monte Carlo tree search does forward-looking rollout evaluation on top of the RL policy to refine patient-specific decisions. The benchmark is against fixed ipilimumab dosing regimens. The headline result — ninety-five percent remission rate matching the highest fixed-dose regimen at ten milligrams per kilogram per dose, achieved with a median total dose of seventy-two milligrams per kilogram, which is comparable to the lowest fixed-dose regimen at three milligrams per kilogram. In plain terms, they get the efficacy of aggressive dosing at the cumulative exposure of conservative dosing — because the reinforcement-learning-plus-tree-search system dynamically adjusts. The interpretability side is why I'm carrying it on this show rather than leaving it as an oncology story — drug-activity rate emerges as the dominant determinant of long-term outcome from the learned policies, meaning the system reproduces a mechanistically sensible PK-PD finding, not a black-box guess. The CNS translation is direct — psychiatric drug programs, especially in schizophrenia and depression, have identical heterogeneity structure and identical fixed-dose ceilings, and a mechanistically-grounded system operating on a properly-fit CNS quantitative-systems-pharmacology model could rewrite dose-finding for psychiatric trials with a fraction of current per-patient exposure risk.

Tenth, on multi-agent AI for biomedical discovery, from Queen's University Belfast. The system they call Octopus is a neuro-symbolic framework combining a localized, privacy-preserving multi-agent language-model swarm with regularized machine-learning predictive environments. The design principle is important — most agentic biomedical systems stop at hypothesis generation from cellular assays. Octopus explicitly chains from CRISPR dependency data through XGBoost feature-attribution cascades, through patient-derived xenograft tumor trajectory prediction, all the way through to human survival data on a colorectal cancer cohort. In an unsupervised sweep over CRC transcriptomes, the pipeline prioritized insulin-like growth factor two as a candidate vulnerability for 5-fluorouracil resistance. The result survives Benjamini-Hochberg correction, and independently predicts tumor volume shrinkage in a held-out xenograft cohort. The architectural point that generalizes beyond CRC — for biomedical agentic systems to move from research demos to clinical decision inputs, the pattern that works is agentic hypothesis generation constrained by orthogonal statistical validation across biological scales. The recipe applies directly to CNS drug repurposing — take the failed psychiatric-medication landscape, run agentic hypothesis generation over CNS transcriptomes and target-validation datasets, and force each candidate to survive orthogonal constraints from human epidemiology or biobank-linked outcome data before it is promoted to a real experiment.

Closing today with the paper that's generated the most curious debate on preprint social media this week — an updated preprint from Liang Wang at Huazhong University claiming that general-purpose large language models possess emergent capability for biological structural discovery. The specific claim — a small GPT-2, one hundred twenty-four million parameters, fine-tuned exclusively on an English-paraphrasing task with no biological data, achieves about eighty-four percent zero-shot accuracy on protein homology detection. Scaling up to Qwen-3 approaches one hundred percent on standard tasks and maintains seventy-five percent precision on remote homology in the sequence-identity-below-twenty-five-percent zone where classical alignment breaks down. Chain-of-thought traces reveal what the authors call mental folding — sequence-level reasoning that goes beyond alignment and appears to leverage implicit structural knowledge. Interpretability analysis identifies specific attention heads that collapse natural-language syntax and biological-sequence representations into a shared difference-detection manifold. The strong claim is that abstract logical structure distilled from human language constitutes a powerful cognitive prior for decoding biology. The skeptical read is that adversarial-paraphrase-style benchmarks can leak signal in ways that inflate zero-shot performance, and that homology detection is inherently statistical enough that a language-model attention pattern can approximate it without the "mental folding" narrative. Even at the skeptical reading, the direction matters for how the field allocates compute in the AI-for-biology arms race. If the specialized architecture premise — build a fresh transformer, train on massive protein data — is only partly the right recipe, then the domain-adaptation route with general models might get very close for a fraction of the cost. That's a conversation the receptor-pharmacology community should be listening in on, because target-family classification and endogenous-ligand identification for orphan GPCRs are exactly the tasks where this kind of cross-modal-priors work could land first.
