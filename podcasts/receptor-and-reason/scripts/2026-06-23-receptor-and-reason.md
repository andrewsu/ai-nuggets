## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason for Tuesday, June 23rd, 2026. Ten items today, opening on a Phase 3 readout that, if you read it side-by-side with a Neuropsychopharmacology paper from the same week, gives you the full mechanism-to-clinic arc on single-dose LSD for major depression. Then a piece on orexin pharmacology and resilience, a regulatory turn on cytisinicline, a nociceptor gene-therapy preprint that may be the most important non-opioid pain story of the quarter, a chemical-chaperone-plus-gene-augmentation combination for an SLC6A1 epileptic encephalopathy variant, a circuit-targeted antipsychotic strategy that recapitulates clozapine without the sedation, and three pieces from the AI side — an MCP server over a curated neuroscience knowledgebase, a foundation model for epileptogenic-zone localization, and a careful piece on persona-conditioned LLM support for people who use drugs.

Definium Therapeutics yesterday announced positive Phase 3 topline from the Emerge study of DT120 — which is what the lysergide orally disintegrating tablet is officially called — in major depressive disorder. A hundred-and-forty-nine adults with DSM-5 MDD and a baseline MADRS of at least twenty-six, across twenty sites, randomized to a single hundred-microgram dose or matching placebo, with a twelve-week double-blind primary phase followed by a forty-week open-label extension. The primary endpoint was change from baseline in MADRS at week six. They hit it cleanly — least-squares-mean change of minus thirteen-point-three on DT120 versus minus five-point-two on placebo, placebo-adjusted reduction of about eight points, p less than point oh-oh-oh-one. Two other numbers matter as much as the primary. The week-one placebo-adjusted effect was minus fourteen-point-two MADRS points, which is the rapid-onset signature you would expect from a serotonergic psychedelic and is bigger than any non-ketamine antidepressant has shown at that timepoint. And the week-twelve placebo-adjusted effect is still minus seven-point-three points — durability out to three months from a single dose. No serious adverse events, no suicidality signal. This is the largest, most rigorous single-dose-psychedelic readout in MDD to date, and the ODT formulation is a meaningful operational improvement over capsule formulations because dose timing and onset are tighter. The other thing worth noting is the FDA priority-voucher landscape around psychedelics — Compass for treatment-resistant depression, Usona for MDD, Transcend for methylone in PTSD — Definium isn't on that list, but a clean Phase 3 with these numbers reshuffles the competitive map regardless.

The natural pairing is a Neuropsychopharmacology paper that landed five days earlier from a Basel group, an acute-and-post-acute neurobehavioral study of LSD in healthy subjects. Forty-five healthy volunteers, twenty-four women, randomized double-blind crossover of the same hundred-microgram LSD dose versus placebo. The novel piece is the post-acute battery — for up to a week after dosing they ran auditory tetanization with EEG, paired-associative stimulation with TMS, and serial peripheral BDNF, and added a sequence-typing motor-learning task one day post-dose and questionnaires on perceived stress and cognitive flexibility at one week. Two things to take from this paper sitting next to the Definium readout. First, the same hundred-microgram dose was used — so the mechanistic and clinical work are at the same exposure, which they often aren't in psychedelic translational science. Second, the post-acute markers they chose — LTP-like plasticity via auditory tetanization, motor-cortex plasticity via TMS-PAS, BDNF as the circulating index — are exactly the substrates that you would hypothesize are doing the sustained antidepressant work. So when the Definium trial shows that a single dose holds out to week twelve, the Basel group is showing you the neuroplastic correlates a week out in healthy brains. The fact that those correlates are detectable in healthy subjects at all, with a single dose, is the most informative thing about the design — it argues against the view that durable LSD effects in MDD are purely state-dependent psychological reconsolidation, and toward a measurable molecular and circuit substrate. I'd take this off the abstract until I can read the post-acute effect sizes, but the two papers together are the strongest mechanism-to-Phase-3 connection psychedelic neuroscience has assembled.

Staying in the same Neuropsychopharmacology issue is a paper on selective orexin receptor cross-over treatment for stress resilience, which is interesting because the orexin field has been bouncing between orexin-1 and orexin-2 selectivity for years without a clean strategy. The framing here is that the anterior basolateral amygdala contains a pro-stress neuronal population identified by orexin-receptor-1 expression — Hcrtr1-positive neurons — and that targeting that circuit with sequenced or combined Orx1R and Orx2R interventions produces a different molecular signature than either alone. The headline is that cross-over treatment increases resilience and induces neuroplastic-signaling-gene expression. Going off the abstract since I don't have institutional access — what would distinguish this from prior work on suvorexant or daridorexant for sleep, or on selective Orx2R antagonists for depression like seltorexant, is that the cross-over framing implies a temporal pharmacology — that you don't pick one receptor and stay on it, you stage Orx1R and Orx2R interventions to drive a specific downstream signaling state. If the gene-expression signature they identify is causal for resilience rather than correlated with it, that is the kind of pharmacological-staging insight that would feed into next-generation orexin-targeted antidepressants. The narcolepsy field has already shown that orexin-2-receptor agonism is on a fast track — oveporexton's recent breakthrough designation makes that clear — and pulling resilience and neuroplasticity into the same pharmacological frame extends the orexin program well beyond sleep medicine.

A regulatory turn. The FDA issued a Complete Response Letter yesterday for the cytisinicline NDA for smoking cessation in adults. Cytisinicline, for context, is a plant alkaloid that's a partial agonist at the alpha-four-beta-two nicotinic acetylcholine receptor — same target as varenicline — but with a much shorter half-life and a cleaner cardiovascular and neuropsychiatric profile in the published trials. Achieve Life Sciences had a PDUFA of June twentieth, and the CRL came two days late. The headline issue cited is manufacturing — specifically primary manufacturing — not efficacy or safety, and the company says it will resubmit at the end of 2026 with a potential decision in the first half of 2027. The substantive point here, for an audience watching the broader psychiatric-and-addiction pipeline, is that varenicline supply has been intermittent for the last couple of years, the nicotine-replacement-therapy options are well-established but modest in efficacy, and a clean alpha-four-beta-two partial agonist with a friendlier side-effect profile would meaningfully change the addiction-treatment toolkit. The other piece worth noting is that Achieve has a Commissioner's National Priority Voucher for the e-cigarette and vaping-cessation indication, which is the much bigger market in younger smokers and which is procedurally separate from this rejected NDA. So the CRL slows the conventional-smoking indication by six to nine months, but doesn't directly affect the vaping-cessation path.

Onto the preclinical neuropharm. A preprint from a Bunnett-group collaboration with Monash and others has posted what may be the most important non-opioid pain paper of the quarter — targeting synaptic vesicle endocytosis in nociceptors with LNP-CRISPR. The premise is that persistent pain signaling requires sustained synaptic transmission between nociceptors and dorsal horn neurons, and sustained transmission requires synaptic-vesicle recycling, and recycling requires endocytic machinery. They identified two endocytic regulators — AAK1 and dynamin one — as candidate nociceptor-specific targets. Nociceptor-specific AAV knockdown of either in Nav1.8-positive dorsal-root-ganglion neurons blocked postoperative and neuropathic hypersensitivity without affecting baseline mechanical or thermal sensitivity, locomotion, or spontaneous behavior. The therapeutic-vehicle piece is the new contribution — lipid nanoparticles encapsulating CRISPR-dCas9-repressor mRNA for sustained, reversible transcriptional repression of Aak1 or Dnm1, delivered intrathecally. The result is durable and reversible analgesia across postoperative, inflammatory, neuropathic, and osteoarthritis pain models, with no impairment of acute nociception or locomotor activity. The mechanistic story is clean — reduced neurotransmitter release probability at nociceptor terminals via disrupted SV recycling. The therapeutic implication is large. Chronic pain pharmacology has been gated by the fact that anything that broadly suppresses nociceptor activity affects acute nociception too, and that opioids are stuck on receptor-level pharmacology with all the addiction and tolerance baggage. A presynaptic, nociceptor-specific, reversibly-tunable epigenetic intervention is structurally different from anything currently in the chronic-pain pipeline. The body of the preprint hasn't been rendered yet so I'm working from the abstract — the questions for the full text are duration of effect after a single intrathecal dose, immunogenicity of the LNP-CRISPR construct, and how the dosing translates to a route a chronic-pain patient would actually accept.

A second gene-therapy-adjacent preprint, this one a combination strategy for an SLC6A1-encephalopathy variant. SLC6A1 encodes GAT-1, the principal neuronal GABA transporter, and loss-of-function variants cause developmental and epileptic encephalopathy through reduced GABA reuptake, impaired trafficking, and haploinsufficiency. The paper reports a newly identified de-novo missense variant — alanine-three-oh-five to valine — in a patient with myoclonic-atonic epilepsy. They compared the variant to a residue-matched control, ran structural-stability predictions across nine algorithms using the cryo-EM-derived GAT-1 template, then biological assays — tritiated-GABA uptake in HEK293T cells and in iPSC-derived astrocytes and cortical neurons, ER colocalization by live-cell confocal, and pharmacologic rescue with three chemical chaperones — 4-phenylbutyrate, TUDCA, and salubrinal. The interesting therapeutic move is combining 4-PBA with GAT-1 cDNA gene augmentation. Each alone is a partial rescue — 4-PBA helps misfolded GAT-1 traffic to the membrane, and gene augmentation provides additional functional transporter — but the combination is super-additive on uptake. The lesson for this class of monogenic encephalopathy is that the two failure modes — folding-trafficking defects and dose insufficiency — call for two distinct interventions, and stacking them is more efficient than maximizing either. This is also a template for variant-specific precision treatment, because the chaperone-responsiveness of the missense variant is what tells you whether the combination is appropriate; a pure loss-of-expression variant wouldn't benefit from the chaperone arm.

A circuit-targeted antipsychotic strategy. A Japanese group has posted a preprint identifying a clozapine-responsive prefrontal ensemble that's enriched for schizophrenia risk genes and that stabilizes value-guided choice. The experimental arc is value-guided decision-making under NMDA-receptor hypofunction — a standard model of schizophrenia-like decision noise — with chemogenetic and pharmacological manipulation. NMDA hypofunction increased value-independent decision noise and weakened the ensemble's pre-choice transient, both rescued by clozapine. Chemogenetic inhibition of the ensemble alone increased decision noise, which is the causal step. The translational move is the cocktail — they integrated receptor-affinity data with mouse and human single-cell transcriptomes from the ensemble cells, and rationally designed a multi-receptor antagonist combination that reactivated the ensemble and rescued decision noise without increasing NREM sleep time or NREM delta power. Clozapine, by contrast, does increase both. The result is ensemble-targeted pharmacology — pick the receptor combination that hits the cell type, not the receptor combination that hits the symptom — as a path toward decoupling antipsychotic efficacy from sedation. This is the kind of paper that will be either cited a lot or quietly ignored depending on whether anyone can replicate the cocktail in another model. The structural advance — using cell-type transcriptomes plus receptor pharmacology to design a cocktail — is what's worth following even if this specific combination doesn't translate.

Pivoting to the AI side. A bioRxiv preprint introducing VFB-MCP from the Virtual Fly Brain consortium and Cambridge — natural-language access to Drosophila neuroscience grounded by an ontology-led knowledgebase. The framing is that connectome and neuroanatomy databases have become rich enough to be useful and complex enough to be inaccessible to anyone who hasn't memorized the schema. They expose Virtual Fly Brain through the Model Context Protocol — the protocol Anthropic introduced last year for connecting LLMs to tools and structured data sources — over the expert-curated, ontology-backed knowledge graph. The benchmark is thirty neuroscience tasks compared against a bare LLM and a web-search-augmented LLM. The MCP-equipped LLM produced precise, verifiable, appropriately quantified answers on twenty-five of thirty tasks, versus fourteen of thirty for web and two of thirty for bare, with statistically significant differences on all pairwise comparisons. The pattern is exactly what you would expect — the MCP advantage is largest for tasks requiring data quantification, where ontology grounding does real work, eighty-nine percent versus eleven percent for web. Why this matters beyond flies is the architectural point — MCP over a domain ontology is now a deployable pattern for any field with a well-curated knowledgebase, and connectomics is just the first one with the right data depth. The same approach would map cleanly onto Allen Brain Atlas data, onto rare-disease registries, onto curated drug-target databases. The Drosophila proof-of-concept is small enough to evaluate rigorously and informative enough to generalize from.

Adjacent. A paper from a McGill-and-Brno collaboration on foundation models for epileptogenic-zone identification in drug-resistant epilepsy. About thirty percent of epilepsy patients fail medication and become surgical candidates, and the surgical workup is gated by accurate localization of the EZ. The standard pipeline is feature engineering on intracranial recordings or graph-based modeling of seizure-network propagation. The contribution here is a transformer-based foundation model pretrained self-supervised on large unlabeled interictal stereo-EEG and MEG recordings, with transfer learning across the two modalities, and explicit incorporation of sleep-stage context — interictal EZ signatures are sleep-stage-dependent in ways that prior feature-engineered methods often ignored. They benchmark against classical deep-learning and graph-based baselines on two open datasets. The clinical thesis is that better unsupervised pretraining on multi-modal interictal data is the right path for EZ localization because the labeled-data bottleneck is severe and patient-specific variability is high. The limitations to flag — the approach still relies on invasive sEEG and MEG, generalization to scalp EEG is untested, and prospective validation in actual surgical workups hasn't happened. But the structural point is that DRE EZ localization is exactly the kind of low-data, high-stakes, multi-modal problem where foundation-model pretraining has the biggest payoff.

Closing on a careful and slightly different AI piece. An arXiv preprint from a Drexel group on persona-conditioned LLM support for people who use drugs — the title is "Self-Stigma Is Not a Monolith, but Generic Empathy Is." The premise is that current LLM-based mental-health interventions for substance-use populations default to a uniform empathetic tone, and that this uniform tone misses the structure of self-stigma — internalized shame, externalized resistance, help-seeking willingness, stereotype endorsement, social withdrawal each behave as distinct phenotypes. They condition responses on those phenotypes and compare against generic empathetic baselines using Gemini three Flash and Llama four Scout. The empirical finding has the right shape — conditioning on high-shame phenotypes improved perceived empathy and relevance, but generic empathy was actually better for individuals with lower internalized stigma. So persona-conditioning is not a win on average; it's a win when correctly matched and a loss when mismatched. The clinical-deployment concern follows directly — without validated screening to assign phenotype before the conversation starts, persona-conditioning can degrade outcomes for half the population it's trying to help. The methodological lesson, which generalizes beyond substance use, is that average-quality metrics over heterogeneous populations hide phenotype-by-treatment interactions that matter clinically. For anyone deploying mental-health LLMs in real settings, the safer default is generic empathy plus a separate validated triage step, not persona conditioning baked into a single endpoint. That's a useful negative result for the field.
