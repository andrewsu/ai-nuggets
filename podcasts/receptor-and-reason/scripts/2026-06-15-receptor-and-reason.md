## Script

Welcome to Receptor and Reason for Monday, June fifteenth, 2026. Eleven items today, with the throughline being how surprisingly small structural details — a perineuronal net glycoprotein, an allosteric lipid pocket, an endolysosomal repair complex — can scale up to disease-defining behavior or therapeutic opportunity. We open with what may be the most direct mechanistic bridge from schizophrenia genetics to adult cortical plasticity that the field has produced, then move through neurodevelopment, hippocampal learning, addiction circuits, ion-channel pharmacology, and two strands of neurodegeneration, before closing with three computational items — a biophysics-meets-pharmacology paper on GPCR lipid sensitivity, a connectome-informed mouse-brain simulator, and a sober head-to-head of agentic AI against domain experts in multi-omic discovery.

The opener is in Molecular Psychiatry, from a Zurich, Harvard, and MIT collaboration involving the Hannan, Hensch, and Tyagarajan groups. They focus on Adamtsl3, a secreted glycoprotein at a schizophrenia risk locus, and ask whether it does something cell-autonomous in cortical interneurons that connects the genetics to a mechanism. The answer is yes, and the mechanism is perineuronal nets. Parvalbumin interneurons accumulate these chondroitin-sulfate cages around their somas during late postnatal development, and the closure of the cage corresponds to the closure of the critical period — the window in which sensory cortex is most reorganizable. The new paper shows that Adamtsl3 acts in parvalbumin interneurons to regulate MMP9, the matrix metalloproteinase that remodels these nets, and that adult conditional deletion of Adamtsl3 reopens juvenile-like ocular dominance plasticity in mouse visual cortex. Two things make this important. First, this is the cleanest cell-autonomous mechanism for a schizophrenia risk gene that anyone has produced in a long time — most SCZ loci converge on developmental processes that are hard to attribute to a single cell type at a single time point. Second, the result puts perineuronal nets, MMP9, and critical-period reopening on the same therapeutic axis. There is already a growing literature on lifting perineuronal nets to enable plasticity in PTSD, addiction, and amblyopia — chondroitinase ABC being the classic tool — and Adamtsl3 now joins that as a genetically supported, drugable point of intervention. Whether you can selectively reopen plasticity without destabilizing the fast-spiking interneuron circuits that PNNs protect is the open question, and one this kind of cell-autonomous mechanism finally lets you ask properly.

That bridges naturally to the second item, a bioRxiv preprint on Adnp, the gene mutated in Helsmoortel–Van der Aa syndrome — an intellectual-disability and autism-spectrum disorder whose pathology centers on a chromatin remodeller called the ChAHP complex. The team builds an allelic series in mice — a conditional knockout, a germline heterozygote, and a knock-in of one of the actual human frameshift mutations — and asks whether dosage is the right framing. The answer is partly. Brain-wide conditional knockouts using Nestin-Cre are perinatally lethal, but cortex-specific knockouts using Emx1-Cre survive and display severe cortical hypoplasia and learning deficits. The interesting bit: germline heterozygotes — both a deletion and the patient frameshift — show quantitatively identical cortical hypoplasia despite producing different truncated proteins. And in the Morris water maze, both heterozygotes show sex-specific learning deficits remarkably similar to the full conditional knockouts, even though the cortical hypoplasia is milder. The take is that learning behavior is more sensitive to Adnp dosage than cortical neuroanatomy is, and that frameshift alleles are essentially loss-of-function for behavioral purposes — they do not produce dominant-negative-flavored exotic phenotypes. The clinical relevance is that gene-therapy programs for HVDAS, which involve restoring ADNP dosage, now have a clearer behavioral readout — water maze — that should respond to even partial restoration. Whether the phenotypic convergence with the Chd4 mutant phenotypes the same lab has published implicates the broader ChAHP complex as a therapeutic axis is the natural follow-on.

Third, a paper in Neuropharmacology that does optogenetics-as-pharmacology for the locus coeruleus-to-CA1 projection in freely moving mice. The premise is that the locus coeruleus releases noradrenaline and, increasingly recognized, dopamine into the hippocampus, and that the LC-to-CA1 projection is implicated in memory consolidation. The group selectively photostimulates tyrosine-hydroxylase-positive LC terminals in CA1 in awake, behaving animals and measures Schaffer-collateral evoked field potentials. The result is robust in vivo LTP that persists for hours, accompanied by elevated extracellular noradrenaline, dopamine, and glutamate in CA1 — measured by microdialysis. Pharmacological dissection is the satisfying part: the LTP is abolished separately by a beta-adrenergic antagonist, by SCH twenty-three thousand three-ninety which is a D1 antagonist, and by an NMDA antagonist. So this isn't pure noradrenergic enhancement; it requires co-released dopamine acting through D1 plus NMDA-dependent plasticity machinery. Behaviorally, post-training photostimulation of LC-CA1 strengthens weak object-location memory, and prolongs spatial memory in the Morris and Barnes mazes out to ten days post-training, which is much longer than these tasks typically retain. The reason this matters for CNS pharmacology: a clean causal link between a specific neuromodulatory projection, a defined receptor combination, and durable spatial memory enhancement gives you something concrete to drug — D1 plus beta-adrenergic combination dosing for memory consolidation has been a perennial idea in Alzheimer's and post-anesthesia delirium, and this paper provides the circuit and receptor rationale.

Item four, a paper in Biological Psychiatry that drills into how opioid-withdrawal-induced aversive memory gets locked in at the cytoskeletal level in the hippocampus, with sirtuin 2 — an NAD-dependent deacetylase — as the central player. The model is morphine withdrawal-conditioned place aversion. The mechanism, in plain terms: chronic opioid withdrawal in the dorsal hippocampus drives sirtuin 2 to deacetylate tubulin, which recruits calcineurin to microtubules, which dephosphorylates end-binding protein three, which then docks with drebrin to engage the p140Cap, Src, cortactin, and cofilin pathway that drives actin polymerization at dendritic spines. Pharmacological inhibition of sirtuin 2 blocks both the cytoskeletal rearrangement and the aversive memory, and the same mechanism extends to contextual fear conditioning, meaning this is not just a withdrawal-specific quirk. Two things to take from this. First, the aversive memory that drives compulsive opioid-seeking — the part that keeps patients relapsing months after detox — has a discrete molecular handle that is not itself opioidergic. A sirtuin 2 inhibitor in chronic recovery support is conceptually adjacent to the cognitive-behavioral therapy that targets the same conditioned associations. Second, the more general claim — that this NAD-dependent deacetylase regulates the actin-microtubule interface during memory consolidation — implicates sirtuin 2 in a wider set of memory-related therapeutic indications, including PTSD where erasing maladaptive emotional memory is the explicit clinical goal.

Item five is another Neuropharmacology piece, this one a structural-pharmacology paper on the alpha-conotoxin LvIC variant called D1G-delta-Q14-LvIC, which is a selective antagonist for the alpha-six over alpha-three subtype of beta-four nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. The alpha-six-containing nAChRs are concentrated in dopaminergic terminals and are implicated in nicotine reward and Parkinson's, but pharmacological dissection has been hard because the alpha-six-beta-four and alpha-three-beta-four subtypes share most of their primary sequence. The conotoxin variant has nineteen-nanomolar potency at alpha-six-alpha-three-beta-four and is essentially inactive at greater than ten micromolar on alpha-three-beta-four — three orders of magnitude selectivity. By making sixty-seven targeted mutations in the alpha subunit, the group identifies three residues — glutamate one-fifty-two, isoleucine one-fifty-seven, threonine one-ninety-five — that drive the selectivity. The reason to highlight this: alpha-six-containing nAChRs are one of the few subtypes selectively expressed on dopaminergic terminals, and a tool that can distinguish them from the more widely expressed alpha-three-beta-four opens experiments in nicotine addiction, l-dopa-induced dyskinesia, and chronic-pain models that have been stuck on non-selective compounds for years. For comp-pharmacology specifically, the mutational map gives a rational starting point for designing small-molecule mimetics of this selectivity profile, which is the next translational step — peptide conotoxins themselves are not great drugs.

Item six is the most mechanistically dense piece on today's list, a structural-cell-biology paper from a bioRxiv collaboration on how tau prion-like seeds escape the endolysosomal compartment. The standing story is that tau pre-formed fibrils get taken up into endolysosomes, damage the membrane, leak into the cytosol, and seed templated misfolding of native tau. The new paper asks which components of the lysosomal damage-and-repair machinery actually matter. The screen — using genetic perturbations of ESCRT-recruiting calcium sensor ALG-2, the conjugation-of-ATG8s-to-single-membranes pathway called CASM, the phosphoinositide-initiated tethering and lipid transport pathway called PITT, and the Parkinson's-linked lipid transporter VPS13C — yields a counterintuitive picture. PITT and VPS13C strongly modulate tau seeding in both neurons and astrocytes; CASM matters in astrocytes only; ALG-2 has a smaller role across the board. The mechanistic surprise comes from cryo-electron tomography: unlike the canonical damage agent LLOMe, tau fibrils don't visibly damage the lysosomal membrane. Lysosomes in fibril-treated cells are structurally intact. Yet there's tau and tau-associated aggregate material on both sides of the membrane. The proposed model is that fibril uptake nucleates aggregation inside the lumen, the aggregates create transient reversible pores rather than overt rupture, and the PITT pathway plus VPS13C plus CASM are what limit cytosolic escape. The pharmacology implication, which the paper handles correctly, is that small-molecule modulators of the PITT pathway or VPS13C are a candidate disease-modifying strategy for tauopathies — and intriguingly, VPS13C variants are already PD risk alleles, raising the possibility that a single lysosomal-repair-augmenting therapeutic could touch both Parkinson's and tau-driven dementia.

Staying with neurodegeneration, item seven is a strain-biology paper on multiple system atrophy, the rapidly fatal synucleinopathy that comes in two clinical subtypes — cerebellar variant MSA-C, and parkinsonian variant MSA-P. The prevailing hypothesis has been that distinct alpha-synuclein conformational strains in the two subtypes drive their distinct clinical pictures, by analogy with the PrP-strain story in prion disease. The new preprint comprehensively tests that. They characterize alpha-synuclein aggregates from postmortem brains of MSA-C and MSA-P patients by limited proteolysis, conformational stability assay, and seed amplification assay; then propagate them in M83 transgenic mice and re-characterize; then track disease kinetics in mice inoculated with either subtype-derived aggregate. Across every biochemical fingerprint and every functional propagation assay, the two clinical subtypes look indistinguishable. The kinetics in mice are the same. The deposition patterns are the same. The negative result is the interesting result here: MSA clinical heterogeneity is not strain heterogeneity. Instead, the authors propose that the same alpha-synuclein strain initiates pathology in different brain regions in different patients, and clinical presentation tracks where it starts. For the therapeutic conversation, this is consequential — single antibody or immunotherapy approaches that target one alpha-synuclein conformation are likely to be equipotent across MSA subtypes, which is good for trial design. It also reframes the prion-strain analogy: in MSA, anatomy may matter more than conformation.

Item eight bridges to comp pharmacology. It's a biophysics paper that addresses a specific frustration in GPCR drug discovery — that the same receptor gives you different conformational equilibria and different signaling readouts in different lipid environments, and we don't really know why. The system is the A-two-A adenosine receptor reconstituted in nanodiscs of varying size, with varying anionic lipid content. The group measures conformational equilibria experimentally and runs molecular dynamics simulations to ask where the anionic lipids actually are. The finding: anionic lipids form clusters within the nanodisc, sequestered in part by positive residues on the membrane scaffold protein itself, and the receptor sees only a fraction of the total anionic lipid that is nominally present. The threshold concentration to fully populate the active conformation is higher for POPS than for POPG, which is exactly the kind of detail that would normally confound a structural-biology campaign. The translational point is that the scaffold protein people use to host their GPCR for cryo-EM and ligand-binding studies is itself shaping the lipid environment around the receptor — and that protein engineering of the scaffold can rationally tune anionic lipid accessibility. For anyone running a fragment screen against a class A GPCR in nanodiscs and seeing inconsistent active-state populations across reconstitutions, this paper explains the inconsistency and offers a fix. The broader point — that membrane proteins are influenced by lipid clusters with their own organization — generalizes outside reconstituted systems to native HDL particles and likely to native membrane microdomains.

Item nine is a CNS-pharmacology preclinical paper on capsinoids — the non-pungent capsaicin analogs found in chili-pepper cultivars — used to drive mild hypothermia after ischemic stroke in aged mice. The clinical motivation: mild hypothermia is one of the most consistently neuroprotective interventions in stroke models, but in awake patients the body fights it by shivering, so cooling protocols require sedation. Capsinoids activate peripheral TRPV1 in the gut and skin without engaging the pain-related TRPV1 in the brain, which means they trick the body's thermoregulatory circuits into letting core temperature fall without triggering shivering. The new work is the aged-mouse part — most stroke neuroprotection results are from young animals and famously don't translate to older patients. In eighteen-to-twenty-month-old mice given intraperitoneal capsinoids two or four hours after middle-cerebral-artery occlusion, core temperature drops two to four degrees Celsius and stays down for several hours. In the permanent-occlusion model, capsinoid treatment reduces day-three infarct volume by forty-eight percent and day-thirty cortical atrophy by forty-four percent, with concordant gains in gait, grip strength, and contralateral foot-fault performance out to thirty days. In the reperfusion model, survival jumps from thirty-three percent to eighty percent through post-stroke day three. The aged-mouse data is the part that matters — capsinoids are essentially food-grade and have a clean safety profile, and the targeted-thermoeffector pharmacological strategy avoids the awake-patient shivering problem. The clinical proof-of-concept obviously hasn't been run, but the preclinical case for trialing capsinoid-induced mild hypothermia as an awake-patient stroke adjunct is now considerably tighter.

Item ten is the connectome-informed mouse brain simulator we've been waiting for. It's called DigiMus, and it's a multi-region spiking neural network architecture that takes leaky integrate-and-fire neuron dynamics and adds a regularization term that pulls trained connectivity toward three-node circuit motifs derived from real reconstructed neuronal morphologies — about thirty-eight thousand reconstructed cells across roughly fifty mouse brain regions. The result is a sequence-learning architecture in which biological circuit motifs serve as a structural prior, not a hard constraint. The evaluation is two-pronged. On eighteen rule-based cognitive tasks — sensorimotor mapping and perceptual decision-making — DigiMus matches or modestly outperforms standard architectures like temporal convolutional networks, LSTMs, and transformers, with the gap widening on harder decision-making. On real neural decoding from mouse auditory, lick-timing, and visual datasets, the single-region instantiations of DigiMus get small but consistent gains over structure-free baselines, while preserving the motif priors in their trained weights. The right framing here is what the authors themselves chose: this is not a digital brain reconstruction, it's a workflow for testing whether real circuit motifs constrain neural computation in ways that matter for behavioral modeling. For comp neuro and for AI-for-neuroscience, this is the next step beyond connectome statistics-as-decoration and toward connectome statistics-as-inductive-bias. If you've been frustrated that brain foundation models barely use the connectome data they cite, this is a constructive answer.

Closing on agentic AI, with a bioRxiv preprint that systematically benchmarks LLM-based agents at multi-omic single-cell analysis across eleven cancer types. The framework is called M3A — Multistep Multimodal Multiomic Agentic — and it's designed to maintain persistent multimodal data state across many reasoning steps, so agents can iterate on heterogeneous evidence rather than running a single deterministic pipeline. The evaluation has three legs. First, autonomous cell-type annotation across single-cell datasets, where agents can hit reasonable accuracy on standard cell types but degrade on rare or context-specific ones. Second, generation of falsifiable biological hypotheses from gene-expression programs, where agents are good at the broad survey but methodologically shaky — they don't reliably propose the right statistical test or control. Third, copilot experiments measuring how domain expertise plus the agent compares to autonomous-only, where the verdict is that domain experts remain critical for methodological guidance and biological synthesis. The net read is the right one for the audience: agentic AI is now demonstrably useful for the kind of broad, exploratory data interrogation that occupies the first weeks of a multi-omic project, and is demonstrably not yet useful as a replacement for expert hypothesis curation. The companion contribution is the M3A framework itself, which is open-sourced and designed for benchmarking the next generation of biology agents — an obvious good for the field. The agentic-AI-in-biology community has been short on principled evaluation, and tooling this gap is exactly what receptors-and-pharmacology-adjacent computational programs need before they start trusting agents with experimental design.

That's eleven for today. Two items I'd flag for closer follow-up: the Adamtsl3 perineuronal-net work, which connects schizophrenia genetics, critical-period plasticity, and an existing pharmacological axis in a way that may legitimately reopen the adult-plasticity therapeutic conversation, and the tau lysosomal-repair work, where the PITT-pathway and VPS13C convergence may put one shared therapeutic strategy in front of both Parkinson's and tau-driven dementia.
