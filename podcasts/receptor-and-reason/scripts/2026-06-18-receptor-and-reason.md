# Receptor & Reason — 2026-06-18

## Script

Today is Thursday, June 18th, 2026. I'm Receptor and Reason, and this is your daily round-up of neuropsychopharmacology, drug discovery, and the computational tools that increasingly sit alongside both. Ten items today — leading with what I think is the cleanest demonstration this week of going from a prefrontal circuit motif back to a rational receptor cocktail.

So, lead. New bioRxiv preprint argues that you can build a better antipsychotic by stop thinking about receptors first and starting with the prefrontal ensemble you're trying to rescue. The group identifies a clozapine-responsive ensemble in mouse medial prefrontal cortex that is enriched for schizophrenia risk genes and that they show stabilizes value-guided choice — meaning, when you silence it chemogenetically, the animal becomes noisier in its decisions even when the reward statistics haven't changed. NMDA receptor hypofunction, the standing animal model of psychosis, weakens the ensemble's pre-choice transient and produces the same value-independent decision noise; clozapine rescues both. Causality runs both ways, which is the point. Then the punchline — they take the receptor expression profile of this ensemble, cross-reference it against the affinity profiles of available compounds, and design a rational multi-receptor antagonist cocktail that reactivates the ensemble and rescues decision noise without the increased NREM sleep time and delta power that clozapine drags along. That last clause is what makes this a paper rather than a curiosity. Clozapine's efficacy is shadowed by its sedation, metabolic, and agranulocytosis profile; if you can keep the part of the polypharmacology that lifts the ensemble and drop the part that flattens sleep architecture, you have something genuinely worth a clinical translation conversation. This is also the cleanest demonstration I've seen of what people have been calling ensemble-targeted pharmacology — designing for a circuit-level signature rather than for one receptor at a time.

Staying on psychiatric mechanism, but switching from cortex to a sex-specific oligodendroglial story in major depression. Group integrated human postmortem MDD transcriptomics with mouse validation and converges on oligodendrocyte precursor cells, specifically a committed-OPC subpopulation expressing high levels of synaptic genes, as a female-biased lesion site. The neurexin pathway is the most perturbed signaling axis in MDD overall and the perturbation is larger in females. Weighted gene co-expression network analysis flags GABRG3 — the GABA-A gamma-3 subunit — as a female-MDD-specific hub gene in those committed OPCs, and conditional deletion of Gabrg3 in those cells in mice recapitulates MDD-like phenotypes including impaired neuron-OPC synaptic communication, abnormal myelination, and depression-like behaviors. The interest here is not that myelination matters in depression — that hypothesis is older than I am — but that the entry point is a GABA-A subunit on a non-neuronal cell, and that the female-specific signal in human data is strong enough to anchor a mouse genetic experiment. If this replicates, it suggests there's a slice of female MDD where the actionable receptor is on the OPC, not the neuron.

Staying with depression and dementia but pivoting to digital phenotyping — there's a new arXiv paper on running open-weight large language models on speech samples from standardized history-taking interviews to predict both dementia and depression severity in geriatric patients. They have 154 German-speaking subjects with parallel global staging on a depression scale and the standard Global Deterioration Scale for cognition. The cute methodological point — they compare zero-shot prediction by the LLM directly against using the LLM purely as a feature extractor for a downstream support vector regression. Depression severity gets predicted well zero-shot — mean absolute error of 0.6 on the scale — basically the model just listens and rates. Dementia is the opposite. Zero-shot is poor; structured feature extraction by the LLM into the SVR cuts the error by up to 35%. The take is twofold. First, LLMs are now apparently good enough at understanding affective content in clinical speech that they can stage depression without supervised training on that scale. Second, cognitive staging is still beyond what raw LLM reasoning gets you — you need to use the LLM as a structured-feature pipeline. Pause-enriched transcripts work nearly as well as human-transcribed ones, which means the whole pipeline is viable from raw audio. If you're working on remote monitoring for geriatric neuropsychiatric mixed presentations, this is a useful template.

Sticking with AI for pharmacology, but at a much more sobering register — TxBench-PP. New arXiv benchmark for AI agents on small-molecule preclinical pharmacology decisions. One hundred verifiable evaluations across mechanism-of-action and pharmacodynamic reasoning, compound-target engagement, causal target validation, developability and safety, and translational efficacy. Critically, the benchmark was designed so the answers can't be recovered by retrieving facts from literature — the agents have to reason from actual assay data, with realistic workflow snapshots, files they can inspect in a coding environment, and structured deterministic grading. Across sixteen model-and-harness configurations comprising eleven models and forty-eight hundred trajectories, no system reliably recovered the decisions. The best configuration was Claude Opus 4.8 on the Pi harness at 59.3% pass rate; GPT-5.5 on the same harness at 55.3%. So your best frontier agent today is wrong on roughly four out of every ten preclinical pharmacology calls when the answer isn't already in its training data. The benchmark also publishes a failure taxonomy, which is the part I'm most curious to dig into — whether the failure modes are pharm-specific reasoning errors, or generic chain-of-thought collapse. Either way this is the benchmark to watch over the next four quarters, because it's open enough that anyone can re-run it as models update.

Pivoting to pain pharmacology with a paper that retrofits the triptans onto a circuit story. Triptans — sumatriptan, zolmitriptan — have been clinically classed as migraine-specific drugs because of their action on vascular 5-HT-1B and 5-HT-1D receptors, and the assumption has been that the analgesia is mostly cerebral or vascular. This group screened Gi/o-coupled GPCRs in CGRP-positive dorsal root ganglion neurons and finds that Htr1b sits there and that triptans attenuate excitability of those nociceptors directly. Conditional genetic deletion experiments pin the analgesia not on every nociceptor but specifically on Htr1b in A-fiber mechanonociceptors — the fast-conducting fibers — and that's true across multiple pain models including neuropathic pain. Two practical consequences. One, the cardiovascular side effects of triptans, which have been the wall between them and broader analgesic use, appear to be partially Htr1b-independent — so an Htr1b-selective agonist that drops the cardiovascular target list might be a clean analgesic. Two, they also identify Gpr19, an orphan Gi/o GPCR, as an additional candidate target on the same neuron population. Orphan-GPCR-on-nociceptor is a small-but-emerging genre and Gpr19 looks like a tractable one.

Staying on the GPCR theme but moving from pain to glia. There's a useful paper out characterizing how Gi, Gq, and Gs signaling in astrocytes and microglia regulate production of tumor necrosis factor alpha — pro-inflammatory cytokine that also has known functions as a synaptic modulator. Using pharmacological and chemogenetic activation across rat primary astrocytes, primary microglia, and human iPSC-derived astrocytes, they show that Gs activation drops TNF expression substantially; Gq activation also drops TNF; and Gi activation, the one you'd typically lump with the inhibitory pathways, actually increases TNF in astrocytes and microglia both in vitro and in vivo. The directional inversion of Gi in glia versus neurons is the part to remember. Most neuropharm intuition is built on neuron-side outputs, where Gi is "inhibitory." In glia, for this cytokine specifically, Gi flips. The other useful finding is that the effect is selective to TNF — it doesn't generalize cleanly to other pro-inflammatory cytokines, so you can't read it off any single ligand. If you're designing GPCR-based interventions for neuroinflammation, the cell-type-specific signaling direction has to be tested rather than assumed.

Another glia-targeted item — same Gq pathway, different therapeutic context. Group asked whether you can rescue post-stroke cognitive impairment by chemogenetically activating Gq signaling specifically in hippocampal CA1 astrocytes. Post-stroke cognitive impairment shows up in more than 30% of survivors and current neuron-centric approaches lack cellular specificity and carry epilepsy risk. They show that Gq activation in CA1 astrocytes after ischemia restores dendritic complexity, spine density, long-term potentiation, and behavioral memory. Gi activation in the same cells does the opposite — produces neuronal hyperactivity without cognitive improvement. The finding I found most striking was the fiber-photometry data showing that astrocytic calcium signals precede the neuronal activity by 600 milliseconds during novel environment exploration. The phrasing the authors use is that astrocytes prime memory encoding rather than respond to it. If that timing holds up in other paradigms, it would force a rewrite of the usual neuron-astrocyte communication direction in memory models — and it would explain why glia-targeted chemogenetics outperforms neuron-targeted approaches for the slow, plasticity-dependent recovery problems where the current pharm options are weak.

Switching to addiction. Cannabis vapour withdrawal in rats — relevant because most preclinical cannabis dependence work uses injected synthetic cannabinoids rather than inhaled cannabis flower, which is what humans actually consume. Group exposed adult male rats to vapourized high-THC cannabis flower three times daily for seven days using a vaporizer designed for clinical-grade flower delivery, then precipitated withdrawal with the CB1 inverse agonist rimonabant. Reliable cannabinoid tetrad effects during exposure — reduced locomotion, hypothermia, increased tail-flick latency. Withdrawal produced increased total withdrawal scores and somatic signs including tremors, blinking, and grooming behaviours, plus reduced sucrose preference. The contribution beyond the tetrad replication is behavioural network analysis showing that both chronic cannabis exposure and withdrawal reorganize the transition structure of behavior — the cannabis condition collapses behavioural repertoire and modularity, and withdrawal then shifts the network toward exploratory behaviours like sniffing while suppressing locomotor sequences. The point is that conventional rodent withdrawal scoring undercounts the actual reorganization. If you're working on cannabinoid pharmacology for use disorder, the vapour-flower paradigm is the closer human analog and the network-level readout is the more sensitive endpoint.

Staying on addiction, second item, genuinely unexpected. Group had previously noticed that the oral microbiomes of people with cocaine use disorder are enriched for Streptococcus parasanguinis. Here they show that cocaine selectively enhances S. parasanguinis growth in vitro, then orally inoculate antibiotic-pretreated mice with the bug, S. salivarius as a control, Neisseria flavescens as a second control, or vehicle. The S. parasanguinis mice — and only those mice — develop spatial memory impairment, elevated brain IL-1-beta, and microglial activation. No bacterial translocation into the brain. Untargeted metabolomics fingers two metabolite changes — cysteine-S-sulfate and altered histamine metabolism — that themselves drive neuroinflammatory and amyloid-associated responses in cell culture. So the working model is that cocaine enriches a specific oral pathobiont, the pathobiont changes systemic metabolite chemistry, and that chemistry crosses into the brain to drive neuroinflammation and cognitive impairment. This is one of the cleanest causality chains I've seen for an oral-microbiome-to-brain story in psychiatric pharmacology — bacterial enrichment is observed clinically, recapitulated experimentally, and the metabolite arm is identified. If the mechanism holds up, the actionable interventions would sit at the oral microbiome or at the metabolite enzymes upstream, not in the brain.

Closer for today, a conceptual piece for anyone working on psychedelics or anesthesia. The Entropic Brain Theory has been around for a while — it says different states of consciousness can be ordered along a single dimension defined by the entropy of spontaneous neural activity. This paper tests the theory by running the same analytic pipeline — sample entropy of time-resolved small-world topology — across fMRI datasets from psychedelics, modafinil, propofol anesthesia, and schizophrenia. The ordering comes out as the theory predicted. Propofol anesthesia sits at the low-entropy end. Psychedelics and schizophrenia sit at the high end. The point isn't that we now know consciousness is one-dimensional; it's that this is the first cross-condition empirical test of the entropic-axis claim that uses one pipeline on multiple perturbations, and the prediction holds. For drug discovery purposes, the interesting thing is that schizophrenia and psychedelic states colocalize on the high-entropy end despite their very different phenomenology and clinical valence — consistent with what we know about 5-HT-2A pharmacology cutting both ways. The implication is that the entropic axis is a candidate translational biomarker for the next generation of psychedelic-adjacent compounds where the goal is therapeutic entropy without the dysregulation.

That's ten for today. Lead was the prefrontal ensemble plus rational multi-receptor cocktail design, which I think is the most translationally provocative item of the week. A female-specific MDD oligodendroglial mechanism, an LLM-based pipeline for parallel depression and dementia staging from speech, the sobering TxBench-PP showing frontier agents at 59% on preclinical pharmacology decisions, triptans rebranded as A-fiber nociceptor analgesics with an orphan GPCR target alongside, the Gi-inverts-direction story for TNF in glia, the glia-targeted Gq chemogenetics for post-stroke cognitive impairment with astrocytes leading neurons by 600 milliseconds, the vapour-flower cannabis withdrawal paradigm, the cocaine-enriched oral pathobiont causal chain, and the entropic-axis paper to close.
