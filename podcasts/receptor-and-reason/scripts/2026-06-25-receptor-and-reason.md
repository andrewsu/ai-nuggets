Welcome to Receptor and Reason for Thursday, June 25th, 2026. Ten items today. We open on a translational AD paper that is one of the more interesting things to come out this week — an Emory plasma-proteomic analysis of APOE-epsilon-four homozygotes across the lifespan that converges on semaglutide as a candidate disease-modifier. Let's dig in.

We start with the Emory APOE-epsilon-four-homozygote lifespan proteomics paper. The setup is one of the cleanest in AD epidemiology — homozygous epsilon four is the closest thing to a deterministic genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's disease, with lifetime risk in the seventy-to-ninety percent range, and yet the upstream biology of what this allele actually does to the proteome over the lifespan has been mostly opaque. They analyzed plasma proteomes from four-hundred-and-thirteen epsilon-four-slash-epsilon-four individuals, with and without cognitive impairment, ages twenty to ninety, and compared them against two-thousand-seven-hundred-and-sixty-four cognitively unimpaired epsilon-three homozygotes across the same age range. The first finding is that multiple biological pathways are altered in young adulthood in epsilon-four homozygotes — well before any cognitive symptoms or canonical AD biomarker changes. The pathways flagged are metabolism, GLP-1 and IGF signaling, mitochondrial function, microtubules, proteostasis, and synaptic biology. The kicker — they then took proteomic data from the STEP1 semaglutide trial, sixty-eight weeks of subcutaneous semaglutide in thirteen-hundred-and-eleven overweight non-diabetic participants, and asked whether semaglutide moves any of the proteins that epsilon-four-slash-epsilon-four moves. The answer is yes. Forty-four of the hundred-and-fifty-three semaglutide-modulated proteins overlap with epsilon-four-slash-epsilon-four-altered proteins, with a p-value of one-point-six-times-ten-to-the-minus-eight, and the directionality of the semaglutide effect is reversal — bringing the epsilon-four-shifted proteins back toward the epsilon-three baseline. The reversal is concentrated in metabolic and synaptic pathways, and it shows up most strongly at preclinical and clinical AD stages rather than in young epsilon-four-slash-epsilon-four carriers. IGFBP2 and GDF15 are the proteins they pick out as showing consistent directional reversal across multiple proteomic assays. Two caveats matter. First, this is a post-hoc cross-trial analysis — semaglutide was not given to epsilon-four homozygotes in a prospective AD-modification trial. Second, no pathway score survives FDR correction across all ontologies and lifespan epochs, so the pathway-level finding is suggestive rather than confirmatory. With those caveats, the interpretation lines up with the EVOKE and EVOKE-plus Phase 3 semaglutide-in-AD trials that just read out, and gives a biologically grounded reason to believe that GLP-1 receptor agonism may be more useful in epsilon-four-stratified populations than in unstratified mild-AD cohorts. The corollary for practice is that the field probably needs to start treating epsilon-four homozygosity as a target enrichment stratum for any AD-modification trial that touches metabolic biology.

Staying on AD repurposing, the second item — a propensity-weighted observational study from Galovic's group asking whether antiseizure medications, which are widely prescribed to older adults, relate to AD biomarkers at the population level. The hypothesis is that network hyperexcitability is prodromal in AD — there's solid evidence of subclinical epileptiform activity in mild cognitive impairment and early AD, and the relevant question is whether suppressing that hyperexcitability with ASMs slows amyloid or tau accumulation. They looked at fifty-two-thousand-five-hundred-and-thirty-seven participants in the National Alzheimer's Coordinating Center longitudinal study, and compared CSF biomarkers and amyloid and tau PET between ASM users and non-users, using inverse-probability-of-treatment weighting with gradient-boosted propensity scores. The result is directionally clean. ASM users have lower amyloid across multiple brain regions, and the effect amplifies in APOE-epsilon-four carriers — Centiloid beta of minus-twenty-five-point-seven, p equals point-zero-zero-seven. All three temporal tau-PET composites are significantly lower in users, with the META-temporal composite beta of minus-point-zero-five, p equals point-zero-one. The amyloid signal then replicates independently in ADNI — Centiloid beta of minus-eight-point-six, p equals point-zero-one. And four comparator drug classes show no amyloid signal, which is the right negative control. This is observational and the usual confounding-by-indication caveats apply — patients prescribed ASMs differ systematically from those not on ASMs — but propensity-weighted with a clean comparator class and an independent replication is about as good as observational work gets. The next step is the obvious one — a prospective trial of low-dose ASM in AD-prodromal APOE-epsilon-four carriers with documented subclinical epileptiform activity, with amyloid and tau accumulation as biomarker endpoints. The hyperexcitability-as-disease-driver hypothesis has been around for a decade — between this and the semaglutide signal, AD repurposing is having a much more interesting month than the field's late-stage anti-amyloid pipeline.

Moving to direct CNS pharmacology, a Neuropsychopharmacology paper on GNE-5729, a GluN2A-selective positive allosteric modulator, and what it does to executive function in mice. The motivation here is that NMDA receptor subunit composition matters — GluN2A and GluN2B containing receptors do different things, and a clean GluN2A-selective PAM is one of the things the field has wanted for years as a tool compound and possibly as a cognitive enhancer for schizophrenia, age-related cognitive decline, and other states of NMDA hypofunction. Allosteric modulation is the right mechanism — unlike a direct agonist, an allosteric modulator only enhances responses in the presence of endogenous co-agonists, so synaptic specificity is preserved. They ran GNE-5729 at three doses across two cognitive tasks — Y-maze spontaneous-alternation for working memory, and attentional set-shifting for cognitive flexibility. The headline finding is state-dependent. In working memory, the drug significantly improves alternation in animals with suboptimal baseline performance — the negative correlation between baseline performance and drug effect is the signature of inverted-U cognitive enhancement. It rescues working memory deficits induced by amphetamine and by scopolamine — pharmacological challenges that disturb dopaminergic and cholinergic neuromodulation respectively. It fails to rescue MK-801-induced deficits, which is mechanistically consistent — an allosteric modulator can't restore NMDA function when the channel is directly blocked. Crucially, it does nothing on set-shifting at the tested dose, despite genetic evidence implicating GluN2A in cognitive flexibility — and the three-milligram-per-kilogram dose produced sedative-like effects without cognitive enhancement, so dose-response is real and the therapeutic window is narrow. Females showed greater sensitivity than males, and the authors flag that estrous cycle was not monitored. The translational read is that GluN2A PAMs are best suited to populations with reduced-but-preserved glutamatergic signaling, where upstream neuromodulatory systems are intact — that's age-related cognitive decline and arguably preclinical AD, not the full schizophrenia hypofunction phenotype, and not direct-NMDAR-blockade models. The field has been hunting for a sub-unit-selective NMDA pharmacology that does something more interesting than ketamine — this is the cleanest in-vivo behavioral data on a GluN2A PAM to date, and the state-dependence is the operative constraint.

Sticking with cortical excitability — a TMS plus midazolam study in younger versus older adults. The methodology is paired-pulse TMS of left primary motor cortex — single-pulse to measure corticospinal excitability, paired-pulse to measure short-interval intracortical inhibition, which is GABA-A-mediated. Fifteen younger adults aged eighteen to thirty-five, fifteen older adults aged fifty to sixty-nine, scanned at baseline and during IV midazolam, a GABA-A positive allosteric modulator. The baseline finding is the substantive one. Younger adults show clear paired-pulse inhibition — conditioned-to-unconditioned MEP ratio of point-seven-three, well below one. Older adults show no inhibition — mean ratio above one-point-two-five — meaning baseline GABA-A-mediated intracortical inhibition is already substantially diminished in middle-aged-and-older adults. Midazolam reduces single-pulse MEP amplitudes in both groups, consistent with global GABA-A potentiation. But the paired-pulse pattern changes only in younger adults — midazolam abolishes their inhibition. In older adults, there's no inhibition at baseline and no detectable change under midazolam. The interpretation is that age-related decline in GABA-A-mediated inhibitory tone leaves less inhibitory headroom for benzodiazepines to act on at the level of intracortical microcircuits. It does not mean midazolam stops working in older adults — single-pulse excitability still drops — but the specific short-interval inhibition mechanism is muted. Clinically this matters for benzodiazepine dosing in elderly patients and for any TMS-based biomarker work that pools across ages — paired-pulse inhibition is being increasingly used as a marker of cortical excitability balance in psychiatric and neurological disorders, and these data say that age-stratification of normative data is non-negotiable.

Moving to GPCR pharmacology — a Niv-lab paper on TAS2R14, the bitter taste receptor that is also expressed in many extraoral tissues — airway smooth muscle, adipose, leukocytes, brain — and is activated by a long list of clinically used drugs at micromolar concentrations. The drug-repurposing question here is whether you can deliberately design or select drugs to engage TAS2R14 in extra-oral tissues for therapeutic effect — bronchodilation in asthma is the running hypothesis, but other indications are on the table. The structural twist is that recent cryo-EM work resolved a previously unknown intracellular binding pocket on TAS2R14 — which raised the question of whether existing drug ligands enter through the extracellular vestibule, the intracellular pocket, or both. This paper takes three of the better-characterized pharmaceutical TAS2R14 ligands — tamoxifen, carbimazole, and lidocaine — and dissects their binding modes using BRET2 G-protein recruitment and IP-One downstream signaling assays, paired with targeted receptor mutations across both pockets. The findings are heterogeneous and the heterogeneity is the point. Tamoxifen and carbimazole activate with EC50s in the low-micromolar range, lidocaine needs substantially higher concentrations. Carbimazole shows strong dependence on intracellular-pocket mutations — it's reaching the receptor through the intracellular face. Lidocaine shows the opposite — extracellular dependence. Tamoxifen is mostly mutation-insensitive across both sites in some assays and modestly sensitive in others — likely a mixed mode, or a binding pose that overlaps both pockets. The practical implication is that whether TAS2R14-directed drug repurposing is viable depends on whether you can achieve effective local receptor concentrations through delivery routes that match the relevant binding pocket — inhaled delivery for extracellular-pocket ligands, systemic absorption for intracellular-pocket ones. It's a reminder that the same target can have multiple pharmacologies depending on the chemotype, and structure-based selection has to account for the route of access, not just receptor occupancy.

Now to a satisfying piece of AI-driven GPCR drug design — the RXFP1-relaxin paper. RXFP1 is a multi-domain leucine-rich-repeat GPCR with serious therapeutic potential — relaxin signaling is implicated in cardiac protection, vasodilation, anti-fibrotic effects, and the H2-relaxin peptide hormone has been tested clinically in acute heart failure as serelaxin, with mixed results. The fundamental problem has been that the receptor activation mechanism was not understood at high enough resolution to design selective small-molecule or peptide modulators. This paper uses AlphaFold-2 to generate a comprehensive RXFP1-relaxin complex model with ipTM of point-nine-three and pTM of point-seven-six — high-confidence, and the model gets validated by reproducing decades of mutagenesis and crosslinking data. The mechanistic insight from the model is that relaxin binding traps the inter-domain extracellular linker against the LRR domain, which positions the LDLa module near the transmembrane bundle, and that's the conformational switch that activates the receptor. The functionally critical linker motif is a six-residue stretch — glycine-aspartate-asparagine-asparagine-glycine-tryptophan, positions sixty-three through sixty-eight — sitting centrally at this interface. With the mechanism in hand, they ran RFdiffusion and BindCraft to design two classes of de-novo mini-protein binders. Antagonists target the LRR domain and block relaxin engagement — eight leads, with the best two showing binding affinities of point-four-six to one nanomolar and functional IC50s around five nanomolar. Agonists engage both the LRR domain and the linker region to recapitulate the activation mechanism — four functional activators, with the best showing EC50 of one-point-five nanomolar and full agonism in native-receptor-expressing cells. Both classes are highly RXFP1-selective and inactive on RXFP2. The folds of these mini-proteins are entirely unrelated to relaxin's own structure — they're convergent-function, divergent-architecture solutions. The methodological point is that this is becoming a workable template for orphan GPCRs — high-confidence AlphaFold model, identify the activation switch, design binders that engage it. The therapeutic point is that low-nanomolar RXFP1 agonists, if they have good PK and tissue distribution, are immediately interesting for heart failure, pulmonary fibrosis, and hepatic fibrosis programs.

Pivoting to agentic AI in biology — a bioRxiv paper that's worth a moment because it's one of the cleaner end-to-end demonstrations of what an agentic biological workflow actually looks like. biomeStat is an autonomous agent designed as a strict deterministic orchestrator — meaning the LLM does not directly produce biological outputs, it writes code that drives established bioinformatics tools, runs them in sandboxed compute environments, and dynamically provisions CPU and GPU resources as needed. The hallucination problem in domain biology is real and well-known — biomeStat sidesteps it by never letting the model generate the biological conclusion, only the orchestration plan. The demonstration is a complete genomic-epidemiology study of one thousand Asian dengue virus genomes sampled across sixteen countries between 2000 and 2025. The agent ran phylogenetic reconstruction with IQ-TREE and TreeTime, Bayesian phylodynamics with BEAST2 on an NVIDIA H200 GPU, selection-pressure analysis with HyPhy, and structural mapping with PyMOL — orchestrating five specialized tools end-to-end. The whole analysis completed in under twenty-four hours of wall-clock time. The biological output identified endemic stability with effective reproduction number around one, eighteen-hundred-and-sixty-nine candidate immune-escape sites that structurally colocalize with B-cell and T-cell epitopes, and one-hundred-and-seventy-six highly conserved drug-target residues across the viral replication complex. They specifically confirm that the binding-site residues for the two emerging dengue antivirals — JNJ-1802 and NITD-688 — are absolutely conserved across all four DENV serotypes, which is the kind of go-no-go answer that materially affects clinical-development risk. The take is that this is what working agentic biology looks like when you constrain the agent's outputs to deterministic tool invocations — not a chatbot answering questions about biology, but a researcher-equivalent that compresses weeks of expert orchestration into a single session with full methodological transparency.

Continuing on agentic biology — SP-Mind, an autonomous reasoning agent for spatial proteomics from a group on arXiv this week. The problem domain is real and underserved — spatial proteomics, the imaging-mass-cytometry and multiplexed-immunofluorescence stack, generates single-cell-resolution protein expression maps with tissue architecture preserved, and it's central to tumor-microenvironment work and a growing share of precision-medicine programs. The analysis pipeline is fragmented across cell segmentation, marker quantification, phenotype calling, neighborhood analysis, and downstream statistics — every lab cobbles together a custom workflow, and that's a scalability and reproducibility ceiling. SP-Mind ingests natural-language queries and produces end-to-end workflows from raw multiplexed tissue images through phenotype discovery, equipped with expert-curated biological analysis skills and specialized computational tools. No task-specific fine-tuning. To evaluate it they built SP-Bench — a hundred-and-two tasks across eighteen distinct categories spanning diverse tissue types — and report state-of-the-art performance against existing open-source biomedical-agent baselines. The interesting comparison this draws is against the same trend we covered last week — domain agents catching up with general LLMs on bench-tasks where the bench-task structure is well-defined. SP-Mind plus biomeStat together start to look like a coherent answer to the question of what an end-to-end agentic biology lab looks like — orchestrators that own a specific modality, with curated tool inventories and benchmark coverage.

Now to a piece of clinical agentic AI with an actual efficacy number attached — RaDaR, an open-source thirty-two-billion-parameter reasoning LLM specialized for rare-disease diagnosis. The clinical problem is straightforward — rare diseases collectively affect hundreds of millions of patients, individual diseases are by definition rare, and the typical patient journey is years of subspecialty referrals before a name lands on the condition. The hypothesis is that a reasoning model trained specifically on rare-disease cases and phenotype-anchored narratives could substantially shorten that arc. RaDaR was trained on forty-nine-thousand publicly available cases plus one-hundred-and-five-thousand synthetic cases, and they evaluated it two ways. First, the randomized AI-physician-assistance trial — physicians did diagnostic reasoning over rare-disease cases either with RaDaR assistance or with internet search alone. Result — RaDaR-assisted physicians showed a twenty-one-point-four-four-percentage-point absolute improvement in diagnostic accuracy over internet search. That's a clinically meaningful effect size, not a marginal one. Second, retrospective replay over historical cases — RaDaR prioritized the final diagnosis ahead of documented clinical suspicion in sixty-one percent of cases, with an estimated potential lead time of one-point-eight-seven months. They also benchmark against larger frontier models — RaDaR is the strongest open-source rare-disease model in the comparison, outperforming the six-hundred-and-seventy-one-billion-parameter DeepSeek-R1, which is the kind of result that says domain-specialization beats raw scale on this task class. The caveats are real — synthetic data ablations show monotonic scaling within the tested range, which suggests a data ceiling somewhere, and AI-assistance trials of this kind have a known blind spot around effect-modification by physician experience. Even with those, the +21-point delta over internet search is the strongest clinical-trial-grade endorsement of specialized reasoning models for diagnostic support that's been published this year.

We close on a single-cell foundation model story — CellOS, a twelve-billion-parameter multi-view single-cell foundation model from a Beijing biotech. The framing is a direct response to last week's VCBench paper that showed linear and nearest-neighbor baselines beating most existing foundation models on four of five testable virtual-cell capability axes. CellOS argues that the problem isn't with the foundation-model premise but with the training objective — most existing single-cell models learn from a single view of gene expression and optimize through reconstruction or next-token prediction, which captures expression abundance but doesn't reconcile complementary perspectives on cellular state. CellOS introduces a multi-view objective — paired expression and perception views — and a three-stage training procedure. First stage is causal cell-sentence language modeling, similar to scGPT. Second is function-preserving dense-to-mixture-of-experts expansion, which lets them scale parameter count without proportional compute. Third is latent-space alignment via an LLM-JEPA objective, which is the joint-embedding-predictive-architecture move from the LeCun-style world-model line of work. Three-hundred-ninety-point-five million single-cell transcriptomes in training. Across cell-state annotation, batch integration, and perturbation-response prediction, CellOS reportedly beats state-of-the-art single-cell foundation models — which, given the VCBench result, is a claim that needs to be re-evaluated against the same linear and nearest-neighbor baselines, not just against other foundation models. The authors are affiliated with Vitaura and a patent is pending — so the usual industry-paper caveats on reproducibility and code availability apply until the model card and weights drop. The methodological contribution to track here is the JEPA-style latent alignment for biology — it's a more principled training objective than masked-language-modeling-on-genes, and if it holds up against the VCBench baselines, it's the first plausible answer to the question of how to train these models so that scale actually buys something.
