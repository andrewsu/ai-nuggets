# Receptor & Reason — 2026-06-29

## Script

Good morning. It's Monday, June 29th, 2026 — Receptor and Reason. Ten items today. We open on a Mayo Clinic transcriptomic dissection of TDP-43 subtypes in Alzheimer's, work through ALS organoid biology, a serotonin-channel arousal pathway, two Parkinson's circuit-mechanism papers, an epilepsy synaptic-mitochondria story, and a prenatal-circadian behavioral phenotype, then close on three agentic-AI and computational-pharmacology pieces — an evaluation of AI research frameworks, agentic interfaces for bioinformatics tools, and a desktop-scale virtual screen against alpha-synuclein.

Leading off — TDP-43 subtypes shape transcriptomic signatures in Alzheimer's disease, from the Petrucelli and Prudencio groups at Mayo with Keith Josephs. The clinical problem: TDP-43 pathology shows up in roughly half of advanced AD cases and tracks with worse cognitive decline, but it always co-occurs with tau tangles and amyloid plaques, which makes it hard to know whether TDP-43 is amplifying tau-driven neurodegeneration or running its own program. The methodological move is to use the natural variation in mixed proteinopathies as a quasi-experiment. They generated regionally resolved transcriptomic profiles from cognitively normal controls, pure AD, AD with limbic-predominant age-related TDP-43 encephalopathy (the AD-LATE phenotype), and frontotemporal lobar degeneration with TDP-43. Each case was further stratified by TDP-43 morphological subtype — types alpha and beta for AD-LATE, types A and B for FTLD — and quantitative phospho-TDP-43 and phospho-tau measures were used as continuous covariates to decouple pathology-specific signals. The headline result has two parts. First, TDP-43 in AD-LATE drives transcriptomic programs that are largely uncoupled from tau burden, *and* those programs are not the same as the ones seen in FTLD with TDP-43. So TDP-43 is not a uniform proteinopathy with one downstream signature; the AD-LATE version is its own thing. Second, the amygdala is the regional convergence point — TDP-43-associated remodeling occurs there in both diseases — while frontal cortex remodeling is largely restricted to FTLD. And when they stratify by morphological subtype within each disease, they unmask biological trajectories — from immune activation to specific cellular vulnerabilities — that completely disappear when you lump subtypes together. The take-home for AD drug discovery is sobering: any program that's stratifying by amyloid and tau alone is collapsing biologically distinct patient subsets. TDP-43 morphology has been a neuropathologist's category, but this paper makes a strong case it should be a stratification variable for trial enrichment, especially for therapies targeting limbic-onset cognitive decline.

Second — and threading directly off the TDP-43 thread, an ALS-associated TARDBP mutation drives cryptic exon inclusion and RNA dysregulation. The team introduced the K181E mutation, an ALS-FTD-associated missense in TDP-43, into human induced pluripotent stem cells and differentiated them into three-dimensional forebrain organoids. The clean experimental setup: no TDP-43 overexpression, no exogenous stress. The mutant organoids spontaneously develop the full pathology — TDP-43 hyperphosphorylation, cytoplasmic accumulation of phospho-TDP-43, RNA dysregulation, pro-inflammatory signaling, and activation of apoptotic pathways. Single-cell transcriptomics combined with enhanced cross-linking immunoprecipitation shows that K181E alters TDP-43's RNA-binding specificity. This matters because the dominant model has been loss-of-function from aggregation-driven nuclear depletion; what the binding-specificity result suggests is that the mutant protein is actively mis-targeting transcripts before any aggregation manifests. The downstream consequence is widespread mis-splicing, including the inclusion of cryptic exons that wouldn't normally be present in mature mRNA. The substantive new finding is in the transcription factor PRDM2 — they identify cryptic-exon-derived PRDM2 peptide immunoreactivity in ALS spinal motor neurons, co-localized with phospho-TDP-43 pathology. So you have a candidate downstream event linking TDP-43 dysfunction to motor neuron death that's directly checkable in patient tissue. PRDM2 cryptic peptides as ALS biomarkers, and PRDM2-pathway interventions as therapeutic targets, both become tractable.

Third — a CO2 sensor in the dorsal raphe-to-VTA pathway mediating hypercapnic arousal. The substrate biology here is genuinely beautiful. Connexin 26, Cx26, is a gap-junction hemichannel that opens in response to elevated PCO2 — that part has been known. What this paper establishes is that Cx26 is expressed specifically on serotonergic terminals projecting from the dorsal raphe to the ventral tegmental area, that brief PCO2 elevations modulate VTA dopamine neuron excitability through serotonergic signaling without touching glutamatergic transmission, and that pharmacological hemichannel inhibition abolishes the effect. The mechanism gets cleaner. By co-expressing Cx26 with the GRAB-5HT serotonin sensor in cultured cells, they show that CO2-opened Cx26 hemichannels are *directly permeable* to serotonin — the channel itself is releasing the neurotransmitter, what they call a channel co-synapse. And selectively removing CO2 sensitivity from Cx26 in dorsal raphe serotonergic neurons in vivo delays hypercapnic arousal from sleep and reduces VTA dopamine activation. So the pathway is: rising CO2 opens 5-HT-permeable Cx26 channels on raphe-VTA terminals, releasing serotonin onto VTA dopamine neurons, which drive arousal. For a CNS pharmacology audience, the implications run in a few directions. Sudden unexpected death in epilepsy involves failed arousal during postictal hypercapnia, and this is a defined molecular target. Sleep apnea pharmacology — orexin agonists, serotonergic compounds — now has a specific channel-level handle. And the channel co-synapse mode itself is a non-canonical release mechanism worth thinking about for any pharmacology that wants to selectively modulate serotonergic output without touching vesicular release.

Fourth — Parkinson's circuit pathology from synaptojanin-1 mutation. Synaptojanin-1 is a brain-enriched phosphoinositide phosphatase that clips PI(4,5)P2 and is central to clathrin-mediated endocytosis at the synapse. The R258Q mutation selectively impairs its Sac1 phosphatase domain and causes early-onset familial parkinsonism. Mutant mice show synaptic vesicle trafficking defects across the brain, but the dystrophic phenotype is selective — only a subset of dopaminergic axons in the dorsolateral striatum show overt structural pathology. Correlative light microscopy with FIB-SEM reconstructs the lesions in three dimensions: focal axonal dilations harboring massive onion-like infoldings of the plasma membrane, enriched for the dopamine transporter, often localized adjacent to neighboring cell bodies and sometimes engulfing evaginations of those neighboring cells. Dopamine release is deficient in the same striatal region where the dystrophic axons are. The interpretation is that Synj1 mutation tilts the balance between exocytosis and endocytosis — the plasma membrane that should be retrieved by endocytosis accumulates instead, and only a vulnerable subset of dopaminergic axons crosses the threshold where compensatory mechanisms fail. The piece that's worth knowing about for pharmacology is that this is a cell-biological lesion, not a transcriptional or aggregation-driven one, and it argues that for at least some familial Parkinson's, the disease-modifying target sits in the endocytic-recycling machinery rather than downstream of alpha-synuclein. Whether that machinery is druggable in adult neurons is a separate, harder question.

Fifth — staying on synaptic biology with a different therapeutic translation, mitochondrial turnover at central GABAergic synapses governs vulnerability to epileptic seizures. The team profiles the mitochondrial proteome and turnover dynamics across hippocampal glutamatergic and GABAergic neurons and finds that experience differentially remodels mitochondrial populations, with distal axon terminals of somatostatin-positive GABAergic interneurons showing the most dramatic turnover changes — a kind of metabolic plasticity coupled to circuit activity. The mechanistic test is to conditionally ablate either MIRO1 or TRAK1 — two mitochondrial transport proteins whose human mutations cause congenital epilepsy. Knocking either out stalls mitochondrial turnover at axon terminals, drives loss of mitochondrial cristae, and reduces synaptic GABA without affecting synapse number or neuron viability. The downstream consequences are exactly what you'd predict from selective GABAergic dysfunction — destabilized network oscillations, hyperexcitability, recurrent seizures, and premature death. And the rescue experiment closes the loop: post-weaning gene therapy reverses the mitochondrial alterations and ameliorates the epileptic phenotype, meaning the metabolic lesion is reversible after the developmental window. The implication for genetic epilepsies linked to mitochondrial dynamics genes is direct — there's a defined drug-target axis here that targets neither GABA receptors nor sodium channels. Whether small-molecule modulators of MIRO1-TRAK1-mediated transport can deliver the same rescue as gene therapy is the next chemistry question.

Sixth — prenatal circadian rhythm disruption induces sex-specific substance use and mood-related phenotypes in mice. The setup is a light-cycle reversal during gestation in C57BL/6J dams — a reasonable proxy for human shift work, which affects roughly twenty percent of working-age Americans, with women disproportionately exposed during reproductive years. Adult offspring underwent reward-related testing — cocaine conditioned place preference, intravenous self-administration — and mood-related batteries including elevated plus maze and forced swim. The sex divergence is striking. Female offspring of disrupted dams developed an anhedonic phenotype: reduced food self-administration, reduced cocaine intake, reduced cocaine reinforcement, increased anxiety-like behavior. Male offspring developed essentially the opposite: increased cocaine preference, increased reinforcement, *decreased* anxiety, increased risk-taking, decreased immobility in forced swim. The mechanistic correlate is sex-divergent corticosterone rhythm disruption. The framing the authors offer is that prenatal circadian disruption predisposes individuals toward distinct psychiatric disorder classes depending on sex — mood disorders in females, substance use disorders in males — which fits the epidemiology in interesting ways. For the field, it's a strong argument that pregnancy-window circadian counseling and chronotherapeutic interventions for shift-working pregnant women deserve a serious clinical translation pass. As a developmental psychiatry mechanism, sex-divergent prenatal programming through HPA-axis rhythm disruption is a cleaner hypothesis than the standard maternal-stress-equals-bad story.

Seventh — Pavlovian appetitive memory reconsolidation interrupted by propranolol and MK-801. The clinical premise is that reactivating a learned cue-reward association briefly makes the memory destabilized — open to disruption by drugs that block reconsolidation. Propranolol, the beta-adrenergic blocker, has been the workhorse here for fear memory in PTSD, but for appetitive cue memories — the kind that drive cue-induced relapse in addiction — the evidence has been inconsistent. The team used a discriminative goal-tracking paradigm in Lister hooded rats, comparing propranolol against MK-801, the gold-standard NMDA antagonist for reconsolidation studies. Both drugs were given thirty minutes before a brief memory reminder, with goal-tracking tested drug-free at one and eight days. MK-801 at 0.1 mg/kg impaired subsequent discrimination — both with non-reinforced and reinforced reminders, which extends prior literature. Propranolol at 10 mg/kg also impaired goal-tracking regardless of reminder type and consistently across both sexes. The substantive finding is that propranolol can disrupt appetitive memory reconsolidation in a paradigm where it has been claimed not to work, when conditions favor memory destabilization. The translational stakes are real — there's an active line of clinical research using cue-exposure-plus-propranolol for substance use cravings, and consistent rodent data has been a sticking point. This paper restores the rodent foundation for that program.

Eighth — turning to agentic AI. A paper that anyone using AI research frameworks should read: Real Science Is Harder Than Benchmarks. The team — Sinitskiy and collaborators — evaluated five advanced AI research frameworks: Kosmos, K-Dense, ToolUniverse, BioAgents from bio.xyz, and the AI Scientist version 2 from Sakana AI. They picked three real-life tasks spanning uncertainty quantification for molecular property predictions, machine learning on the Therapeutic Data Commons benchmarks, and agent-based modeling — including two recently published papers used as the gold standard for what a competent group can produce. The framing matters: this is not a leaderboard, it's a reproducibility test. The strengths the frameworks demonstrated are real — generating original hypotheses, executing routine data acquisition and coding, producing statistical confidence measures that the original papers often omitted, and assembling well-formatted reports. The failures are also real, and they're the ones the benchmark culture has been hiding. No framework matched the scope or depth of the original studies. Results varied across multiple runs of the same framework with the same prompt — irreproducibility within a single system. They documented severe hallucinations in final reports, literature-coverage gaps, and overconfident conclusions. And verification of any AI output required substantial domain expertise — meaning the frameworks aren't replacing the scientist, they're requiring one to audit each run. The honest take from the authors is that these tools are valuable for prototyping directions and stress-testing completed studies, but they are not yet a substitute for a competent group doing the actual work. The infrastructure limitations look largely tractable; the irreproducibility and hallucination problems are harder. For computational pharmacology groups deciding how much to lean on AI scientists, this is the closest thing to an honest evaluation we have.

Ninth — and pairing well with the framework-evaluation paper, client-server interfaces enable efficient agent-driven variant calling. The premise is that bioinformatics tools were built for human experts driving them from a shell, but LLM agents now have to drive those same tools from the documentation written for humans — spending many turns, tokens, and tool calls just to figure out installation, configuration, and execution. The Hong Kong team re-architected Clair3, the widely used deep-learning long-read variant caller, into a client-server system they call Clair3-Connect. The client handles all the genomics processing locally and holds the identifiable data; the server runs only neural-network inference and receives only feature tensors. The agent invokes the system through single structured calls against a schema-defined interface — no shell wrangling. The numbers are striking. On an APOE diplotyping task, all sixty agent runs were correct. The agentic interface used twelve thousand tokens in three turns versus eighty-one thousand to one hundred sixty-three thousand for shell-driven baselines — six-point-eight to fourteen times fewer tokens — at about a quarter of the wall-clock time, and with far more stable token usage variation, four percent versus thirty-five percent. Dropping the pileup and phasing stages to keep the client light cost only zero-point-one to zero-point-three F1 points at fifty-x coverage. Mutual TLS plus AES-256-GCM encryption added seven percent overhead. The general claim — agentic interfaces should be a first-class deliverable of bioinformatics tool development, not a third-party wrapper — is the right one. Tool developers know defaults and conventions that wrappers can't recover. For comp-pharm groups building LLM-agent workflows, the lesson is to insist on developer-built agentic interfaces for the tools you depend on, and to be wary of wrapper-driven pipelines that the agent has to figure out from documentation.

Tenth and last — closing on a comp-pharm screening paper with a real wet-lab validation, desktop-scale hit-point discovery for intrinsically disordered alpha-synuclein. The target biology is hard: alpha-synuclein is intrinsically disordered, and the non-amyloid component spanning residues sixty-one to ninety-five drives Parkinson-associated aggregation. Most AI drug discovery responds to the chemical-space problem by docking a small sublibrary and ranking, which inherits all the biases of the sublibrary. The ISTP Tech team built an engine integrating what they call the Local Information Criticality Principle with a Discrete Phase-Interference Search Operator. The compression chain: from an eight-hundred-forty-six-million-molecule mirror down to a ten-million-molecule active set — about an eighty-five-fold reduction — and then through scoring to a short, safety-gated list of commercially available candidates plus de novo designs. The whole campaign ran on a single desktop workstation. The validation is the part that matters: three engine-prioritized commercially available compounds — 2-D08, uralenol, and herbacetin, all natural-product-adjacent polyphenolic scaffolds — were tested against alpha-synuclein aggregation in a thioflavin-T assay at one hundred micromolar, with epigallocatechin gallate as the positive control. All three nominated compounds suppressed aggregation. Two produced at least eighty percent plateau reduction. That's three-out-of-three prospective inhibitor-call concordance — not retrospective overfitting, prospective. The micromolar potency is what you'd expect from a screening hit, not a clinical candidate, and the natural-product chemistry means selectivity and CNS penetration are both open questions. But as a proof-of-concept that a state-space-compressed virtual screen against a famously hard intrinsically disordered target can deliver experimentally validated hits without an HPC cluster, this is a useful counterweight to the narrative that you need a server farm and a foundation model to do early-stage discovery.

That's ten. The piece I'd come back to first is the TDP-43 subtypes paper — if it holds up in independent cohorts, the implication for AD trial design is that any program with a limbic-onset arm needs TDP-43 subtype stratification, not just biomarker quartiles. The Cx26 channel-co-synapse for serotonergic arousal is the most molecularly satisfying single result of the day, and the Real Science Is Harder Than Benchmarks evaluation is the agentic-AI reality check that anyone running an AI research workflow should read end to end. Have a good Monday.
