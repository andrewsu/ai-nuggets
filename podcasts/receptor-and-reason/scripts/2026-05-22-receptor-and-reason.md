## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. Ten items today, anchored on a remarkable nonhuman-primate paper that simultaneously samples five neurochemicals in cortex and striatum during cognition, then branching out through a tight cluster of mechanism work — a psychedelic-mechanism in-vitro recording of cortical hyperexcitability under DOI, an astrocyte G-protein-coupled-receptor paper that ties Gi signaling in temporal cortex to fear-memory retrieval, an astrocytic acyl-coenzyme-A synthetase knockout that reduces alpha-synuclein pathology in a Parkinson's mouse, and an oligodendrocyte-precursor paper that nominates estrogen-receptor-beta agonism as a tractable therapeutic for Angelman syndrome. Then a schizophrenia mitochondrial-complex-one modulator, two addiction items — a glucagon-like-peptide-1 receptor agonist for opioid use disorder and a hippocampal-engram-reactivation circuit-therapy paper — a zuranolone repurposing study for delirium, and a closer that I find particularly clean — a de-novo-designed cyclic peptide melanocortin-4-receptor agonist with in-vivo proof of concept in mice. Let's go.

Lead item, the freshest paper in today's bioRxiv crop and easily the most ambitious — cognitive engagement induces area-specific fingerprints of dopamine, acetylcholine, serotonin, glutamate, and gamma-amino-butyric-acid in prefrontal cortex and striatum, from a Womelsdorf and Pawliszyn collaboration at the University of Waterloo. The methodological core is a miniaturized solid-phase microextraction probe — anchor the technique in one sentence — a diffusion-based sampling fiber that extracts the unbound neurochemical fraction from extracellular fluid for offline mass-spectrometry quantification, sampling five neurotransmitters concurrently from the same anatomical site. Until now, almost everything we know about how dopamine, acetylcholine, and serotonin coordinate during cognition has been inferred from studies that probe one or two neuromodulators at a time, with the others reconstructed from separate experiments and separate animals. The Waterloo group did it in nonhuman primates, in the same animal, during a cognitive set-shifting task — switching from a rest condition into active engagement on a flexible-rule task — with simultaneous sampling in prefrontal cortex and dorsolateral striatum. The pattern is genuinely area-specific. In prefrontal cortex, the transition into cognitive engagement is best predicted by changes in gamma-amino-butyric-acid and acetylcholine. In striatum, the predictive signals shift to dopamine and acetylcholine. Glutamate co-modulates with acetylcholine across states in both structures, suggesting a coordinated excitatory-cholinergic axis. Serotonin moves in parallel in cortex and striatum, consistent with a common external modulator — likely the raphe driving both targets in synchrony — rather than each region setting its serotonin tone independently. The pharmacological read-through is the one to sit with. Drugs that engage a single neuromodulatory system — a serotonin reuptake inhibitor, a muscarinic positive allosteric modulator, a dopamine partial agonist — are being applied to a network whose state is jointly defined by five neurochemicals at once, with area-specific dominance. The clinical signature of those drugs, and the variability across patients, almost certainly reflects how the perturbation interacts with the rest of the fingerprint in that specific brain region. The translational handle is that a fingerprint-aware view of state — rather than a one-neurochemical-at-a-time view — becomes a more honest framework for both pharmacology and biomarker design. The next experiments are the obvious ones — replicate in disease-model primates, test pharmacological perturbations one neuromodulator at a time and watch how the other four respond.

Item two, a serotonergic-psychedelic-mechanism paper from Ido Haber and collaborators at the University of Wisconsin Madison — acute DOI exposure drives cortical hyperexcitability and functional network remodeling. DOI is the canonical experimental serotonin-two-A agonist, used as a tool compound across the psychedelic-mechanism literature when investigators want a selective 5-HT2A engagement without the pharmacokinetic profile of psilocybin or LSD. The Wisconsin group recorded dissociated primary rat cortical cultures on spatially defined microelectrode arrays, in a within-culture repeated-measures design — before-and-after on the same network — with a separate arm where the 5-HT2A antagonist ketanserin was pre-applied to test receptor specificity. After acute DOI, mean firing rate rose across every culture, bursts accelerated, and functional connectivity — quantified with the rate-corrected spike-time tiling coefficient, which corrects out the artifact you'd otherwise get from firing-rate-driven connectivity inflation — shifted toward shorter characteristic path lengths. Translated into circuit language — the network reorganizes into a state where signals can traverse from one neuron to another with fewer intermediate hops. Ketanserin pre-application blocked the population-bursting and inter-burst-interval effects, confirming the 5-HT2A dependence, but interestingly, the path-length shortening persisted under ketanserin, suggesting that part of the network-topology shift may not require classical receptor occupancy. Two things are useful here. First, the cortical hyperexcitability under acute psychedelic exposure has been hypothesized from in-vivo electrophysiology but is hard to pin to a clean network-topology readout. The microelectrode-array platform gives you that — a tractable, reduced preparation where the molecular and the network levels are directly addressable in the same experiment. Second, the rate-corrected connectivity metric is the methodological move worth borrowing. A lot of network-rewiring claims in the psychedelic-mechanism literature come from connectivity estimators that aren't firing-rate-corrected — which means a global excitability increase can manufacture a fake connectivity shift. The Wisconsin pipeline shows the topology effect surviving the correction, which makes the rewiring claim much stronger.

Item three, astrocytes again, this time as causal modulators of fear-memory retrieval — temporal cortex astrocytic G-i-coupled GPCR signaling regulates learned threat responses, from the Melzer group at the Medical University of Vienna. The conceptual frame the field has been converging on — astrocytes are not passive substrates for neuronal computation; they actively shape memory and behavior through their own G-protein-coupled-receptor signaling, and the pathway repertoire — Gs versus Gq versus Gi — matters for which behaviors get modulated. The Vienna group used chemogenetic designer-receptors to selectively activate one G-protein pathway at a time in temporal-cortex astrocytes, paired with in-vivo fiber photometry of astrocyte calcium dynamics during auditory fear conditioning and retrieval. The headline result is pathway-specific — Gi activation in temporal-cortex astrocytes enhanced fear-memory retrieval, while Gs and Gq activation did not. The mechanism is consistent with the photometry — temporal-cortex astrocytes normally show robust calcium transients to both conditioned auditory cues and aversive stimuli, and Gi activation attenuated the cue-evoked calcium transients during retrieval. Read that the way the field is starting to read these — astrocytes are not just amplifying the neuronal signal, they're gating it, and the Gi pathway is the gate. Two pharmacological implications. First, the cortical-astrocyte Gi-coupled receptor repertoire is broader than recent surveys had suggested, so the druggable surface is real. Second, the receptor class that includes the most prominent Gi-coupled neuromodulator receptors — alpha-two-adrenergic, cannabinoid CB1, kappa-opioid, several serotonin subtypes — has historically been thought of as neuronal. The implication that some of those drugs may be operating substantially through astrocyte Gi signaling is one the field will have to take seriously. Worth keeping the temporal-cortex-astrocyte readout in mind when interpreting fear-conditioning pharmacology going forward.

Item four, a Parkinson's-disease mechanism paper from the Zoghbi lab at Baylor College of Medicine that lands cleanly in the astrocyte-as-target conversation — astrocytic depletion of the acyl-coenzyme-A synthetase ACSBG1 improves lipid-cytokine signaling and attenuates alpha-synuclein pathology in a Parkinson's mouse model. Background. The Zoghbi group's prior genome-wide screens identified ACSBG1 — an astrocyte-enriched long-chain-fatty-acid activation enzyme — as a candidate regulator of alpha-synuclein levels, but the mechanism linking lipid metabolism to neuroinflammation and synuclein pathology was unclear. This paper closes the loop. In cytokine-stimulated primary astrocytes — the in-vitro proxy for reactive astrocyte states — ACSBG1 knockout astrocytes mount an attenuated inflammatory transcriptional response and secrete fewer inflammatory mediators including interleukin-six, RANTES, and macrophage-inflammatory-protein-three. They also secrete less of one particular long-chain sphingosine species, sphingosine 20:1, and the lipid arm matters for the downstream neuronal phenotype — exogenous sphingosine 20:1, or conditioned medium from wild-type reactive astrocytes, induces phosphorylation of neuronal alpha-synuclein at the disease-relevant serine-129 site. In vivo, crossing the ACSBG1 knockout into a Thy1-driven alpha-synuclein overexpression Parkinson's model rescued behavior, reduced astrogliosis, and reduced both total and phospho-alpha-synuclein in brain. The translational angle is the right one to flag. Targeting astrocyte lipid metabolism — rather than the alpha-synuclein protein directly, or the dopaminergic neuron, or the neuroinflammatory cytokine output — is a relatively unexplored therapeutic surface in Parkinson's. ACSBG1 is enzymatically tractable, expressed in the right cell type at the right anatomical loci, and the knockout phenotype is genetically clean. The next experiments are the obvious ones — pharmacological inhibition rather than genetic knockout, and a model that engages dopaminergic-neuron loss more aggressively than the Thy1-synuclein model does.

Item five, oligodendrocyte biology as a therapeutic surface for Angelman syndrome — targeting UBE3A and downstream estrogen-receptor-beta signaling to restore oligodendroglial homeostasis in Angelman syndrome, from the Huang lab at Brown University. Angelman syndrome is caused by loss-of-function mutations in UBE3A, an E3 ubiquitin ligase that's been studied almost exclusively in neurons. The Brown group asks the question the field has been avoiding — UBE3A is also expressed in oligodendrocyte lineage cells, so what happens when it's lost there. Using human induced-pluripotent-stem-cell-derived oligodendrocyte precursor cells, they ran a targeted compound screen and identified that UBE3A loss collapses estrogen-receptor-beta signaling, which in turn impairs oligodendrocyte-precursor self-renewal. Mechanistically, UBE3A sustains estrogen-receptor-beta protein levels in oligodendrocyte precursors. In Angelman-deficient cells, the receptor falls, the proliferation program fails, and oligodendrocyte-precursor density doesn't recover. The therapeutic test is the cleanest part — selective estrogen-receptor-beta agonists restored oligodendrocyte-precursor self-renewal and myelination deficits in Angelman patient-derived cell systems, and rescued behavioral abnormalities in Angelman mouse models. Two things to take from this. First, the cell-type-specific perspective on a neurodevelopmental disorder pays off — the neuron-only view of Angelman missed an oligodendrocyte-precursor pathway that's directly druggable. Second, selective estrogen-receptor-beta agonists are an existing pharmacological class — the prototype compound diarylpropionitrile and several investigational tool compounds have been in development for other indications, including anxiety and cognitive deficits. Repositioning into Angelman is plausible if the rescue holds up in vivo at clinically relevant doses. Watch this space.

Item six, a small-molecule schizophrenia paper that takes the mitochondrial-dysfunction story seriously enough to treat it as a target — a small-molecule mitochondrial-complex-one modulator improves behavioral and mitochondrial dysfunction in schizophrenia, from the Rudolph group at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign. Conceptual frame — mitochondrial complex one is the entry point of the electron transport chain, and weak partial inhibition of complex one has been shown in cell and animal models of Alzheimer's and Huntington's to be paradoxically neuroprotective by upregulating mitochondrial biogenesis through PGC-one-alpha. The schizophrenia literature has steadily accumulated evidence for mitochondrial dysfunction in patient tissue and in mouse models, but the field hadn't seriously tested whether a complex-one modulator could move the behavioral phenotype. The compound is a small-molecule tricyclic pyrone they call CP2 — anchor it as a moderate-affinity complex-one binder that produces weak inhibition rather than full blockade. The model is a glycine-decarboxylase-gene-copy-number-variant mouse, an established schizophrenia model where elevated GLDC drives a glycine-deficiency phenotype with behavioral and metabolic correlates of the human disease. Eight weeks of CP2 treatment reversed spontaneous-alternation deficits in the Y maze, startle-habituation deficits, and social-novelty deficits in the three-chamber social interaction test — three independent behavioral domains that all read out for distinct aspects of the schizophrenia-like phenotype. Mechanistically, the treatment restored PGC-one-alpha and the mitochondrial voltage-dependent anion channel VDAC1, both consistent with rescued mitochondrial biogenesis. The translational implication is that mitochondrial complex-one modulation moves into the schizophrenia therapeutic conversation as a candidate small-molecule class with the right cross-indication footprint — already supported in Alzheimer's and Huntington's models, and now in schizophrenia. The next steps the field needs are the obvious ones — does the effect generalize beyond the GLDC model to dopaminergic-circuit hyperactivity models, and does the partial-inhibition signature hold up at chronic dosing.

Item seven, a fresh preclinical data point in the increasingly substantial glucagon-like-peptide-1-for-substance-use story — semaglutide attenuates intravenous self-administration of fentanyl in female rats, from Jacques Nguyen's group at Baylor University. The piece I covered yesterday — the Psychiatric Times critical review of GLP-1s for alcohol use disorder — laid out the human side of this conversation. Today's paper closes the preclinical loop on the opioid side. Female Wistar rats with implanted jugular catheters were trained to self-administer fentanyl on a fixed-ratio-one schedule under either short-access or long-access conditions, then dosed with semaglutide at zero-point-one milligrams per kilogram subcutaneously before subsequent self-administration sessions. Semaglutide dose-dependently reduced fentanyl rewards under both short-access and long-access conditions. On a progressive-ratio schedule — the assay that quantifies motivation by escalating the work required for each reward — semaglutide significantly reduced breakpoint, meaning the animals worked less hard for fentanyl. And in the short-access cohort, semaglutide-treated animals showed less withdrawal-like behavior after drug discontinuation, though the same protection didn't extend to the long-access cohort, which is the group with more entrenched compulsive use. The translational read-through fits the broader picture. GLP-1 receptor agonism appears to be modulating reward motivation through the mesolimbic system across multiple substance classes — alcohol, nicotine, psychostimulants, now opioids — without the aversive profile of older anti-craving agents. The female-only design here is the genuinely useful contribution. Opioid-self-administration pharmacology has historically been done in males, and the population that needs OUD pharmacotherapy is roughly half female with documented sex differences in opioid reinforcement. A clean female-rat dataset on a clinically-available GLP-1 is exactly the right preclinical evidence to support the human trials that are now starting. The long-access protection gap is the bit to watch — if semaglutide loses efficacy in the compulsive-use phenotype, that limits the addressable patient population to earlier-stage OUD.

Item eight, an addiction circuit-therapy paper that has more conceptual upside than its small footprint suggests — artificial reactivation of a cocaine-associated engram in the dorsal dentate gyrus attenuates cocaine-prime-induced reinstatement of drug-seeking, from Stephanie Grella's group at Loyola University Chicago. The TetTag approach — briefly, a transgenic tool that lets investigators tag the specific population of neurons active during a defined experience with an optogenetic actuator, so the same population can be artificially reactivated later. The Grella group tagged dorsal-dentate-gyrus neurons active during either the first or the fourth cocaine exposure, then ran the animals through a conditioned-place-preference, extinction, and reinstatement sequence. Two findings carry pharmacological implications. First, reactivation of the engram tagged during the first cocaine exposure attenuated cocaine-induced reinstatement, while reactivation of the engram tagged during the fourth exposure did not. The early engram is protective; the later engram is not. The interpretation the authors favor is that the early-exposure ensemble encodes a recoverable contextual memory that, when reactivated, interferes with the subsequent retrieval of the reinstatement-driving association. Second, reactivation of a saline-associated engram reduced cocaine reinstatement in females but not males — suggesting a sex-specific interference or neuromodulatory channel that the protocol isn't yet resolving. The therapeutic relevance, even at this stage, is real. The cue-induced relapse problem has been the dominant clinical failure mode in cocaine pharmacotherapy — patients abstain during structured care, then relapse on contextual or interoceptive triggers. The implication that early-phase memories carry latent protection against later cue-driven relapse argues for therapies that selectively reactivate or stabilize those earlier representations — pharmacologically through reconsolidation-blocker or memory-modulator strategies, or behaviorally through cue-exposure interventions that engage early-phase associations. Worth tracking as the engram-pharmacology field continues to mature.

Item nine, a repurposing study with a clear regulatory path — zuranolone mitigates delirium-like bispectral-electroencephalography changes, behavioral deficits, and neuroinflammation across surgical and inflammatory mouse models, from Gen Shinozaki's group at Stanford. Zuranolone — briefly anchor it — is the orally bioavailable neuroactive steroid positive allosteric modulator of synaptic and extrasynaptic gamma-amino-butyric-acid-A receptors, approved by the FDA in 2023 for postpartum depression. The Stanford team tested whether zuranolone could also be deployed against delirium — the acute fluctuating brain-dysfunction syndrome that follows surgery and systemic inflammation, that disproportionately affects older adults, and that has essentially no validated pharmacological intervention. Two complementary mouse models — a lipopolysaccharide systemic-inflammation model and a postoperative-delirium model, each run in young, aged, and super-aged animals. The continuous EEG readout — a bispectral-EEG metric the group has validated for translation from rodent to clinical use — captured delirium-like slowing. Zuranolone dose-dependently attenuated the LPS-induced EEG slowing in young mice and retained efficacy in aged mice. In the postoperative-delirium model, the prophylactic-versus-post-surgery dosing distinction matters — prophylactic dosing was modest in young animals, but post-surgery treatment reduced EEG abnormalities, and in aged mice prophylactic dosing suppressed the postoperative pattern. In super-aged mice, prophylactic zuranolone improved survival after postoperative-delirium induction. Mechanistically, zuranolone reduced microglial density and activation markers — Iba-one and CD68 immunoreactivity — at twenty-four hours after the inflammatory insult, with effects in the peri-incisional tissue in young animals and broader cortical distribution in aged animals. Two things to take from this. First, the GABA-A positive allosteric modulator class has an unexpected neuroimmune signature — zuranolone is dampening microglial activation, not just neuronal excitability. Second, an already-approved drug with established safety and dosing data is among the most efficient regulatory paths into a delirium indication. The trial design is the question — bispectral-EEG as a continuous outcome measure in postoperative populations is feasible, and the perioperative-prophylaxis-versus-acute-rescue distinction the mouse data flag will need to be settled in humans.

Closing item, a computational-pharmacology highlight that fits Receptor and Reason's design-side mandate — de-novo-designed cyclic melanocortin-4-receptor peptide agonist reduces food intake in mice, from a Gubra-led team coordinating across multiple Danish and European sites. Melanocortin-4 receptor — briefly, the central nervous system regulator of appetite and energy balance, and the target of setmelanotide, the approved drug for monogenic obesity caused by leptin-melanocortin-pathway mutations. The Gubra group ran what they call an end-to-end de-novo-peptide-agonist workflow. Starting point — an AlphaFold-two-based hallucination protocol implemented in the open-source ColabDesign framework, run to generate more than five thousand linear and head-to-tail cyclic candidate peptides directed at the melanocortin-four orthosteric pocket. Functional screening of a prioritized subset hit a remarkable fraction of measurably active designs — seventy-four percent of linear peptides and twenty-three percent of cyclic peptides showed measurable activity. From that, they identified a cyclic agonist with a half-maximal effective concentration of three-hundred-and-forty nanomolar — modest, but real — despite the peptide lacking the canonical melanocortin activation motif His-Phe-Arg-Trp. The interesting bit is what came next. Systematic in-vitro maturation by deep mutational scanning identified an alternative activation motif centered on the four-residue sequence Ala-Pro-Trp-Arg, and a single-site substitution to proline at position five — they call the optimized variant E5P — pushed potency to six-point-seven nanomolar against the human receptor. Central administration of E5P at ten nanomoles into mouse brain reduced acute food intake in vivo. Three things to take from this. First, the design pipeline works for a real pharmacological target, with a clean potency-maturation curve from initial design to in-vivo proof of concept. Second, the novel-activation-motif discovery is the genuinely surprising result — it means the canonical pharmacophore is one solution to melanocortin-four agonism, not the only one, and the agonist chemical space is larger than the medicinal-chemistry literature suggests. Third, the cross-show story — receptor-and-reason and the agentic-biology corpus are converging on workflows where the language model and the structure model handle the design, the medicinal chemistry handles the maturation, and the in-vivo validation tells you whether you've done the previous two steps honestly. This is what that endpoint looks like when it works. That's the slate. Back tomorrow.
