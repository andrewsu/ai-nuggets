# Receptor & Reason — 2026-07-01

## Script

Good morning. It's Wednesday, July 1st, 2026 — Receptor and Reason. Ten items today. We open on a dogma-challenging psychopharmacology paper out of the Roth lab that pulls the rug from under the 5-HT2A-mGluR2 heterodimer hypothesis; then a four-paper Alzheimer's block spanning the cerebrovascular transcriptome, an NPTX2-centered resilience program, mitochondrial chaperonin proteostasis, and virtual-reality fMRI navigation phenotyping in preclinical disease; then a cerebellar microRNA and Purkinje firing story with a schizophrenia link, a STING-does-the-opposite result in epileptogenesis, a ventral-striatal astrocyte reinforcement-learning paper, a social-isolation miRNA-and-pattern-separation study, and close on a Microsoft healthcare agent benchmark that puts current frontier agents at roughly forty percent success.

Leading off — no evidence for direct physical interaction of 5-HT2A and mGluR2 receptors in vitro or in vivo. This is Bryan Roth's lab, and it's a direct challenge to a mechanistic model that has anchored a fair amount of psychedelic and antipsychotic drug-discovery reasoning for the last decade. The setup: mGluR2 agonism is well known to attenuate the behavioral and electrophysiological effects of LSD and other 5-HT2A-active psychedelics. Two competing explanations have been in play. One — the González-Maeso hypothesis — is that mGluR2 and 5-HT2A physically heterodimerize on layer-five pyramidal neurons, and mGluR2 signaling directly modulates the Gq-linked psychedelic response through that heterodimer. The other is that mGluR2 is presynaptic on glutamate terminals, and mGluR2 agonism reduces glutamate release that would otherwise excite the same pyramidal cells through 5-HT2A. Roth's group replicated the classical head-twitch attenuation by an mGluR2 agonist against the psychedelic DOI. Then they went at the heterodimer claim with multiple orthogonal approaches — including engineered mice expressing tagged mGluR2 and 5-HT2A receptors — and found no evidence for receptor colocalization or oligomerization at baseline or under 5-HT2A agonist exposure, either in vitro or in vivo. Radioligand binding and kinetic analyses showed no mGluR2-mediated modulation of 5-HT2A ligand binding. The take-home is that the presynaptic-glutamate model is the surviving mechanism for how mGluR2 modulates 5-HT2A-driven psychedelic action, and the 5-HT2A-mGluR2 heterodimer as a discrete drug target — with the biased-signaling and receptor-complex-selectivity papers that have accumulated around it — needs a hard reread. For anyone thinking about mGluR2 PAMs or dual-mechanism scaffolds in schizophrenia or psychedelic-adjacent indications, the mechanistic frame moves from a receptor complex on the postsynaptic side back to presynaptic glutamate control, which changes what you'd screen for and what you'd expect out of an animal model.

Second — a big-cohort human vascular story. Cerebrovascular single-nucleus RNA-seq of postmortem inferior temporal gyrus in Alzheimer's disease and three primary tauopathies. The group profiled seven controls, sixteen AD cases, seven corticobasal degeneration, eight Pick's, and thirty-three progressive supranuclear palsy donors, using the same NeuN-negative Olig2-negative sort to enrich for vascular and immune cells that they'd used in their earlier AD-only paper. Two pieces of the result: first, differential expression across endothelial cells, pericytes, smooth muscle, perivascular macrophages, fibroblasts, and immune cells is largely disease-specific — CBD, Pick's, PSP, and AD each carve out their own transcriptional programs. But heat-shock response genes are consistently upregulated across every disease, in every vascular cell type. That's a conserved cerebrovascular stress signature across four different proteinopathies, and it lands on cells whose role in these diseases has been historically underweighted. Second, AD specifically shows marked vascular remodeling — going beyond the shared stress response — and AD GWAS risk-locus genes cluster in endothelial cells with disease-associated dysregulation. Immunofluorescence with tissue clearing and light-sheet microscopy validated key vascular remodeling markers. Two implications worth carrying: the neurovasculature is now empirically a shared substrate across neurodegenerative proteinopathies, not just AD; and the heat-shock chaperone axis in vascular cells is a testable target common to AD and primary tauopathies rather than being AD-specific.

Third — and this pairs naturally with the vascular paper because it's about proteostasis in a different compartment. NPTX2-centered cognitive resilience mechanisms in the context of AD pathology. NPTX2 is an immediate-early gene product that gets released from excitatory terminals onto parvalbumin interneurons and organizes AMPA-receptor recruitment there — so it sits at the core of E-I balance in behaviorally engaged neuronal ensembles. Prior work has shown NPTX2 is decreased in AD postmortem cortex but preserved in "asymptomatic AD" — people with AD pathology at autopsy who stayed cognitively normal — making it a resilience-factor candidate. This new paper is a multi-cohort integration: bulk RNA-seq on 575 middle temporal gyrus samples across four cohorts, plus parallel-reaction-monitoring mass spec on 135 of them, focused on twenty curated synaptic, trafficking, lysosomal, and regulatory proteins. NPTX2 falls in a graded pattern — control higher than MCI higher than AD — at both RNA and protein. The interesting part is the co-regulation structure. NPTX2 sits inside a tight activity-dependent plasticity cluster with BDNF, VGF, SST, and SCG2, and that cluster is embedded on a neuronal-mitochondrial-integrity axis inversely coupled to a lysosomal-and-chromatin-stress axis. In AD, transcript-level correlations across this plasticity cluster weaken, and stress-linked transcriptional regulators — FOXJ1, ZHX3, SMAD5, JDP2, ZIC4 — over-correlate with NPTX2 protein loss. So NPTX2 loss of function isn't just a synaptic-protein depletion, it's a coordinated collapse of a plasticity-and-mitochondria program under increasing stress-regulator activity. If you're thinking about interventions in early AD, the operational corollary is that raising NPTX2 in isolation may miss the point — the co-regulated module is the target, and stress-transcription-factor axis is the upstream lever.

Fourth — the mitochondrial chaperone side of the same proteostasis story. Mitochondrial Hsp60/10 client protein decline reveals Braak-and-tau- and cognition-linked proteostasis vulnerabilities in Alzheimer's disease. Hsp60 and Hsp10 form the mitochondrial chaperonin complex that folds dozens of AD-implicated mitochondrial matrix proteins, but they've been comparatively underexplored as an integrated proteostasis axis. This group used ROSMAP transcriptomic, proteomic, neuropathology, and cognition data to test whether Hsp60/10 client proteins are selectively vulnerable in AD. The result: client protein abundance declines much more strongly than client RNA in late-stage disease, and the decline is stronger for clients than for abundance-matched non-client mitochondrial proteins — so it's a client-specific vulnerability, not a general mitochondrial one. The clients with the biggest late-stage protein drop are the ones most central in the Hsp60/10 interaction network. Client abundance is inversely associated with Braak-tau burden and positively associated with preserved cognition. The prioritization framework pulls out mitochondrial translation clients and TCA-cycle-pyruvate-redox clients as the highest-priority subnetworks. This is a nice mechanistic frame linking mitochondrial dysfunction, proteostasis, and clinical progression through a defined chaperonin — and it converges with the NPTX2 paper on the same idea, that AD is dominated by coordinated collapse of a mitochondria-plus-plasticity module rather than any single-protein hit.

Fifth — closing the AD block with a phenotyping paper that is going to influence how the field designs preclinical trials. Functional neural signatures of navigation impairment in early Alzheimer's disease, out of Christian Doeller's lab. They combined virtual-reality navigation with fMRI in aged controls, preclinical AD, and early AD participants, and cross-referenced the imaging phenotypes with CSF amyloid and tau biomarkers and APOE genotype. Three dissociable alterations emerged. Amyloid-beta linked to hippocampal hyperactivity during memory-demanding navigation. Tau linked to reductions in hexadirectional modulation near entorhinal cortex — that's the grid-cell signature — with the effect concentrated in a spatial window matching where tau accumulates first. And there were altered object-centered representations in posterior cingulate and entorhinal-adjacent regions that related to how participants encoded and retrieved target locations. The most robust signal, and the only one that separated preclinical individuals from controls, was hippocampal hyperactivity. The methodological upside is that navigation-based fMRI phenotypes can be sensitive enough to preclinical AD to serve as functional readouts for early-intervention trials, which is exactly the window where anti-amyloid and anti-tau programs need biomarker-level engagement outcomes.

Sixth — pivoting to a cerebellar microRNA story with a schizophrenia link. Cerebellar microRNA-206 tunes Purkinje neuron firing dynamics to control sensorimotor gating. This is Paul Kenny's lab. miR-206 has been repeatedly picked up in schizophrenia GWAS and psychiatric transcriptomics as a hit, but its function in the CNS was unclear. The first striking result is expression: miR-206 in the postnatal brain is restricted to Purkinje cells in the cerebellum. Not broadly expressed, not distributed across circuits — Purkinje-specific. It's dispensable for Purkinje cell fate specification, dendritic morphogenesis, and cerebellar motor coordination — so it's not developmental. But an integrated transcriptomic profile combining single-nucleus RNA-seq, spatial transcriptomics, Ago2 HITS-CLIP, and TRAP-seq shows miR-206 controls translational programs governing intrinsic excitability. Functionally, miR-206 deficiency increases tonic firing of Purkinje cells; elevating miR-206 shifts them from tonic to high-frequency burst firing. Behaviorally, either constitutive or Purkinje-specific miR-206 deletion impairs prepulse inhibition of the acoustic startle — the classic conserved sensorimotor gating assay disrupted in schizophrenia. Rescue is bidirectional: restoring miR-206 in Purkinje cells reverses the PPI deficit in knockouts, and elevating miR-206 in wild-type Purkinje cells impairs PPI. So a schizophrenia-linked microRNA tunes cerebellar Purkinje firing dynamics, and cerebellar output shapes a canonical schizophrenia-relevant behavior. That's a substantive extension of the cerebellum-into-psychiatric-circuitry story — and it's a rare example where a psychiatric-associated non-coding variant has a defined cell type, a defined molecular target set, a defined firing consequence, and a defined behavioral readout that maps cleanly onto a clinical construct.

Seventh — a STING-does-the-opposite-of-what-you-expected paper. STING restrains calcium-dependent microglial phagocytosis and protects against neuronal loss and cognitive impairment during epileptogenesis. STING has been characterized as a pro-inflammatory innate immune activator downstream of cytosolic DNA sensing, and the natural hypothesis is that in epileptogenesis STING loss should attenuate neuroinflammation. The group tested it in the kainic acid model of temporal lobe epilepsy — and STING deficiency instead amplified microglial activation. STING-deficient microglia showed pronounced lysosomal expansion, enhanced phagocytic engulfment of neurons, increased hippocampal neuronal loss, and worsened cognitive impairment. Mechanism: STING loss increases expression and alters subcellular distribution of STIM1, the endoplasmic-reticulum calcium sensor that gates store-operated calcium entry, and elevates SOCE activity. Pharmacological SOCE inhibition reduced microglia-neuron interactions, cut phagocytic engulfment, protected CA3 hippocampal neurons, and rescued cognitive deficits after status epilepticus. So STING is repositioned in this context as a negative regulator of microglial activation, and a STING-STIM1-SOCE axis constrains calcium-dependent microglial phagocytosis during epileptogenesis. Two things worth carrying: microglial calcium signaling — specifically SOCE — is a defined therapeutic axis in TLE distinct from cytokine-focused anti-inflammatory strategies, and STING-modulating drugs should be evaluated in the context of the tissue-specific direction of effect, not the canonical pro-inflammatory framing.

Eighth — a paper closing the loop between astrocyte biology and reinforcement learning. Ventral striatal astrocytes contribute to reinforcement learning. The group trained mice on a probabilistic reversal-learning task — the classical two-armed bandit — and attenuated astrocyte calcium dynamics selectively in three striatal subregions using an AAV-delivered PMCA calcium extruder driven by an astrocyte-specific promoter. Attenuating astrocyte calcium in dorsomedial or dorsolateral striatum had little effect on learning-rate or decision-noisiness parameters. In ventral striatum, however, calcium attenuation decreased inverse temperature — meaning the decisions became noisier — and impaired reward-guided choice specifically by cutting win-stay behavior. In-vivo fiber photometry then showed that ventral striatal astrocyte calcium dynamics correlate with model-inferred reward-prediction errors and persist for many seconds after outcome. And on a trial-by-trial basis, astrocyte calcium fluctuations predict choice variability, again mostly in the win-stay direction. Ex-vivo slice electrophysiology and computational modeling suggested two candidate mechanisms — regulation of presynaptic excitatory-inhibitory balance, and RPE-signal-sharing across MSN populations via the astrocyte network. This is a substantive reframing: astrocytes are not just supporting cast — they are a component of the algorithmic apparatus of value-based decision-making. For any pharmacology touching astrocyte calcium or striatal excitation-inhibition balance, the behavioral consequence class you'd predict now includes decision noise and win-stay stability, not only classical motor or reward-consumption endpoints.

Ninth — a shorter mechanistic story that connects social stress to hippocampal cognition through a specific microRNA. Social isolation alters hippocampal miR-30e-5p expression and impairs pattern-separation behavior in adult mice. Adult BALB/c males held in ten weeks of social isolation showed increased miR-30e-5p in ventral hippocampus, decreased expression of its target Neurod1, and impaired object pattern separation — a hippocampal-dependent memory task that reflects dentate gyrus function. Isolated mice did not show clear anxiety- or depression-like behaviors, and they had a blunted acute-stress response — indicating this is a cognition-specific hit through post-transcriptional gene regulation rather than a general affective phenotype. Why this matters: a human SNP in miR-30e that increases mature miRNA expression is associated with impaired cognition, EEG waveform latency alterations, depression, and schizophrenia. The mouse work provides a mechanism — miR-30e-5p suppressing Neurod1 in ventral hippocampus and depleting the pattern-separation substrate — that's consistent with the human genetics. For depression and schizophrenia risk pipelines, this is a candidate handle on the transition from social-stress exposure to cognitive dysfunction that doesn't route through the classical mood axes.

Tenth — closing on agentic AI in the clinical workflow. HealthAgentBench from Microsoft, a benchmark suite of fifty-four agentic healthcare tasks across seven categories, each with its own environment. Every task is an end-to-end clinical workflow — an agent is handed minimal instructions and has to explore raw healthcare data, operate within a complex environment, and execute a multi-step solution that goes beyond naive prompting. Task categories span EHR pipeline construction, medical imaging, and compositional-reasoning problems over large search spaces. The headline: the strongest and most cost-effective agent, Codex GPT-5.5, hits only about forty-two percent overall success. Frontier agents show real promise in automating research modeling pipelines on top of EHR data, but medical imaging remains especially difficult — particularly for Claude Code models, while Codex GPT-5.5 shows emerging capability. Tasks that combine large search spaces with compositional reasoning stay hard for all current agents. For anyone building or evaluating agentic tooling for psychiatric or neurological workflows — where the input is often longitudinal EHR plus imaging plus scale-based endpoints — the practical read is that the aggregate accuracy number covers substantial category-level variance, and the modality mix in your task is going to dominate whether you're in the forty-percent regime or the sub-twenty regime.

That's ten. The piece to read first is the Roth 5-HT2A-mGluR2 paper — it's a real dogma correction with immediate consequences for how you'd design mGluR2-side or 5-HT2A-side compounds in schizophrenia and psychedelic-therapeutic programs. The cerebrovascular snRNA-seq paper is the highest-utility Alzheimer's paper of the day because it hands you a shared vascular target across four proteinopathies. And the Kenny lab miR-206-Purkinje-PPI story is the most methodologically satisfying single result — one non-coding regulator, one cell type, one firing consequence, one behavior. Have a good Wednesday.
