## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. It's Friday, July third, and today's episode is a little heavier on the agentic-AI side than usual because that end of the corpus was unusually rich this week — but we've still got a very meaty structural biology item on chronic pain, a provocative schizophrenia genomics paper that you should read with your skeptical hat on, and a handful of neuromodulation and drug discovery pieces to round it out. Ten items today.

Let's start with a paper that sits right at the intersection of what this show cares about — an agentic architecture aimed at psychiatric care. It's called Copewell, out on arXiv this week. The pitch is a multi-agent swarm system for what the authors call equitable mental wellness support — the framing being that around three-quarters of people in low- and middle-income countries with a mental health condition get no treatment, and single-persona chatbot solutions have very high abandonment rates. Copewell has three technical wrinkles worth calling out. First, it fuses self-reported, physiological, and contextual signals into a multi-source assessment, explicitly to fight the algorithmic bias that shows up when you rely on self-report alone. Second, it routes users to specialized sub-agents using valence and arousal from Russell's Circumplex Model of Affect — so different quadrants of the emotion space get different specialized agents, rather than one generic conversational agent trying to be everything. And third, and I think this is the most interesting bit, they have a dedicated Ethics Supervisor agent embedded in the orchestration layer, which is a very different architectural stance than bolting a content filter onto a monolithic model. Big caveat — this is a design and beta-deployment paper, not a controlled trial. There's no efficacy readout yet, no comparison against standard-of-care digital CBT, no evidence the affect-routing actually improves outcomes over a monolithic agent. So treat this as an architecture proposal to argue about, not as clinical evidence. But if agentic architectures are going to touch psychiatry — and they are — the design choices in this paper are the ones the field will be debating.

Next, a paper that is going to make anti-NGF pain therapy people happy — and worried opioid-crisis people should also be paying attention. This is the structural basis for direct NGF-TrkA blockade by an analgesic antibody. The nerve growth factor and TrkA receptor axis is a well-validated peripheral pain pathway, but the field's history with it is bruised — the tanezumab-class systemic NGF sequestration antibodies got tangled up with rapidly progressive osteoarthritis, and Trk kinase inhibitors have off-target CNS effects because they hit TrkB and TrkC as well. So the argument for targeting the TrkA extracellular domain directly is that you should be able to get isoform selectivity that pan-Trk kinase inhibitors can't, without sequestering NGF systemically. This group solves the two-point-six-angstrom cryo-EM structure of the TrkA extracellular domain in complex with the Fab of a neutralizing monoclonal called forty-two-F-five-fifteen, and the mechanism turns out to be orthosteric, not allosteric — the antibody epitope overlaps the NGF binding interface, which had actually been debated. Importantly the epitope hits residues that are conserved in TrkA but divergent in TrkB and TrkC, which is your structural rationale for how you avoid the CNS off-targets. And they show in vivo that the antibody attenuates mechanical allodynia. So you have a receptor-selective, extracellular, non-opioid analgesic target with a real structure to design around. Not a clinical candidate yet, but a clean starting point for one.

Moving on to a paper you should read carefully but where I'd urge some skepticism about the framing. It's titled "State-dependent transcriptomic collapse of the brain's lactate and ketone thermodynamic sensors in schizophrenia." The underlying observation is genuinely interesting. The authors take schizophrenia GWAS summary statistics, overlay 3D chromatin conformation from Hi-C, and pull multi-tissue transcriptomic data, and they argue that the schizophrenia risk architecture is heavily concentrated in non-coding structural variance flanking the HCAR2 and HCAR1 locus — the hydroxycarboxylic acid receptors that respond to lactate and to beta-hydroxybutyrate. Their claim is that the topologically associating domain around those two receptors is fractured in a way that downregulates HCAR1 sensing on the three-prime side and paralyzes HCAR2 sensing on the five-prime side, offering a mechanism for the long-known CSF lactate elevation and the absent-niacin-flush phenotype in schizophrenia. And they connect it to the recent metabolic-psychiatry ketogenic-diet results in treatment-resistant patients. Now the interesting core is real — the GWAS-Hi-C-eQTL integration and the double-dissociation pattern is worth chewing on, and it does offer a testable model. But the paper's rhetoric goes well past its data — the abstract literally reframes schizophrenia as an "evolutionary fuel mismatch" and calls HCAR2 the brain's cooling switch. That's a hypothesis, not a demonstration, and there is no mechanistic circuit experiment or intervention here. So my take — the receptor topology finding is worth following up. The grand unified theory framing, less so.

Now let's talk about a real drug discovery paper — small molecules targeting the ARF1 interaction with the C9orf72-SMCR8-WDR41 complex, for ALS and frontotemporal dementia. The premise is that C9orf72 loss-of-function mutations, which cause much of familial ALS/FTD, disable a GAP complex that normally deactivates the small GTPase ARF1. That leaves ARF1 stuck in the GTP-bound state, dysregulating Golgi trafficking. The authors first show, in postmortem sporadic and C9 ALS motor cortex, that phospho-ASAP1 at tyrosine seven-eighty-two is elevated — that's a marker for ASAP1 being inactivated, and therefore for ARF1 being stuck on. Then they run a rational-design plus virtual screening campaign on a forty-million-compound library targeting the ARF1-CSW interface. Top hit is a compound they call MCULE-fifty-ninety-five, and it binds ARF1 in the nanomolar range, reduces GTP-bound ARF1 on stimulation, and reorganizes ARF1-dependent Golgi morphology in cells. It's a tool compound, not a clinical candidate — no in-vivo data, no ADME beyond in-silico — but it's a proof of concept that the CSW-ARF1 interaction is drugmable, and the target has a coherent story from human tissue through cells.

Sticking with pharmacology for one more — this one is on prenatal cannabinoid exposure and adult prefrontal cortex circuit function. The context here is real: CBD use in pregnancy is climbing sharply because of a widespread perception that it's safer than THC. This group ran a sex-disaggregated study in mice, exposing dams to three milligrams per kilogram of either compound from gestational day five to day eighteen, then studied the adult offspring at post-natal day one hundred and beyond. Two things worth flagging. First, both compounds abolished endocannabinoid-mediated long-term depression at layer-five medial prefrontal neurons — meaning it's not a THC-specific effect. Second, the compensations were sex-specific — CBD-exposed males showed a saturating ceiling in bidirectional plasticity with elevated AMPA-over-NMDA ratios and slowed NMDA receptor kinetics, while CBD-exposed females had an intact excitation-inhibition ratio but a scaled-up architecture, and THC-exposed females moved pro-excitatory through loss of inhibitory tone. Behaviorally, only CBD-exposed females showed a risk-appraisal deficit that dissociated from classical anxiety readouts. The single-word summary is that prenatal CBD is not neutral, and the mechanism at the receiving synapse is different from prenatal THC even though both hit the endocannabinoid LTD ceiling. Sample sizes and dose selection are the obvious things to scrutinize when the paper goes to review, but the eCB-LTD phenotype is likely robust.

Pivoting to computational drug design — a paper called StructureSAFE. This is a structure-aware chemical language model that tries to resolve a real trade-off in structure-based generative design. Three-dimensional graph-based models can natively incorporate a protein pocket but often generate chemically implausible molecules because their training-data leverage is limited. Chemical language models generate chemically plausible molecules but are structure-blind. StructureSAFE combines a protein structural and evolutionary encoder with the SAFE — sequential attachment-based fragment embedding — molecular representation, and then supports both de-novo hit identification and lead-oh optimization subtasks within the same model. Reported gains are on MolGenBench and on held-out targets, with the biggest deltas showing up on chemical plausibility versus the graph-based baselines. The reason a medicinal chemistry team should care is that lead optimization — scaffold hopping, R-group enumeration, activity-cliff avoidance — has been chronically undersupported by generative models built for the from-scratch case. If StructureSAFE actually generalizes, that's the productive workflow slot.

Next in that same neighborhood — Active-GRPO out on arXiv. This one is about how you train a policy for molecular optimization when you're doing reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards but the reward signal is sparse. The clever bit is a mechanism they call active imitate-reinforce. The policy starts by imitating a reference dataset molecule, and on a per-instance basis it decides — based on whether its own candidate is already better than the reference — whether to keep imitating or switch to reinforcing its own discoveries. It also does active referencing, meaning the reference itself gets upgraded whenever the policy generates something better, so the imitation target keeps climbing. The reported gains on TOMG-Bench MOLOPT are modest but statistically robust across logP, molar refractivity, and QED. The reason to notice this paper isn't the absolute numbers — it's the general recipe. Most SFT-then-RL pipelines lock in a low-quality reference forever. If you build in an active upgrade loop, you get out of the reference-quality ceiling that has been quietly capping this whole class of methods.

Related — MolSafeEval, also on arXiv, accepted at ACL Findings. This is a benchmark that finally takes safety seriously for AI-generated molecules. Current molecular-generation benchmarks reward complexity and property alignment, but they ignore the possibility that your model generates a toxic, reactive, or otherwise hazardous compound — which for anything that will feed into synthesis or a wet-lab handoff is a real problem. MolSafeEval fuses toxicological databases and hazard rules into a molecular safety knowledge graph, and uses LLM-based reasoning on that graph to detect and explain unsafe features. They then evaluate four generation modes — unconditional, property-optimized, target-conditioned, and text-conditioned — and show that current models fail on safety in ways that toxicity-predictor-based evaluations miss. If you're building or evaluating anything generative in chemistry, this is a benchmark you should probably run before you claim your model is deployment-ready. The interesting future question is whether safety supervision at the RL reward level actually closes the gap or just teaches the model to game the safety graph.

Two more clinical items and we're done. The first is a neuroimaging-plus-neuromodulation paper — cerebellar normative modeling identifies neuroanatomical biotypes predicting deep-TMS response in spinocerebellar ataxia type 3. The methodological headline is normative modeling — analogous to a pediatric growth chart but for cerebellar volume — built from twenty-one hundred healthy controls, then applied to a hundred-and-fourteen genetically confirmed SCA-three patients. Unsupervised clustering pulls out two biotypes. Biotype one has relative structural preservation in posterior cerebellum. Biotype two has anterior motor-cerebellum atrophy centered on lobules one through six. Clinically, biotype two starts worse. The counterintuitive result — and this is why this paper matters — is that biotype two shows a bigger response to deep TMS, while biotype one responds better to conventional repetitive TMS. So the patients whose anterior cerebellum is most damaged are actually the ones who benefit from the more penetrating stimulation modality. If this holds up, it becomes a real precision-medicine stratification for a disease with essentially no disease-modifying therapy. The imaging framework is the transferable idea — TMS-response prediction from a normative growth chart is exactly the kind of biomarker that could work in TRD or OCD too.

And the last item — a small mechanistic sleep-neurodegeneration study. Ten healthy older adults, one night of slow-oscillatory tDCS during NREM sleep versus sham, then five consecutive stimulation nights, with plasma p-tau one-eighty-one, amyloid-beta forty-two, forty, and total tau measured overnight and longitudinally. The stimulation increased slow-oscillation power and slow-oscillation-spindle coupling. Overnight plasma p-tau went up after stimulation, and — this is the interesting dissociation — a shift in coupling phase toward the slow-oscillation up-state was associated with overnight increases and then longitudinal decreases in amyloid-beta forty-two. The authors interpret this as glymphatic clearance being enhanced. Ten subjects is ten subjects, and I want you to weight this appropriately. But the reason to note it is that so-tDCS is one of the few sleep-microstructure interventions that gives you a plausible, non-pharmacological handle on amyloid and tau clearance dynamics — and the field of anti-amyloid immunotherapy urgently needs a companion intervention that doesn't cause ARIA. A five-hundred-person confirmatory trial with this design would be genuinely informative.
