## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. It's Friday, July tenth. Ten items today. The through-line is agentic AI meeting mechanism-first pharmacology. We open with the first DREAM Challenge to run an AI Agent Track — an Alzheimer's neuropathology prediction contest that surfaced a sharp lesson about what single-nucleus atlases actually encode. Then a cryo-EM tour of endogenous brain metabotropic glutamate receptor complexes with real consequences for schizophrenia drug design. Then a cross-modal blood-brain-barrier model that handles both small molecules and peptides, a peptide GPCR target for progressive multiple sclerosis, opposing GIPR circuits in the brainstem, a TMS dissection of two frontoparietal nodes controlling alcohol approach, seizure susceptibility as a consequence of what a network is computing, two arXiv items on depression and psychiatric benchmarking with large language models, and we close on a benchmark that lets you actually evaluate drug-repurposing models on rare disease.

Lead item — the SEA-AD DREAM Challenge, out this week from the Seattle Alzheimer's Disease Brain Cell Atlas team, published as a preprint that also introduces something new to the DREAM Challenge format: an AI Agent Track. The setup was a classic model-to-data crowdsourced competition. Participants got single-nucleus RNA-seq from the SEA-AD cohort and were asked to build containerized models that predict Alzheimer's disease neuropathological staging from the transcriptomes alone. The prediction targets covered the full canonical neuropathology axis — overall AD neuropathologic change, Braak stage for tau, Thal phase for amyloid, CERAD score for plaques — plus quantitative amyloid-beta and phospho-tau burden read directly from the 6E10 and AT8 immunostains. Seventeen teams from fifteen countries submitted, and the headline numbers look extraordinary. The best submission hit a quadratic weighted kappa of one point zero for the overall AD neuropathological change score. That's essentially perfect agreement with the pathologist ground truth. On the quantitative burden side the best concordance correlation coefficient was zero point four eight, competitive but honest. So far this looks like a triumph for transcriptomics as a neuropathology readout. But the paper's most important result is the post-hoc perturbation analysis. When the organizers stripped or scrambled donor-level metadata — age, sex, cohort membership, batch identifiers — the top categorical models collapsed. The near-perfect kappas were being driven by donor-level side channels, not by the transcriptome. The quantitative burden predictions, by contrast, held up under those same perturbations and were supported by cell-type-associated features with plausible AD biology. That is a general warning about deeply phenotyped human brain challenges: metadata that seems benign leaks the answer, and if you don't perturb it explicitly, you cannot tell whether your model is doing biology or reading the label off the sample. The AI Agent Track is the other piece of news. It's the first DREAM Challenge track where autonomous and human-guided agent systems compete against human-built pipelines on a single-cell neuroscience benchmark. The paper positions this as an early benchmark; it is going to be cited for years as the reference for what agentic model development looks like in a real biomedical challenge. Both the leakage lesson and the agent-track precedent are worth internalizing before designing your next community benchmark.

Second — cryo-EM structures of endogenous brain-derived G protein-coupled receptors, focused on metabotropic glutamate receptor two, mGluR2. Most of what we know about mGluR2 structural pharmacology comes from recombinant, over-expressed, tag-purified receptor in detergent or nanodiscs. This paper uses CRISPR-mediated protein tagging in mouse brain, an immunoaffinity purification, and cryo-EM to visualize what the receptor actually looks like in native tissue. They resolve at least eleven distinct assemblies. Two things stand out. First, mGluR2 in the brain exists both as mGluR2 homodimers and as mGluR2/mGluR3 heterodimers — and the heterodimers were only detected in active-state complexes, potentially reflecting basal activation of the mGluR3 partner by ambient chloride, which is an established mGluR3 allosteric modulator. Second, the endogenous ternary complexes with a single G-alpha-o heterotrimer differ from what recombinant systems have shown. If you have been designing biased or subtype-selective mGluR2 or mGluR3 modulators — a live area given the collapse of the pomaglumetad program and the continuing interest in mGluR2/3 negative allosteric modulators for cognition, schizophrenia, and PTSD — this is a structural framework worth checking your compound against. The heterodimer selectivity story in particular means an mGluR2-selective compound designed against homodimer structure may behave differently on the endogenous heterodimer population that dominates in some brain regions.

Third — BBBP-Atlas, a bioinformatics preprint on blood-brain-barrier permeability prediction that finally handles both small molecules and peptides in a unified model. Existing BBB predictors have two chronic problems. They lean on precomputed physicochemical descriptors optimized for drug-like small molecules, so they extrapolate badly to peptides. And the standard training sets are fragmented, inconsistently standardized, and full of near-duplicates that inflate reported performance. BBBP-Atlas takes a graph representation over atoms and bonds directly, so a peptide and a small molecule are just different topologies in the same feature space. The training set, OmniBBBP, is ten thousand-plus compounds with about nine thousand small molecules and nine hundred peptides after redundancy filtering. On a balanced external benchmark of two hundred compounds they hit AUC around zero point nine one, Matthews correlation coefficient around zero point seven, beating LightBBB by an absolute six-point MCC gain. The case studies are useful sanity checks: lorlatinib comes out BBB-permeable, vancomycin comes out impermeable, both with high confidence. If you build CNS drug pipelines that include peptide modalities — GLP-1 analogs, oxytocin peptides, glucagon-family macrocycles — you have not really had a shared permeability predictor across modalities. This is worth benchmarking against whatever in-house tool you're using.

Fourth — nociceptin and orphanin FQ, the peptide ligands of the nociceptin opioid receptor, in the progression of multiple sclerosis. The nociceptin receptor sits in the opioid family but is only very weakly engaged by classical opioids, and it has been an on-and-off target for depression, addiction, and pain. This paper opens a new front: MS progression. The earlier work from the same group showed that nociceptin blocks premature oligodendrocyte differentiation during development and that its brain expression drops as myelination gets going. This paper shows that in cerebrospinal fluid from patients with the most severe progressive forms of MS — as opposed to relapsing-remitting — nociceptin is highly elevated. They ask whether the developmental brake on remyelination is being reactivated in progressive MS. To test that, they induce experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis in older mice, at an age where in the human disease the risk of transitioning from relapsing-remitting to progressive climbs. The older EAE mice develop persistent debilitating symptoms with brain and spinal-cord demyelination, and elevated brain nociceptin, mirroring the human progressive picture. Treating them with a nociceptin-receptor antagonist reverses clinical scoring, raises the oligodendrocyte-to-progenitor ratio, increases myelination, and reduces reactive astrocytes. They also show that human astrocytes upregulate nociceptin secretion in response to pro-inflammatory cytokines, giving a plausible glia-glia-mediated mechanism for the elevated CSF levels. Nociceptin receptor antagonists are pharmacologically well-defined — LY2940094 is the best-characterized clinical tool compound, tested in depression and alcohol use disorder. The idea that the same receptor is a progressive-MS remyelination target is genuinely new and testable in humans.

Fifth — opposing GIPR brainstem circuits and how they control feeding, out of Cambridge. The obesity pharmacology world has spent the last two years converging on the strange dual reality that a GIP receptor agonist and a GIP receptor antagonist can both produce weight loss when combined with GLP-1. Central GIPR signaling is required for that efficacy. Nobody has cleanly explained why. This paper resolves it. GIPR neurons in two adjacent brainstem nuclei — the area postrema and the nucleus tractus solitarius — have opposite effects on ingestion. Area postrema GIPR neurons dampen post-ingestive satiation, meaning they permit hyperphagia. NTS GIPR neurons are anorectic. And, critically, appetite suppression by pharmacological GIPR antagonism requires Gipr expression specifically in the area postrema — not the NTS. So an antagonist works by pulling the brakes off the area-postrema-driven hyperphagia signal, and an agonist works, in part, through the anorectic NTS population. Both nuclei get remodeled differently by obesity, which further shifts the balance. If you're modeling GIPR-based obesity pharmacology, or trying to explain why some agonists and antagonists both reduce body weight in the same trial, this circuit-level dichotomy is now the framework to use.

Sixth — a TMS pharmacology-adjacent paper dissociating two frontoparietal nodes in alcohol approach tendencies. Compulsive alcohol intake is sustained in part by an approach bias — cues automatically pull the person toward the drink even against explicit intention to avoid. Frontoparietal cortex is the usual suspect for regulating that bias, but the specific contributions of the right dorsolateral prefrontal cortex versus the right posterior parietal cortex have not been resolved. The design here is clean: continuous theta burst stimulation over one node or the other, active-versus-sham within-subject, in non-clinical alcohol users, with the Alcohol Approach-Avoidance Task as readout. Right DLPFC stimulation selectively slowed push responses toward alcohol without affecting pull responses. Right PPC stimulation produced a bidirectional shift — slowed pushes and accelerated pulls, both alcohol-specific. Same net direction, dissociable behavioral signature. The proposed division of labor is that DLPFC sustains the regulatory signal that drives avoidance over the prepotent approach, while PPC gates whether that signal actually reaches behavior when competing cue-driven signals are present. For clinical TMS in alcohol use disorder — which has focused overwhelmingly on DLPFC as the target — this is a real reason to consider PPC as a distinct therapeutic node, and to think about combined or sequential protocols.

Seventh — a computational neuroscience paper making a striking claim: the computations a network is doing determine how vulnerable it is to focal seizures. The comparison is between networks supporting continuous representations, like grid-cell attractors, and networks stabilizing discrete, well-separated states, like memory-associative dynamics. When you apply a matched seizure-like perturbation, continuous-attractor networks respond with higher activity and earlier performance decline than the discrete-attractor networks. That's the theoretical prediction. The experimental confirmation: in vivo recordings from medial entorhinal cortex, which has grid cells with continuous attractor dynamics, versus CA3, which supports discrete memory storage. Medial entorhinal drives stronger involvement in acute epileptiform discharges and shows smoother state trajectories during those discharges. Selective silencing of entorhinal input abolishes the difference. So the geometry of the manifold the network operates on partially determines its epileptogenicity. Practical implications: focal-onset epilepsy has always been treated as an anatomical problem, with pharmacology overlaid on top. This says that even in the absence of structural pathology, the computational role of a region — whether it is representing continuous variables or discrete ones — biases risk. That reframes the question of why certain cortical regions are epilepsy foci and others are not.

Eighth — a large-scale observational study on how people with depressive symptoms actually use ChatGPT. Participants — seven hundred and sixty-six of them, all consented, all completed the PHQ-8 — donated their ChatGPT conversation histories, one hundred and eighty-seven thousand conversations total. Participants above the PHQ-8 moderate-symptom threshold, meaning a score of ten or higher, used the model differently. More conversations about mental health, interpersonal difficulties, loneliness, self-focused topics, and general support-seeking. More late-night use, more month-level recurring patterns. More first-person singular pronouns and more absolutist language — canonical linguistic markers of depression that show up here in a completely naturalistic context, not on a screening instrument. High-disclosure conversations were more common in the higher-PHQ group, but the model did not redirect them to professional care at higher rates. Language-based prediction of PHQ status from conversations was modest — an AUROC of zero point five nine, well below what you'd want for screening. The framing the authors adopt is important: these histories should not be treated as clinical screening data. They should be treated as evidence that large language models have become an informal support infrastructure — private, persistent, responsive, and available late at night when other options aren't. For clinicians thinking about digital adjuncts and about who is falling through the cracks between symptomatic distress and professional care, this is direct empirical data on where those people are actually going.

Ninth — MentalHospital, an arXiv preprint proposing a virtual evaluation environment for large language models performing full psychiatric clinical encounters. Existing psychiatric benchmarks isolate tasks — a diagnosis prediction here, a treatment planning question there. This one instantiates the full S dot O dot A dot P workflow — subjective interview, objective examination, diagnostic assessment, treatment planning — using skill-augmented standardized patient agents constructed from nearly twelve hundred de-identified EHR cases spanning all major ICD-11 categories and seventy-six disorders. Each encounter is scored two ways: objectively against the EHR-derived reference, and subjectively against a five-dimensional rubric covering communication empathy, interviewing professionalism, note quality, diagnostic rigor, and treatment appropriateness. To scale expert judgment, they train five specialized evaluator models — MentalEval — using rubric-grounded supervised fine-tuning followed by expert-guided direct preference optimization. The evaluators agree with clinician judgments at a quadratic weighted kappa around zero point nine four. Twenty-two clinicians rated the environment as clinically faithful at just under four out of five. The benchmark result: the best current LLM trails clinicians by thirty-seven percentage points on objective psychiatric competence, with mental-status assessment as the bottleneck. That number is worth remembering — it is a much more honest gap than what isolated psychiatric task benchmarks report. If you have a psychiatric-LLM safety story, this is the environment you'll be measured against.

Closing — a benchmark paper from a systems biology group tackling something small but load-bearing: drug repurposing evaluation across rare diseases. If you have a computational model that proposes moving a drug from indication A to indication B, how do you know it's making decisions the way a real pharmaceutical investment committee would? The paper offers two complementary datasets. IxIDN, the Indication Expansion Investment Decision Network, is a positive benchmark built by projecting drug-indication associations from actual clinical trials into a disease-disease network — every edge represents disease pairs that have actually had a drug enter trials for both, capturing real indication-expansion decisions. About six hundred rare diseases, over five thousand edges. ORDON, the Orphanet Rare Disease Ontology Negative-network, is the matched hard-negative set — maximally distant disease pairs by curated hierarchy and inheritance pattern, about eight hundred diseases, five thousand edges. Together they give the field a decision-oriented cross-evidence generalization test that goes from clinical trials to disease ontology, which is a much more realistic yardstick than the ad-hoc positives-and-random-negatives most repurposing papers use. If you're evaluating a rare-disease repurposing model — LLM-based, graph-based, mechanism-based — this is now the benchmark to run.
