## Script

Welcome back to Receptor and Reason. It's Monday, July sixth. Twelve items today. The through-line is receptor pharmacology, with a run of clean circuit-level manipulations up front — a kappa opioid receptor knockdown study on morphine withdrawal, a cocaine-abstinence transcriptional-plasticity paper, and a new PACAPergic hub for threat memory — plus two callback papers that develop threads we ran with yesterday. And a strong closing block on GPCR structural pharmacology, dynamic druggable pockets, orphan-target hypothesis generation, and LLM-guided mechanistic modeling for rare diseases.

Let's open with what I think is the most immediately consequential paper of the day, out of the Liu-Chen lab. The question is whether the kappa opioid receptor pool in the anterior paraventricular nucleus of the thalamus — the aPVT — is what mediates the aversive side of KOR pharmacology, since the PVT expresses KOR at high levels and sits in the center of stress, arousal, and reward circuitry. The design is a conditional knockdown — AAV-Cre injected into aPVT of Oprk1-floxed mice, receptor autoradiography with tritiated U69,593 confirming substantial KOR loss. Behavioral consequences of losing aPVT KOR — reduced anxiety-like behavior in the elevated plus maze, reduced cue-induced freezing after fear conditioning, and reduced naloxone-precipitated morphine withdrawal jumps. All three phenotypes appeared in both sexes. But aversion to the KOR agonist U50,488H in the conditioned place aversion assay was attenuated only in males. And notably, the analgesic and antipruritic effects of U50,488H were unaffected — meaning aPVT KOR is dispensable for the desirable therapeutic effects but essential for the aversive dysphoric ones, at least in males. Two implications. First, this is a specific circuit-level rationale for why the biased-agonist and peripherally restricted KOR development programs — nalfurafine, difelikefalin — have been working better than expected on pain and pruritus without the dysphoria: they're avoiding the aPVT KOR pool. Second, the widespread axonal collateralization from aPVT to nucleus accumbens, central amygdala, BNST, prefrontal cortex, and reticular thalamus means aPVT KOR is a single node that broadcasts to nearly every stress-and-reward-relevant region. That's a promising target for centrally-restricted KOR modulators in opioid use disorder — where morphine withdrawal is exactly the phenotype you want to attenuate. Sex-difference in the aversion arm is a real complication for translation, and the trials will need to be stratified.

Staying with drugs of abuse but shifting to cocaine — a paper from the West lab at Duke on the transcriptional plasticity that sets up the incubation of cocaine seeking during abstinence. Two things here are worth carrying with you. First, this uses in vivo dCas9 CRISPR interference — meaning you can knock down transcription of a specific gene in VTA neurons of a live animal — and they use it to show a causal role for BDNF transcription during the drug-free abstinence period, not during cocaine exposure itself. So BDNF in the VTA is doing something specifically after cocaine stops. Second, single-nucleus sequencing of dopaminergic and glutamatergic VTA neurons over the abstinence period identifies a gene program induced selectively by the prolonged absence of cocaine — a transcriptional signature of the withdrawal state itself, not of the drug exposure. The clinical read — the incubation-of-craving problem, which is the central obstacle to relapse prevention in cocaine use disorder, is not a passive decay of drug memories but an active, transcriptionally-driven adaptation. Any pharmacotherapy targeting the abstinence window has to engage this program. BDNF-TrkB modulation is obviously fraught given the pathway's widespread role in learning and neurotrophism, but the specificity to VTA and to the abstinence period is a much narrower therapeutic window than the field typically works with.

Now a functional-anatomy paper worth attention because it opens a new PACAP circuit. The bed nucleus of the anterior commissure — the BAC — is a small, PACAP-rich glutamatergic cell population sitting at the intersection of anterior commissure, stria terminalis, stria medullaris, and fornix. Using PACAP-Cre viral tracing they show BAC axons densely innervate the posterior basolateral amygdala and the anterior dorsal thalamic nucleus. Behaviorally, they built a looming-threat memory test in a labyrinth — mice learn where the shelter is, then get an overhead looming stimulus. Chemogenetic silencing of BAC PACAP neurons breaks shelter-directed escape and increases freezing. Selective deletion of PACAP itself — separating the peptide role from the neuron role — blocks the looming-induced Fos response in the anterior dorsal thalamus specifically, but not in the basolateral amygdala or the periaqueductal gray. Preliminary ex vivo recordings show PACAP modulating intrinsic excitability in anterior dorsal thalamic and basolateral amygdala neurons. Why this matters — PACAP has been a repeatedly-flagged psychiatric target, especially for PTSD given the human PACAP-and-PAC1 genetic association with startle and fear circuitry. But the field has struggled to nominate specific circuit substrates for PACAP intervention beyond BNST and amygdala. The BAC is a new, small, tractable node, and the fact that peptide release is separately manipulable from neuronal firing gives you the kind of two-lever handle that constructive circuit pharmacology needs. If the anterior dorsal thalamus turns out to be the specifically PACAP-gated output, that's a much cleaner target for a peptide analog or PAC1 modulator than the whole BAC.

Now a callback item. Yesterday we covered a paper showing that oxytocin receptor blockade during postnatal days two through twelve was sufficient by itself to program lasting pain hypersensitivity and cognitive deficits. Today, the flip side — early postnatal oxytocin administration completely prevents neonatal maternal separation from producing spatial memory deficits in adult males. Not just behaviorally — they show that maternal separation suppresses long-term potentiation at CA1 Schaffer collateral synapses, and neonatal oxytocin restores it. Molecularly, maternal separation upregulates neuro-inflammatory and GABAergic and synaptic transcripts in dorsal CA1, and oxytocin normalizes those changes. So blocking OTR early makes things worse; agonism of OTR early makes things better. The postnatal-window sensitivity is real and bidirectional. But note the sex-specificity — the spatial memory deficit and the LTP rescue are both male-specific in this study, whereas yesterday's paper found the recognition memory deficit female-specific and the pain phenotype in both sexes. So the OT system doesn't program a uniform sex-generalized outcome — the phenotypes are dissociable by sex and by domain. For drug developers pursuing OTR agonists like carbetocin for neurodevelopmental indications, the takeaway is that the postnatal window is a real intervention opportunity, but the phenotype-by-sex matrix means the endpoints and populations for a pediatric trial can't be blurred together.

Another callback item — yesterday's oxytocin-antagonist paper flagged NKCC1-to-KCC2 chloride homeostasis as a candidate mechanism. Today, a paper from the De Koninck lab on how KCC2 is actually regulated. The setup: both BDNF-TrkB and NMDA-receptor signaling downregulate KCC2, which is a critical determinant of inhibition efficacy, and KCC2 hypofunction is at the root of pain hypersensitivity, epilepsy, and several psychiatric disorders. How the two pathways interact was unclear. The finding is that they use distinct calcium sources to differentially engage function, internalization, and degradation. TrkB signaling requires ryanodine-dependent intracellular calcium release and modulates KCC2 function without changing expression. NMDAR activation drives calcium influx that internalizes KCC2. Calpain-mediated KCC2 degradation, in turn, requires calcium specifically through voltage-gated channels. And these processes scale hierarchically — short stimuli modulate function, prolonged stimuli internalize, and only sustained stimuli degrade. The framing worth carrying with you — a co-transporter can be regulated through more than surface expression: it's a continuum from post-translational functional modulation to internalization to degradation, scaling with stimulus strength and time. For pharmacology, this means small-molecule KCC2 activators that are still in preclinical development for neuropathic pain, epilepsy, and possibly autism should be evaluated not just on total KCC2 protein but on the functional, trafficking, and degradation arms separately. The three arms may respond differently to different chemotypes.

Now a related tauopathy pair. First, a paper on hippocampal metaplasticity in PS19 tauopathy mice at eight to ten months. The unexpected finding — CA1 shows increased basal synaptic transmission despite profound deficits in both maintenance and early induction of LTP. Dentate gyrus goes the other way — reduced basal transmission plus impaired LTP. And transregional metaplasticity — where prior activity in the stratum oriens normally inhibits subsequent LTP in wildtype — is occluded in PS19 mice, meaning the tauopathic hippocampus is stuck in a state where future LTP is already inhibited. Neuroinflammatory markers are pan-hippocampal, but the C3 astrocyte marker is upregulated specifically in stratum oriens and CD68 microglial lysosomes concentrate in oriens and lacunosum-moleculare. The laminar specificity is what makes this a druggable story — anti-inflammatory or anti-microglial interventions in tauopathy have failed largely because they've been broad, and this argues that the biologically relevant target is a specific hippocampal layer. Complementing that, the second paper — from a super-resolution imaging group — uses two-color 3D dSTORM to map native surface AMPARs in intact PS19 mouse brain slices at six months, before overt neurodegeneration. The finding is that AMPAR nanoscale organization is disrupted in the hippocampus but not in the neighboring cortex, even at that early age. Two things worth carrying — first, wildtype mice already show substantially more extra-synaptic AMPAR in CA1 hippocampus than in motor and somatosensory cortex, so hippocampal excitatory synapses are structurally different at baseline. Second, PS19 disruption of AMPAR clustering happens before you'd see neurodegeneration on a stain — meaning nanoscale surface AMPAR organization is a candidate very early biomarker for tauopathy. Both papers argue the tauopathic hippocampus starts breaking at the level of synaptic organization and plasticity well before the cell death that clinical trials chase.

Now moving to systems-level neurology. A comparative transcriptomic paper on vessel-enriched human brain data across Alzheimer's disease, frontotemporal dementia, and Huntington's disease. The framing is important — the blood-brain barrier has been increasingly implicated in neurodegeneration, but nobody had systematically asked whether the vascular signature is shared across neurodegenerative disorders or is disease-specific. The answer is both. There's a partially conserved transcriptional signature of vascular dysfunction across all three diseases. But there are also disease-specific changes in endothelial, pericyte, and perivascular populations. Endothelial remodeling is most prominent in capillary and venous segments — meaning segment-specific vulnerability along the arterio-venous axis. They identify two molecularly distinct human pericyte subtypes and find that the matrix-type pericyte fraction is consistently reduced across all three diseases. And endothelial-pericyte communication is reorganized around laminin, collagen, fibronectin, NCAM, plus Notch and VEGF. Two implications for CNS drug delivery and development. First, the blood-brain-barrier isn't just a passive drug-delivery obstacle in these diseases — it's dysregulated in a disease-specific way, which means BBB-permeable drugs may still hit different pharmacokinetics in AD versus FTD versus HD brains. Second, the pericyte-fraction loss is a specific rediscovery — the matrix-type pericyte subtype is druggable in principle through the ECM-associated signaling that connects it to endothelial cells, and it's shared across the three diseases, unlike most neurodegeneration mechanisms that are pathology-specific. That gives you a rare pan-neurodegenerative vascular target.

Now three computational-pharmacology picks. First, a class A GPCR paper that I think will be quietly influential. The problem — GPCRs share a conserved fold but are studied case-by-case, and the description of activation states through microswitches like the CWxP motif, the tyrosine lock, and the NPxxY motif is patchy — many receptors literally lack the conserved residues that define the microswitches. This group uses coevolution and machine learning on all class A GPCR structures from GPCRdb to derive a sequence-independent collective variable — a mathematical description of activation state built from evolutionarily-conserved and coevolving inter-residue distances. Then they bias molecular dynamics simulations along that collective variable to drive twenty different class A receptors across twenty subfamilies through the activation transition. All twenty transition within a microsecond of simulation time. Critically, the same CV drives conformational transitions in an orphan receptor that wasn't in the training set — GPR183. And they can converge free-energy surfaces of activation for the beta-2 adrenergic receptor in the presence of different ligand modalities — meaning agonists, inverse agonists, and antagonists come out with distinguishable free-energy landscapes on the shared coordinate. Practically, this is what the GPCR pharmacology field has needed to enable structural-and-dynamics studies of hundreds of orphan or understudied receptors without waiting on individual structural work. For CNS pharmacology specifically — where a lot of the interesting orphan GPCR biology sits — this is a serious enabling tool.

Second computational — a benchmark study on dynamic-pocket detection across molecular-dynamics ensembles. Standard pocket-finding tools like Fpocket, P2Rank, DeepPocket, and AutoSite were designed for single crystal structures. Cryptic and transient pockets — the kind that open only during specific conformational states — are precisely where the interesting allosteric drug targets are, and the field has needed a principled way to detect them across MD trajectories. This paper systematically applies six tools spanning geometry-based, energy-based, and machine-learning-based approaches to MD ensembles of GLUT1 as a dynamic transporter and aldose reductase as a cryptic-pocket reference. They combine inter-tool concordance, HDBSCAN spatial clustering, IoU volume overlap, and temporal persistence. Two useful conclusions. First, different algorithmic classes capture complementary aspects — energy-based methods are more sensitive to transient cryptic regions, geometry-based methods rely more on pre-formed cavities. Second, they nominate a consensus-oriented framework — dynamic pockets should be scored by multi-tool agreement across trajectory ensembles, not any single tool's output. This is the kind of methods paper that quietly changes what a well-run drug-discovery pipeline looks like. For anyone building a CNS structural-based-design workflow with cryptic-pocket targets — GPCR allosteric sites, ion channel state-dependent modulators, kinase back pockets — this is a template to adopt.

Third computational — a fresh preprint just posted yesterday on target-hypothesis generation for orphan natural products. Most microbial natural products with documented bioactivity have no identified molecular target, which locks their development. The pipeline combines a pan-proteome deep-learning drug-target-interaction model — graph neural network for the ligand, ESM-2 protein language model for proteins, bidirectional cross-attention between them — with bias-corrected ranking and control-anchored molecular docking. Two design choices worth noting. First, they explicitly correct for frequent-hitter bias by benchmarking losartan against known targets — a standard problem in DTI models is that some proteins get high scores for everything, and this pipeline actively debiases the output. Second, they use positive and negative controls at the docking step — so the docking score gets compared to a known agonist for the top candidates. Applied to ligiamycin A — a 2022-described Streptomyces natural product with no known target — the top candidate that survives control-validated docking is the orphan GPCR GPR35. Ligiamycin A scores minus-eight-point-one kilocalories per mole at the GPR35 agonist pocket, comparable to the known GPR35 agonist zaprinast at minus-eight-point-three, with a non-binder floor of minus-five-point-five. Alternative candidates like FFAR1 fail the negative-control test. Now, this is still a computational hypothesis awaiting wet-lab validation — but the pipeline design matters more than this specific hit. Control-anchored DTI plus debiased ranking is a much more publishable-looking workflow than the naive "top-scoring hit" pipelines that dominate the DTI literature and that we all know sit inflated with false positives.

Closing with an arXiv piece on LLM-guided mechanistic modeling that I think is genuinely useful for rare-disease pharmacology — AgentODE. The setup — mechanistic ODE models are the interpretable substrate for clinical dynamics, but in rare diseases both the model structure and the parameters are unknown, individual data is scarce and noisy, and privacy constraints often mean you only have population-level summary statistics. No prior method jointly discovers ODE structure and refines parameter distributions from summary statistics alone. AgentODE uses an LLM to propose candidate ODE structures while a tool-augmented inference agent iteratively refines parameter distributions in a diagnosis-update loop, all on population-level summary statistics. They evaluated on three benchmark problems plus two clinical datasets, including recessive dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa with two-hundred-thirty-one observations across forty-six patients. AgentODE recovered functionally consistent ODE structures across all settings. And the RDEB experiment made a subtle but important point — baselines with individual-level data access actually recovered implausible structures despite better predictive performance, whereas reasoning from summary statistics forced more mechanistically principled discovery. So restricting the model to summary-level data isn't just a privacy concession — it's an inductive bias toward mechanism. For pharmacometrics and rare-disease QSP, this is a very interesting direction — most rare-disease datasets are exactly at the intersection of scarcity, noise, heterogeneity, and privacy where standard pharmacometric methods struggle.

That's the show today. A KOR-and-aPVT story I think will be discussed a lot, two callback items on the developmental oxytocin and chloride-homeostasis threads, and a strong computational block on GPCR structural pharmacology and target-hypothesis generation. See you tomorrow.
