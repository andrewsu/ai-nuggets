# Receptor & Reason — 2026-07-02

## Script

Good morning. It's Thursday, July 2nd, 2026 — Receptor and Reason. Ten items today. We open on a big Alzheimer's cell-atlas paper that also happens to use a multi-agent AI workflow to nominate a mechanistic hypothesis from its own differential-expression data, so it's Tier 1 and Tier 2 in one paper. Then a female-cohort ketamine study in Wistar-Kyoto rats that pulls apart delayed anxiolysis from acute-phase effects and adds an oxidative-stress twist, a long-range inhibitory circuit that generates the classical cholinergic-interneuron pause in striatum, a hippocampal-microcircuit epilepsy story, a thalamus-development-and-schizophrenia-risk-zone mapping paper, a sensorimotor-continuity paper linking psychosis and chronic cannabis use, hippocampal dopamine tuning meal-related episodic memory, a PKA regulatory-subunit knockout study with Alzheimer's implications, an open-source fully agentic drug-discovery engine, and close on a large-scale Claude-Sonnet-driven audit of what actually changes between bioRxiv preprints and their published journal versions across seventy-two thousand pairs.

Leading off — multiregional single-cell profiling of Alzheimer's disease from the Seattle AD Brain Cell Atlas team. They extended SEA-AD from three regions to ten cortical regions spanning the canonical Braak arc, profiling roughly seven million nuclei from eighty-four donors with single-nucleus RNA-seq, ATAC-seq, and Multiome, alongside quantitative neuropathology and whole-genome sequencing. Every nucleus was mapped onto an expanded BRAIN Initiative reference taxonomy of two hundred and seven cell types, and a hierarchical pseudo-progression model derived continuous donor-level pathology burden per region and across the brain by jointly modeling amyloid-beta and phospho-tau. Two headline results. First, cellular vulnerability is highly selective but strikingly coherent across regions — only about thirty percent of cell types shift in relative abundance, and those that do move in the same direction wherever they show up. The earliest changes are subsets of somatostatin, LAMP5, VIP, SNCG, and parvalbumin inhibitory interneurons and myelinating oligodendrocytes falling in preclinical donors with minimal pathology, together with emergence of AD-associated microglia. Layer-2/3 and selected deep-layer excitatory losses, sharper microglial increases, and reactive-astrocyte emergence come later. Regionally specialized populations also drop out — including, unexpectedly, primary visual cortex layer-4 IT excitatory neurons, which the field had considered relatively resilient. Every key change replicates across three independent cohorts covering another seven hundred donors. Second — and this is where the paper crosses into what Alan cares about on the computational side — they applied a multi-agentic AI workflow that constructed literature-grounded hypotheses from differential-expression signatures. Fed the layer-4 IT and the somatostatin signatures, the agent stack converged on hyperexcitability as the shared vulnerability phenotype — mediated in part by high NMDA-receptor expression for the layer-4 IT neurons, and enriched for AD GWAS-prioritized genes in the somatostatin interneurons. The take-home: you get a coherent cross-region cellular framework for AD progression, an unexpected early role for a specific set of interneuron subclasses plus V1 layer-4 pyramidals, and a working example of using agentic AI to lift a mechanistic hypothesis out of a DE table rather than treating single-cell atlases as endpoints. For anyone thinking about early-intervention pharmacology — this pushes the target list toward interneuron-excitability control before the classical excitatory dropouts happen.

Second — a ketamine paper in a female cohort of Wistar-Kyoto rats. WKY is a validated genetic model of treatment-resistant affective dysfunction, and female subjects are historically underrepresented in ketamine mechanism work. Animals got a single intraperitoneal dose of saline or racemic ketamine at 5, 10, or 15 mg/kg, and were tested on acoustic startle at 24 hours and again at 7 days. What breaks nicely is a dose-and-time dissociation: 15 mg/kg drove the strongest immediate behavioral effects at 24 hours, and 10 mg/kg drove the strongest delayed anxiolytic-like response at 7 days. The interesting orthogonal readout is that ketamine did not change parvalbumin interneuron counts — so the interneuron-loss frame that has anchored a lot of ketamine mechanistic reasoning is not supported in this model — but it did significantly increase 8-oxo-dG oxidative-stress markers globally in the basolateral amygdala and prelimbic prefrontal cortex, and specifically inside the parvalbumin interneurons of the BLA. So delayed anxiolysis is co-occurring with elevated regional oxidative stress rather than being blocked by it. Two things worth carrying. The persistence-of-anxiolysis-despite-oxidative-stress result argues that ketamine's therapeutic window in female treatment-resistant models runs through region-specific stress-circuit modulation rather than through preservation of interneuron viability — which changes how you'd interpret the parvalbumin-loss literature that dominates chronic-ketamine safety reasoning. And the 10 mg/kg dose selectively driving delayed effects while 15 mg/kg drives acute effects sharpens the case that acute and sustained ketamine responses are separable pharmacology, which is directly relevant to how a clinical dosing schedule maps to therapeutic vs. abuse-liability parameters.

Third — a circuit paper on the classical cholinergic-interneuron pause in striatum. Striatal CINs display a transient pause in tonic firing in response to salient stimuli that becomes synchronized with reinforcement learning. Thalamostriatal and dopaminergic inputs have both been implicated, but the exact GABAergic mechanism has been contested for years. This group combined optogenetics, patch-clamp electrophysiology, and cell-type-specific genetic tools. Synchronized activation of CINs in striatal slices elicits robust feedback inhibition in the CIN network — pause-like responses mediated by GABAA receptors and requiring beta-2-containing nicotinic acetylcholine receptors. Thalamostriatal activation can recruit the same circuit. Dopamine is not required but modulates it through D2 receptors. The counterintuitive result: cell-type-specific silencing and local striatal deletion of beta-2 nAChRs excluded local striatal GABAergic interneurons as the source, but retrograde deletion of the same receptor abolished inhibition — meaning the inhibitory source is extrastriatal, projecting into striatum, and the CIN acetylcholine has to reach a beta-2 nAChR on that upstream population to close the loop. So the canonical CIN pause is generated by a long-range circuit, not a local one. Practical implication for anyone reasoning about muscarinic or nicotinic drug programs in the striatum, or about D2 dysfunction in Parkinson's or psychosis — the pause is nicotinic-receptor-gated but on the projection cell, not on the CIN itself, and the D2 modulation sits on top of that non-locally-sourced GABA input rather than driving it.

Fourth — a hippocampal-microcircuit epilepsy paper. The group focally knocked out the beta-3 subunit of the GABAA receptor from CA1 pyramidal cells and showed that this selectively impairs parvalbumin-mediated inhibition onto deep pyramidal cells, but not onto superficial pyramidal cells. That is a microcircuit-selective perturbation, and it drove robust interictal epileptiform discharges at the site of focal knockout, with features consistent with a CA2-to-deep-CA1-pyramidal generation mechanism. Under physiological conditions, CA2 inputs potently drive deep CA1 pyramidals, so the model fits. And crucially, optogenetic stimulation of CA2 reliably evoked epileptiform discharges in the beta-3 focal knockout mice. Two implications. First, this is a nice experimental instantiation of the idea that epilepsy in the hippocampus is not a global excitation-inhibition imbalance problem but a microcircuit-selective one — deep versus superficial CA1 pyramidals gate different susceptibility. Second, for antiepileptic mechanism development, this argues that layer-specific parvalbumin-onto-deep-pyramidal inhibition and the CA2 input to CA1 are testable pharmacological handles distinct from the classical whole-hippocampus targets. Circuit selectivity matters, and the animal models we use to screen anticonvulsants generally do not resolve at this level.

Fifth — a developmental thalamus paper that ties in schizophrenia risk. The group combined several large developmental imaging datasets to build a fine-scale portrait of thalamic tissue maturation across childhood. Thalamic nuclei do not follow one trajectory — some mature early, some mature late, and the ones with the most protracted developmental trajectory are also the ones at the greatest risk for later clinical differences in schizophrenia. The spatial pattern across thalamic nuclei tied to early-psychosis risk overlaps with a specific neuroreceptor fingerprint, and the fingerprint pattern relates to symptom severity. This is a substantive addition to the developmental-risk story in schizophrenia because it moves the discussion off of gross cortical or striatal targets and onto a nucleus-resolved thalamic map with associated neuroreceptor coordinates. For pharmacology programs, the interesting operational corollary is that the receptor targets you'd care about most for a developmentally-timed early-psychosis intervention are the ones enriched in the late-maturing thalamic nuclei, not the ones distributed uniformly across the thalamus. It also gives a testable neuroimaging endpoint for developmental studies of at-risk populations — you can look at the maturation trajectory of the implicated nuclei, not just the aggregate volume.

Sixth — a clinical sensorimotor paper connecting psychosis and chronic cannabis use through a shared submovement phenotype. The setup: continuous visuomotor tracking of a moving dot on a screen with a finger, in seventeen patients with psychosis, twenty-one heavy cannabis users, and seventeen healthy controls. Submovements — the elementary units of motor output that make up the trajectory — are analyzed for frequency, variability, and responsiveness to observed movement. Both psychosis patients and heavy cannabis users showed less frequent and more variable submovements than controls. When the task involved interacting with a pre-recorded human kinematic profile, both groups showed attenuated responses to the observed submovements, with the effect more pronounced in patients with psychosis. So there is a shared sensorimotor-integration signature between chronic cannabis use — a known risk factor for psychosis — and psychosis itself, visible in a low-invasive continuous motor task. Two carry-forward observations: for early-detection pipelines, submovement analysis is a cheap noninvasive readout that resolves a subclinical shared phenotype without requiring imaging or scale-based rating, and for mechanism-of-action arguments about cannabinoid signaling and psychosis, the shared phenotype gives a specific target — sensorimotor integration — for asking whether CB1 pharmacology sits on the causal path or is a shared consequence.

Seventh — a hippocampal-dopamine and meal-related-memory story. Dopamine in mesolimbic reward pathways is well characterized, but dopamine function in the hippocampus has been much less studied. This group used fiber photometry with GRAB-DA sensors to look at dopamine binding in dorsal hippocampus before, during, and after meals in food-restricted rats. Two headline results: hippocampal dopamine binding is significantly elevated in the post-meal relative to the pre-meal state after standard chow, high-fat diet, or liquid sucrose — but not after a low-calorie sweetener. So the signal is nutrient-consumption-dependent, not simply orosensory. And pharmacological blockade of hippocampal D2 receptors both increased food intake and impaired meal-related episodic memory. Two things worth carrying. The finding argues that hippocampal D2 signaling participates in food-intake regulation through a memory-encoding pathway rather than through classical reward — which is a clean mechanistic entry point for how anti-obesity drugs that touch dopamine systems, or GLP-1-adjacent programs whose satiety endpoints route through cortico-limbic circuits, might get modulated by concurrent psychiatric medication. And it opens a hippocampal-dopamine axis as a potential mediator of eating-behavior deficits in psychiatric conditions with disrupted D2 signaling like schizophrenia.

Eighth — a proteomic paper on PKA regulatory subunits with a distinct Alzheimer's angle. PKA is a master signaling regulator sitting at the intersection of energy metabolism, synaptic plasticity, and stress response. Its catalytic activity is gated by two nonredundant regulatory-subunit classes — Type I and Type II — whose divergent functions have been unclear. The group generated double knockouts of the Type I regulatory subunits and, separately, of the Type II subunits, and did MS-based proteomic and phosphoproteomic profiling under basal and glucose-perturbed conditions. What emerges is that RI and RII loss drive distinct, often opposite, remodeling. Both KOs blunt metabolic flexibility to glycolytic stressors, but RI KO cells show elevated glycolytic signaling while RII KO cells show depressed glycolytic signaling. Interestingly, RI KO increases the abundance and kinase activity of the PKA catalytic subunit isoform, driving up substrate phosphorylation; RII KO decreases the same. So the two regulatory subunit classes are not redundant safety catches on PKA — they wire the catalytic subunit into different metabolic and signaling regimes. The Alzheimer's-implication piece comes from the fact that dysregulated PKA signaling has been repeatedly implicated in AD and other neurodegenerative diseases, and the paper reframes what a target-engagement readout should look like: modulating PKA activity in AD context is not one dial, it is at least two orthogonal ones — Type I and Type II regulatory-subunit-mediated — and any pharmacological attempt to modulate one class will pull the other in an opposite direction.

Ninth — pivoting to Alan's agentic-AI-in-drug-discovery interest. F.A.D.E., the Fully Agentic Drug Engine — an open-source multi-agent conversational platform for drug discovery. The stated goal is to convert natural-language queries into candidate drug candidates and substantially lower the expertise barrier for advanced computational drug discovery. The architecture is a three-branch hierarchical pipeline that adapts to how much structural information is available for a given target — integrating structure prediction, binding-pocket detection, equivariant-diffusion-based de novo ligand generation, and binding-affinity estimation into one automated system. Validation targets are the epidermal growth factor receptor kinase domain — a well-established oncology target with a lot of ground truth to check against — and cellular retinol-binding protein — a less-studied target where the system's structure-prediction and pocket-detection stages have to carry more weight. It's not the first agentic drug engine — there are several now — but the combination of open-source-conversational access, equivariant-diffusion-based ligand generation, and target-adaptive architecture is a useful reference for anyone building or evaluating agentic tooling for neuropharmacology-specific chemistries. The interesting practical question is not whether the aggregate benchmarks look competitive, but whether these systems generalize outside their validation targets — for CNS drug discovery specifically, where blood-brain-barrier penetration and neuroreceptor selectivity dominate the win-loss ratio, that generalization test is where the agentic-pipeline literature still has the least evidence.

Tenth — closing on a large-scale LLM-driven meta-audit of biomedical preprints versus their published counterparts. The group compiled every bioRxiv preprint posted between 2018 and 2025 that matched by DOI to a peer-reviewed published version, yielding seventy-two thousand six hundred and forty-four preprint-publication pairs. They then used Claude Sonnet 4.6 to parse each preprint-publication abstract pair into one primary and two secondary claims, and classified each pair on content change — unchanged, minor, major — and hedging shift — more cautious, more confident, or unchanged. On a validation subsample, the model's inter-rater agreement with two independent domain experts was in the same range as the two experts agreed with each other — Cohen's kappa in the 0.63 to 0.66 band — which is a useful methodological anchor for anyone thinking about LLM-as-adjudicator work at scale. The primary result is that the primary claim was unchanged in about forty percent of abstracts, minorly revised in fifty percent, and substantially revised in only about ten percent. Hedging shifts were uncommon and asymmetric — twice as many claims became more cautious as became more confident during peer review. Two things to carry: the ten-percent substantive-revision rate is a defensible empirical anchor for anyone deciding whether to act on a biomedical preprint in a clinical or portfolio-planning setting, and the Claude-Sonnet-versus-expert agreement result is a template for using a frontier LLM as a scale-out adjudicator on structured meta-scientific questions where a large enough validation subsample exists to calibrate its performance against domain experts.

That's ten. The multiregional SEA-AD paper is the one to read first — it's the highest-utility Alzheimer's data drop of the week and its multi-agentic-AI hypothesis-generation section is a working example of how atlases and agents can be composed into a single workflow. The ketamine WKY paper is the most immediately-actionable psychiatric-pharmacology item because of the acute-versus-delayed dose dissociation in a female cohort, and the striatal CIN long-range-inhibition paper is the most methodologically satisfying single result today — one behavior, one circuit, one negative outcome that reroutes what everyone thought was a local inhibitory loop. Have a good Thursday.
