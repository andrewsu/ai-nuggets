## Script

This is The Blackbird Brief, Sourcing Radar for Saturday, July 11, 2026. Today is a U-M-B day. Three separate labs at the University of Maryland, Baltimore have posted translational work over the past four days that, taken together, is the strongest single-week U-M-B pipeline signal we have seen in months. The lead item is a hereditary hearing loss gene therapy paper out of the Ahmed lab at the U-M-B School of Medicine that lands directly on top of the Aletira portfolio thesis. That is where we start.

The paper is by Sakina Rehman, Arnaud Giese, Abigail Dragich, Saima Riazuddin, Gregory Frolenkov, and Zubair Ahmed as senior corresponding, published in Molecular Therapy on July 9. The Ahmed lab and the closely allied Riazuddin group at U-M-B run what they call the c-E-A-R Laboratory — a translational program on the genetic basis and molecular mechanics of the inner ear. Ahmed and Riazuddin were the co-discoverers, roughly a decade ago, of the role of the C-I-B two gene — calcium and integrin binding protein two — in the hair cell mechano-electrical transduction apparatus. Pathogenic variants in that gene are one of the leading genetic causes of prelingual, non-syndromic hearing loss, and the disease designation is D-F-N-B forty-eight. Losing C-I-B two in a mouse causes exactly what you would expect: severely disorganized stereocilia bundles at the top of the hair cell, broken mechano-electrical transduction, and profound deafness. What the new paper does is take that all the way from mechanism to a gene therapy proof of concept, and then does something unusual on top of it.

The straightforward result first. They packaged a normal C-I-B two coding sequence into an adeno-associated virus vector and delivered it via semicircular canal injection into C-I-B two mutant mouse pups at several different ages within the early postnatal window. The postnatal day zero through day four window produced sustained, though partial, recovery of hearing thresholds, and a real rescue of stereocilia architecture at the level of the hair bundle in the apical to middle cochlear turns. Not a complete rescue; not every frequency; but a durable, mechanism-consistent response with a clear age dependence — the earlier the treatment, the better the outcome. That is exactly the kind of preclinical data package you want to see if you are underwriting a gene therapy startup: a defined dosing window, a mechanistic pharmacodynamic marker in the stereocilia architecture, and durable functional readout on the audiogram.

Here is the unusual part. C-I-B two has a paralog called C-I-B three. Both proteins are known to bind to T-M-C one and T-M-C two — the pore-forming subunits of the actual mechano-electrical transduction channel — but they bind through partially distinct molecular interfaces. So the question the Ahmed lab asked is whether over-expressing human C-I-B three, delivered intracochlear by A-A-V at postnatal day zero to one, could substitute for loss of C-I-B two. And the answer, strikingly, is yes: in C-I-B two mutant mice, A-A-V driven expression of C-I-B three restored hearing function at the frequencies corresponding to the apical to middle cochlear turns. That is a module-level rescue rather than a gene-specific one. The channel needs a C-I-B family occupant; supply either family member, and the machinery can be reconstituted. We are working from the abstract on the paper here — Molecular Therapy is behind an Elsevier paywall and the group has not posted the pre-print — so the exact viral dose, capsid serotype, and the quantitative decibel restoration are the kind of details we will want to pull from the full text before diligence. But the mechanistic and translational shape of the result is clear.

Why this matters for Blackbird. Aletira Therapeutics, the flagship spinout out of the Bio-Hub, is a hereditary hearing loss and cell-type-selective genetic medicine company. The S-E-L-E-X-O-N platform uses alternative R-N-A splicing to restrict transgene expression to the specific cell type you want to reach — it bolts onto any vector, including A-A-V, and it is the exact chassis you would want on top of an A-A-V C-I-B two payload to make sure the transgene is expressed in inner hair cells and outer hair cells but not the surrounding supporting cells or the vestibular sensory epithelium. The Ahmed lab paper is a payload; Aletira has the delivery precision layer. This is not the observation that Aletira should license this paper directly — the target is a U-M-B invention with a long inventor track record and U-M Ventures presumably has a first look. It is the observation that the U-M Ventures × Blackbird co-investment surface — the same track that produced aSKY on the oncology side — now has, sitting on it, an A-A-V C-I-B two program that plays cleanly into the Aletira technology thesis. That is a very natural conversation for Esther Park and Hemaka to be having on the ventures side over the next four to six weeks.

The competitive read. The gene therapy hearing loss field has moved fast over the past three years. Regeneron acquired Decibel Therapeutics in 2023 to consolidate a set of A-A-V hearing loss programs. Eli Lilly acquired Akouos in 2022 and has since presented positive Phase one/two clinical data for an A-A-V O-T-O-F gene therapy — that is D-B minus O-T-O in the Akouos naming — for autosomal recessive deafness driven by O-T-O-F mutations. Sensorion in France is developing S-E-N-S minus five zero one, another O-T-O-F A-A-V. What every commercial program in this space has in common is that they are targeting O-T-O-F, which is auditory neuropathy — synaptic-vesicle biology at the base of the hair cell. C-I-B two D-F-N-B forty-eight is an entirely different disease, a mechano-transduction defect at the top of the hair cell, and no commercial gene therapy program in the world is currently addressing it. That is target-white-space inside a modality that regulators have started to recognize and a delivery route that is now clinically de-risked. It is a genuine sourcing lead.

Two things to run down. First, does U-M Ventures have an active patent estate on C-I-B two and C-I-B three composition-of-matter around gene therapy use, or is this still pending — because the two-payload optionality here has commercial implications. C-I-B two rescues its own disease; C-I-B three rescues C-I-B two disease as a broader module-level intervention. That opens the door to a follow-on strategy across C-I-B family-adjacent hearing loss genotypes and possibly other mechano-transduction complex defects. Second, what is Zubair Ahmed's disposition on spin-out versus licensing — his prior patent trail is on Justia and he has been publishing steadily in the field for fifteen years, so the question is not whether the science is credible but whether the founder is ready to move it out of the university.

That is the lead. Two more items in the U-M-B triple this week, both worth thirty seconds on your radar.

Second item. Robert Ernst's lab at the U-M-B School of Dentistry, Department of Microbial Pathogenesis, published two vaccine-adjuvant papers in essentially the same window — Vaccine at the end of May, and mSphere on July 10 — on their B-E-C-C 470-S molecule. B-E-C-C stands for Bacterial Enzymatic Combinatorial Chemistry, which is the lab's technology name for engineered lipid-A analogs that engage the T-L-R four innate immune receptor. B-E-C-C 470-S is a fully-synthetic, lead-optimized T-L-R four agonist that outperforms the field-standard monophosphoryl lipid-A and P-H-A-D comparators — those are the reference T-L-R four adjuvants that G-S-K and Corixa have used in their vaccine programs. The Vaccine paper is a seasonal H-one-N-one recombinant hemagglutinin flu vaccine formulation with B-E-C-C 470-S, and the headline data are strong T-helper-one skew, robust immunoglobulin G-two-A responses, rapid viral clearance by day seven post-challenge in the murine model, and — most importantly for a commercial adjuvant — durable immune memory maintained for eighteen months after immunization. That is a shelf-life story that starts to look like a real product profile. The mSphere paper is the H-five-N-one pandemic-preparedness companion: same B-E-C-C 470-S adjuvant, recombinant H-five hemagglutinin antigen, complete survival against the matched two thousand and four H-five-N-one challenge strain, with the useful caveat that neutralizing activity did not extend to the twenty twenty-two and twenty twenty-four H-five-N-one isolates. Binding breadth broadened; functional neutralization did not follow. That is a real limitation that the next optimization cycle will need to close, but it does not diminish the platform story.

Ernst has an active N-I-H NIAID adjuvant development contract running through twenty twenty-eight for I-N-D-enabling studies on the B-E-C-C platform, and two B-E-C-C molecules — 438 and 470-S — are in the formal N-I-A-I-D adjuvant development program pipeline. So there is federal non-dilutive funding already carrying the tox and manufacturing work that any commercial partner would want to see completed. The commercial framing for Blackbird is that this is a licensable T-L-R four adjuvant chassis inside U-M-B and specifically inside the School of Dentistry — a location that a translational venture-office scan of the U-M-B campus would not, honestly, prioritize by default. The competitive set here is Dynavax with the C-p-G one zero one eight adjuvant that carries Heplisav-B and increasingly other formulations, G-S-K with monophosphoryl lipid-A and the A-S-oh-one system, and Novavax with Matrix-M. B-E-C-C 470-S is not a spinout candidate today — the more likely structure is a licensing carve-out or an asset drop into a vaccine adjuvant-focused NewCo when the N-I-A-I-D I-N-D-enabling package matures. But for Blackbird's purposes, this is exactly the kind of translationally advanced platform I-P inside a partner institution that our audience should have visibility on before an outside adjuvant consolidator gets there first. Watch item: U-M Ventures posture on the composition-of-matter estate around B-E-C-C 470-S, and whether Ernst's group has any existing option agreements out to strategic vaccine developers.

Third item, and the shortest. Istvan Merchenthaler at the U-M-B School of Medicine Department of Epidemiology and Public Health, together with Laszlo Prokai at the University of North Texas Health Science Center, published in the Journal of Neuroscience on July 8 the primate proof of concept for their brain-selective estradiol prodrug, called D-H-E-D. Chemically, D-H-E-D is a 10-beta, 17-beta-dihydroxyestra-1-4-dien-3-one molecule; the point is that when it is taken orally, it is inert in the periphery — it does not raise estrogen levels in liver, breast tissue, or uterus — but it is enzymatically converted to 17-beta-estradiol specifically in brain tissue, restoring central estrogen signaling with essentially no peripheral estrogenic exposure. Merchenthaler and Prokai first published this platform in Science Translational Medicine in 2015 in a rodent model. The J-Neuroscience paper is the marmoset primate readout: in male and female marmosets treated with the aromatase inhibitor letrozole — a model of the estrogen-deprived state that patients on adjuvant hormonal therapy for estrogen-receptor-positive breast cancer experience — chronic oral D-H-E-D raised brain estradiol substantially without touching peripheral estrogen, improved memory in a short-delay hippocampal-dependent task, prevented letrozole-induced cognitive slowing, normalized hippocampal neuron membrane excitability, and reduced sleep fragmentation. There was a sex-differentiated effect on thermoregulation that the authors flag as needing more work. The unmet-need framing is straightforward: the aromatase inhibitor cognitive and sleep side-effect burden is well documented and drives significant non-adherence in the E-R-positive breast cancer adjuvant setting, which is a large, defined patient population. There is nothing else in class — hormone replacement is a low-fidelity substitute, and the selective estrogen receptor modulators like raloxifene and bazedoxifene act peripherally, not centrally.

Merchenthaler and Prokai are named inventors on the D-H-E-D estate, which is jointly held between U-M-B and the University of North Texas Health Science Center. The reason this is on the radar this weekend is that this is an eleven-year-old platform — first landed in 2015 — that has been publishing steadily but has not moved into a company vehicle, and non-human primate efficacy in the disease-relevant model is exactly the diligence bar that a translational neurotherapeutics investor asks to see before writing a seed check. This is a calendar moment. The obvious question is whether U-M Ventures has a live option agreement on the joint estate and whether it has any conversation footprint with the North Texas side of the intellectual property. This is more of a scouting call than a lead — but it is the kind of platform Blackbird's neuropsychiatric-adjacent thesis, alongside the Lieber Institute G-P-R fifty-two program, would recognize as a fit.

Three separate U-M-B labs — School of Medicine hair-cell biology, School of Dentistry T-L-R four adjuvant chemistry, School of Medicine central-nervous-system endocrinology — three translational papers over four days. The Ahmed lab C-I-B two, C-I-B three gene therapy paper is the highest-value item because it plays directly into Aletira and because there is genuine target-white-space in a modality that has now been clinically de-risked by the Akouos O-T-O-F data. The Ernst lab B-E-C-C 470-S adjuvant is a platform-I-P block with N-I-A-I-D federal money carrying I-N-D-enabling work — a licensable asset rather than a spinout. The Merchenthaler-Prokai D-H-E-D marmoset readout is the calendar moment for a platform that has been quiet for a decade but now has the primate efficacy data a seed-stage neurotherapeutics investor asks for.
