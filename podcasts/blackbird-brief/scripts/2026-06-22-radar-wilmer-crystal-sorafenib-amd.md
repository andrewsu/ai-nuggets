## Script

Good morning, Matt — this is Sourcing Radar for Monday, June twenty-second. The lead today is a Wilmer paper that turns a generic kinase inhibitor into a months-long wet A-M-D injection, and the second item is a Lieber Institute psychiatry paper that's worth flagging as a platform proof point.

The lead is out of the Wilmer Eye Institute, Elia Duh's lab corresponding, with Joshua Doloff and Scott Wilson on the bioengineering side. They've taken sorafenib — the generic multi-receptor tyrosine kinase inhibitor that's been an oral oncology drug for almost twenty years — and developed a crystalline solid-state formulation that you can drop into the vitreous as a single injection. In two mouse models of wet A-M-D, the laser-induced choroidal neovascularization model and the V-L-D-L-R knockout model, one shot suppressed both neovessel growth and vascular leakage for at least twenty-eight days. About thirteen percent of the dose was still sitting in the eye at day twenty-eight, which extrapolates to a multi-month duration of action. No measurable effect on intraocular pressure, no O-C-T-detectable retinal changes, no E-R-G hit on neuroretinal function.

The Blackbird angle on this is, I think, the most attractive sourcing lead we've seen out of Wilmer in a while. Let me walk it.

One — the commercial pull is enormous and clearly defined. Wet A-M-D is a thirteen-billion-dollar global market that's still dominated by monthly to every-other-month injections of aflibercept, ranibizumab, and faricimab. The single biggest unmet need in the disease is durability — patients hate the injection burden and undertreat themselves, and outcomes fall off in real-world clinical practice compared to the trials. Roche's Susvimo port-delivery system and the high-dose Eylea formulations have moved that bar a little, but the field is wide open for an injectable that lasts six months from a routine in-office shot. That's exactly what a crystallized small molecule with this kind of release kinetic is set up to deliver.

Two — the mechanism is differentiated. Sorafenib hits V-E-G-F-R-one, two, and three plus P-D-G-F-R and a couple of RAF kinases. So unlike a pure anti-V-E-G-F antibody it covers the platelet-derived growth factor pathway that drives pericyte biology and arguably contributes to the thirty-percent or so of patients who are incomplete responders to aflibercept. That gives you both a first-line durability story and a clear pivotal-trial wedge in the incomplete-responder population.

Three — the IP is defensible in a useful way. Sorafenib itself is off-patent — which de-risks the molecule but doesn't help composition-of-matter. The valuable I-P here is the crystal form and the formulation, the same playbook EyePoint Pharmaceuticals ran with vorolanib and the DURAVYU intravitreal insert. EyePoint is a roughly billion-and-a-half-dollar company on Phase three durability data with a comparable thesis. So the commercial framing for an investor is concrete — you can point to a public comp with a clean valuation.

Four — the team is the right team. Duh runs a serious neovascular A-M-D translational program at Wilmer. Doloff and Scott Wilson sit in Hopkins' Translational Therapeutics and Regenerative Engineering Center, which is the Wilmer-B-M-E joint operation that exists specifically to take ocular delivery technology to companies. Doloff trained in Bob Langer and Dan Anderson's labs at M-I-T, so the controlled-release pedigree is real. This is not a single-paper one-off; it sits inside a center that has been building toward exactly this kind of asset.

Five — and this is the platform point — the authors call the crystalline approach a proof of concept for any hydrophobic ocular drug that's been gated by solubility. That's a long list. Half of the interesting ophthalmology pipeline molecules across diabetic retinopathy, geographic atrophy, and dry eye are insoluble small molecules that can't be intravitreally delivered today. If the crystallization platform works for sorafenib, it's a pipeline-in-a-formulation story, not a single-asset story.

The honest read on what's still open. This is mouse data, not non-human primate yet, and not in patients. Twenty-eight-day duration in a mouse vitreous is encouraging but you want to see six-month durability in rabbit or n-h-p eyes before you size the program. Inflammation and dispersion of the crystal depot in the human vitreous is the standard risk for any intraocular crystal — Triesence had a long safety history but it's still a watch-item. And the competitive set is crowded — EyePoint's DURAVYU, the Aerpio derivative work, several gene-therapy and biologic durability plays. So pricing the deal will depend on how the head-to-head durability looks against vorolanib insert, and on whether the platform claim can be backed up with a second molecule.

What I'd want to do is set up a call with Duh and the T-T-R-E-C team through Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures. The questions are — is the crystalline platform already a filed I-P family with multi-asset claims, or just this single composition; is there a sponsored research conversation with one of the bigger ophthalmology players already happening; and what's the rabbit and n-h-p plan. If the I-P is broad and the platform claim holds, this is the kind of program Blackbird Laboratories should run a translational grant into for a six-month duration package in larger animals, then spin out behind a single asset with a platform claim on the side.

The second item is a Lieber Institute paper that I'm flagging as a platform proof point rather than a direct sourcing lead. Michael Breen's group at Mount Sinai, with Ran Tao, Tom Hyde, and Joel Kleinman at the Lieber Institute, published last week in Cell Reports a large postmortem transcriptomics study across eating disorders and O-C-D — eighty-six controls, fifty-seven eating-disorder cases, twenty-seven O-C-D cases — paired profiling of prefrontal cortex and caudate. The standout finding is the cross-disorder correlation. Transcriptome-wide effects between eating disorders and O-C-D correlate at zero-point-six-seven in cortex and zero-point-seven-five in caudate. That's high. The joint analysis pulls out a cortico-striatal signature dominated by depressed G-A-B-A-ergic interneuron markers — somatostatin, parvalbumin-related transcripts — plus neuroendocrine genes V-G-F and corticotropin-releasing hormone and a mitochondrial-translation module. Genetically regulated expression analyses also nominate five risk genes converging on a dense locus on chromosome three.

For Blackbird, this is not a chemical-matter pick. There's no probe, and the five chromosome-three genes are not obvious drug targets in their current form. The reason it matters is that it's a clean validation of the Lieber asset — their post-mortem brain bank — applied to a transdiagnostic question that the field has been talking about for a decade but has not had the tissue to test rigorously. That's the same platform we co-built the G-P-R fifty-two program around, and it's now generating publishable transdiagnostic neuropsych targets on a regular cadence. Anorexia nervosa specifically has essentially no approved pharmacology and the highest mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder, so a Lieber-derived G-A-B-A-ergic interneuron lead with O-C-D and anorexia indications would be genuinely white-space. Worth a check-in with Tom Hyde and Dan Weinberger on whether anything chemistry-enabled is coming out of this — and on the Mount Sinai end, whether the Breen group is interested in collaborative tool-compound work.

That's the radar today. One actionable Wilmer lead on long-acting intraocular delivery, and one Lieber platform signal on transdiagnostic neuropsych. Have a good week.
