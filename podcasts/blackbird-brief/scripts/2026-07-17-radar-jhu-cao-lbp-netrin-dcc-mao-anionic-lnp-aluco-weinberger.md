## Script

You're listening to The Blackbird Brief for Friday, July seventeenth, twenty twenty-six. This is a Sourcing Radar episode, and today the Baltimore ecosystem has delivered a Xu Cao Nature Communications paper out of Johns Hopkins Orthopedic Surgery that assembles the first mechanistically-anchored, druggable axis in non-specific chronic low back pain — an unmet-need the venture community has been staring at for decades without an intervention target. Behind it, a Hai-Quan Mao lab platform paper from the Johns Hopkins Institute for NanoBioTechnology on cleavable-crosslinked cholesterol lipid nanoparticles that stabilize the anionic corner of the L-N-P design space and bias mRNA expression to splenic T cells — a formulation chassis with real optionality for in-vivo cell therapy and tolerance induction. And a fresh piece of Lieber Institute intelligence — a Weinberger-corresponding Nature Communications discovery paper on schizophrenia that surfaces a name Blackbird has not yet had on the board: Aluco BioSciences, a San Carlos, California, neurology and endocrine biotech incorporated last year, with a former Lieber staff scientist named as employee and equity holder.

Pick one, and today's headline. Dayu Pan first author. Mengxi Shen, Elizabeth Abatan, Mei Wan, Junying Zheng coauthors on the Hopkins side. Xu Cao senior corresponding, out of the Department of Orthopedic Surgery and the Department of Biomedical Engineering and Institute of Cell Engineering at the Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine. Also on the manuscript — the Iatridis group at Mount Sinai Orthopedics, Zhaozhu Qiu in Hopkins Physiology, and Bradley Taylor at the Pittsburgh Center for Pain Research. Published open access in Nature Communications this Tuesday, July fourteenth. Title reads "Dorsal horn D-C-C amplification loop induced by endplate osteoclasts generates chronic nociplastic low back pain in male mice."

Here is what this paper actually is. Cao's group started from the clinical observation everyone has been living with for two decades — non-specific chronic low back pain is the world's leading cause of years lived with disability, there is no mechanism-defined therapy for it, and the interventions on offer are anti-inflammatories, opioids, and surgery that has to be justified case-by-case because a matching structural finding on M-R-I is often not there. The Cao lab has been working the skeleton-as-signaling-organ axis for a decade — this paper builds a full end-to-end amplification loop that starts at the vertebral endplate and terminates in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord, and every step of it has a druggable node.

The mechanism reads as follows. Spine degeneration produces porous endplates. Osteoclasts inside those porous endplates secrete netrin-one — a classical axon-guidance molecule that Cao's lab and others have been repurposing into peripheral bone-pain biology over the last several years. Netrin-one then activates its receptor, D-C-C, on the terminals of dorsal-root-ganglion sensory neurons that innervate the endplate. That is the input. The novel finding is what happens downstream. D-C-C activation triggers Src-kinase phosphorylation and S-N-A-R-E-complex docking in the presynaptic terminals — the sensory-neuron terminals ramp up glutamate release into the dorsal horn of the spinal cord. And here is the amplifier. The glutamate they now release drives dorsal-horn neurons to express both netrin-one and D-C-C themselves — a form of postsynaptic plasticity that closes a positive-feedback loop back onto the same axis in the spinal cord. The whole system self-amplifies. On top of that, netrin-one and D-C-C expression extend upward into the parabrachial nucleus in the brain, which is the ascending pain-affect circuit.

The validation package hits the bar. Conditional netrin-one knockout in T-R-A-P-positive osteoclasts — that's the osteoclast-restricted deletion — reduces spinal hypersensitivity. Conditional D-C-C knockout in advillin-positive dorsal-root-ganglion neurons — sensory-neuron-restricted — reduces spinal hypersensitivity. Both genetic legs of the axis are causal. The effect replicates in naturally-aged mice, meaning this is not just a lesion-model artifact — the biology is showing up in the age-related low-back-pain phenotype the actual patient population lives with.

The commercial beat. Two clean therapeutic-intervention points fall out of this. First, a netrin-one or D-C-C-directed biologic — a neutralizing antibody, an aptamer, or a soluble decoy at the endplate-D-R-G interface. Netrin-one is a small-enough target that a peptide-mimetic or fragment-based small-molecule approach is not out of the question either, though the standard read is biologic first. Second, and this is the Cao-lab-strength move, an osteoclast-directed intervention aimed at the porous endplates — a targeted small molecule, or a bone-affinity-tagged construct, that shuts down the netrin-one output at its source. That angle sits inside the bone-therapeutics chemistry space where the Cao lab and the broader Hopkins Orthopedic Surgery group has a fifteen-year publication and I-P track record. Consider it a natural fit for what a translational venture office knows how to build around.

The I-P moment. The competing-interests statement reads verbatim "the authors declare no competing interests." The funding is exclusively N-I-H National Institute on Aging — three grants, P-oh-one and two R-oh-ones, all to Xu Cao. That is the "no licenses issued yet, N-I-H grants paying for it, single-institution" fact pattern Blackbird wants to see when a J-H-T-V provisional-filing conversation is the natural next step. Cao is a senior figure in the department; whether J-H-T-V already has a provisional on the netrin-one-D-C-C spinal amplification loop is the first question. If not, this week is the moment.

Diligence questions in three places. First, sex specificity. The title says "in male mice," and that is not a rhetorical hedge — the paper stops short of demonstrating the loop in female mice, which is a real limitation the follow-up cycle needs to address before a program is under-writable. Sex-stratified pain phenotyping is now the industry-standard bar, and post-Huganir July ninth PSD-turnover paper we flagged, that expectation is only sharpening. Second, translation to humans. Every non-specific-L-B-P failure over two decades — nerve-growth-factor antibodies, TRPV1 antagonists, cathepsin-K inhibitors — flunked at the human-efficacy step. Human tissue and post-mortem validation of the netrin-one-D-C-C axis in patient endplates and D-R-G is the next-cycle question a Series-A committee will ask. Third, competitive intelligence — netrin-one has been developed in cancer immunology by NetrisPharma out of Lyon and has moved into oncology trials with an anti-netrin-one antibody, N-P-one-three-seven-nine, which means the therapeutic-antibody path against this ligand is already precedented. What is not is the D-C-C-targeted or osteoclast-endplate-targeted path in the pain indication. Blackbird sits closer to that white space.

So-what for Blackbird. This is the strongest Johns Hopkins direct sourcing lead the Sourcing Radar has surfaced this month. Chronic non-specific low back pain is a global-disability winner with no mechanism-defined therapy and a J-P-Morgan-sized patient population — one in six adults at any given moment. Xu Cao's group is a translational-caliber lab, single-institution I-P, N-I-H-funded, C-O-I-clean at publication. Both intervention paradigms — an anti-netrin-one or anti-D-C-C biologic and an osteoclast-endplate-targeted small molecule — are start-up-scale hypotheses. Action for Eddie and Hemaka this week — a J-H-T-V conversation on whether a provisional is filed and where the field-of-use structure stands. If a filing is not yet in prosecution, this is a shape-of-a-company moment, and Cao is exactly the caliber of senior operator who can carry a company. If a filing is in prosecution, licensing terms and founder disposition are the two conversations to have first.

Pick two — a Hai-Quan Mao lab platform paper published Thursday in A-C-S Applied Materials and Interfaces. Yunhe Su first author. Hai-Quan Mao senior corresponding. All authors at the Johns Hopkins Institute for NanoBioTechnology, the Department of Biomedical Engineering, the Department of Materials Science and Engineering, and the Translational Therapeutic and Regenerative Engineering Center — Hopkins-only manuscript. Title reads "Stabilizing anionic mRNA lipid nanoparticles by cleavable crosslinking of cholesterol."

What the Mao group has built is a chemistry patch — a bolt-on cleavable cholesterol crosslinker plus a P-E-G-diamine — that stabilizes anionic lipid nanoparticles without swapping any of the primary lipids in the parent formulation. Which is the actual product problem behind why anionic L-N-Ps are not commercial. If you want to bias L-N-P expression away from the liver, you can add anionic helper lipids that redirect biodistribution toward the spleen — the tissue where you want to be if your payload is a tolerogenic mRNA for autoimmunity, an in-vivo m-R-N-A-C-A-R-T construct, or a T-cell-directed cancer vaccine. But anionic helpers weaken the R-N-A-lipid interaction, so the nanoparticles fall apart during purification. Every anionic-L-N-P program hits this ceiling. The Mao group's crosslinking chemistry closes it without touching the primary composition — you keep whatever ionizable-lipid work your program has already invested in, add the cleavable cholesterol tether, and the particle survives its own purification. Acid-cleavable means the tether releases in the endosome, so payload release is preserved.

The results are what a formulation-diligence read wants to see. The optimized crosslinked L-N-P produces a significant increase in splenic mRNA expression at twelve hours over the uncrosslinked control. Thirty-three percent of splenic C-D-forty-five-positive tdTomato-positive cells identified by flow are T cells — meaning the payload is not just landing in the spleen, it is transducing lymphocytes. That is the exact result an in-vivo cell-therapy program is trying to buy. Mechanistic analyses point to controlled mRNA release and altered intracellular processing, which reads as: the crosslinker window is tunable, and the medicinal-chemistry landscape is real. This is a platform paper, not a therapeutic asset — but platform I-P wraps around every downstream therapeutic program that wants to touch the anionic corner of L-N-P design space.

So-what for Blackbird. Mao is one of Johns Hopkins' more prolific translational-materials benches — his group has a serial history of nanotechnology commercialization through the Institute for NanoBioTechnology. The specific application space this chemistry unlocks — splenic-T-cell tolerogenic mRNA for autoimmunity, in-vivo mRNA-C-A-R-T that skips the ex-vivo T-cell engineering step commercial C-A-R-T programs are struggling to make economically viable, and mRNA cancer vaccines targeted at splenic marginal-zone antigen presentation — sits at the intersection of three separate multi-billion-dollar therapeutic areas. And it is bolt-on to any anionic-L-N-P program a Blackbird portfolio-adjacent NewCo might be building. The natural sourcing conversation is J-H-T-V's I-P status on the crosslinker composition and the tunable-length claim, and Mao's disposition toward carving this out as its own platform vehicle versus adding it to an existing partnership. Blackbird's cathepsin-B-C-A-R-T story from Tim Luetkens's group at U-M-B has natural interest here — an in-vivo m-R-N-A-C-A-R-T formulation chassis pairs well with an adjunct that preserves C-A-R-T persistence.

Pick three, and this is a landscape-with-fresh-intelligence beat. Eugenia Radulescu at the Lieber Institute for Brain Development is first and co-corresponding. Daniel Weinberger is senior corresponding. Cambridge Psychiatry contributes on the co-expression-network methods side — Petra Vértes and Ed Bullmore, both long-standing Weinberger collaborators. Nature Communications published this Thursday, July sixteenth. Title reads "Analysis of gene co-expression connectivity dynamics implicates aberrant neuron-oligodendroglia interactions in schizophrenia."

The scientific content is a Weinberger-flavored discovery paper — bulk R-N-A-sequencing across three brain regions from post-mortem donors, dorsolateral prefrontal cortex at two hundred ninety-seven donors, hippocampus at two hundred fifty, caudate at three hundred forty-nine. New network-analytic methods identify differentially connected genes in schizophrenia versus neurotypical brains, and the enrichment lands on neuron-oligodendroglia interaction pathways. It is the myelination-connectivity story of schizophrenia, told in a co-expression-network framework. Confirmed in single-nucleus R-N-A-seq and in four independent iPSC-derived brain organoid data sets. Directionally, this is validation for the neuron-plus-multi-cellular-tissue-state framing that Keri Martinowich, the Lieber C-S-O, wrote about in Neuropsychopharmacology two weeks ago and that we flagged as the biomarker-and-P-D specification for the Lieber neuropsych NewCo's development-candidate declaration. Neuronal alone will not collapse the disease phenotype; oligodendroglial coordination has to be captured in the biomarker strategy. This paper reinforces that.

But the Blackbird-relevant surface is not the science. It is the competing-interests statement. Read it carefully. Daniel Weinberger continues to serve on Sage Therapeutics and Pasithea Therapeutics S-A-Bs. Ed Bullmore is consulting for Boehringer Ingelheim, S-R One, G-S-K, Sosei Heptares — now Nxera Pharma, the direct G-P-R-fifty-two-agonist competitor to the Lieber NewCo we flagged on the twelfth — and Monument Therapeutics. Petra Vértes consults for LinusBio. All expected. Here is what is new. Quote — "Jennifer A. Erwin is an employee of Aluco BioSciences and holds equity in the company." Erwin's affiliation on this paper is listed only as Aluco BioSciences, San Carlos, California. Not Lieber Institute. But the funding statement notes that when the study was performed, Erwin was funded by a Maryland Stem Cell Research Fund Launch Program grant — a Lieber-and-JHU-anchored mechanism. The read is Erwin was a Lieber-based investigator during the science, and she has since moved to Aluco BioSciences in California as an employee-with-equity.

Here is what we know about Aluco. Aluco BioSciences was incorporated in California on March twenty-fourth, twenty twenty-four. Located at seven-thirty-three Industrial Road, San Carlos. Their public website describes their focus as neurological and endocrine disease areas. Their technology positioning language is "novel approach to drug development" and "compounds tailored to specific disease indications." That is not enough to know what they do. What is enough is the manuscript-COI-plus-funding signal — a Lieber-based staff scientist working on a Weinberger-corresponding schizophrenia discovery paper is now equity holder at a new California neurology biotech, one that Blackbird has not yet had on the board.

So-what for Blackbird. This is intelligence, not a sourcing lead. Two implications. First, Aluco BioSciences should be on the tracked-company list, and the Chief Business Officer should get a first-read on what they are actually developing — publicly the website is a soft-launch placeholder, so the intel channel is direct. Whether Aluco is licensing Lieber Institute or Johns Hopkins-anchored I-P, whether Weinberger has any relationship to it beyond his existing Sage and Pasithea affiliations, and whether this is a schizophrenia-adjacent asset in the same design space as the GPR52 Lieber NewCo are the three questions to answer. Second — this is the seed for a broader question the Blackbird BioVentures side should be thinking about. Two Lieber-adjacent assets in the neuropsych space have now cleared the Baltimore corridor and landed on the West Coast for their commercial vehicle — Erwin at Aluco in San Carlos and, from the earlier Portfolio Watch, Kynexis in the Netherlands taking the U-M-B KAT-two-schizophrenia biology to Amsterdam. The pattern is Baltimore-anchored discovery science leaving Baltimore for its Series-A-and-later capital environment. That is the exact leakage the U-M Ventures and Blackbird BioVentures co-investment agreement is meant to prevent — and the Lieber-side equivalent needs to be structurally competitive on the same axis. Worth Matt raising with the Lieber board.

That's the radar. Three items — the Xu Cao lab Johns Hopkins Orthopedic Surgery paper on the endplate-osteoclast netrin-one-slash-D-C-C amplification loop as the first druggable mechanism in non-specific chronic low back pain, single-institution, C-O-I-clean, J-H-T-V-conversation moment; the Hai-Quan Mao lab Johns Hopkins Institute for NanoBioTechnology paper on cleavable-crosslinked cholesterol anionic L-N-Ps for splenic T-cell mRNA delivery, platform chassis for tolerogenic mRNA and in-vivo m-R-N-A-C-A-R-T; and the Radulescu-Weinberger Lieber Institute Nature Communications paper as a landscape signal but more importantly as the surfacing of Aluco BioSciences as a Lieber-adjacent California neurology spinout Blackbird has not yet had on the board.
