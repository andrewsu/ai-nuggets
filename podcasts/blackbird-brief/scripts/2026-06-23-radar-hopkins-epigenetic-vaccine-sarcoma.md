# Sourcing Radar — Hopkins wakes immune-cold pediatric sarcomas with an epigenetic GVAX

## Sources

- Recho A, Gatla HR, Resch EE, Phillips MJ, Glavaris S, Doucet M, Looi ANM, Barbato MI, Llosa NJ, Koldobskiy MA, Ladle BH (corresponding, Johns Hopkins School of Medicine — Sidney Kimmel Comprehensive Cancer Center, Pediatric Oncology). "Combination epigenetic-targeted therapy increases the immunogenicity of poorly immunogenic sarcomas." bioRxiv, posted June 18, 2026, version 1. DOI 10.64898/2026.06.18.733244. Full text via r.jina.ai mirror.

## Script

Good evening, Matt — this is Sourcing Radar, bonus edition, for the back half of Tuesday, June twenty-third. One pick today, and it's the kind of paper where I want to spend the whole episode on the angle rather than splitting time. It's a Brian Ladle and Mike Koldobskiy preprint out of the Sidney Kimmel pediatric oncology group at Johns Hopkins, posted to bioRxiv last Thursday, on using sequential epigenetic drugs to turn immunologically cold pediatric sarcomas into vaccines against themselves.

Let me walk the science first, then the Blackbird angle.

The premise of the paper is the central reason immunotherapy has flopped in pediatric solid tumors. Pediatric sarcomas — rhabdomyosarcoma, Ewing sarcoma, osteosarcoma, the soft-tissue subtypes — have very low mutation burden, very few neoantigens, and an immunosuppressive microenvironment. Every checkpoint inhibitor trial run in these tumors over the last decade has been a clinical disappointment. There is essentially no T cell repertoire for checkpoint inhibition to release.

The Ladle group's question is the right one. If the cancer doesn't have antigens for T cells to see, can you make some? They use a sequential two-drug regimen — decitabine, the hypomethylating agent that's the standard of care in M-D-S, followed by entinostat, the histone deacetylase inhibitor that Syndax has been pushing for years. In a Kras-p53-driven murine sarcoma model called K-P Sarc, the combination drives broad transcriptional reactivation of epigenetically silenced genes — including, importantly, the cancer testis antigens that the immunotherapy field has spent twenty years trying to harness. Decitabine plus entinostat induces about a sixteen-fold increase in M-H-C class one expression on the tumor surface compared to untreated cells. And those gene-expression changes persist in vivo for at least a week after the five-day treatment course ends, so this isn't a wash-out effect.

But here's the key experiment. They take K-P Sarc cells, treat them in vitro with decitabine plus entinostat, irradiate them, and mix them with a G-M-C-S-F-secreting bystander cell line to make what's called a G-VAX whole-cell tumor vaccine. They vaccinate mice with the epigenetically-primed G-VAX, wait a week, and then challenge with live, epigenetically-treated K-P Sarc tumor cells in the leg. Eighty-five percent of those mice never grow a tumor when they add checkpoint blockade — anti-PD-one plus anti-CTLA-four — after the challenge. The unprimed G-VAX gives no protection. And crucially, the primed G-VAX gives no protection against untreated tumor cells either — so the immunity is specifically against the epigenetically-upregulated antigen set. They then prove the response is T cell-dependent with depletion experiments, show immune memory by re-challenging long-term survivors at sixty days and seeing complete rejection, and — this is the most interesting bit — they show cross-tumor protection. Mice immunized against epigenetically-primed K-P Sarc reject epigenetically-primed M-three-nine-M, a completely unrelated syngeneic sarcoma. The shared antigens are induced by the same epigenetic program in both tumors.

Then they take three human pediatric sarcoma cell lines — two rhabdo lines with the PAX-three-FOXO-one fusion, one Ewing line with EWSR-one-FLI-one — and show the same gene-expression pattern. Decitabine plus entinostat upregulates H-L-A class one across all three. And they identify a set of two hundred and forty genes upregulated in all three human lines, with cancer testis antigens dominating the high-confidence shared targets. The list includes MAGE-A-four, which is the target of Adaptimmune's FDA-approved synovial-sarcoma T-cell therapy, PRAME, which is the target of Immatics and three or four other T-cell-engager companies, and LRRC-fifteen, which has its own ADC and CAR-T plays around it.

OK. So the Blackbird angle. There are a lot of threads here so let me take them one at a time.

One — the unmet need is acute and the regulatory pull is real. Pediatric sarcomas combined are roughly fifteen hundred new patients a year in the U-S, with overall survival in relapsed metastatic disease still under twenty percent. Five-year survival hasn't moved in thirty years. The pediatric oncology agencies — F-D-A's pediatric oncology branch, the European pediatric committee — actively pull novel mechanisms into this indication with orphan exclusivity, rare pediatric disease vouchers, and accelerated pathways. The Adaptimmune precedent is the right comp. Tecelra, the MAGE-A-four-targeted T-cell therapy, got accelerated approval in synovial sarcoma in December twenty-twenty-four against a tiny patient population with one tumor type. That whole franchise was the basis of the now-pending GSK partnership and arguably of Adaptimmune's last few hundred million in market cap. The Ladle approach goes wider in two ways — the vaccine spans the full antigenic cocktail rather than one antigen, and the priming step is platformable across sarcoma subtypes.

Two — the mechanism is differentiated from the standard immuno-oncology approaches. The dominant playbook for low-mutation tumors right now is to introduce antigen synthetically — either through engineered T cells with a defined target like the MAGE-A-four product, or through ADCs against tumor-restricted surface proteins. Both have shown signal but both depend on identifying and validating a single antigen per asset. The Ladle approach does the opposite. You drive the tumor itself to express its own broad antigen catalog, the immune system selects which of those antigens it can see, and the response is polyclonal by construction. It's much more like how an effective viral vaccine works than how a single-target T-cell therapy works. That has implications for resistance — single-antigen escape is much harder when the immune response is hitting fifteen different cancer testis antigens at once.

Three — the I-P story has two layers and both are interesting. The composition-of-matter molecules are off-patent — decitabine has been around since two thousand-six, and entinostat is Syndax's clinical asset where they're past their first big regulatory miss. So the molecule layer is not where the value sits. The value sits in two pieces. First, the use-and-method I-P around the specific sequential schedule of epigenetic priming followed by a tumor-specific antigen presentation step — which is a process patent the Hopkins group can lock down cleanly. Second, the broader platform claim — epigenetically-conditioned whole-cell or m-R-N-A vaccines as a general approach to low-mutation tumors — is the bigger ask but it's the one that justifies a real biotech-scale check.

Four — there is a Hopkins heritage story here that I think matters. The G-VAX whole-cell vaccine architecture was invented at Johns Hopkins, by Drew Pardoll and Elizabeth Jaffee, in the early nineteen-nineties. They founded a company around it. The pancreas G-VAX, GV-A-X-pancreas, became algenpantucel, was the basis of Aduro Biotech, which I-P-O'd, eventually got acquired by Chinook, and that lineage of assets ended up in Novartis. The honest read is that historical G-VAX programs failed at Phase three in pancreas, prostate, and a few other indications. What's different now is the substrate. The original G-VAX bet was that G-M-C-S-F as an adjuvant alone was enough to drive an anti-tumor response against whatever antigens the tumor happened to be expressing. The Ladle work re-imagines the bet — the adjuvant matters, but you also have to give the immune system a much richer antigen target set, which is what epigenetic priming delivers. The Hopkins infrastructure to run that kind of vaccine clinically — the manufacturing, the regulatory experience, the C-O-G clinical network for pediatric — is still in place and arguably stronger than at any other academic center.

Five — the right honest pushback. This is mouse data with two sarcoma models on the same C-fifty-seven black six background. Inbred mouse cross-protection is not the same problem as outbred human cross-protection. The human cell line data is encouraging but cell lines aren't patients. Manufacturing whole-cell vaccines in a regulatory-grade way is hard, and Phase one will need either a centrally-manufactured allogeneic vaccine cell line — the Aduro approach, which is what made G-VAX-pancreas scalable — or an autologous workflow, which doesn't scale. Entinostat itself, in its history as Syndax's asset, did not hit primary endpoint in the E-twenty-one-twelve Phase three combo with exemestane in breast cancer, so the regulatory perception of entinostat is mixed even though the mechanistic case here is independent of that program. And finally — Ladle has not been a founding C-E-O of a venture-backed company, so the operator question is open.

Six — what I'd do. The pull-through here is a J-H-T-V meeting with Ladle, Koldobskiy, Llosa, and ideally Pardoll. The questions are — first, is the I-P family already filed and how broad is the platform claim, second, can the work transition into the human sarcoma cell-line panel with patient-derived tumors and an N-S-G-mouse vaccine challenge model to bridge to a clinical study, third, what is the manufacturing model for a centralized epigenetically-conditioned allogeneic vaccine, and fourth, does a Children's Oncology Group Phase one in relapsed pediatric sarcoma represent the most efficient first clinical readout. If the answers line up, this is a Blackbird translational grant into the I-N-D-enabling package, with BioHub as a natural incubation home and Pardoll as the obvious scientific advisor. The exit narrative is straightforward — the immuno-oncology majors are spending heavily on pediatric and rare-disease franchises in two thousand twenty-five and twenty-twenty-six, and a polyclonal sarcoma vaccine that's mechanism-validated against the Adaptimmune class would be a clean acquisition target.

That's the radar tonight. One paper, one strong sourcing lead — Ladle and Koldobskiy at Sidney Kimmel using sequential epigenetic priming to turn low-mutation pediatric sarcomas into G-VAX-vaccinable cancers — and a Johns Hopkins technology lineage that gives the program both the heritage and the operating bench to take it forward. Have a good evening.
