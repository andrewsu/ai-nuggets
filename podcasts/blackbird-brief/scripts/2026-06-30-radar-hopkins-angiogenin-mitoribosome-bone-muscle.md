## Context (not spoken)

### What the ANG paper actually shows

- ANG (angiogenin, RNASE5) is the founding member of the RNase A family, long studied as a secreted angiogenic factor, an ALS-mutation gene (Greenway/Wu/Cudkowicz original 2006 paper; multiple LoF mutations identified — R31K, R121H, K40I, K17I), and a stress-induced cytoplasmic ribonuclease that generates tiRNAs from tRNAs to suppress translation under stress. None of the prior biology pointed to a constitutive mitochondrial role.
- Shen et al show that upon initiation of cell-cell fusion, ANG translocates **from nucleus to mitochondria**, where it acts as a **mitochondrial-tRNA 3'-end processing enzyme**. Using transcriptome-wide PARE and 5' RACE, they demonstrate ANG cleaves mt-tRNA 3' ends at junctions bordering mt-rRNAs and mt-mRNAs in mitochondrial polycistronic precursors — releasing those rRNAs and mRNAs for mitoribosome assembly and translation of respiratory complex subunits. The model: in fusion-initiating cells, the energetic demands of membrane fusion drive a sudden need for new mitoribosomes, and ANG is the rate-limiting step in mt-tRNA processing that enables that biogenesis.
- The K40I mutant (ALS-associated, catalytically dead) does not rescue. Mechanism depends on the ribonucleolytic activity, not the secreted-angiogenic function.

### In vivo bone and muscle phenotypes

- Ang⁻/⁻ mice have **elevated bone mineral density and elevated trabecular bone volume**, with significantly **reduced osteoclast numbers and surface area** in both primary and secondary spongiosa. The dominant phenotype is suppressed bone resorption due to impaired osteoclast precursor fusion (osteoclasts are multinucleated and require myeloid-precursor fusion to mature). The high bone mass effect dominates an indirect coupling-mediated decrease in osteogenesis. ANG inhibition therefore phenocopies an anti-resorptive — directionally the same as bisphosphonates and denosumab.
- Ang⁻/⁻ mice also have **reduced gastrocnemius muscle weight**, smaller myofiber cross-sectional area, fewer myonuclei per myofiber, and a 45.98% reduction in treadmill exhaustion total work versus wild-type. Mechanism: impaired myoblast fusion during regeneration (skeletal muscle is multinucleated; satellite-cell-mediated regeneration requires myoblast fusion to existing fibers). Local viral wtANG injection robustly rescues BaCl₂-induced muscle injury regeneration; K40I mutant ANG does not. ANG activation directionally is muscle-anabolic / regenerative.
- The bidirectional translational read is clean: **ANG inhibitors** for osteoporosis (rationale: suppress osteoclast precursor fusion, reduce resorption), **ANG activators / mimetics** for sarcopenia and skeletal muscle regeneration disorders (rationale: enhance myoblast fusion, promote regeneration).

### IP and competitive landscape

- ANG-mimetic peptides have been in development for ALS since the 2010s. Guo-Fu Hu (Tufts; co-author on the Shen paper) is the central figure in ANG/neurodegeneration biology; he has authored extensively on ANG-mimetic peptides for ALS. The ALS pharmacology is the established lane (PD-L1 / motor neuron survival rationale; ANG augmentation is the strategy). Trial activity has been modest — the ALS therapeutic window is hostile and ANG-mimetics have not progressed past early proof-of-concept.
- The Wan/Cao bone+muscle lanes are **method-of-use** territory rather than composition-of-matter — the molecules already exist (recombinant ANG, ANG-mimetic peptides, small molecules from Hu's screens). The new IP is the indication: ANG modulators *for osteoporosis* and *for sarcopenia / muscle regeneration disorders*. Both indications have far better-validated commercial markets than ALS.
- Anti-resorptive market: bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid, alendronate — generic, $4B+ legacy market), denosumab (Amgen Prolia / Xgeva — $5B+ peak), romosozumab (Amgen Evenity, sclerostin antibody — anti-resorptive + anabolic). Postmenopausal osteoporosis remains 10M+ US patients, atypical femur fracture concerns and rebound resorption on denosumab discontinuation leave room for differentiated mechanism.
- Sarcopenia / muscle wasting market: no approved drug. Recent activity around myostatin/activin pathway (apitegromab — Scholar Rock for SMA, ALS readouts ongoing; bimagrumab — Lilly's myostatin antibody, weight-loss indication after GLP-1 muscle loss concerns; trevogrumab — Regeneron). The GLP-1-induced lean-mass-loss problem has created a renewed therapeutic window for muscle-anabolic agents. ANG-mediated myoblast-fusion biology is mechanistically distinct from the myostatin/activin axis and could be combined.

### What the EMRE paper actually shows (second pick)

- Kumari et al (Vivek Garg lab, UMB Pharmacology) develop a new high-sensitivity assay to quantify mitochondrial calcium uniporter complex (MCUcx) function in intact mitochondria. The assay enables direct, single-mitochondrion-resolution measurement of Ca²⁺ flux through MCU.
- Using the assay, they establish two roles for EMRE: (1) EMRE is required for matrix-Ca²⁺-dependent inhibition of MCU through a juxtamembrane site, mechanistically distinct from MICU1-mediated low-cytosolic-Ca²⁺ inhibition; (2) EMRE actively promotes ion permeation through MCU, elevating its role from a structural scaffold to an active throughput determinant.
- The translational frame the authors state explicitly: "highlight the utility of our assay for probing MCUcx biophysical mechanisms and enabling the discovery of uniporter modulators."
- MCU pharmacology has been a stalled space — known modulators (Ru360, mitoxantrone, MCU-i4 derivatives) lack drug-like properties or have weak validation. The field has been blocked by the lack of a high-throughput intact-mitochondrion functional assay. Garg's lab developed exactly that assay. The cardiac IR-injury rationale (calcium overload triggers permeability transition pore opening and cardiomyocyte death) and the neurodegeneration rationale (mitochondrial Ca²⁺ dysregulation in PD, AD) are both old hypotheses waiting for tool compounds.

The lead pick is a paper from Mei Wan, Xu Cao, and the Hopkins Orthopaedic Surgery group, published yesterday in Bone Research, on angiogenin as a mitochondrial RNA processing enzyme. The reason it is the lead pick is that it opens a fresh bone-and-muscle pharmacology lane on a protein that the ALS field has been chasing for fifteen years. Let me walk it.

Angiogenin — also called RNase five — is a long-studied protein. It is the founding member of the RNase A enzyme family. It was originally characterized as a secreted angiogenic factor. In 2006 the Cudkowicz group at Mass General identified loss-of-function mutations in angiogenin as a cause of familial A-L-S. Since then, angiogenin-mimetic peptides have been in development for A-L-S, primarily out of Guo-Fu Hu's lab at Tufts — who is a co-author on this Hopkins paper. The pharmacology has been viable but the A-L-S therapeutic window is hostile and the programs have not progressed past early proof-of-concept. That is the existing chemical matter and the existing context.

What the Wan-Cao group shows is a completely new role for angiogenin — and a completely new indication lane. Upon initiation of cell-cell fusion, angiogenin translocates from the nucleus into the mitochondria, where it acts as a mitochondrial-tRNA three-prime end processing enzyme. They use a transcriptome-wide cleavage-site mapping technique called PARE plus five-prime R-A-C-E, and they show that angiogenin cleaves the three-prime ends of mitochondrial tRNAs at the junctions where those tRNAs border mitochondrial ribosomal RNAs and messenger RNAs. That cleavage releases the rRNAs and mRNAs for new mitoribosome assembly. In other words — when a cell is about to fuse with another cell, the energetic demands of membrane fusion require a rapid biogenesis of new mitoribosomes, and angiogenin is the rate-limiting enzyme that processes the mitochondrial polycistronic precursor RNA to permit that biogenesis. The K40I mutant — which is an A-L-S-associated catalytically dead variant — does not rescue. The mechanism depends on the ribonucleolytic activity.

Now the translational reading. Cell-cell fusion is required for two tissues that matter for medicine. Osteoclasts are multinucleated cells. Mature osteoclasts form by fusion of myeloid precursor cells. And skeletal muscle fibers are multinucleated — satellite-cell-mediated muscle regeneration after injury requires myoblast fusion to existing fibers.

The in vivo data are clean on both. Angiogenin knockout mice have elevated bone mineral density and elevated trabecular bone volume, with markedly reduced osteoclast numbers and surface area on both primary and secondary spongiosa. The dominant phenotype is a high-bone-mass phenotype driven by suppressed osteoclast-mediated bone resorption. Angiogenin knockout mice also have reduced gastrocnemius muscle weight, smaller myofiber cross-sectional area, fewer myonuclei per myofiber, and a forty-six percent reduction in total work on a treadmill exhaustion test versus wild-type. Local viral angiogenin injection robustly rescues skeletal muscle regeneration after barium chloride-induced injury; the catalytically dead K40I mutant does not rescue.

So the bidirectional pharmacology is genetically validated. **Inhibit angiogenin** for osteoporosis — direction-of-effect is the same as bisphosphonates and denosumab, but the mechanism is distinct: suppress osteoclast precursor fusion rather than osteoclast survival or function. **Activate angiogenin** — or deliver angiogenin-mimetic peptides — for sarcopenia and skeletal muscle regeneration disorders, where the rationale is enhancing myoblast fusion during satellite-cell-mediated regeneration.

Three reads for Blackbird.

First — both indications have far better commercial frames than the A-L-S work that has dominated angiogenin pharmacology. Osteoporosis is a ten-million-patient U-S market with multibillion-dollar approved agents; denosumab discontinuation rebound resorption and atypical femur fracture on bisphosphonates leave room for a differentiated anti-resorptive mechanism. Sarcopenia has no approved drug — and the G-L-P-one-induced lean-mass-loss problem has created a renewed therapeutic window that the myostatin and activin antibodies are racing to fill. Angiogenin-mediated myoblast-fusion biology is mechanistically distinct from the myostatin axis and could be combined.

Second — the I-P angle here is **method-of-use** rather than composition-of-matter. The molecules already exist. Recombinant angiogenin, angiogenin-mimetic peptides from Hu's group at Tufts, small-molecule modulators from earlier A-L-S screens — these are the existing chemical matter. The new I-P is the indication. **Method-of-use claims on angiogenin modulators for osteoporosis and for sarcopenia and muscle regeneration disorders.** That is a faster path to a clinical asset than a de novo discovery campaign.

The headline is a new high-sensitivity assay that quantifies M-C-U function in intact mitochondria, paired with mechanistic results establishing two distinct roles for the regulatory subunit E-M-R-E. The assay enables what the field has been waiting for — a high-throughput functional readout for M-C-U pharmacology in physiologically intact organelles. M-C-U has been a stalled drug target for fifteen years; the rationale is solid — calcium overload through M-C-U triggers permeability transition pore opening and cardiomyocyte death in cardiac ischemia-reperfusion injury, and M-C-U dysregulation is implicated in neurodegeneration — but the field has lacked tool compounds, and the existing modulators like Ru-three-sixty are not drug-like.
