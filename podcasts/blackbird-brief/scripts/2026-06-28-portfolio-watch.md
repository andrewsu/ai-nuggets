## Context (not spoken)

### Sangamo Chapter 11 + asset sales (06-23)
- Filing: Chapter 11 in Delaware, debtor-in-possession financing up to $30M. Restructuring cuts ~40% of US workforce (51 of ~128 roles).
- **Lilly** stalking-horse bid: $50M + assumed liabilities for the **capsid delivery platform, zinc-finger platform, modular integrase (MINT) platform, and prion disease program ST-506.** This is the headline gene-therapy IP. Lilly already owns **Akouos** (hereditary hearing loss AAV gene therapy) and **Verve Therapeutics' base-editing assets** (via the 2025 acquisition) and has run an aggressive in-house gene-therapy buildout since 2023.
- **Astellas** stalking-horse bid: $50M for the Fabry disease program **ST-920 / isaralgagene civaparvovec**.
- Total ceiling: $100M combined for what was historically one of the most-cited gene-editing IP estates in biotech. Sale is under Section 363 — any party can submit a higher bid through the auction process.
- Sangamo was the foundational zinc-finger nuclease (ZFN) gene-editing company; their AAV delivery platform was widely licensed (Pfizer, Biogen, Novartis at various points). The platform's depressed sale price recalibrates valuations for the broader AAV-delivery-and-engineering IP class.

### Aletira / Akouos read
- Aletira's SELEXON platform is alternative-RNA-splicing-driven cell-type-selective transgene expression — bolt-on to any AAV vector. Aletira's signaled lead indication is hereditary hearing loss.
- Lilly's gene-therapy stack now: Akouos hearing (otoferlin AAV in clinic), Verve base-editing cardiovascular, and pending the Sangamo deal — capsid platform, ZFN platform, MINT integrase platform, prion program.
- Two implications for Aletira: (a) **Validation**: Lilly is paying real (if depressed) money to stack AAV delivery + editing capabilities specifically. The commercial premise behind SELEXON — that cell-type selectivity is the next AAV problem to solve — is being underwritten by the same buyer that owns Akouos. (b) **Competitive threat**: Lilly will inherit Sangamo's capsid engineering and capsid-evolution methods, which are an alternative path to tissue selectivity that does not require splicing-based regulation. SELEXON's differentiation against capsid-engineering approaches needs to be sharpened in the next investor and BD pitch.
- BD action item: Geoff Lynn should know what is in the Sangamo capsid library specifically — in-ear-targeting capsids would change Aletira's positioning materially. Worth a courtesy outreach to the Lilly Akouos team to map the post-deal portfolio.

### AbbVie acquires Apogee for $10.9B (06-22)
- Apogee Therapeutics: clinical-stage antibody biotech, Cambridge MA. Lead asset **APG777 (zumilokibart)** is an extended-half-life IL-13 monoclonal in Phase 3 for atopic dermatitis; second asset **APG808** is an IL-4Rα antibody (Dupixent target); **APG273** is the IL-13/TSLP bispecific being positioned for asthma and atopic-derm combinations.
- AbbVie pays ~$10.9B, year's #2 biotech buyout after the GSK/Nuvalent deal earlier this month ($10.6B). Strategic frame: AbbVie's post-Humira immunology franchise has Rinvoq + Skyrizi + ABBV-712 cycle but Sanofi/Regeneron's Dupixent is encroaching on the atopic-derm asset class via IL-4Rα + IL-13 combination biology. APG273 (IL-13/TSLP bispecific) is a counterweight to Sanofi/Regeneron's amlitelimab and the broader TSLP wave (Amgen tezepelumab, Eli Lilly's pending Cou-TSLP).
- Read for Blackbird: not a direct portfolio touch. Indirect signal is two-fold. (a) **Immunology M&A continues hot**: AbbVie paying $11B for clinical-stage atopic derm biology says big-pharma immunology buying remains intact, which underwrites exit-comp pricing for any inflammation-or-fibrosis adjacent program. The Slusher gut-restricted GCPII IBD program lives in that adjacent market. (b) **Apogee was a Fairmount Funds / Atlas Venture late-stage biotech that IPO'd in 2023 at ~$300M and exits at $11B in three years**. Mean trajectory for a clinical-stage immunology asset under good conditions. Useful internal datapoint for exit-comp on Blackbird IBD work.

### Remix Therapeutics × Passage Bio reverse merger + $100M PIPE (06-24)
- Structure: Passage Bio (Nasdaq PASG) is being absorbed into Remix Therapeutics in an all-stock reverse merger. Remix gets Passage's Nasdaq listing and ~$100M PIPE concurrent. Pre-merger Remix holders end with ~93%; pre-merger Passage holders with ~7%. Closing Q4 2026.
- Remix lead asset: **REM-422**, an oral small-molecule **mRNA degrader** targeting MYB transcription factor (historically undruggable). Ph1/2 data at ASCO 2026: 43% ORR at the recommended Phase 2 dose in biomarker-positive adenoid cystic carcinoma (ACC), 100% DCR, durable responses. ACC is the lead indication; AML is the second.
- Read for Blackbird: indirect but real. Remix is in the broader **RNA-modulating small molecule** category — the same conceptual lane as Blackbird's RNA-translation-activator platform asset. Remix *degrades* mRNA; Blackbird's platform *upregulates* mRNA translation. Opposite direction, same conceptual neighborhood. A $100M PIPE raise into a fresh Nasdaq listing for an RNA-modulating small-molecule platform is a meaningful market-validation signal that this category is fundable — useful tailwind for Avi when the Blackbird translation-activator program is positioned to outside investors.
- Secondary read: Passage Bio's reverse-merger exit (after FDA pushback on their lead gene-therapy asset, GM1 gangliosidosis) is the second-bankruptcy-or-restructuring outcome in the AAV-gene-therapy class in two weeks — Sangamo last week and now Passage. The market has clearly decompressed around AAV gene therapy and recompressed around RNA-modulating small molecules. Strategic implication for Aletira's positioning narrative: lean into the "SELEXON makes AAV gene therapy *more selective and therefore commercially viable*" frame rather than the generic "gene therapy is the future" frame, because the generic frame is taking valuation damage right now.

Good morning again, Matt — this is Portfolio Watch for the week ending Sunday, June twenty-eighth. Three items move the thesis this week, plus a couple of macro framings.

The biggest piece of news in our sourcing universe is Sangamo Therapeutics filing Chapter eleven on Tuesday the twenty-third and entering stalking-horse asset sale agreements with Lilly and Astellas. I want to spend most of the time on this one because the implications for Aletira are real and bidirectional.

Sangamo, twenty-some years old, was the original zinc-finger-nuclease gene-editing company. They are filing in Delaware with debtor-in-possession financing of up to thirty million dollars, cutting roughly forty percent of US headcount. Lilly is the stalking-horse bidder on the headline gene-therapy I-P — fifty million dollars plus assumed liabilities for the entire capsid delivery platform, the zinc-finger platform, the modular integrase platform called M-I-N-T, and the prion disease program S-T-five-oh-six. Astellas is the stalking-horse bidder on the Fabry program S-T-nine-twenty for up to another fifty million. Both sales are under Section three-sixty-three, which means a higher bidder can come over the top at the auction. Combined ceiling for the entire I-P estate of one of the foundational gene-editing companies in biotech is one hundred million dollars.

For Aletira and the SELEXON platform, this is a two-sided signal.

On the validation side — Lilly already owns Akouos, the hereditary hearing loss A-A-V gene therapy program. Lilly already owns Verve's base-editing cardiovascular assets from the twenty-twenty-five acquisition. Lilly is now adding the Sangamo capsid library, Z-F-N platform, and M-I-N-T integrase platform on top. That is the same buyer that holds Aletira's commercial-validation logic on hearing-loss otology gene therapy, and that buyer is paying real money — depressed but real — to stack delivery and engineering capabilities specifically. The market premise behind SELEXON, which is that cell-type selectivity is the next A-A-V problem to solve, is being directly underwritten by the player that holds Aletira's most natural strategic acquirer slot.

On the threat side — Lilly is inheriting Sangamo's capsid engineering and capsid-evolution methods, and capsid engineering is the *alternative* path to tissue and cell-type selectivity. Aletira's bet is that splicing-based regulation, applied at the transgene level, is a better answer than capsid evolution. After this deal closes, the largest most-likely acquirer for an otology gene-therapy company will have a capsid-engineering arsenal in-house, and SELEXON's differentiation against capsid-engineering needs to be sharper in the next BD pitch — not just versus academic capsid groups but versus an integrated Lilly stack.

Two — does it make sense for Geoff to make a courtesy outreach to the Lilly Akouos team to map the post-deal portfolio and start the BD relationship before the integration completes, rather than after? My read is yes — early relationship-building before the assets are absorbed is much higher-leverage than relationship-building after Lilly has decided what is core and what is shelved.
