## Script

This is the Blackbird Brief Sourcing Radar for Wednesday, July twenty-second, and today's single best lead is a brand-new, liver-restricted RNA target out of Johns Hopkins endocrinology that hands you a first-in-class drug program aimed squarely at the biggest liver-disease market there is.

Let me set the scene first, because it was a thin week on the preprint servers. The bioRxiv and medRxiv sweep for the last four days turned up almost nothing with a Johns Hopkins, U-M-B, or Lieber corresponding author and a real asset attached. So today's two picks are both peer-reviewed, both Johns Hopkins, and both landed this week — one a genuinely novel target you could build a company around, the other a strong translational result where the honest answer on the intellectual property is more complicated. Let's take them in order.

The lead comes from Xiangbo Ruan's lab in the Division of Endocrinology, Diabetes and Metabolism at Johns Hopkins, working with the Institute for Fundamental Biomedical Research at Johns Hopkins All Children's. The paper identifies a long noncoding RNA they call h-L-M-R-one — human liver metabolic regulator one — and the reason it matters is what it does and where it lives. It is hepatocyte-specific and human-specific, and it acts as a brake on the liver's ability to break down amino acids and run the urea cycle. In metabolic-dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease — the fatty liver disease formerly called NASH, now MASLD, and its advanced form MASH — patients have impaired amino acid catabolism and impaired urea production, and nobody had a molecular explanation for why those genes get switched off. This is the explanation. h-L-M-R-one is high in those livers, and it is holding the catabolic program down.

The mechanism is the elegant part, and it's the part that makes it druggable. Using a humanized-liver mouse — animals in which human hepatocytes have replaced somewhere between fifteen and eighty percent of the mouse liver — they showed that knocking h-L-M-R-one down broadly wakes up the amino acid degradation genes and the urea-cycle enzymes. Then they mapped how: the lncRNA base-pairs directly to the pre-messenger-RNAs of those catabolic genes through a short complementary stretch, nucleotides five-eighty-seven to five-ninety-eight. Delete just that twelve-nucleotide motif and the whole repressive function collapses — overexpressing full-length h-L-M-R-one completely blocks the normal glucagon-driven induction of those enzymes, but the motif-deleted version does nothing. And the human relevance holds up: expression climbs as you go from healthy liver to MASH, moving in lockstep against the catabolic genes, and a one-week low-carbohydrate intervention pushes h-L-M-R-one back down and the enzymes back up.

Here's the so-what for Blackbird. This is a first-in-class target, not a me-too. It's a liver-restricted, human-specific RNA whose entire job is repression, which means you win by knocking it down — and the authors say so explicitly, pointing at antisense oligonucleotides or gal-nack-conjugated siRNA as the delivery route. That is the single most de-risked modality in the business right now: the Ionis and Alnylam gal-nack-to-liver playbook is clinically validated and pours drug precisely into hepatocytes, which is exactly and only where this target lives. The competitive backdrop is a MASH field that just got its first approval in resmetirom from Madrigal and is crowded with FGF21 and incretin players — but everyone there is working lipid and fibrosis biology. Nobody is drugging hepatic nitrogen metabolism, and the amino-acid and urea-cycle angle also opens a second door toward hyperammonemia and urea-cycle-disorder indications where a liver siRNA that restores ureagenesis is a clean orphan story. On the intellectual property, the disclosure is the tell: no competing interests, and no patents mentioned. For a novel-target discovery in a blockbuster indication, that means the provisional window is very likely still open — this is a Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures conversation to have now, not in six months. The candid read: it's early — target validation in a humanized mouse, no therapeutic molecule yet — but that's the right stage for a venture studio. You're not buying a program, you're founding one around ownable, foundational biology. This is the strongest sourcing lead of the week, and I'd put Avi and Yixuan on the target-novelty and the freedom-to-operate, and Eddie on Ruan and the J-H-T-V filing status.

The second pick is a rare-pediatric-cancer result from Eric Raabe's group in Pediatric Oncology at Johns Hopkins, published in Neuro-Oncology, with Philip Cole at Harvard and Rhoda Alani at Boston University on the paper. The disease is atypical teratoid rhabdoid tumor — A-T-R-T — the most common malignant brain tumor in infants, driven by loss of SMARC-B-one and a dismal prognosis. The insight is that when SMARC-B-one is gone, the tumor leans on the CoREST repressor complex to keep critical genes silenced. So they hit CoREST with corin, a bifunctional small molecule that tethers an L-S-D-one inhibitor to a histone-deacetylase inhibitor in one molecule. Corin slowed A-T-R-T growth across every epigenetic subgroup, forced the cells to differentiate down a neuronal path, triggered apoptosis, and — the result that matters most — significantly extended survival in orthotopic xenografts, with the expected on-target chromatin marks showing up in the tumors. And they nailed the mechanism genetically: knocking down R-COR-two, a core CoREST subunit, reproduced the drug's effect and made the cells stop responding to corin — clean target validation.

The so-what here is more nuanced, and I want to be candid about it. The unmet need is enormous and the economics are real — a differentiation therapy for an infant brain tumor is exactly the kind of program that earns a rare-pediatric-disease priority review voucher, which is worth real money on resale. But corin is not a Johns Hopkins molecule — it's Philip Cole's compound, and Cole is now at Harvard and Brigham and Women's. So the composition-of-matter sits elsewhere, and what Johns Hopkins actually owns here is the use — the CoREST-dependency-in-SMARC-B-one-null-tumors thesis and the A-T-R-T method of treatment. That makes this a licensing-and-partnering conversation more than a clean spin-out: you'd be assembling rights across two institutions, and the broader L-S-D-one inhibitor field already has clinical-stage programs you'd be racing. One intelligence note worth logging — a co-author on this paper, Jeffrey Rubens, is now at Pfizer, which tells you where some of this pediatric-neuro-oncology talent and interest is flowing. My read: worth a look as a landscape and relationship item, and a good reason to keep a line open to the Raabe lab, but the fractured IP means it's not the founding-a-company opportunity that the first pick is.

That's the radar for today — a novel, ownable liver-RNA target in fatty liver disease as the lead, and a rare-pediatric-oncology differentiation result as the watch-and-partner item. Two Johns Hopkins picks, one clear action. I'm back tomorrow.
