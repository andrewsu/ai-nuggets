## Context (not spoken)

- mKRAS-VAX: pooled synthetic long peptide vaccine targeting six KRAS hotspot mutations — G12D, G12V, G12C, G12R, G12A, G13D — with poly-ICLC (Hiltonol, Oncovir) adjuvant; weekly x4 prime with ipi+nivo, then nivolumab maintenance with vaccine boosters q8w.
- Metastatic MMR-p / MSS CRC arm (this paper): N=13, pretreated. Both primary endpoints (safety, immunogenicity within 17 weeks) met. All vaccine-attributable AEs grade 1–2. mKRAS-reactive T cells in 8/12 (75%) biomarker-evaluable patients by direct ex vivo IFN-γ ELISpot; 12/12 (100%) after in vitro expansion. Secondary efficacy endpoints (RECIST 1.1) reported as supportive; full ORR/DCR/PFS/OS in the supplement, not in the abstract.
- Resected PDAC arm (companion Feb-2026 paper): N=12. 11/12 immune responders. Median DFS-from-vax 6.35 months, median OS 29.59 months. 4/12 patients remained disease-free at last follow-up — all G12V or G12R. Top-quartile-immune-response patients: DFS 18.8 months vs. 2.76 months bottom quartile.
- Spinout: Adventris Pharmaceuticals (Y Combinator-backed; Liz Jaffee co-founder + CSO; Mark Yarchoan and Neeha Zaidi hold equity; vaccine technology licensed from Johns Hopkins). Adventris is the Hopkins commercialization vehicle for the mKRAS-VAX and related neoantigen-vaccine IP.
- Competitive context: Elicio Therapeutics (Cambridge, MA) — ELI-002 amphiphile-conjugate mKRAS vaccine that traffics to lymph nodes via endogenous albumin. ELI-002 2P (2-peptide formulation): 21/25 (84%) immune response, AMPLIFY-201 PDAC + CRC MRD. ELI-002 7P (7-peptide): 99% immune response in AMPLIFY-7P PDAC MRD; phase 2 fully enrolled (135 patients). ELI-002 designs without checkpoint inhibitor combo; achieves equivalent immunogenicity to mKRAS-VAX-plus-CPI via lymph-node-targeting delivery rather than adjuvant + CPI amplification.
- Portfolio adjacency: aSKY Therapeutics (UMB, Krupnick lab; Blackbird co-investment with UM Ventures) — disclosed AVS Bio antibody-engineering partnership with a KRAS frame; specific MOA not yet public. The peptide-vaccine vs. antibody-engineering distinction is the key differentiation question for aSKY.

## Script

Good morning, Matt — this is Sourcing Radar for Saturday, June twenty-seventh. The lead today is a Liz Jaffee paper out of the Bloomberg-Kimmel Institute in Nature Communications on Tuesday — the metastatic colorectal-cancer arm of the pooled mutant KRAS peptide vaccine combined with ipilimumab and nivolumab. The pancreatic-resection arm published in February. This is the second readout. The reason it matters for Blackbird is not the trial itself — it is the spinout, the competitive picture, and the read-through to aSKY.

Let me walk it.

Mutant KRAS is the largest unaddressed cancer target by deaths. About forty percent of metastatic colorectal cancers carry an activating KRAS mutation at one of six hotspot codons — G-twelve-D, G-twelve-V, G-twelve-C, G-twelve-R, G-twelve-A, G-thirteen-D — and microsatellite-stable, mismatch-repair-proficient disease, which is the majority of metastatic colorectal cancer, is the setting where checkpoint inhibitors essentially do not work. That population — pretreated, M-S-S, M-M-R-proficient, metastatic, KRAS-mutant — is roughly the worst-prognosis subgroup in solid tumor oncology and has been the white whale of cancer immunotherapy for fifteen years.

The Hopkins approach is mKRAS-VAX — a single, off-the-shelf pooled synthetic long peptide vaccine that contains all six common KRAS-mutant peptides, adjuvanted with poly-I-C-L-C, given in combination with ipilimumab and nivolumab as the immune amplifier. Same vaccine product for every patient regardless of which KRAS mutation they carry. Weekly priming for four doses with both checkpoint inhibitors, then nivolumab maintenance with vaccine boosters every eight weeks.

The current paper. Thirteen pretreated metastatic M-M-R-proficient C-R-C patients. Both primary endpoints met. All vaccine-attributable adverse events were grade one or two — no incremental severe immune-related AEs beyond what dual ipi-nivo already produces. Immunogenicity: mutant-KRAS-reactive T cells expanded in eight of twelve biomarker-evaluable patients ex vivo on interferon-gamma ELI-spot — that is seventy-five percent — and in twelve of twelve after in vitro expansion. The proof-of-mechanism endpoint is convincing in the hardest population to immunize.

The pancreatic-resection arm, published earlier this year out of the same trial, is the stronger efficacy signal. Twelve patients with node-positive resected pancreatic cancer. Eleven of twelve immune responders. Top three quartiles of immune response — median disease-free survival eighteen-point-eight months, versus two-point-eight months in the lowest quartile. Median overall survival twenty-nine-point-six months from vaccination. The strongest correlate of disease-free survival was the patient's own mutant-KRAS-reactive T-cell response, not any baseline tumor or patient feature. Causal logic on a single-arm phase one is always provisional, but the immune-response-to-D-F-S correlation across two tumor types in the same trial is real.

Now the Blackbird-relevant part. The vaccine technology is licensed out of Johns Hopkins to a company called Adventris Pharmaceuticals. Adventris was co-founded by Liz Jaffee — who is the senior and corresponding author on both the C-R-C paper and the P-D-A-C paper, and who is one of the most decorated cancer-immunology investigators in the country. Mark Yarchoan and Neeha Zaidi, both also on the author list, hold equity. The company is Y-Combinator backed — unusual posture for a Hopkins faculty spinout, signals operator-style execution. Adventris is the commercialization vehicle for the Hopkins KRAS-vaccine program and the broader Jaffee-lab neoantigen-vaccine IP.

Three things this means for Blackbird.

First — competitive landscape for the mutant-KRAS vaccine space, because aSKY sits adjacent. Adventris is one player. The leader on read-outs is Elicio Therapeutics in Cambridge, with E-L-I-double-zero-two — an amphiphile-conjugate KRAS vaccine that traffics to lymph nodes through endogenous albumin binding. Elicio's seven-peptide formulation hit ninety-nine percent immune response in their pancreatic-M-R-D phase one and their phase two with one hundred thirty-five patients is fully enrolled — that read-out is the next industry-defining data point in this space. The Hopkins vaccine gets to similar immunogenicity in the resected pancreatic arm — but it needs dual checkpoint blockade to do it, where Elicio gets there without C-P-I. Different mechanistic bets — adjuvant-and-amplifier versus delivery-and-trafficking — and both can win different sub-indications, but the next twelve months of clinical reads will sort the spaces. Strategics — Bristol Myers Squibb, Merck, AstraZeneca, Moderna's I-N-T arm — are watching this directly.

Second — the aSKY read-through, which is the immediate portfolio question for Hemaka. aSKY's disclosed work-in-progress with AVS Bio is a KRAS-frame antibody-engineering effort, not a peptide vaccine, and the specific mechanism is still nondisclosed. The strategic positioning question for Krupnick and the aSKY team is: in a landscape where Elicio and Adventris are about to define the M-R-D and metastatic peptide-vaccine standards across colorectal and pancreatic, what is aSKY's modality differentiation, and what tumor sub-segment is it best positioned for? Antibody approaches to mutant-KRAS recognition — there is precedent here, Astellas-acquired Xyphos / Convergence work, and more recently the Hopkins-Berkeley peptide-MHC mutant-KRAS antibody — are a real lane, but the lane needs sharpening relative to where the vaccine work has now reached.

Third — Adventris itself as a sourcing question. Y-C-backed, Hopkins-licensed, Liz Jaffee as scientific founder, two phase-one readouts now public — this is squarely a company Blackbird BioVentures should already have a position on, either invested, passed with a thesis, or actively monitoring. The pull-through is whether the Convergence Institute relationship with Adventris is structured in a way that gives Blackbird any preferred-access angle to follow-on Jaffee-lab neoantigen-vaccine programs — for example mutant T-P-fifty-three, mutant E-G-F-R, or the broader pan-cancer driver-mutation neoantigen lane. Eddie should know the J-H-T-V deal structure on Adventris in detail if he does not already.

One short tail item.

A bioRxiv preprint posted Tuesday from the same Hopkins immune-oncology ecosystem — Ludmila Danilova, Kellie Smith, and Leslie Cope at Johns Hopkins School of Medicine. They released an R package and Shiny app called replicate-Fest for analyzing functional T-cell-receptor-repertoire data from F-E-S-T assays. F-E-S-T — short-term peptide stimulation followed by T-C-R sequencing — is the workhorse assay for identifying which T-cell clones expand in response to which neoantigens. It is the assay infrastructure underneath every neoantigen-vaccine immunogenicity readout, including the mKRAS-VAX work we just walked through. It is a method paper, not a sourcing lead. The reason it is on the radar is that Kellie Smith at the Bloomberg-Kimmel Institute runs one of the deepest neoantigen-T-C-R-discovery groups in academia, and her tooling generally precedes her therapeutic concepts by twelve to twenty-four months. Worth Avi and Esther knowing the group's output cadence, because the next personalized-neoantigen-T-C-R therapeutic concept out of Hopkins in this space is likely to have Smith's name on it.
