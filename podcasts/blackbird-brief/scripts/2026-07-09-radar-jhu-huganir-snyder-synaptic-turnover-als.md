## Script

You're listening to the Blackbird Brief for Thursday, July ninth, twenty twenty-six. This is a Sourcing Radar episode, and I'll be honest with you up front: today is a lean day. After Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday's radar cleared out the strongest translational items from the past ten days — the AsclepiX suprachoroidal minipig tox, the Wong lab caspase-three-tau axis, and yesterday's triple pick of Kennedy Krieger's A-C-S-V-L-three glioblastoma target, the Cahan-Raman D-D-X-three pediatric osteosarcoma combo, and the Luetkens cathepsin-B C-A-R T-cell adjunct — the Baltimore preprint funnel has quieted. What's on the table today is two peer-reviewed Hopkins papers plus one Lieber Institute item, and I want to frame all three as platform signal rather than licensable sourcing leads. It's the "what are the marquee labs working on right now" read, not the "would a translational investor take this meeting tomorrow" read.

With that framing, let's go.

The lead is a PNAS paper published Monday from Richard Huganir's group in the Solomon Snyder Department of Neuroscience at Hopkins. Huganir has run Snyder Neuroscience for years — he's Bloomberg Distinguished, and if you look at the map of who trains American postsynaptic-density biologists, most paths lead back through this lab. The paper is basic mechanism, no drug, but the mechanism it stakes out is a novel target-selection lens for C-N-S drug discovery, and that's why it matters to Blackbird.

They used stable-isotope labeling in mammals — S-I-L-A-M — to measure the half-lives of forty-one hundred proteins in the postsynaptic density of mouse hippocampus. Feed a heavy-lysine diet to breeding pairs and their offspring until ten weeks old, switch to a light-lysine diet, and then over the following weeks watch how quickly the heavy label washes out of each protein. Proteins that keep the heavy label longer are long-lived. Two findings matter.

First, they identify a small set of exceptionally long-lived synaptic proteins. Extracellular matrix components — versican, neurocan, hyaluronan-and-proteoglycan link proteins — dominate the top of the list, and Huganir's own AMPA-receptor subunit GluA4 sits alongside them with the slowest turnover of any AMPA subunit measured. That maps neatly onto the perineuronal-net literature and the idea that the mature brain builds physical structures at the synapse that are meant to persist and provide a scaffold for long-term memory. Second, and this is the sharper finding, the turnover rates are sex-differentiated. The GABA-A receptor gamma-two subunit — Gabrg2, an autism-spectrum-disorder risk gene — is significantly more stable in male mice than in female mice. Multiple other A-S-D risk genes show the same sex-dependent stability pattern. And on top of that, contextual fear conditioning stabilizes Shank-three, another A-S-D and neurodevelopmental risk gene, presumably as part of the memory-consolidation machinery.

The so-what for Blackbird. This is not a paper that spins out a company. Huganir has no competing interests declared, and the work is federal-grant-funded. But it changes how a translational neuroscience investor should think about two things. First, sex differences in psychiatric risk — A-S-D more common in males, major depression more common in females — may be traceable to sex-differentiated stability of specific postsynaptic-density proteins. That's a testable, molecular hypothesis for a set of clinical observations that up to now have been epidemiological. And second, if you're building a schizophrenia or A-S-D or intellectual-disability program, this argues that dosing biomarker development and clinical trial stratification need sex as a primary axis of thinking, not an afterthought. It's directly relevant to Blackbird's Lieber neuropsych NewCo — the G-P-R fifty-two schizophrenia program — because the same reasoning applies: pharmacodynamic biomarkers coupled to synaptic function will read differently in male and female brains, and the differences may be substrate-driven, not just receptor-density-driven. Worth having a coffee with the Snyder Neuroscience group. Not this quarter's licensing conversation, but this quarter's platform-visibility conversation, and Huganir is the anchor P-I to keep on the map.

Second item, and this one extends an arc the show has been walking through the past week. A PLoS Genetics paper published yesterday from Jiou Wang's group in the Biochemistry and Molecular Biology department of the Bloomberg School and Neuroscience at Hopkins School of Medicine. The paper does a multi-regional transcriptomic profile of two hundred thirty-nine A-L-S patients — forty-three with the C9orf72 hexanucleotide repeat expansion and one hundred ninety-six without — plus seventy-seven controls, sampled across ten C-N-S regions. Cerebellum, three levels of spinal cord, motor cortex, frontal cortex, hippocampus, occipital cortex, temporal cortex.

Two findings matter for the Blackbird conversation. First, the C-nine-positive and C-nine-negative A-L-S cases diverge molecularly. C-nine cases enrich for lipid-homeostasis and immune-modulation pathways; non-C-nine cases enrich for protein-synthesis pathways. That divergence has been suspected but not cleanly mapped across regions before. Second, and this is the sharper investor-relevant finding, sex matters within subtype. Female non-C-nine patients have significantly higher microglial cell-type-deconvolution scores than males in the cervical spinal cord, and that microglial burden correlates with shorter clinical duration. Female non-C-nine A-L-S may be a distinct pharmacological population from male non-C-nine A-L-S, and both are distinct from C-nine A-L-S.

The so-what. The paper is funded by the Packard Center for A-L-S Research at Hopkins — which is exactly the Hopkins hub Blackbird already had visibility on via the July fourth Packard imaging item. What this paper argues, in the authors' own language, is that A-L-S therapeutic development needs subtype-and-sex-specific stratification frameworks. Now zoom back out to the two TDP-forty-three items we walked through last week — the Packard imaging platform on July fourth and the Wong lab caspase-three-tau mechanism on July seventh, both pointing at cryptic-splicing biology as the actionable axis, both suggesting the market can expand from A-L-S-and-F-T-D into Alzheimer's disease with TDP-forty-three co-pathology. This paper is the patient-stratification layer that any TDP-forty-three-directed program — Ionis-Amylyx, QurAlis, Trace, and whatever ends up spinning out of the Wong lab — is going to need to build into its clinical development plan. It's not a target. It's a framework paper, and it's a strong one, and the Hopkins TDP-forty-three surface is now three well-populated data points in the past week for a Blackbird-relevant thesis. Worth putting on Esther's and Hemaka's reading list; the pattern here is consistent enough that I'd flag it as a candidate area for a Blackbird translational grant into the Wang lab's next question.

Third and briefly, a signal item rather than a lead. A behavioral neuroscience paper out Friday from the Lieber Institute — Gregory Carr's group, with James Barrow on the authorship. Carr and Barrow together are the medicinal chemistry and behavioral pharmacology core at Lieber, which is to say they're your platform partner on the G-P-R fifty-two schizophrenia NewCo. The paper takes post-weaning social isolation in mice and shows it increases reward-seeking behavior on a touchscreen task in adulthood. Straight behavioral neuroscience, no target, no drug. What's worth noting is that Barrow being on the authorship on a paper about isolation-driven motivational phenotypes in mice tells you the Lieber platform is actively working the intersection of environmental stress, adolescent development, and reward biology — three domains that are increasingly the frontier of what a next-generation schizophrenia or bipolar program has to speak to. If you want a coffee-length update on where the platform partner's core scientific attention is right now, this is that snapshot. Not a sourcing lead, and not a sourcing recommendation. A read on the shop.

That's the radar for Thursday. Two Hopkins peer-reviewed papers with platform-level significance for how Blackbird thinks about sex-stratified C-N-S drug development and A-L-S patient stratification, plus one Lieber Institute behavioral note. Nothing that spins out this week; a lot that shapes how you should read the next few weeks. The funnel is thin today after a heavy Monday-through-Wednesday. That's what a healthy sourcing pipeline looks like — not every day has a licensable asset on the table.
