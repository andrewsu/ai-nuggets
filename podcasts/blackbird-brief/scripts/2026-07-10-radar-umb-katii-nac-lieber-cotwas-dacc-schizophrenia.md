You're listening to the Blackbird Brief for Friday, July tenth, twenty twenty-six. This is a Sourcing Radar episode, and today the funnel converged in an unusually clean way. Three papers, all schizophrenia, all partner-institution, all published within the same seventy-two hours — one from U-M-B, two from Lieber Institute. Taken together they change the read on Blackbird's own Lieber neuropsychiatric NewCo, and one of them carries a UM Ventures playbook lesson that Matt, Eddie, and Esther should sit with. Let me walk them.

The lead is a medRxiv paper posted Monday out of the Maryland Psychiatric Research Center at the University of Maryland School of Medicine. Stephanie Hare first author, Robert Buchanan senior corresponding — Buchanan runs the M-P-R-C treatment-refractory schizophrenia group and is a fixture on Merck, Newron, Roche, and Karuna advisory boards. But the name that matters most on this paper is Robert Schwarcz, who is the P-I on the funding grant. Schwarcz is the person who has been staking out kynurenic acid biology in schizophrenia for thirty-plus years, working from a lab at U-M-B, and by this point the kynurenine pathway is essentially his intellectual franchise.

The design is a challenge study on fifty-eight patients with schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder — a double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover. Each patient came in twice. On one challenge day they got up to fifteen grams of N-acetylcysteine — plain N-A-C, the over-the-counter mucolytic — pre-treatment, followed thirty minutes later by six grams of tryptophan. On the other challenge day they got placebo followed by tryptophan. The trick is that oral tryptophan floods the kynurenine pathway on demand, forcing peripheral and central production of kynurenic acid — which we'll call KYNA — an endogenous alpha-seven-nicotinic and NMDA-receptor antagonist that Schwarcz's lab has argued for decades sits upstream of the cognitive impairment in schizophrenia. N-A-C is being used as the intervention because Schwarcz's group showed in the last couple of years that N-A-C directly inhibits the KYNA-synthesizing enzyme K-A-T-two — kynurenine aminotransferase two — at an I-C-fifty of about five hundred micromolar in recombinant enzyme assays. So this is a challenge study asking a specific question: if we hit K-A-T-two in humans with a blunt over-the-counter probe, does the biology bend.

The biology bent. The tryptophan-induced rise in serum KYNA at the four-hour timepoint was cut significantly — a p-value of point zero-zero-two, and the effect size makes the intent-to-treat picture unambiguous. Cerebral blood flow in white matter, which had been climbing under tryptophan, was blunted by N-A-C pre-treatment. And most importantly for a cognition-impairment-of-schizophrenia trial, the composite score on the MATRICS cognitive battery — the F-D-A-recognized cognition endpoint for schizophrenia — was worsening on placebo and was preserved with N-A-C. Spatial working memory performance actually improved. Symptoms did not change, safety was clean. The loop closes: block K-A-T-two, less KYNA, less alpha-seven-nicotinic and NMDA antagonism, cognitive performance holds up.

Now the commercial angle is where this paper gets sharp, and where the U-M Ventures conversation gets uncomfortable. Read the competing-interests statement at the bottom of the manuscript. Robert Schwarcz is disclosed as a scientific co-founder of a company called Kynexis, based in Naarden in the Netherlands, launched November of twenty twenty-three with a fifty-seven-million-euro Series A led by Forbion, with Ysios Capital and Sunstone participating. Kynexis licensed a purpose-built K-A-T-two inhibitor called KYN-fifty-three-fifty-six from Mitsubishi Tanabe Pharma. That molecule is currently in a Phase Two trial for cognitive impairment associated with schizophrenia, expanding into an Alzheimer's indication. The molecule is not U-M-B I-P. Kynexis got the composition-of-matter from a Japanese pharma library. But the human-proof-of-mechanism read that will de-risk KYN-fifty-three-fifty-six is exactly what this medRxiv paper delivers — using a tool compound, on U-M-B patients, run by U-M-B's world-leading K-A-T-two research group, funded by an N-I-H program-project grant to a U-M-B P-I who happens to be Kynexis' scientific co-founder.

So there are three read-outs here for Blackbird. Read one is the validation. This is a human proof-of-mechanism for a non-dopaminergic, non-muscarinic path to cognitive improvement in schizophrenia. That is a tailwind for the Lieber G-P-R fifty-two program on the same disease and the same symptom domain — it says the F-D-A endpoint moves when you hit the right node upstream of glutamate and nicotinic signaling. Read two is the competitive-clock signal. KYN-fifty-three-fifty-six will read out from Phase Two on a schedule that puts it well ahead of the Lieber G-P-R fifty-two NewCo's development-candidate declaration. If KYN-fifty-three-fifty-six works, it becomes the first-in-class asset in the space, and Blackbird's G-P-R fifty-two differentiation story has to be sharpened. Read three, and this is the harder one for the U-M Ventures × Blackbird playbook, is that the K-A-T-two franchise — with U-M-B holding the world's leading K-A-T-two biology and the world's leading K-A-T-two trialist under a single roof — was captured by an offshore V-C-led rollup because U-M-B tech-transfer did not have a molecule to compete for. Kynexis licensed from Mitsubishi Tanabe. Second item, and now we move over to the Lieber Institute, which walked two big journal papers into the newsroom on the same day, Wednesday. The first is a Nature Genetics paper — Fabiana Rossi and Leonardo Sportelli as first authors, both trainees split between the University of Bari in Italy and Lieber, with Giulio Pergola as senior corresponding and Daniel Weinberger co-senior. Weinberger is the founding director of the Lieber Institute, the person whose relationship with Third Rock built the neuropsychiatric NewCo that Blackbird co-authored, and one of the two or three people you can call a founding figure of modern schizophrenia genetics. The paper introduces two computational frameworks — they call them INGENE and MODULE — that sit on top of a standard transcriptome-wide association study. The intuition is worth understanding. Standard TWAS is entirely local — it only considers genetic variants within a megabase of the gene whose expression it is trying to predict. Pergola's frameworks push out into long-range regulation: INGENE uses one gene's genetically-predicted expression to predict distant genes it coexpresses with, and MODULE captures S-N-P association with the eigengene of whole coexpression modules. In plain terms, they are asking what long-range network effects on gene expression schizophrenia risk is running through that a nearby-only TWAS could not see.

The answer is: quite a lot. Six brain regions — amygdala, caudate, dorsal and subgenual anterior cingulate cortex, D-L-P-F-C, and hippocampus — applied to the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium wave-three schizophrenia G-W-A-S. Seven hundred sixty-six schizophrenia-associated genes total, and here is the number that matters: six hundred forty-one of those are novel — meaning previously unreported by a full generation of prior schizophrenia TWAS work. That is an eighty-four percent novel-discovery rate, which is a very unusual number for a well-worn G-W-A-S dataset. The pathway enrichment concentrates on glutamate signaling, cell-cell communication, immune processes, and brain development. The trans-only predictions — where the frameworks have no cis signal at all — are where more than half of the novel associations come from, which is the technical read: long-range trans regulation is a large fraction of the schizophrenia genetic architecture, and prior TWAS just could not see it.

For Blackbird, this is priority-hit-sheet infrastructure for the neuropsychiatric NewCo. Six hundred forty-one candidate genes is too many to work manually, but the pathway architecture — glutamate signaling as the biggest bucket, immune, brain development — nominates target classes that either complement the G-P-R fifty-two program or open second-and-third-asset conversations at the Lieber platform. And there is a distinct commercial angle underneath the target list itself: INGENE and MODULE are patentable computational methods, and re-running them on other Psychiatric Genomics Consortium wave-three datasets — bipolar, major depression, autism, O-C-D — will nominate targets in every disorder the Institute is presumably already positioning around. It is worth asking Hemaka to open a specific J-H-T-V conversation about the framework itself as a licensable substrate, separate from the schizophrenia gene list. Weinberger's team has now built a target-generation engine that could plausibly seed multiple assets across the Lieber NewCo platform.

Third item, still Lieber Institute, same Wednesday newsroom drop. Kinnary Shah and Michael Totty as first authors — both from Stephanie Hicks' Biostatistics group at the Bloomberg School — Keri Martinowich as senior corresponding, and Stephanie Hicks and Kristen Maynard as the key senior middle-authors. Hicks is the Hopkins Biostatistics faculty member who wrote spatialLIBD, the analysis stack the Lieber group uses for its spatial-transcriptomics atlases. This is Cell Reports. Paired single-nucleus RNA sequencing and 10x Visium spatial transcriptomics on the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex from ten neurotypical donors — critically, the same ten donors the group used for the D-L-P-F-C atlas they published last year, which means this is a matched-donor, cross-region comparison rather than two disconnected datasets.

Three findings a translational investor should notice. First, they confirm at spatial-molecular resolution that dorsal A-C-C has no Layer four. The agranular architecture cytoarchitecturalists have described for decades now has a molecular signature — canonical Layer-four markers like RORB and PVALB collapse into Layer five in this region. Second, they nominate two entirely distinct Layer six sublayers, six-a and six-b, with divergent marker genes from D-L-P-F-C — K-C-T-D-eight for six-a, A-D-R-A-two-A and C-R-H-B-P for six-b. That is fresh territory for target discovery in cognitive control. Third, and this is the one worth flagging for the neuropsych conversation, they map von Economo neurons — the spindle-shaped, evolutionarily-recent neurons whose loss is documented in schizophrenia, suicidal psychosis, and autism — to deep Layer five, and use mouse retroviral tracing to infer they project out to subcortical emotion-and-motivation circuits including amygdala, ventral tegmental area, and pons. That is the first molecular-level connectivity read on the V-E-N population.

Commercial angle is platform-substrate rather than direct spinout. This is the Lieber Institute laying down its second Visium atlas in a year — first D-L-P-F-C last year, now dorsal A-C-C — matched-donor cross-region, publicly released with an interactive research portal. The platform-atlas positioning strengthens the case for a spatial-omics conversation between Lieber Institute and Reza Kalhor's 3-D-E-E-P group at Hopkins B-M-E, which was the Sourcing Radar lead from July fifth. A four-hundred-micron-depth spatial method paired with the Lieber donor collection is a natural next-cycle collaboration — the paper waiting to be written. And the atlas itself nominates the dorsal A-C-C as the tissue block to profile for target discovery in cognitive control, reward processing, and pain — three symptom domains that the neuropsych NewCo pipeline should be probing for second-and-third assets beyond the G-P-R fifty-two lead.
