## Script

Good morning, Matt — this is The Blackbird Brief, Sourcing Radar for Tuesday, July twenty-first. One pick today, and I want to be straight with you up front about why. The preprint window this week has been dry for our thesis — I ran the full sweep across bioRxiv and medRxiv for the last four days, and Johns Hopkins, U-M-B, and the Lieber Institute produced no corresponding-author preprint with a commercializable asset in it. The one strictly-qualifying Hopkins preprint was worm developmental biology with no drug hook. So rather than pad the show with off-thesis filler, I'm going to spend the whole episode on the one item that genuinely clears the bar — and it's a good one, because it isn't just a paper. It's a Baltimore company, spun out of Johns Hopkins, that just dropped a new data point in a disease-modifying space that is very much heating up. This is a regional sourcing lead, and it's exactly the kind of program that should already be on Eddie's desk.

The company is Islex Therapeutics — a Hopkins spinout, headquartered right here in Baltimore, founded in 2023. The academic engine is the Dax Fu lab in the Department of Physiology, Pharmacology and Therapeutics at Johns Hopkins School of Medicine, and the CEO and co-founder is Shumei Yun, who is on the papers. Yesterday, July twentieth, the group published in the journal Diabetes — the title is "islet-targeted zinc-transporter-eight antibodies protect pancreatic beta cells from inflammatory stress." Let me give you the platform first, because that's the durable IP story, and then the new result, and then the so-what.

Here's the platform, and this is the part I've read in full. The target is zinc transporter eight — Z-n-T-eight — which is one of the major autoantigens in type 1 diabetes. The special thing about it is where it lives: zinc transporter eight presents an extracellular epitope essentially only on the surface of pancreatic beta cells, and it does so in a way that's coupled to insulin secretion — when the beta cell is stimulated by glucose and secretes insulin, more of the transporter cycles to the cell surface. So it is, in effect, a molecular zip code for the beta cell. The Fu lab cloned an antibody against the native surface epitope — they call it mab forty-three — and in the foundational 2023 paper they showed it binds with subnanomolar affinity, it's strictly conformation-specific to the folded surface loop, and when you dose it systemically into a mouse it homes to the pancreas with a pancreas-to-serum ratio in the twenties to sixties, with essentially no signal anywhere else. Glucose stimulation increased antibody uptake into the beta cell roughly thirty-fold. They then bolted a payload onto it — an interleukin-2 mutein — and showed you could deliver an immunomodulatory cargo selectively to the islet. So the core asset is a beta-cell-homing antibody chassis: a way to address the pancreatic islet specifically, in vivo, through the bloodstream, without touching the rest of the body. That's the platform, and that's the piece with real defensibility.

Now, to the new paper — and this is where the mechanism gets more interesting than a simple delivery vehicle. In the 2024 paper they'd already shown the cell-surface zinc-transporter-eight antibody could prevent and reverse autoimmune diabetes in the NOD mouse — the standard spontaneous-autoimmune-diabetes model. In yesterday's paper they describe a humanized, Fc-silenced version — they call it Isle forty-three — and the finding, as reported in the abstract, is that the antibody is internalized and then acts as a selective molecular chaperone for zinc transporter eight inside the beta cell. Practically, that means it enhances the beta cell's endoplasmic-reticulum protein-folding capacity — it relieves the proteostatic stress that inflammation piles onto these cells. And two things fall out of that which matter a lot for a drug. First, it attenuates the over-expression of H-L-A class one, which is the flag the immune system uses to recognize and kill the beta cell — so the cell becomes less visible to the autoimmune attack. Second, it robustly induces P-D-L-one, the immune checkpoint ligand, which reinforces local immune tolerance right at the islet — and they use P-D-L-one as a direct pharmacodynamic readout that the drug is engaging its target. The in-vivo headline, again from the abstract, is dose-dependent reversal of new-onset type 1 diabetes in the NOD mouse, with sustained remission after they stop dosing, plus protection of human islet-graft function in vivo. I want to flag clearly: the full text of this specific paper is paywalled and there's no preprint, so I'm going off the published abstract for the new results and the fully-read 2023 paper for the platform. But the arc is coherent — this group has been building the same beta-cell-targeting story across three papers now, and the mechanism has matured from "mask and deliver" to "repair the beta cell's proteostasis and flip the local immune switch."

So what for Blackbird. Start with why the space is hot, because the timing is the point. Disease-modifying type 1 diabetes has finally become real. Teplizumab — the anti-C-D-three antibody, sold as Tzield — is approved and validated the entire premise that you can intervene in the autoimmune process and change the course of the disease, but it's a systemic immunosuppressant, dosed early, with all the tradeoffs that implies. On the other end, the cell-replacement players — Vertex with its stem-cell-derived islet program, Sana with hypoimmune engineered islets — are putting up genuinely striking insulin-independence numbers, but those are transplant products that either require immunosuppression or complex cell engineering. Islex sits in a different, and frankly under-occupied, lane: preserve and protect the patient's own beta cells, using the islet's own surface address to deliver the therapy locally, without systemic immunosuppression and without a transplant. A humanized, Fc-silent antibody with a clean pharmacodynamic biomarker in P-D-L-one is a very developable-looking profile. That is a differentiated mechanism in a validated market, and that combination is exactly what a translational investor wants to see.

Now the candid read, because this is early. Islex is grant-stage. The disclosed funding is an N-S-F small-business award — S-B-I-R Phase 1, two hundred seventy-five thousand dollars, running from late 2024 into 2025. That's a company that has proof-of-concept and a humanized lead and essentially no institutional capital behind it yet. Which is precisely the window Blackbird exists to operate in — and precisely the window in which a Baltimore program tends to get pulled to Boston or the Bay Area if nobody local engages it. We've watched that pattern twice this month already with the neuro assets. So the actions are concrete. Eddie — this is a Blackbird BioVentures sourcing conversation, and it's time-sensitive: is Islex raising, what does the cap table and the JHTV license look like, and is there a seed or co-investment structure that fits, ideally with a BioHub bench so we keep them in Baltimore. One diligence note there — the 2023 platform paper declared no patents and no conflicts, so the question of exactly what intellectual property Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures has filed on the zinc-transporter-eight antibody chassis, and what's been licensed to Islex versus what's still with the university, is the first thing to pin down. Avi and Yixuan — the science diligence is the mechanism-novelty claim and the human-islet-graft data: is the antibody-as-intracellular-chaperone story as clean as the abstract makes it sound, and does the P-D-L-one biomarker give us a real translational readout into a first-in-human design. And add Dax Fu at Johns Hopkins Physiology, Pharmacology and Therapeutics, and Shumei Yun at Islex, to the tracked list — this is a founder-and-company pair worth a direct meeting.

That's the one that cleared the bar today. The rest of the window was a quiet cardiac-reprogramming target out of a Hopkins cardiology collaboration where the lead author sits in La Jolla — I've logged it as a watch item, not a lead. A dry preprint week is a real thing, and I'd rather give you one strong Baltimore company than three thin papers. Tomorrow the search resets. This has been The Blackbird Brief.
