Good morning, Matt — this is Sourcing Radar for Saturday, July eighteenth. Three items today. Lead is a genuine Johns Hopkins first-in-human milestone in Cancer Discovery on Thursday — Neeha Zaidi and Liz Jaffee report the phase one readout for their mutant K-RAS peptide vaccine, this time not as a treatment but as a cancer interception in people with hereditary predisposition to pancreatic cancer. Pick two is a University of Maryland cytoglobin-and-soluble-guanylate-cyclase paper posted on bioRxiv on Thursday from Paola Corti's lab in Biochemistry, which explains why inhaled nitric oxide works in hypoplastic left heart syndrome and puts a clinically validated drug class on the whiteboard for congenital heart disease. Let me start with the Zaidi paper because it is the most consequential single readout of the week for the Johns Hopkins immuno-oncology franchise. The trial is a phase one, first-in-human study of the pooled six-peptide mutant K-RAS vaccine — the same off-the-shelf product that showed a signal in metastatic microsatellite-stable colorectal cancer last month in the Jaffee lab's Nature Communications paper, and in resected pancreatic adenocarcinoma in February — but the clinical population here is different in a way that matters commercially. Enrollment is twenty individuals carrying a hereditary predisposition variant — germline BRCA-one, BRCA-two, PALB2, Lynch syndrome, familial pancreatic cancer kindreds — plus a radiographic pancreatic abnormality on surveillance imaging. These are people at high risk of eventually developing pancreatic cancer who have not yet developed it, and where current surveillance detects only a minority of precursor lesions early enough to intervene. The clinical question is whether pre-immunizing the T-cell repertoire against mutant K-RAS can generate durable T-cell surveillance that intercepts the disease before it becomes symptomatic.

The primary safety and immunogenicity endpoints both cleared. Adverse events were grade one to grade two only. Eighteen of twenty participants — that is ninety percent — mounted a mutant-K-RAS-specific T-cell response. Longitudinal T-cell receptor sequencing shows persistence of the vaccine-induced clonotypes for up to two years after the priming series, which is the durability signal the field has been waiting for from a peptide-plus-adjuvant approach. And over a median sixteen-and-a-half-month follow-up, no participant progressed to pancreatic adenocarcinoma. That last number is descriptive not statistical — twenty patients and sixteen months is not powered for an incidence endpoint — but the direction is right and the mechanism is coherent.

Why this matters for Blackbird is not the immunogenicity number. It is the paradigm shift and the spinout. The paradigm shift is that this is the first vaccine paper in solid-tumor oncology to make the case for cancer interception, not cancer treatment. Interception is a different market entirely. You are dosing people who are healthy today with an off-the-shelf product to prevent a disease that arises over ten to fifteen years. The commercial economics are payer-driven prevention, not oncology-clinic infusion revenue, and the addressable population is defined by germline sequencing not by tumor staging. The FDA has been circling around a cancer-interception framework since the AACR-Cancer Interception Task Force papers a few years back; a durable T-cell persistence signal from a peptide vaccine in a well-defined high-risk cohort gives the first-mover regulatory template for that pathway.

The spinout piece is Adventris Pharmaceuticals, which is a Y Combinator-backed Johns Hopkins commercialization vehicle for the entire mKRAS-VAX platform — Liz Jaffee is co-founder and Chief Scientific Officer, and Zaidi and Yarchoan hold equity. Adventris was already framed on treatment programs across metastatic colorectal cancer and adjuvant pancreatic. This paper is the pipeline expansion into interception, which is a distinct clinical development plan, a distinct commercial thesis, and — worth flagging — a distinct set of Series-B financing sources. Adventris is not a Blackbird company, but it is the Johns Hopkins-licensed asset that most directly touches our neoantigen-vaccine and cancer-interception conversations. Pick two is Paola Corti's cytoglobin paper posted on bioRxiv Thursday. Corti runs the Biochemistry lab at the University of Maryland School of Medicine — this is exactly the U-M-Ventures sourcing surface the co-investment agreement is designed to catch — and this paper is a beautiful mechanism story that lands directly on a druggable axis with a clinical-stage drug class already in hand for adjacent indications. Here is the setup. Hypoplastic left heart syndrome is a lethal congenital heart disease — a small, compact, underdeveloped left ventricle in newborns. It affects a few thousand babies a year in the United States, and there is no drug therapy for the underlying malformation. Clinically, neonatologists give inhaled nitric oxide to these babies to reduce pulmonary vascular resistance and stabilize cardiopulmonary function. Everyone knows inhaled nitric oxide helps, but nobody knew whether nitric oxide signaling was actually contributing to the underlying pathology or was just providing symptomatic support.

Corti's paper closes that loop. The mechanism is that cytoglobin — a heme protein previously thought to just scavenge nitric oxide — actually enhances nitric-oxide signaling through the canonical soluble-guanylate-cyclase-to-cyclic-G-M-P pathway during embryonic heart development. Loss of cytoglobin in a zebrafish knockout disrupts nitric-oxide signaling exactly during cardiac progenitor migration in the anterior lateral plate mesoderm, so the cardiac progenitors fail to converge and compact properly, producing a ventricle that is smaller, more compact, and thicker-walled — with reduced diastolic filling and reduced stroke volume. It recapitulates the human hypoplastic phenotype. Genetic knockout of the soluble guanylate cyclase alpha-one subunit — the direct downstream effector — phenocopies the cytoglobin mutant. And here is the pharmacology beat. Two rescues both work. A nitric-oxide donor rescues stroke volume and ventricular architecture in cytoglobin mutants. And the soluble-guanylate-cyclase activator Cinaciguat — Bayer's clinical-stage sGC activator, previously in phase two for acute decompensated heart failure — fully restores stroke volume and normalizes ventricular wall thickness when given during the critical early developmental window.

Why this matters for Blackbird — the soluble-guanylate-cyclase activator and stimulator class is now a real clinical franchise. Merck's Verquvo — vericiguat — is approved for heart failure with reduced ejection fraction. Bayer's Adempas — riociguat — is approved for pulmonary arterial hypertension. There is an active industry medicinal chemistry effort around next-generation sGC stimulators and activators. What this paper does is open a completely new indication — hypoplastic ventricular disease and congenital heart disease more broadly — for the class, with a mechanism narrative that connects a bedside clinical practice, inhaled nitric oxide in HLHS neonates, to a molecular pathway that the class already engages. And critically, Corti's paper is a mechanism-first paper — first author Adam Clark, senior corresponding Corti, competing-interests declaration is clean, funded by an NIH T32 training grant and an NHLBI R01. That IP-timing pattern — foundational mechanism paper first, no company or patent disclosed — is the exact moment a translational venture office should be having a conversation with the lab about provisional filings before somebody else does. And second — this is our second UMB Shock-Trauma-and-Anesthesiology-adjacent cardiovascular druggable-axis paper this week, coming off the Chao lab TLR7-ITGAM ischemic-reperfusion paper Wednesday. U-M-Ventures has a mini-portfolio of translationally-mature cardiovascular biology accumulating quickly, and the pattern is worth flagging in the next monthly with the U-M-Ventures partnership team.

Third item is the tail read — a Nature Communications paper published Friday from a Johns Hopkins Computer Science co-corresponding author, Yiqing Shen, called EnzGFM. It is an enzyme-specific protein language model — a Mamba-Transformer hybrid architecture with hierarchical pre-training — that beats standard protein language models by thirteen to twenty percent across four benchmarks including kinetic-parameter prediction, enzyme-reaction mapping, enzyme-commission-number classification, and mutation-effect assessment, at two to five times the inference throughput of transformer-only baselines. It also ships with an agent-based reasoning wrapper, EnzGFM-Agent, that enriches beneficial enzyme variants in small experimental candidate pools.

I am flagging this as a landscape signal rather than a sourcing lead for one very specific reason. Eight of the nine authors are at Chinese institutions — Henan Medical University, Shanghai Jiao Tong University, Shanghai AI Laboratory — and all three co-corresponding-author slots go to that group. Shen at Johns Hopkins is the ninth author and the single U-S-institution corresponding author, but the funding is entirely Chinese, no patents are disclosed, and the composition-of-workflow intellectual property posture is going to be led from the Chinese authorship side. This is not a Johns Hopkins-anchored sourcing conversation the way the Barrick lab protein-design paper in Nature Chemical Biology last week was. What it is instead is diligence framing. Every AI-first protein-design pitch cohort walking through Blackbird — Cyrus, Xaira, Chai Discovery, EvolvePro, and increasingly Basecamp Research and Ginkgo — is claiming platform advantages in enzyme design specifically. The EnzGFM benchmark set now becomes a public reference point that any pitch team should be pressed on. If the pitching platform cannot beat EnzGFM on the same four public benchmarks or explain why its enzyme-specific pre-training data is meaningfully differentiated, that is a diligence red flag. And the fact that the state of the art in enzyme-specific foundation models is now getting to Nature Communications from a mostly-Chinese consortium is itself a scouting datapoint on where enzyme-engineering foundation-model competitive dynamics are heading, which we should be tracking because the same trajectory is coming for antibody and effector-protein design and directly touches Aletira's SELEXON extension conversations.

And EnzGFM is the diligence question you should ask every AI-first protein-design pitch this month. Have a good Saturday.
