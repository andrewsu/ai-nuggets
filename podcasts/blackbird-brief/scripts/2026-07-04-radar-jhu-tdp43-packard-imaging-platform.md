### The Jiou Wang eLife paper — what it shows

- **The problem.** Studying TDP-43 dynamics in live cells is a mess. Every commonly-used fluorescent tag — GFP, YFP, mCherry — is bigger than TDP-43's regulatory domains and perturbs the phase separation, mislocalization, and aggregation behavior that *are* the disease phenotypes. YFP-TDP-43 forms aberrant nuclear puncta that do not appear in endogenous protein; GFP-G3BP1 in stress granules is significantly less mobile than the untagged native protein. The field has been reading its own artifacts.

- **The solution.** Genetic code expansion (GCE) with the polarity-sensitive fluorescent noncanonical amino acid **Anap** (3-((6-acetylnaphthalen-2-yl)amino)-2-aminopropanoic acid) — incorporated at a single amber-stop-codon-reassigned position using an engineered orthogonal aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase / tRNA pair. Anap is intrinsically fluorescent (no post-incorporation dye labeling), ~200 Da, and, when placed at a site that does not disturb function, is functionally invisible to the host protein. Wang's team used rational structure-guided site selection: G3BP1 F337 (avoids the NTF2 domain that drives stress-granule assembly and the RRM/RGG RNA-binding regions) and TDP-43 V100 (in the linker, preserves the two RRMs, the NLS, and the aggregation-prone C-terminal low-complexity domain).

- **The receipts.** In FRAP experiments, Anap-labeled G3BP1 recovers with **~53% mobile fraction** inside stress granules; conventional GFP-G3BP1 recovers **~33%** — GFP measurably rigidifies the condensate. Anap-labeled TDP-43 stays diffuse and nuclear at baseline (matches endogenous), forms liquid-like nuclear puncta only under mild stress, and shows **~45% FRAP recovery** — vs. YFP-TDP-43 which forms constitutive solid-like nuclear puncta with **~22% recovery**. Anap incorporation preserved TDP-43-dependent RNA splicing activity; YFP tagging did not. Anap-labeling detected previously-invisible native nuclear G3BP1 pools that antibody-based methods miss.

- **The commercial hook.** This is a **screening platform**, not a therapeutic asset. Two immediate use-cases: (1) high-content live-cell screens for compounds that dissolve TDP-43 aggregates or normalize stress-granule dynamics without touching the splicing machinery — the exact profile any TDP-43-directed ALS therapeutic needs; (2) target validation and mechanism-of-action deconvolution for pipeline programs (e.g., the XL20 compound in the same-day Nature Aging paper below, or ATXN2-directed ASOs from Ionis / Biogen). The platform is generalizable to other phase-separating disease proteins — FUS, hnRNPA1/A2, EWS — with the same GCE architecture and rational site selection. Wang is corresponding, senior, and JHU-only.

- **The IP path.** Nothing filed publicly per the eLife COI block ("no competing financial interests" declared, no patents disclosed). But the GCE-Anap-TDP-43 system + specific site-selection rules are patentable know-how; the tRNA/synthetase pair engineering represents standard chemistry-biology methods that may already be licensed. ### The Nature Aging XL20 / TDP-43 CR paper — what it shows

- **The target.** The α-helical **conserved region (CR)** of TDP-43 — residues 320-340 in the low-complexity C-terminal domain. Prior structural work (Conicella 2016; Guenther 2018) established that the CR is a conformational switch driving liquid-liquid phase separation and irreversible aggregation but not required for splicing. Deleting the CR in cells suppressed TDP-43-induced neuronal death — proof-of-concept the CR is dispensable for essential function but critical for pathology. The Wang lab (Arizona) took this as a targetability hypothesis.

- **The compound.** Structure-based virtual screening against an NMR-derived CR model identified a brain-penetrant small molecule the paper labels **XL20**. Surface plasmon resonance shows micromolar binding to full-length TDP-43; CR deletion abolishes binding; Trp334 mutation markedly reduces the SPR signal — mechanistically clean.

- **The efficacy stack.** (1) Rat primary neurons: XL20 rescues TDP-43-induced death at 6.25 μM. (2) HEK293 LLPS assays: XL20 reduces endogenous nuclear liquid-like condensate formation at 100 μM. (3) iPSC-derived human motor neurons carrying the ALS mutation p.Q331K: XL20 at 20 μM for 7 days restores spine density and cytoplasmic TDP-43 levels to isogenic-control baseline, and restores mitochondrial length and membrane potential. (4) **In vivo** — TDP-43 p.A315T transgenic ALS mice (a standard model): oral XL20 extends survival meaningfully in males, eliminates hindlimb clasping at day 70, restores rotarod performance to non-transgenic-littermate levels, and reduces motor neuron loss and muscle atrophy on histopathology. (5) **Critical control** — XL20 does **not** affect TDP-43-dependent RNA splicing (ITPR3 alternative splicing preserved). This is the fidelity signal that distinguishes the CR-targeting strategy from ASO knockdown approaches — the drug hits pathology without touching essential function.

- **The mechanism.** CR engagement suppresses TDP-43 mitochondrial localization; TDP-43 condensates normally engage mitochondria in a "kiss-and-run" pattern that becomes prolonged (and toxic) under bioenergetic stress. XL20 restores mitochondrial bioenergetics — increased basal + maximal respiratory capacity, Complex I recovery. Working model: CR-mediated LLPS drives mitochondrial toxicity → XL20 disrupts pathogenic LLPS at CR → mitochondrial function preserved → neuroprotection.

- **The IP structure — this is the sourcing point.** Ju Gao (co-first), Xinglong Wang (senior), and the majority of coauthors are at the **University of Arizona College of Pharmacy** (Department of Pharmacology and Toxicology). The primary IP — the XL20 compound structure, the CR-binding screening methodology, and any composition-of-matter claims — sits at Arizona. **JHU contribution is Pan P. Li's lab + Fan Tang** in the Division of Neurobiology, JHU Psychiatry & Behavioral Sciences; based on Li's publication history her group likely contributed the CR-mediated LLPS validation and the mitochondrial-localization biology. Li is an Assistant Professor with a durable ALS/TDP-43 program at JHU. **The Blackbird sourcing question:** does Pan Li have adjacent JHU-owned TDP-43 chemistry (fragments, screening hits, structural biology tools) that could form a NewCo behind XL20's landscape wave — or is JHU strictly a validation-partner site to Arizona? **Esther Park (Associate Director of Ventures) is the natural point** to walk that diligence via a Fellow-style scoping conversation. ### The Alzheimer PWAS paper — what it shows

- **The design.** Alzheimer's disease proteome-wide association study (PWAS) integrating publicly-available plasma cis-pQTL data (1,348 European-American + 1,385 African-American genetically-determined protein models) with AD dementia GWAS summary statistics. In parallel, colocalization + summary-data-based Mendelian randomization (SMR) for causal inference.

- **The result.** European-American PWAS identifies 18 genes with putative causal relationships to AD via cis regulation of plasma protein abundance; 13 supported by colocalization + SMR. **Four proteins not previously detected in AD GWAS**: CD55 (complement decay-accelerating factor), **LILRB1** (leukocyte immunoglobulin-like receptor B1), SCARA5 (scavenger receptor class A member 5), **SIRPA** (signal regulatory protein alpha). African-American PWAS identifies APOE as a candidate + multiple suggestive genes. LILRB1 and SIRPA show the strongest mechanistic linkage to AD in the European-American cohort.

- **Why this matters for Blackbird.** LILRB1 and SIRPα are **already drugged in oncology**. SIRPα is the receptor pair of CD47 — the entire CD47-SIRPα "don't-eat-me" I-O checkpoint axis has produced clinical-stage assets from Gilead/Forty Seven (magrolimab), Trillium/Pfizer (TTI-621, TTI-622), Arch Oncology, Innovent/AbbVie, and multiple SIRPα-Fc fusion programs. LILRB1 is a member of the leukocyte-immunoglobulin-like-receptor family with several oncology programs targeting LILRB1/2/4 (Merck, Immune-Onc Therapeutics, Nextcure). Both proteins are peripheral, druggable via biologics, and — if the causal inference in the PWAS holds up — could support **AD indication expansion of oncology-drugged targets**. This is a repurposing-thesis paper, not a novel-molecule paper — but for a Blackbird evaluating adjacent-indication swim lanes, it puts two peripherally-druggable AD targets on the map, both of which have real chemistry / biologics already in clinical development.
