## Script

Good morning, Matt — this is Sourcing Radar for Thursday, June twenty-fifth. The lead today is a Lieber Institute paper published in Nature Genetics on Monday that I want on the top of Esther and Hemaka's queue. Giulio Pergola, with Dan Weinberger as senior, built a target-discovery engine on top of L-I-B-D's post-mortem brain dataset and dropped six hundred and forty-one new schizophrenia-associated genes onto the field. This sits directly adjacent to the G-P-R fifty-two program Blackbird is already incubating with Lieber, and the platform itself is the kind of thing a pharma counterparty pays to license.

Let me walk the lead.

The premise is the limitation that has been throttling psychiatric drug discovery for a decade. Standard transcriptome-wide association studies — T-WAS — train models that predict gene expression from local, cis-acting variants near the gene, then use those models to interpret psychiatric G-W-A-S signal. The problem is that local cis variation captures only a small fraction of the heritable variance in expression. The rest lives in distant, trans-acting regulatory effects — variants that act through coexpression networks, transcription factors, splicing machinery — and trans is hard because the search space is huge and most of the signal is below the statistical threshold of any single brain dataset.

The Lieber group attacks this with two coordinated models. The first, called I-N-G-E-N-E, models how cis-regulated coexpression partners of a target gene influence that gene through trans effects — so instead of asking which S-N-Ps near gene X predict gene X, you ask which S-N-Ps near gene X's coexpressed neighbors also predict gene X. The second, called M-O-D-U-L-E, identifies S-N-Ps associated with the eigenvalue of a gene's coexpression module — the coordinated regulatory effect across a whole gene set. Both use elastic-net regression in cross-validation, and critically, they require replication across L-I-B-D, G-Tex, and CommonMind to filter out spurious trans signal. That replication step is what makes this differ from previous trans-T-WAS attempts that have not held up.

The scale matters. They train on L-I-B-D postmortem brain across six regions — dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, hippocampus, caudate, amygdala, dorsal and subgenual anterior cingulate — and apply the models to genotype data from a hundred and two thousand subjects across the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium wave three. The output is seven hundred and sixty-six schizophrenia-associated genes at F-D-R less than one percent. Six hundred and forty-one of those have not previously been reported by transcriptome-wide analysis. Cross-cohort concordance is sixty-nine percent. I-N-G-E-N-E predicts one-point-eight times more genes than cis-only models and outperforms ep-iX-can — the best prior cis-plus-epigenetic baseline. The G-W-A-S effect-size correlation roughly doubles, from low cis levels to point-two-eight to point-four-two with the trans models.

The biology converges on familiar but operationally important nodes. In dorsolateral prefrontal cortex they see synapse organization and A-M-P-A receptor trafficking; in prefrontal and cingulate regions, antigen-processing and M-H-C pathways. M-A-P-K-three sits at the top of replicable hits — it links glutamatergic transmission to synaptic plasticity. G-A-T-A-D-two-A is in the nucleosome-remodeling-and-deacetylase complex. Excitatory neurons show pronounced disease-associated expression changes; interneurons show downregulation — the excitation-inhibition imbalance story, but now with a much longer list of pursuable targets.

The Blackbird angle. Two reads.

First, this is target-discovery infrastructure that Lieber owns. Pergola's group has built — and just demonstrated in print — a platform that converts L-I-B-D's unique post-mortem cohort into a high-quality novel-target shortlist for schizophrenia. That's a platform play, not a single-asset play. Lieber's existing relationship with Boehringer Ingelheim is the validation that pharma counterparties pay for this kind of asset. The pull-through is a conversation with Tom Hyde and Dan Weinberger about whether the I-N-G-E-N-E and M-O-D-U-L-E pipeline is something Blackbird should be helping operationalize — turn into a recurring license stream or into the platform layer underneath a series of single-asset spin-outs from L-I-B-D. The existing G-P-R fifty-two program is the proof that the model — Lieber I-P, Blackbird capital, top-tier V-C — works for a single asset. The platform itself is the next-order question.

Second, the six hundred and forty-one new genes contain a sourcing list that intersects modalities Blackbird's molecular discovery team can actually pursue. A-M-P-A receptor trafficking, M-H-C-driven antigen processing in cortex, M-A-P-K-three linking glutamatergic transmission to plasticity, chromatin-remodeling factors — these are not generic schizophrenia hand-waves. They are specific nodes. Worth Avi running the six-forty-one through internal druggability filters — small-molecule tractable, antibody-tractable, splice-modulation-tractable — and pulling the intersection with what Hemaka can plausibly route into a meeting at Lieber. The candor caveat: trans-T-WAS associations are statistical predictions, not causal claims, and the authors are explicit about that. Druggability triage matters before this becomes a pipeline.

One supporting item I want on the desk, with a freshness disclosure up front. Johns Hopkins press pushed a story on Monday about a Shreesh Mysore paper on the P-L-T-i circuit — an evolutionarily ancient brainstem inhibitory region that gates selective spatial attention through the superior colliculus. The paper itself published in Nature Communications back in April; this week's Hopkins press push is what brought it back into view. Mysore's group in Psychological and Brain Sciences shows that bilaterally silencing P-L-T-i neurons in freely behaving mice doing a spatial attention task severely disrupts target selection — without breaking perception or motor execution. It's an anatomically defined attention switch, and silencing or restoring it produces a clean A-D-H-D-like phenotype and reversal. The commercial angle is first-in-class. Stimulant A-D-H-D therapy is monoamine-targeted, sixty years old, and has known abuse and tolerability limits. A circuit-level brainstem target for selective attention is mechanistically distinct, and the modality is open — small molecules acting on whatever the P-L-T-i transmitter system turns out to be, gene-therapy circuit tools, or device-based modulation. It is early — there is no annotated receptor or ligand here yet — but the I-P runway is wide open. Worth flagging to Hemaka as a future-watch on the Mysore lab; this is a name to know, not a meeting to take this week.
