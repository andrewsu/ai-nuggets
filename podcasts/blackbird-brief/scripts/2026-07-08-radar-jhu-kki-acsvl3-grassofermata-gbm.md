## Script

You're listening to the Blackbird Brief for Wednesday, July eighth, twenty twenty-six. This is a Sourcing Radar episode, and the lead today is a first-in-class metabolic target for glioblastoma coming out of Kennedy Krieger and Johns Hopkins — a novel enzyme, a small-molecule tool compound that already has a xenograft point of concept, and a story that reads like a licensable asset waiting for a company to be built around it.

Let's start there.

The lead is a preprint from Paul Watkins' group at Kennedy Krieger and Johns Hopkins School of Medicine, posted late yesterday. The target is an enzyme called very long-chain acyl-CoA synthetase three — A-C-S-V-L-three. It's one of a family of fatty-acid activating enzymes, and the Watkins lab has been mapping their physiologic roles for years. The finding that matters commercially is a clean expression differential: A-C-S-V-L-three is widely expressed across glioma cell lines and glioma tumors, and essentially absent in normal glial cells. When they knock it down or knock it out in U-87 and Mayo-22 glioma cells, the malignant phenotype collapses — slower growth, less invasion, and importantly, the cells start to differentiate. G-F-A-P, the astrocyte marker, goes up. That's a genetic validation of the target.

Then they went hunting for a small-molecule inhibitor and landed on a natural-product-derived compound called grassofermata, code name C-B-five. The pharmacology tracks the genetics almost point for point. Three-micromolar treatment reproduces the knockdown phenotype in U-87 cells — slower, differentiated, less invasive, reduced beta-oxidation of long-chain fatty acids from sixteen to twenty-four carbons. Ten micromolar is toxic to glioma cells but has no effect on normal human fibroblasts, which is the therapeutic-window read you want to see this early. In vivo, subcutaneous U-87 xenografts in immunocompromised mice treated at two milligrams per kilogram per day intraperitoneal show slowed growth and shrinking tumors by day twelve of dosing, and the animals tolerate up to thirty-two milligrams per kilogram per day with no visible side effects.

Two candid caveats before the commercial read. First, the second xenograft model — Mayo-twenty-two, a patient-derived line maintained as a subcutaneous tumor — did not show a statistically significant effect. That matters. It means we have a positive result in one immortalized line and a null in a more clinically representative model, and any diligence conversation has to open with "why the split and what does it predict for GBM heterogeneity." Second, this is subcutaneous xenograft, not orthotopic, and there's no blood-brain-barrier data in the paper. Grassofermata is a small natural product, so there's a reasonable prior it crosses, but that's a wet lab question, not an inference.

The so-what for Blackbird. Glioblastoma has had essentially no new drug of consequence in twenty years. The market is thin because the field is a graveyard, and the field is a graveyard because the biology is hard and the delivery is harder — which is exactly the shape of problem where a first-in-class metabolic vulnerability with a tool compound and a Kennedy Krieger plus Hopkins IP stack should get a meeting. This is a lab with more than a decade of A-C-S family biology, an N-I-H grant on the mechanism, a natural-product chemotype to iterate against, and a clear differentiation story — grassofermata induces astrocytic lineage commitment in G-B-M cells, which is a mechanism thesis you can build a narrative around. This is a Watkins-lab conversation worth having, and it slots naturally alongside the aSKY oncology thesis as a metabolic-target platform play. My read: worth a closer look.

Second item. A preprint from July first out of Chris Cahan's group in Hopkins Biomedical Engineering and Venu Raman in oncology and radiology at the Sidney Kimmel Comprehensive Cancer Center, co-led with Danny Loeb at Einstein and Montefiore. The paper is about D-D-X-three inhibition in pediatric bone sarcomas — osteosarcoma and Ewing sarcoma. D-D-X-three is a helicase, and its role in cancer has been complicated because it does opposite things in different cell types. In tumor cells it unwinds double-stranded R-N-A and suppresses interferon signaling. In macrophages it promotes an inflammatory program. That dichotomy is what makes it interesting: inhibiting it flips both dials in the same direction — you get double-stranded R-N-A accumulation in the tumor, a type-one interferon response, and macrophages polarizing toward the M-one pro-inflammatory state.

The compound they use is R-K-thirty-three — a Hopkins-invented small-molecule D-D-X-three inhibitor from the Raman lab. This is not a new molecule, and that's the point: the composition-of-matter is already inside the Hopkins estate. What's new in this paper is the immuno-combination story. R-K-thirty-three plus mifamurtide — the only immunostimulant approved for osteosarcoma, and only in Europe, not the U-S — in immune-competent mouse models produces a significant reduction in metastatic burden. And critically, the effect is conditional on M-Y-C status. In M-Y-C-overexpressing backgrounds, the combination fails. In non-M-Y-C-amplified osteosarcoma, it works. That is a biomarker-driven patient-selection strategy landing in the same package as the pharmacology, which is what modern oncology diligence is looking for. Pediatric metastatic osteosarcoma survival has been around twenty-five percent for decades. A Hopkins-owned small molecule with a rational immuno-combo and a genomic biomarker is a defensible spin-out shape. Worth a closer look — and if the Raman lab hasn't been formally engaged on a company-formation conversation, this is the kind of paper that should trigger one.

Third item, and this one is from University of Maryland School of Medicine — from Tim Luetkens' lab in Microbiology and Immunology and the U-M-G-C-C-C Fannie Angelos Cellular Therapeutics G-M-P Lab, with Ken Dietze as first author. Published in *Signal Transduction and Targeted Therapy*, one of the Nature portfolio journals, in mid-June. The mechanism is a failure mode of C-A-R T-cell therapy that has been suspected but not cleanly targeted. Using lattice light-sheet microscopy with the Upadhyaya lab at Maryland College Park, they show that C-A-R T-cells physically rip fragments of the target antigen off tumor cell surfaces and put those fragments onto themselves — a form of C-A-R-mediated trogocytosis. The consequence is dual damage: the tumor cell drops target antigen and dodges further killing, and the C-A-R T-cell becomes shorter-lived. That mechanism is on the leading list of hypotheses for why most C-A-R T-cell relapses happen within five years.

What they found next is what makes this a Blackbird conversation. Cathepsin-B — a lysosomal protease — is required for the ripping process. Block cathepsin-B, and the C-A-R T-cells stop stripping tumor antigen, stay active longer, and control tumor better in animal models. That is a clean small-molecule combo thesis that layers onto every commercial C-A-R T-cell product without touching the cell engineering — the CD-nineteens, the BCMAs, and the next-generation CD-two-twenty-nines that U-M-G-C-C-C is running in its own first-in-human trial. Cathepsin-B inhibitors are a well-trodden medicinal-chemistry space, and there's an obvious repurposing angle plus a clean new-composition-of-matter angle for a selective, cell-permeable, biologically stable series. The U-M-B I-P sits inside the U-M Ventures pipeline, which is the same co-investment agreement that produced aSKY. Worth a closer look — and worth flagging to Sasha Krupnick's team as an adjacency that touches U-M-B cell-therapy operations.

Three picks, three institutions on the map — Kennedy Krieger and Hopkins, Hopkins B-M-E plus S-K-C-C-C, and University of Maryland School of Medicine. The two Hopkins items are both small-molecule oncology with defensible mechanism-first stories; the U-M-B item is a cell-therapy adjunct that expands the aSKY-adjacent surface area. That's the radar for Wednesday.
