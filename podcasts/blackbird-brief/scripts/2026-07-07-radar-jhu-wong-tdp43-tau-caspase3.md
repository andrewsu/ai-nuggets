## Context (not spoken)

### What the paper shows — mechanism

- **Model system 1 — genetic mouse.** *Tau4R;CaMKII-CreER;Tardbp^f/f^* triple-genotype forebrain conditional model. The floxed *Tardbp* allele plus tamoxifen-induced CaMKII-driven CreER selectively deletes TDP-43 in forebrain excitatory neurons; the *Tau4R* transgene provides a genetic tau-seeding substrate (four-repeat microtubule-binding domain of human tau). This is Wong's standard forebrain conditional TDP-43 knockout backbone that his lab has run for a decade in the ALS/FTD/AD context; the *Tau4R* transgene extension is what makes this paper new — it lets them do a direct in-vivo test of whether TDP-43 loss-of-function *causally* amplifies pathological tau conversion.

- **Model system 2 — human iPSC cortical neurons.** TDP-43 loss-of-function was induced in human induced-pluripotent-stem-cell-derived cortical neurons. This is the human-cell readout that lets them run the mechanistic dissection at scale — TDP-43-dependent cryptic splicing readouts, followed by caspase-3-mediated tau endoproteolysis measurements. The temporal order they establish is important: **cryptic splicing precedes caspase-3-dependent tau cleavage.** That's the causal direction — TDP-43 LOF happens first, cryptic splicing follows, then caspase-3 activation, then tau proteolytic cleavage.

- **The mechanism claim in three steps.** First — TDP-43 LOF in forebrain neurons exacerbates tauopathy-dependent brain atrophy in mice. Second — those vulnerable neurons are specifically sensitive to caspase-3-dependent cleavage of endogenous tau; that cleavage generates a tau species that seeds further tauopathy. Third — using the *Tau4R* genetic seeding approach, they show that the amount of tau seed correlates with caspase-3-dependent tau cleavage, accelerated tauopathy, and the loss of vulnerable neurons deficient in TDP-43. So: TDP-43 LOF → cryptic splicing → caspase-3 → tau endoproteolysis → tau seed → tauopathy amplification → selective vulnerable-neuron loss.

- **What "vulnerable neurons" means here.** Wong's lab has been building this concept across the 2020-2026 literature: TDP-43 LOF does not kill all neurons equally. Specific populations of forebrain excitatory neurons in the entorhinal cortex, hippocampus, and cortex — the exact populations that die in AD — are selectively sensitive. This paper extends the vulnerability concept into an *interaction* with tau seed dose: more tau seed × TDP-43 LOF = accelerated proteotoxicity + selective neuron loss. Two-hit model that maps directly onto AD/ADRD neuroanatomy.

### Why this is a sourcing signal for Blackbird — indication expansion

- **The TDP-43 platform's original addressable market was ALS/FTD.** Roughly 30,000 ALS patients in the US at any given time plus ~50,000 FTD patients. Small-population indications; premium orphan pricing is the model. The dominant clinical assets — Amylyx-Ionis TDP-43-splicing ASOs, QurAlis QRL-101 in ALS, Trace Neuroscience STMN2-cryptic-exon ASO in ALS — all price against those small populations.

- **This paper reframes the addressable market.** If TDP-43 LOF is *upstream* of tau proteotoxic conversion in AD/ADRD — meaning TDP-43 dysfunction is a proximal driver of tau pathology in the ~40% of AD patients who have TDP-43 co-pathology at autopsy (well-documented in the LATE-NC literature) — then a TDP-43-splicing-restoring or cryptic-exon-blocking asset acquires **Alzheimer's-scale market access**. That is a 5–10-million-patient reframe. Same molecule, dramatically bigger opportunity.

- **The specific target-nomination this paper puts on the table.** Two possible therapeutic entry points, both drug-hunt-actionable. Entry point one — restore TDP-43 splicing function, which is the strategy already anchored by the ASO camp (Ionis-Amylyx-QurAlis-Trace). Entry point two — the *newer* nomination, block the caspase-3-mediated tau endoproteolysis specifically in TDP-43-deficient neurons. Caspase-3 has been considered undruggable in neurodegeneration for a long time because global caspase-3 inhibition breaks physiological apoptosis; but Wong's paper implies a *context-specific* caspase-3 vulnerability that in principle could be addressed by allosteric or substrate-competitive tau-cleavage inhibitors, or by pathway-selective caspase-3 modulators. Novel target. Fresh IP.

Published Friday, July fourth, in Molecular Neurodegeneration. The title — TDP forty-three dysfunction facilitates the pathological conversion of tau.

Philip Wong is a name the audience knows. His lab is the group that showed back in twenty fifteen that TDP forty-three normally represses cryptic exons in a large panel of neuronal RNAs, and that loss of that repression — cryptic splicing failure — is a proximal event in A-L-S and frontotemporal dementia. Every TDP forty-three-directed drug program in the clinic today — the Amylyx-Ionis A-S-O splicing-restoration platform, QurAlis's TDP forty-three splicing program, the Trace Neuroscience S-T-M-N-two cryptic-exon program — traces its scientific origin back to that Wong lab discovery.

What is new this week is a mechanism paper that extends that story into Alzheimer's. Here is what they show, and why it matters.

Prior autopsy literature already established that roughly forty percent of Alzheimer's patients have TDP forty-three co-pathology alongside tau tangles and beta-amyloid plaques. The clinical read on that co-pathology is that it makes the disease worse — faster decline, worse memory phenotype, more atrophy. What the field did not have was a causal mechanism connecting TDP forty-three dysfunction to accelerated tau pathology. Was TDP forty-three co-pathology a passive consequence of the tauopathy, or was TDP forty-three itself driving the tauopathy?

This paper makes a clean causal claim for the second option. They deleted TDP forty-three in mouse forebrain excitatory neurons — the specific neuronal population that dies in Alzheimer's — using a conditional CaM-kinase-two CreER driver, and they combined that with a genetic tau-seeding approach expressing the four-repeat microtubule-binding domain of human tau. What they see is exacerbated brain atrophy, exacerbated tauopathy, and — this is the key mechanism — selective loss of the vulnerable neurons, driven by a caspase-three-dependent proteolytic cleavage of endogenous tau. In human i-P-S-C-derived cortical neurons, they replicate the same causal chain — TDP forty-three loss-of-function first, then TDP forty-three-dependent cryptic splicing, then caspase-three-mediated endoproteolysis of tau. The temporal order matters. Cryptic splicing precedes the tau cleavage. That means the tau proteotoxic conversion is *downstream* of TDP forty-three dysfunction, not parallel to it.

So the picture is — TDP forty-three loss-of-function drives cryptic splicing, cryptic splicing activates caspase-three-dependent cleavage of tau, that cleavage generates a tau species that seeds further tauopathy, and the tau seed selectively kills the neurons that are already TDP forty-three-deficient. It is a two-hit interaction — the amount of tau seed and the TDP forty-three-deficient neuron pool both scale together to drive selective vulnerable-neuron loss.

Here is the commercial reframe. The TDP forty-three drug-discovery platform was priced against an A-L-S plus F-T-D addressable market — call it eighty thousand patients in the U-S. This paper is the mechanism argument that the TDP forty-three platform's real addressable market may be the Alzheimer's plus Alzheimer's-related dementia population that carries TDP forty-three co-pathology — roughly forty percent of the AD population, which is a five-to-ten-million-patient reframe. Same drug modality — a splicing-restoration A-S-O or a small molecule that blocks the caspase-three-tau cleavage in TDP forty-three-deficient neurons — but a dramatically larger opportunity if the mechanism holds.

Two therapeutic entry points come off this paper. Entry point one — restore TDP forty-three splicing function, which is the existing ASO camp's playbook. That camp already exists — Ionis-Amylyx-QurAlis-Trace. What is new is that the AD indication now becomes their expansion play, and Blackbird's role there is either sourcing a differentiated platform or partnering around a JHU splicing-restoration filing. Entry point two — the newer and less-crowded nomination — block the caspase-three-mediated tau cleavage that this paper identifies in the TDP forty-three-deficient neuron context. Caspase-three has been considered undruggable for neurodegeneration for years because global caspase-three inhibition breaks physiological apoptosis. But a *context-specific* caspase-three vulnerability, addressable by a substrate-competitive or allosteric approach, is a different medicinal-chemistry conversation. That is the fresh IP angle here.
