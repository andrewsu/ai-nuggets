## Script

This is the Blackbird Brief Sourcing Radar for Thursday, July twenty-third, and I'll be straight with you up front: it was another thin week on the Johns Hopkins, U-M-B, and Lieber preprint servers, so today is a short, two-item radar — one that is the most company-shaped thing I saw all week but comes with a caveat I'll flag honestly, and one fresh Johns Hopkins paper I was able to read cover to cover.

Let me set the scene. The four-day bioRxiv and medRxiv sweep for corresponding authors at our partner institutions turned up almost nothing with a real, ownable asset attached — a worm developmental-biology paper, a preprint on maturing stem-cell heart muscle whose full text hasn't rendered yet, and some clinical and epidemiology work with no drug hook. So that's the third straight lean preprint week. Both of today's picks are peer-reviewed Johns Hopkins work, and I'm going to be precise about how deeply I've been able to vet each one.

Let me start with the item I think deserves the most attention, which is a new druggable cancer target called D-C-T-P-P-one, out of a genuinely heavyweight Johns Hopkins team — Jim Berger, who directs the Institute for Basic Biomedical Sciences, and Vasan Yegnasubramanian, who runs inHealth precision medicine, with Bill Nelson, the director of the Kimmel Cancer Center, on the paper. It's in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences. And here is the caveat I promised, because it matters for how you weight this: the paper published in mid-June and the Hopkins newsroom pushed it last week, which is how it surfaced. The full body is behind a paywall — no preprint, not yet in PubMed Central — so I'm briefing you off the authors' own significance summary, the abstract, and the press materials, not a full read. Treat this as a flag-and-chase lead, not a finished diligence memo.

With that said, here's why it's worth chasing. D-C-T-P-P-one is a housekeeping enzyme that patrols the cell's pool of DNA building blocks and chews up chemically modified ones so they don't get misincorporated into the genome. That sounds obscure until you realize what it does to a whole class of chemotherapy: decitabine, and its cousin azacitidine, are hypomethylating agents used in myelodysplastic syndrome and acute myeloid leukemia, and they only work by getting incorporated into DNA. D-C-T-P-P-one is, in effect, a built-in resistance factor — it degrades exactly the modified nucleotides those drugs depend on. So if you inhibit D-C-T-P-P-one, you should make decitabine hit harder. The team ran a ten-thousand-compound screen, found several inhibitor classes with sub-micromolar potency, and — this is the part that makes it a company rather than a hypothesis — solved crystal structures of three distinct chemical classes sitting in the enzyme's active pocket, which gives you a structural starting point for medicinal chemistry. Then in prostate cancer cells they showed real synergy: the lead inhibitors plus decitabine blocked tumor-cell growth, and knocking the enzyme up or down confirmed the effect was on-target.

Here's the so-what for Blackbird. This is a resistance-breaker play on top of already-approved oncology drugs — you're not inventing a new mechanism from scratch, you're unlocking a class that's been stuck at modest efficacy, with an obvious installed market in myelodysplastic syndrome and A-M-L and a demonstrated path into solid tumors like prostate. The IP tell is the important one: the competing-interests statement says the authors have already filed for a provisional patent. So the Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures conversation is live, and the value you'd capture is the new chemical entity — the inhibitor — layered onto a generic backbone. The team is a serious founding and scientific-advisory nucleus; Berger brings the structural biology, Yegnasubramanian and Nelson bring the cancer and the clinical translation. The honest diligence questions before this becomes real: how novel is the chemical matter, because the authors describe both new and previously-known inhibitor classes, so freedom-to-operate is a real question; and combination economics, because building a business around potentiating someone else's generic takes a clear regulatory and pricing story. But as a sourcing flag, this is the most fundable shape I saw this week. Action: Eddie on the J-H-T-V filing status and whether Berger and Yegnasubramanian are thinking about a vehicle; Avi and Yixuan to pull the full paper and the patent once it publishes and pressure-test the chemistry and the freedom-to-operate.

Now the paper I could actually read in full, and it's a different kind of item. This is Sanjay Jain's group at Johns Hopkins, published this week in Nature Communications, on host-directed treatments for tuberculous meningitis. TB meningitis is the deadliest form of tuberculosis, and the damage is driven less by the bacteria than by a runaway immune response in the brain — the standard of care adds a steroid, dexamethasone, which cuts mortality by about a quarter but does nothing for neurological damage. So Jain's team took twelve immune-modulating drugs that are already approved or in trials for other things, and tested them as add-on therapy in two systems: a mouse model of TB meningitis with clinical-style endpoints — mortality, neurological deficits, brain M-R-I — and, notably, a newly built immune-vascularized human brain organoid model of the disease, backed by patient blood cells. Four drugs beat the steroid on both mortality and neurological deficits: bestatin, the leukemia drug imatinib, the P-D-E-four inhibitor roflumilast, and a PARP inhibitor. The whole effect runs through calming neuroinflammation.

The so-what here, and I want to be candid, is more about a platform than a product. On the drug side, these are all repurposed, mostly generic agents — the paper declares no competing interests and no patent — so there's no ownable composition of matter, and TB meningitis is a global-health indication that lives on B-A-R-D-A and foundation money, not venture returns. That's not a Blackbird spin-out. What is interesting to us is the tool they had to build to do this work: an immune-vascularized human brain organoid model of neuroinflammation. That is a licensable screening platform, and it sits right next to the kinds of Baltimore imaging-and-model chassis we've been tracking as underwriteable cross-institution assets. It's also a relationship worth having — Jain's group pairs infectious disease with molecular imaging, and that imaging capability has its own IP history. So my read: log it as a platform-watch and a relationship item, not a program. If the organoid model gets spun out or licensed as a neuroinflammation screening tool, that's the piece we'd want a look at.

That's the radar for a lean Thursday: a heavyweight Johns Hopkins cancer target with a provisional already filed as the lead to chase — caveated because I've only seen the summary and the press — and a fresh, fully-read TB-meningitis paper whose real Blackbird value is the brain-organoid platform underneath it. One clear action, one thing to watch. I'm back tomorrow.
