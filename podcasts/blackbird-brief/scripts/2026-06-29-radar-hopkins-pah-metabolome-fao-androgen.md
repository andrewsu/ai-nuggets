# Sourcing Radar — Johns Hopkins PAH metabolome: a fatty-acid-oxidation axis predicts adverse RV-PV physiology, and a transpulmonary 5α-reduced androgen gradient is protective — two pharmacological lanes in the PAH market that sotatercept did not close

## Context (not spoken)

### What the paper actually shows

- WGCNA (weighted gene co-expression network analysis) applied to untargeted Metabolon HD4 platform metabolomics on plasma from PVDOMICS (n=412 PAH) and replicated on CALIPSO (n=89, Johns Hopkins single-center cohort). 16 distinct, biologically coherent metabolite modules; cross-cohort replication recovered the core architecture and conserved hub metabolites.
- Tripartite fatty-acid handling axis: brown module (substrate pool — long-chain unsaturated FAs, odd-chain FAs, medium-chain FAs); blue module (long-chain acylcarnitines + dicarboxylic FAs — products of mitochondrial β-oxidation + peroxisomal ω-oxidation); green module (acyl-glycine / acyl-glutamine conjugates + hydroxyacylcarnitines — disposal products). Sequential FA handling from uptake → oxidation → disposal.
- Blue + green modules associate with adverse hemodynamics. Per IQR increase in blue module eigenmetabolite: PVR +1.69 WU [1.12–2.26], mPAP +6.24 mmHg [4.42–8.06], lower RVEF -2.05%/IQR. Per IQR green: PVR +1.26 WU, mPAP +4.51 mmHg, RVEF -2.61%/IQR. Persisted with fluid + vasodilator challenge.
- Acylcholine module: enriched in long-chain acylcholines, co-clustered with phospholipid intermediates; associated with **lower** pulmonary pressures and improved RV function. Prior Simpson work showed higher serum acylcholines protective against PAH development in scleroderma cohort. Biology incompletely defined but reproducibly protective.
- Adrenal steroid axis split into two related modules. Yellow (precursor; contains DHEA-S) — *not* associated with RV-PV indices in this analysis. Cyan (5α-reduced androgen derivatives — downstream conversion products) — clearly protective: associated with lower right-sided pressures and improved RV-PA coupling.
- Transpulmonary gradient analysis (samples at multiple sites in pulmonary circulation + peripheral vein): yellow precursor module shows transpulmonary uptake; cyan reduced-androgen module shows release. Pulmonary circulation is an active site of steroid conversion. This is mechanistically novel — suggests the pulmonary vasculature itself is doing 5α-reduction of androgen precursors, and that the efficiency of that conversion, not the precursor pool, determines the protective effect.
- Direct corroboration via EDIPHY trial: randomized placebo-controlled crossover study of DHEA supplementation in PAH showed no improvement in RV longitudinal strain and only modest, inconsistent secondary endpoints despite robust circulating DHEA-S increases. Consistent with the paper's reading: supplementing the precursor doesn't help because conversion is the rate-limiting step. The therapeutic target is the conversion machinery, not the precursor.
- Bile acid modules (salmon = primary conjugated, tan = secondary): both increased in coherence in PAH disease state vs. healthy controls and associated with adverse hemodynamic and RV functional measures. Authors discuss hepatic congestion as a confound but note Harvey et al recent paper on NCOA7-deficiency / lysosomal dysfunction / transpulmonary oxysterol-bile-acid metabolism intrinsic to pulmonary endothelium in PAH. Bile acid metabolism may be both consequence (congestion) and contributor (endothelial dysfunction).
- Odd-chain fatty acid (OCFA) hubs in the brown substrate pool — 17-, 19-, 15-carbon — have not previously been linked to PAH. Terminal product of OCFA β-oxidation is propionyl-CoA, which can regenerate TCA cycle when succinyl-CoA is depleted (the prior CALIPSO work showed TCA intermediates depleted in PH with RV dysfunction). Authors hypothesize OCFA as adaptive substrate switching under RV pressure-overload stress — i.e., a compensatory anaplerosis pathway.

### Market and competitive context

- US PAH prevalence ~50-60k. Established standard of care: ERAs (bosentan / ambrisentan / macitentan = Opsumit, J&J), PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil / tadalafil), prostacyclin pathway agents (epoprostenol / treprostinil = Tyvaso DPI, United Therapeutics; selexipag = Uptravi, J&J), riociguat (sGC stimulator, Bayer Adempas), and the new addition sotatercept (Winrevair, Merck — activin-IIA ligand trap, approved March 2024, peak-sales forecast $3-5B+; STELLAR + ZENITH event-driven trials).
- Despite all of the above, 5-yr mortality in PAH still ~40-50%. RV dysfunction remains the primary determinant of mortality. **No** therapy targets fatty-acid-oxidation pathology in PAH. No therapy targets the steroid conversion pathway directly — DHEA supplementation (EDIPHY) failed, vindicating Simpson's conversion-is-rate-limiting framing.
- Pharmacology surface that exists today and can be repurposed or rebuilt:
    - **FAO partial inhibitors**: trimetazidine (Servier, approved in EU/Asia for angina, not FDA approved), ranolazine (Gilead Ranexa, approved FDA for angina) — both are partial FAO inhibitors with cardiac history. Trimetazidine inhibits long-chain 3-ketoacyl-CoA thiolase; ranolazine has FAO partial inhibition as one of several activities. Repurposing in PAH has been tried in small underpowered trials but never properly run in a biomarker-enriched cohort. Etomoxir (CPT-1 inhibitor) failed historically for hepatotoxicity. New chemistry on CPT-1 / FAO-pathway selective inhibitors is open.
    - **5α-reductase inhibitors**: finasteride (Merck Proscar/Propecia, generic), dutasteride (GSK Avodart, generic) — approved for BPH and androgenetic alopecia. Inhibit 5α-reduction, *opposite* directionality of what the Simpson data suggest is needed in PAH. The biology here is interesting in the reverse — could **activating** or facilitating 5α-reductase activity in pulmonary endothelium produce the protective downstream androgen profile the data flag? This is a novel pharmacology question. Sex hormone biology in PAH has been a long-running PAH research theme (PAH is 3-4x more prevalent in women, but men have worse outcomes; the so-called estrogen paradox) — this paper redirects the question from precursors to conversion efficiency.
    - **Biomarker enrichment**: FAO module abundance as a patient-stratification biomarker for clinical trials of next-generation PAH therapeutics. Direct trial-enrichment use even before any new drug — could be partnered into Merck/sotatercept follow-on studies, United Therapeutics ralinepag development, Tenax levosimendan (FREEDOM trials).
- The Harvey et al NCOA7 paper Simpson cites in the discussion is worth understanding — NCOA7 deficiency / lysosomal dysfunction / transpulmonary oxysterol and bile acid metabolism / pulmonary endothelium. That paper opens a separate lane on lysosomal-endothelial biology in PAH which is also pharmacologically interesting (lysosomal acidification / V-ATPase modulators, oxysterol metabolism enzymes).

## Script

Good morning, Matt — this is Sourcing Radar for Monday, June twenty-ninth. One pick today, because it is a good one and it deserves the airtime.

Catherine Simpson, Paul Hassoun, Steve Mathai, and the broader Hopkins pulmonary group posted a preprint last Sunday on the metabolomic architecture of pulmonary arterial hypertension. The paper is a network analysis on the PVDOMICS consortium — four hundred twelve P-A-H patients across multiple national sites — replicated on the Hopkins CALIPSO biobank, eighty-nine patients. It is a clinical-translational paper, not a drug discovery paper, but it points at two pharmacological lanes that the P-A-H market did not close even after the sotatercept approval. Let me walk it.

Quick framing on the market first. P-A-H is roughly fifty thousand U-S patients. The standard of care today is four mechanism classes — endothelin-receptor antagonists, P-D-E-five inhibitors, prostacyclin-pathway agents, soluble guanylate cyclase stimulators — plus sotatercept, Merck's activin-trap, the first disease-modifying agent to enter the market in a decade. Sotatercept is good. Five-year mortality is still roughly forty to fifty percent. R-V dysfunction is what kills these patients, and no approved therapy directly addresses R-V metabolism. That is the gap this paper points at.

What Simpson and the group did is apply weighted gene co-expression network analysis to untargeted metabolomic data on four hundred twelve patients. They identify sixteen distinct, biologically coherent metabolite modules and they replicate the core architecture on the Hopkins CALIPSO cohort. The biology is the part to focus on.

There are two clean signals.

The first is a tripartite fatty-acid-oxidation axis. They find one module of fatty-acid substrate pools, a second module of long-chain acylcarnitines and dicarboxylic acids — the downstream products of mitochondrial beta-oxidation and peroxisomal omega-oxidation — and a third module of acyl-glycine and acyl-glutamine conjugates, which are disposal products of beta-oxidation. The handling is sequential. The substrate pool itself is not strongly associated with hemodynamics. The oxidation and disposal modules are strongly associated with adverse hemodynamics. Per interquartile increase in the oxidation module, pulmonary vascular resistance goes up by one-point-seven Wood units and mean pulmonary artery pressure goes up by six-point-two millimeters of mercury. R-V ejection fraction goes down by two percent. These are direct disease-severity associations, with high statistical confidence, persisting across fluid and vasodilator challenges, and replicating on the CALIPSO cohort.

The translational reading is that increased flux through fatty-acid oxidation pathways, with incomplete oxidation and engagement of alternative disposal, is a metabolic signature of a struggling pressure-overloaded right ventricle. That is consistent with a decade of preclinical work on R-V substrate switching, but this paper puts the human signal on the table in a national-consortium cohort with replication.

The second signal is a steroid metabolism axis, and this one is sharper. The adrenal steroid axis splits into two related modules. One contains the precursor — D-H-E-A-sulfate, the canonical PAH sex-hormone biomarker that has been studied for fifteen years. The other contains five-alpha-reduced androgen derivatives — the downstream conversion products. In this analysis, the precursor module is *not* associated with R-V-pulmonary-vascular indices. The downstream conversion module *is* clearly associated, and the direction is protective — lower right-sided pressures, improved R-V-pulmonary-artery coupling. They then do a transpulmonary gradient analysis, sampling plasma at multiple sites across the pulmonary circulation, and they show that the pulmonary vasculature is actively taking up sex-hormone precursors and releasing reduced androgen derivatives. The pulmonary circulation is doing the conversion.

This is the mechanistic redirect that matters. The EDIPHY trial — randomized, placebo-controlled, crossover, D-H-E-A supplementation in P-A-H — showed no benefit in R-V longitudinal strain and only inconsistent secondary endpoints, despite robust D-H-E-A-sulfate increases. The Simpson framing explains the failure cleanly. Supplementing the precursor does not help if the rate-limiting step is the conversion. The therapeutic target is the conversion machinery — five-alpha-reductase or downstream pathway enzymes in the pulmonary endothelium — not the circulating precursor pool. That is a novel, actionable pharmacology target lane that nobody is currently developing on for P-A-H.

Three reads for Blackbird.

First — this is a sourcing lead. Blackbird does not currently have a P-A-H program in the portfolio. P-A-H is a large, underserved cardiovascular market with one new disease-modifier in fifteen years and still forty to fifty percent five-year mortality. Hopkins has a structural advantage in this disease — they co-lead the national PVDOMICS consortium, they own the CALIPSO single-center biobank, and they have the right-heart-catheterization plus cardiac M-R-I plus Metabolon plasma data integration in-house. That is the kind of clinical-translational scaffolding that produces investor-ready preclinical packages on a six- to eighteen-month horizon. Worth Esther and Hemaka adding the Simpson-Hassoun-Mathai group to the partner-institution radar even if no immediate action follows.

Second — two pharmacological lanes flagged in the data. One, fatty-acid-oxidation pathway modulators in P-A-H, with the F-A-O module as a clinical-trial enrichment biomarker. Trimetazidine and ranolazine are existing partial F-A-O inhibitors with cardiac history but neither has been run in a properly biomarker-enriched P-A-H cohort. Etomoxir failed historically for hepatotoxicity, which leaves novel chemistry on selective C-P-T-one inhibitors or 3-ketoacyl-CoA thiolase inhibition as open territory. Two, five-alpha-reductase pathway pharmacology in the pulmonary endothelium — *opposite* directionality from the prostate finasteride-dutasteride class. This is novel pharmacology, no clean precedent, and the biology question is whether facilitating or augmenting pulmonary-endothelial five-alpha-reduction is tractable as a small-molecule or biologic target. Both lanes are pre-target-validation today and would benefit from an in-vivo P-A-H rodent program plus a P-K-P-D readout in a CALIPSO-style biobank. Exactly the kind of work a Blackbird translational grant funds.

Third — diligence question for Hemaka. What is the J-H-T-V relationship with the Simpson-Hassoun group at the moment? Are they shopping a target hypothesis to a strategic, are they queuing a follow-on paper with in vivo readouts, or is the program at the open-territory stage where a Blackbird-grant-supported in-vivo plus lead-discovery package would be the right intervention? The answer determines whether this is a near-term sourcing conversation or a one-year-out watch-item. Worth a courtesy J-H-T-V-routed outreach this week to find out.

Three — Avi to internal scope on whether either pharmacology lane has any composition-of-matter angle that a Blackbird-incubated chemistry effort could anchor, separate from the legacy F-A-O partial-inhibitor or finasteride-class molecules.
