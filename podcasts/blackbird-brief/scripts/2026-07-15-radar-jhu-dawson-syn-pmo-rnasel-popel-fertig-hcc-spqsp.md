## Script

You're listening to The Blackbird Brief for Wednesday, July fifteenth, twenty twenty-six. This is a Sourcing Radar episode, and the Baltimore preprint pipeline has refilled on schedule. The lead is a Ted Dawson lab paper in yesterday's Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences that introduces an entirely new class of oligonucleotide modality for alpha-synuclein lowering, with the provisional composition-of-matter application filed jointly by Johns Hopkins and the University of Chicago. Two portfolio-adjacent Baltimore reads behind it — a Popel-Fertig computational-oncology paper in the same issue that carries a rare Johns Hopkins-and-U-M-B co-corresponding structure and a first public disclosure of Popel equity in OptaNova Pharma; and a Richman Center iPSC memantine platform paper out of Vasiliki Mahairaki and Constantine Lyketsos with three Lieber Institute contributors on the roster.

Pick one, and today's headline. Ning Wang first author, Xiaobo Mao and Jingxin Wang co-corresponding, Ted Dawson senior. Johns Hopkins Neurology and the Institute for Cell Engineering, with Kennedy Krieger's Mingyao Ying on the roster. Title reads "Targeted alpha-synuclein messenger-R-N-A degradation by phosphorodiamidate morpholino-based R-N-A-degrading chimeras." What that means in company-formation language is an entirely new class of alpha-synuclein-lowering oligonucleotide, and the provisional patent is filed today at Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures with University of Chicago as co-owner, and explicitly not yet licensed to anyone.

Here's what's new. Every alpha-synuclein-lowering program that has reached humans so far runs on antisense oligonucleotides — the R-Nase H mechanism Biogen and Ionis use across their neuro pipeline. The Biogen alpha-syn A-S-O failed its Phase 1 futility read last year and is off the board. Prasinezumab and cinpanemab as antibodies already washed out at Phase 2. Alpha-synuclein is a decade-long graveyard for direct-binding modalities against an intrinsically disordered target. The field has been looking for orthogonal ways in.

What the Dawson lab has built is essentially a RIBOTAC in phosphorodiamidate morpholino chemistry. You take a P-M-O — the Sarepta backbone, the safest and most translationally established oligonucleotide chemistry we have — and conjugate it to a small-molecule warhead that binds and activates R-Nase L. The morpholino tags the five-prime untranslated region of the S-N-C-A messenger-R-N-A; the warhead recruits R-Nase L to that site; the endogenous cytoplasmic nuclease cuts the transcript. That is two orthogonal differentiations at once. P-M-O backbone rather than gapmer A-S-O, and cytoplasmic R-Nase L recruitment rather than nuclear R-Nase H. Either differentiator alone is a modality story a translational investor will take a meeting on. Together they define a class.

The preclinical package is complete for a seed round. A screen of nine chimeras narrowed to a lead they call four-D-one. Dose-dependent S-N-C-A knockdown at both the messenger-R-N-A and protein level in a human cell line, and critically, R-Nase-L-dependent — knock out R-Nase L in the cell and the effect vanishes, which is the exact target-engagement control an F-D-A reviewer will ask for. The effect replicates in primary cortical neurons from a humanized S-N-C-A mouse, and in patient-relevant induced-pluripotent-stem-cell-derived cortical neurons. Four-D-one blocks prion-like seeding by patient-derived alpha-synuclein fibrils, rescues fibril-driven cytotoxicity, and lowers alpha-synuclein messenger-R-N-A in vivo in the humanized S-N-C-A mouse. That is the exact preclinical sequence a Series A committee wants to see.

The intellectual-property beat. Wang, Mao, and Wang are named inventors on a provisional application titled "Phosphorodiamidate morpholino oligonucleotides and uses thereof," jointly filed by the University of Chicago and Johns Hopkins University. Explicitly not licensed, no royalties. Which is Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures telling anyone paying attention that this program is at term-sheet moment. The founding cast is on the manuscript — Dawson as foundational P-D figure, Mao on alpha-synuclein transmission from materials science and the Institute for NanoBioTechnology, Jingxin Wang on medicinal chemistry, Mingyao Ying anchoring Kennedy Krieger.

So-what for Blackbird. Exactly the kind of first-in-class translational-stage R-N-A modality out of Johns Hopkins Neurology plus a Kennedy Krieger co-inventor the Life Sciences Research Initiative will backfill over the next two years — but composition-of-matter is filed today, sitting at Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures, unlicensed. Alpha-synuclein is a market defined by direct-binding-modality failures, which makes a differentiated modality actually valuable. Multiple system atrophy and dementia with Lewy bodies are open follow-on indications on top of Parkinson's. Diligence questions cluster in three places. First, whether target-conditional R-Nase L activation via chimera avoids the interferon-response side effects that global R-Nase L activation would trigger — the theory is on-target only; chronic-dosing innate-immune-signaling readouts need to be pulled from the supplement. Second, C-N-S delivery of P-M-O — Sarepta's P-M-O chemistry is I-V and subcutaneous for muscle; C-N-S delivery is not their franchise, and Ionis has A-S-O-to-C-N-S know-how competitors do not. Third, in-vivo route, dose, magnitude, and biodistribution in the humanized S-N-C-A mouse — where the supplement will tell you whether this clears bar. Direct action — Hemaka or Avi, flag Ted Dawson's group for a Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures conversation this week.

Pick two — same journal, same issue, a Blackbird-adjacent double header. Shuming Zhang first author, Aleksander Popel and Elana Fertig co-corresponding — Popel at Johns Hopkins Biomedical Engineering, Fertig at U-M-B School of Medicine's Institute for Genome Sciences and the University of Maryland Institute of Health Computing. Atul Deshpande on the Johns Hopkins Convergence Institute side; Elizabeth Jaffee and Mark Yarchoan on the Sidney Kimmel side. Title reads "Quantitative calibration of a spatial Q-S-P model identifies fibroblast impact on H-C-C immunotherapy." Rare Johns Hopkins-and-U-M-B co-corresponding structure, exactly the cross-institution model the U-M Ventures co-investment agreement is meant to catalyze.

The science in one line. Take a spatial quantitative-systems-pharmacology model of hepatocellular carcinoma — agent-based, single-cell resolution. Fit its parameters to real human spatial-transcriptomics data using an Approximate Bayesian Computation Sequential Monte Carlo pipeline — a way of evolving a population of parameter sets so the simulated tumor architecture matches the cellular neighborhoods you actually see in patient tissue. The calibrated model reproduces fibroblast-mediated exclusion of C-D-eight T cells, predicts response to atezolizumab plus tyrosine-kinase-inhibitor combination therapy in an independent cohort, and names pretreatment spatial biomarkers.

Two so-whats. First, the platform itself. Virtual-clinical-trial infrastructure with pretreatment spatial-biomarker readouts is a live licensable asset. Genentech, AstraZeneca, and Bristol Myers Squibb all run internal Q-S-P groups but none of them has spatial-omics-calibrated versions in production. Popel has been publishing this direction for a decade; code and calibrated models are deposited on GitHub and Zenodo, which lowers friction for a licensing dialog. This is the maturation moment for a computational-oncology NewCo conversation.

Second, and this is the update to yesterday's memory. Popel discloses equity in OptaNova Pharma and Terebra Therapeutics. That is the first public disclosure of active equity in OptaNova since we flagged it last week as stealth-stealth, with only a Delaware L-L-C filing and no press release, no website, no PitchBook page. OptaNova is real, Popel is on the cap table. That updates the Wilmer-and-Johns Hopkins-B-M-E peptide-therapeutic cluster from three shell entities to a confirmed active-founder cluster — Popel, Green, Pandey on the peptide side, plus this new computational-oncology axis with Fertig at U-M-B and Deshpande on the Johns Hopkins side.

So-what for Blackbird. Popel and Fertig are the founding cast for a computational-oncology virtual-trial NewCo sitting between Johns Hopkins Biomedical Engineering and U-M-B's Institute of Health Computing. Direct question for Eddie — has Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures or U-M Ventures had that conversation yet, and if not, this week is the moment.

Pick three, shorter — landscape signal rather than a fresh sourcing lead. Cristina Zivko first author, Vasiliki Mahairaki senior corresponding, Constantine Lyketsos senior — the Richman Family Precision Medicine Center of Excellence in Alzheimer's Disease at Johns Hopkins, with Brady Maher and three Lieber Institute contributors on the roster. Published open access in Alzheimer's and Dementia Translational Research and Clinical Interventions on July twelfth. Nineteen patient-derived induced-pluripotent-stem-cell lines differentiated to cortical neurons — twelve Alzheimer's patients, seven controls, all sourced through the Johns Hopkins Alzheimer's Disease Research Center and the Memory and Alzheimer's Treatment Center. Two functional assays — glutamate-plus-glycine-driven calcium influx, and menadione-induced oxidative stress. Twenty-four-hour memantine pre-treatment reduces calcium influx by an average of about ten percent, ranging from essentially zero to negative thirty-nine percent, and reduces reactive oxygen species by an average of twenty-six percent, ranging from negative four to negative seventy-two percent. Substantial across-line heterogeneity, mirroring the well-documented heterogeneous clinical response memantine shows in the A-D population.

Why it lands on the Blackbird radar. Memantine is a generic that costs pennies and helps roughly a third of the patients who take it. A lightweight iPSC-neuron functional-assay panel that pre-selects responders is a companion-diagnostic template — and the same template extends to donanemab, lecanemab, and the next wave of Alzheimer's symptomatic and disease-modifying drugs at real per-patient cost. The Richman Center sits inside the same Johns Hopkins Alzheimer's Disease Research Center that Lyketsos runs — the natural home for iPSC-based patient stratification for Alzheimer's. And the Lieber contributor list — Farinelli, Ostlund, Das, and Maher — extends the Lieber footprint from neuropsychiatry into A-D precision medicine, a natural pipeline expansion route for the G-P-R fifty-two-anchored neuropsych NewCo when it gets to a second and third asset. Lyketsos brings one of the deeper industry rolodexes in academic A-D — Roche, Karuna, Axsome, Otsuka, IntraCellular, BMS, Merck, and roughly a half-dozen more on the consulting side. Landscape signal, not a sourcing lead today. Watchlist for how the Richman Center opens to industry partnering.

That's the radar. Three items — the Dawson lab alpha-synuclein R-Nase-L-recruiting P-M-O chimera as the lead sourcing item, provisional filed at Johns Hopkins Technology Ventures and unlicensed, foundational Parkinson's franchise; the Popel-Fertig spatial-Q-S-P hepatocellular carcinoma virtual-trial platform as a Johns Hopkins-and-U-M-B ecosystem read, with OptaNova now confirmed as an active Popel-founded entity on the peptide-therapeutic cluster; and the Richman-Lieber iPSC memantine platform as an Alzheimer's companion-diagnostic template signal for the second-and-third-asset conversation on the neuropsych NewCo.

Sign-off. Full paper links and provisional-patent identifiers are in the show notes. The Blackbird Brief will be back tomorrow. Thanks for listening.
