Pick one is the Barrick lab, out of Johns Hopkins Biophysics. Nature Chemical Biology, Thursday. First author Matt Sternke, and Sternke's affiliation is worth naming up front. It's a dual affiliation: T.C. Jenkins Biophysics at Johns Hopkins and Protein Design and Informatics at G-S-K in Collegeville, Pennsylvania. Sternke did the work in the Barrick lab, moved to G-S-K's industrial protein-design team, and the paper carries both institutions. That's a scouting datapoint on where Johns Hopkins protein-engineering talent is flowing.

The paper's setup. Barrick uses Potts models — the same class of sequence-covariance models that underpin most modern AI-first protein design — to build synthetic sequences that isolate two ingredients. Single-site amino-acid preferences, meaning how conserved a given position is across an evolutionary alignment. And pairwise correlations between positions, meaning how often two residues co-vary because they co-evolved together. Most current protein-design tools optimize both jointly and don't tell you which piece contributes what to the final protein. Barrick separates them. He designs one batch of sequences that optimizes only the single-site preferences, another batch that also captures the pair correlations, and then measures both stability and enzyme activity on each.

The finding is genuinely counterintuitive. On stability, the single-site sequences win by a large margin. For homeodomain proteins, folding free energy about minus fourteen kilocalories per mole for the single-site batch versus minus eight for the pair-correlation batch. That's not a small effect. Same pattern across three enzyme families — adenylate kinase, dihydrofolate reductase, phosphoglycerate kinase. Pair correlations, it turns out, are not what stabilize a protein. They are what makes it active. Enzyme turnover numbers are substantially higher in the pair-correlation designs, and D-N-A binding for the homeodomain drops by roughly forty-fold — about nine nanomolar affinity versus three hundred forty-three nanomolar — when you strip out the pair correlations. Stability and activity live in different features of the sequence.

The so-what for Blackbird. Two angles.

First, this is a diligence discipline point for any AI-first protein-design pitch Blackbird will see over the next twelve months. And you'll see them — Cyrus Bio, Xaira, Chai Discovery, EvolvePro, Isomorphic Labs, the whole cohort. Any platform claim about designing thermostable-and-active proteins in one shot should be pressed on which objective the model actually optimizes. If the pitch is "our design pipeline gets you stable and active together," the Barrick result says that's a trade-off, not a free lunch. Blackbird's diligence question — for the venture partners on the receiving end of those pitches, Anthony, Hemaka, Jonathan, Virginia, Matt — is: does the platform quantify that trade-off explicitly, or does it hide it under a joint objective?

Second, this is a design rule the portfolio should have on the shelf. Aletira's SELEXON platform stacks alternative-splicing modules on top of A-A-V — the payloads are gene-therapy transgenes today, but any move into engineered effector proteins, say more selective recombinases or Cas variants for gene editing, will confront the same stability-versus-activity trade Barrick isolates. Same story for any future portfolio play involving engineered enzymes or antibody scaffolds.

Pick two is a Neuropsychopharmacology Hot Topics commentary, also published Thursday. Sang Ho Kwon first, Keri Martinowich senior corresponding, both at the Lieber Institute. Martinowich is Lieber's chief scientific officer — the same senior corresponding on the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex atlas Lieber press-released last week. So when Martinowich writes a synthesis commentary, it's her explicit framing on how to interpret the schizophrenia genetics-to-mechanism arc — and that framing directly bears on the G-P-R fifty-two program Blackbird is building with Third Rock.

The commentary's argument is short but pointed. Schizophrenia genetics — the P-G-C wave-three genetic lines Blackbird has been tracking through the Pergola trans-e-Q-T-L paper and the six-hundred-forty-one novel gene list from last month — points to neuronal populations as the primary carriers of genetic risk. But the postmortem molecular literature, including Lieber's own spatial-transcriptomics program, keeps finding alterations that live across a much broader multicellular landscape. Astrocytes. Oligodendrocytes. Microglia. Endothelial cells. Perineuronal nets. Neurovasculature. Two apparently contradictory readings of the same disease.

Kwon and Martinowich argue that these are not contradictory. They're complementary — the neuronal genetic-risk populations and the broader multicellular tissue states are integrated through shared developmental programs, cell-cell interactions, environmental input, and adaptive responses. The question they leave the field on is: how does neuronal vulnerability become wired into a broader multicellular pathobiology.

The so-what for Blackbird's G-P-R fifty-two program. G-P-R fifty-two is a neuronally-expressed orphan G-P-C-R. The Blackbird-Lieber-Third Rock development thesis is: agonize G-P-R fifty-two on the right neuronal population, get engagement across all three symptom domains of schizophrenia — positive, negative, cognitive. Martinowich's commentary is a soft warning to the field, and by extension to any G-P-R fifty-two developer, that the neuronal-target-alone framing may not be fully sufficient. If the schizophrenia phenotype is a stable multicellular state that includes glial and vascular contributions, then a purely neuronal agonist may not collapse it end-to-end.

I'm not saying this changes the G-P-R fifty-two thesis. Downstream circuit engagement should still drive functional outcomes, and Third Rock's development plan already contemplates broad symptom-domain readouts. What it does is name explicitly, in Lieber's own voice, where the biomarker and pharmacodynamic strategy should go. Pair the G-P-R fifty-two-driven neuronal engagement biomarker with a multicellular tissue-state readout. That could be spatial transcriptomics on a dosed Lieber post-mortem cohort — the Martinowich-Maynard-Hicks matched-donor atlas program is exactly the infrastructure for this — or an equivalent imaging correlate that captures glial and vascular components.

Hemaka — this is the kind of framing worth surfacing directly with the Third Rock team ahead of the development candidate declaration expected later this year. It's not a criticism of the neuronal target thesis. It's a specification of what "engagement across all three symptom domains" needs to look like in a development plan you take to the F-D-A. And it's coming from Lieber's C-S-O, so it's the framing the Third Rock partner will be reading anyway.

That's the radar. Two picks, both Johns Hopkins-anchored. One Nature Chemical Biology methodology paper with immediate application to how Blackbird diligences AI-first protein-design pitches — and a scouting-signal on Johns Hopkins protein-engineering talent flowing into G-S-K.
