## Script

You're listening to The Blackbird Brief for Thursday, July sixteenth, twenty twenty-six. This is a Sourcing Radar episode, and today's lead is the sharpest U-M-B methods-of-use disclosure the Baltimore ecosystem has produced in weeks — Wei Chao's group at the University of Maryland School of Medicine has filed a P-C-T on Toll-like-receptor-seven antagonism for cardiac ischemia-reperfusion injury, with preclinical infarct-size reduction across every dosing window they tried and human-clinical-stage tool compound already available from Merck K-G-a-A. Behind it, a U-M-B and Johns Hopkins and Kennedy Krieger cross-institution imaging platform for tracking cortical organoid transplants in stroke, and a landscape read on U-M-B Neuropharmacology's thought-leadership positioning in the ketamine-and-psychedelic drug class.

Pick one, and today's headline. Yijia Li first author. Yang Yang, Chanhee Park, Boyang Ren coauthors. Wei Chao senior corresponding. All out of the Translational Research Program in the Department of Anesthesiology and the Center for Shock Trauma and Anesthesiology Research at the University of Maryland School of Medicine. Title reads "T-L-R-seven inhibition limits ischemic cardiac injury by disrupting I-T-G-A-M-dependent immune-endothelial interactions." Published open access in J-A-C-C Basic to Translational Science this Tuesday, July fourteenth.

Here is what this paper actually is. Chao's group used a mouse coronary-artery-ligation model — forty-five minutes of ischemia, twenty-four hours of reperfusion — to test whether Toll-like receptor seven, the endosomal single-stranded-R-N-A sensor, is doing something specific and druggable in the sterile inflammation that follows re-perfusing an ischemic heart. Both genetic knockout of T-L-R-seven and pharmacologic inhibition with enpatoran cut infarct size significantly. The genetic and pharmacologic effects are non-additive, which tells you the drug is working through the intended target and not off-target. Single-nucleus R-N-A sequencing then localizes the mechanism to a T-L-R-seven-driven inflammatory endothelial program and a coordinated myeloid program, both converging on upregulation of the alpha-M integrin, gene name I-T-G-A-M — which most people know as C-D-eleven-b, the leukocyte-adhesion integrin. Antibody blockade of I-T-G-A-M reproduces the T-L-R-seven-knockout phenotype: significantly smaller infarct, markedly reduced neutrophil and monocyte infiltration, epistasis with T-L-R-seven loss confirming the axis. The whole story reads as a clean, mechanistically-anchored, small-molecule-accessible target-validation package for cardiac reperfusion injury, an indication that has resisted every anti-inflammatory approach thrown at it for two decades.

The commercial beat is where this gets interesting for Blackbird. The tool compound Chao's group used is enpatoran, a selective T-L-R-seven-and-eight antagonist developed by Merck K-G-a-A for systemic and cutaneous lupus and for myositis. That is an existing clinical asset. Merck K-G-a-A owns the composition of matter. But U-M-B has filed a P-C-T covering methods of treating ischemia-reperfusion injury with T-L-R-seven-and-eight antagonism — application number P-C-T-U-S-twenty-twenty-five-slash-thirty-seven-one-three-three, titled "Methods of Treating Ischemia-Slash-Reperfusion Injury." Which is the exact structural setup Blackbird would want to see. Enpatoran's tox and clinical safety are being paid for by Merck K-G-a-A in autoimmune indications. U-M-B holds the methods-of-use for cardio-reperfusion. Whoever wants to develop enpatoran into cardio needs a license from U-M-B — and enpatoran-like backups from other T-L-R-seven-and-eight inhibitor programs need one too. That's leverage.

Effect sizes are non-trivial. Enpatoran at five milligrams per kilogram intraperitoneally cut the infarct-over-area-at-risk ratio by thirty-five to seventy-six percent across four dosing windows — one hour before ischemia, thirty-five minutes into ischemia, thirty minutes into reperfusion, and two hours into reperfusion. The prevention and early-treatment windows worked. Late rescue at two hours started to fall off. That translates into a plausible peri-procedural window for stent placement and other percutaneous coronary interventions — the actual clinical use case here — and a clear dosing-window story for a Phase 2 protocol.

Diligence questions cluster in three places. First, whether cardiac T-L-R-seven inhibition disrupts the acute host response to viral infection in a way that matters in the peri-procedural setting — T-L-R-seven is a viral R-N-A sensor and enpatoran is being developed for autoimmunity precisely because you want to blunt that signaling. In a several-hour peri-P-C-I window this is likely fine; you would want to see the immunopharmacology characterized. Second, whether the methods-of-use claims cover competitor T-L-R-seven-only or T-L-R-seven-plus-eight molecules broadly, or are narrowed to enpatoran specifically. Third, the human-heart translation — the C-D-eleven-b axis is conserved but reperfusion biology varies enough between mouse and human that the diligence read wants a large-animal replication or human ex-vivo tissue signature before moving to the clinic.

So-what for Blackbird. This is a direct sourcing lead on the U-M Ventures cross the Blackbird BioVentures co-investment surface — same surface that produced aSKY. U-M-B School of Medicine methods-of-use I-P on a cardio-reperfusion indication where enpatoran's autoimmune development at Merck K-G-a-A is de-risking the tox package for you. The natural next steps are two. Whether U-M Ventures has a licensing conversation open with Merck K-G-a-A on cardio-reperfusion — and if not, whether U-M-B is positioned to shop the methods-of-use claim to a cardio-focused strategic. Bristol Myers Squibb, Novartis Cardiovascular, and CSL Behring all have interventional-cardiology franchises where a peri-procedural anti-inflammatory adjunct would slot cleanly. Action for Hemaka and Eddie — flag the Chao lab and Center for Shock Trauma and Anesthesiology Research for a U-M Ventures conversation this week. The Wei Chao group has an N-I-H R-thirty-five, which is the maximum-security-of-funding position an academic P-I can hold, and the Center for Shock Trauma is arguably the deepest translational-anesthesiology infrastructure in the country. That's a spinout conversation waiting for the right partner.

Pick two — a cross-institution platform paper published Tuesday in Advanced Science. Jinghui Wang first author. Yajie Liang senior corresponding at U-M-B Diagnostic Radiology and Nuclear Medicine. Piotr Walczak co-supervisor, also on the U-M-B side. Kexin Wang from Johns Hopkins Biomedical Engineering and Jiadi Xu from the Kennedy Krieger F.M. Kirby Center for Functional Brain Imaging on the M-R-I analysis. Title reads "Intravital multimodal imaging of human cortical organoid transplantation in a mouse model of chronic stroke."

What they've built is called M-I-P-O-T, standing for Multimodal Imaging Platform for Organoid Tracking. Four modalities integrated on one animal — nine-point-four Tesla M-R-I with diffusion-tensor readouts, bioluminescence imaging via a Luc-two-and-m-Cherry dual reporter driven by a human ubiquitin-C promoter, intraoperative surgical microscopy, and intravital two-photon fluorescence microscopy using a multicolor viral labeling system that co-expresses cytosolic and nuclear reporters plus a G-CaMP calcium indicator. The engineering novelty is a two-component three-D-printed removable head-fixation adapter that lets the same animal cycle through metal-free M-R-I, bioluminescence, and rigid-fixation two-photon microscopy in sequence — S-T-L files provided in the supplement. Grafted in this study — patient-derived induced-pluripotent-stem-cell-derived cortical organoids at day fifty-six of differentiation, injected into cleared photothrombotic-stroke cavities in male C-fifty-seven-B-L-six-J mice ten days after infarct maturation, with cyclosporin A immunosuppression.

Here is the honest read. The graft-persistence data is not encouraging. Bioluminescence signal dropped to about fifty percent by day four, twenty-five percent by day seven, and roughly five percent by day fourteen — the paper's own numbers. Endpoint histology confirmed limited persistence of human cells at two and four weeks. The authors are candid about it — no behavioral efficacy, no functional recovery data, and they explicitly frame the paper as a platform for tracking rather than a therapeutic candidate. Which is the right framing.

So-what for Blackbird. Two beats. First, this is a licensable imaging chassis, not a therapeutic asset. M-I-P-O-T sits alongside Reza Kalhor's 3-D-E-E-P deep-tissue spatial transcriptomics platform we flagged on July fifth — both are cross-institution Baltimore imaging plays that Blackbird could underwrite as tools rather than as drug programs. The M-I-P-O-T framework is generalizable per the discussion to any organoid or cell-therapy transplant experiment, not just stroke, so it applies to the Neuraly-and-Valted-and-Aletira orbit of anyone developing C-N-S cell therapies. Second, and this is the U-M Ventures ecosystem read, this paper crosses U-M-B Diagnostic Radiology with Johns Hopkins Biomedical Engineering with Kennedy Krieger Kirby Center in a single manuscript, and it is the exact operating pattern the U-M Ventures cross Blackbird co-investment surface is meant to seed. Piotr Walczak and Yajie Liang are the U-M-B translational-imaging bench Blackbird should be tracking anyway; this paper puts them on the map with a real cross-institution product. Third, and briefly — the honest twenty-five-percent-graft-persistence readout is a reality check on the CNS-cell-therapy sector broadly. Every group graft-transplanting into infarct cavities needs longitudinal imaging like this to stop misreading their own experiments. That's a licensable diligence tool.

Pick three, short and framing. Todd Gould at the University of Maryland School of Medicine — Psychiatry, Pharmacology-Physiology and Drug Discovery, and Neurobiology — co-authored a Journal of Psychopharmacology paper this Sunday with Sanjay Mathew at Texas A-and-M, Maurizio Fava at Mass General, and Anantha Shekhar at Pitt Medicine. Title reads "Enhancing plasticity to treat depression and other central nervous system diseases using event-driven pharmacology." What they're doing is proposing a design framework — they call it event-driven pharmacology — for the class of C-N-S drugs where a transient binding event produces sustained changes in neural plasticity that outlast the exposure. Ketamine is the paradigm case. Classical psychedelics fit. Their argument is that developing rapid-acting neuroplastogens using the traditional receptor-occupancy dose-finding model is actually counterproductive — you optimize the dose for stable receptor occupancy over time, and paradoxically you get few or no durable benefits and more side effects, because you are missing the pharmacology of the actual mechanism.

Why this lands on the Blackbird radar. Todd Gould is the U-M-B faculty member who co-invented two-R-six-R-hydroxynorketamine — the active metabolite of ketamine that Perception Neuroscience licensed and that A-T-A-I Life Sciences acquired via Perception. U-M-B owns royalties on that estate. Gould's group is one of the more productive translational-neuropharmacology benches in the country. When Gould publishes a framework paper on how to think about designing this class, that shapes how the diligence conversation goes on every ketamine-adjacent and psychedelic-adjacent asset the venture community sees for the next two years. Applied to Blackbird's Lieber Institute neuropsych NewCo, the G-P-R-fifty-two agonist program sits on the opposite side of the design framework — it is a chronic-agonist, occupancy-driven asset targeting a schizophrenia-risk-gene G-P-C-R for all three symptom domains. That is by design, not by omission. But the framing question this paper raises for the pipeline expansion conversation is whether the second-and-third-asset shots at the neuropsych NewCo should sit in the plastogen class rather than the chronic-agonist class. That's a design-space read for Hemaka and Third Rock, not a sourcing lead. Landscape.

That's the radar. Three items — the Wei Chao group T-L-R-seven-and-I-T-G-A-M cardiac reperfusion story out of U-M-B Anesthesiology and the Center for Shock Trauma as the lead sourcing item with a filed P-C-T on methods of treating ischemia-reperfusion injury and enpatoran's clinical safety already being paid for by Merck K-G-a-A; the M-I-P-O-T multimodal organoid-tracking platform out of Walczak and Liang at U-M-B Diagnostic Radiology with Johns Hopkins Biomedical Engineering and Kennedy Krieger Kirby Center on the roster as a licensable cross-institution imaging chassis and an honest twenty-five-percent-graft-persistence reality check on the C-N-S cell-therapy sector; and the Todd Gould event-driven-pharmacology framing paper out of U-M-B Neuropharmacology and Drug Discovery as a design-space read on where the plastogen class of C-N-S therapeutics is heading.
