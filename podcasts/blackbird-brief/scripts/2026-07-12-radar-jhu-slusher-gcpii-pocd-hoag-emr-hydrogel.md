The lead item on the radar is a Barbara Slusher paper published two days ago in Alzheimer's and Dementia that is, in effect, an indication-expansion signal for the JHU Drug Discovery G-C-P-two platform that Blackbird is already invested in for inflammatory bowel disease. It reads like a very natural second-indication conversation. That is where we start.

The paper is by Yuto Hasegawa as first author and Atsushi Kamiya as senior corresponding, out of the Johns Hopkins School of Medicine, with Barbara Slusher of Johns Hopkins Drug Discovery and the Department of Pharmacology and Molecular Sciences as a senior middle author. Gianluca Ursini and Tomoyo Sawada from the Lieber Institute are on the author list as well, which is worth noting for the neuropsychiatry connective tissue. Journal is Alzheimer's and Dementia. No competing interests declared. Funding is a stack of N-I-H aging, drug abuse, and mental health institute grants, so no encumbering industry money on this paper.

The disease framing first. Perioperative cognitive dysfunction is the memory and executive-function decline that older patients experience in the days and weeks after surgery. It affects a meaningful fraction of adults over sixty-five undergoing elective procedures — the practical scale, in the United States alone, is on the order of ten to twenty million surgeries a year in that age bracket where a subset of patients come out cognitively worse than they went in. There is no approved pharmacologic intervention. It has been described mechanistically as a neuroinflammatory phenomenon, but the causal biology has never converged on a druggable node.

What this paper does is deliver exactly that node, and it is glutamate carboxypeptidase two — G-C-P-two, the enzyme that hydrolyzes N-acetyl aspartyl glutamate to release glutamate. Slusher's laboratory has been the Hopkins home base for G-C-P-two biology and inhibitor chemistry for more than twenty years; the lab's foundational compound is 2-P-M-P-A, and the small-molecule inhibitor class has been through multiple rounds of Slusher-lab optimization already. The framework here is aged C-57 black six mice undergoing abdominal surgery. What the group reports is a male-specific vulnerability to postoperative cognitive impairment. Aged male mice come out with real deficits in recognition and spatial memory after surgery; aged female mice do not, or do so much less. Single-cell RNA sequencing of the hippocampus identifies a specific astrocyte subpopulation that upregulates G-C-P-two after surgery. The astrocyte upregulation of G-C-P-two is accompanied by loss of aquaporin four polarization at the astrocytic end-feet — that is the machinery of the glymphatic system, the brain's overnight waste-clearance flow. When they knock down G-C-P-two selectively in astrocytes, or when they treat the animals with 2-P-M-P-A, the astrocytic pathology is rescued, aquaporin four re-polarizes, hippocampal glutamate levels come back down, and memory performance is restored. It is a clean pharmacology story on top of a clean genetic story on top of a clean mechanistic story.

The commercial framing for Blackbird. Blackbird already has G-C-P-two as a portfolio target on the inflammatory bowel disease axis — Slusher's gut-restricted G-C-P-two inhibitor for Crohn's and ulcerative colitis, engineered so the drug does not cross into systemic circulation. The rationale for a peripheral, oral, gut-restricted G-C-P-two program in I-B-D is a bimodal effect on inflammation and enteric-nervous-system pain. This new paper opens a second, brain-selective indication in the same enzyme portfolio — postoperative cognitive dysfunction — where you actually want central-nervous-system penetration. Different pharmacology profile, same target family, same lab, same technology base. The Slusher pipeline has multiple G-C-P-two chemotypes; C-N-S-penetrant matter has been generated and characterized in the group. So this is not a new company; it is a natural second-asset conversation on the existing Slusher I-B-D program, or a candidate indication for the same C-N-S-penetrant back-up chemotype if one already exists in the estate.

Male-specific vulnerability with a mechanism-level explanation gives you a patient-selection biomarker built into the Phase two design — enroll male patients over sixty-five, measure aquaporin four polarization by imaging surrogate or biomarker, and run a placebo-controlled peri-operative dosing study. That is exactly the kind of biomarker-selectable proof-of-concept protocol that a translational investor wants to see for a novel small-molecule C-N-S indication with no active competitors. Second item. Stephen Hoag at the University of Maryland School of Pharmacy, working with Mouen Khashab and Saowanee Ngamruengphong on the Johns Hopkins gastrointestinal endoscopy service and Rebecca Krimins in Hopkins Radiology, published on Friday in Advanced Healthcare Materials a sodium alginate submucosal lifting hydrogel for endoscopic mucosal resection and endoscopic submucosal dissection. Now, if you have not scrubbed for a G-I procedure recently — the submucosal lifting agent is the fluid that the endoscopist injects underneath a mucosal lesion to create a cushion, lifting the lesion up off the muscular wall so it can be resected safely without perforating the bowel. Every colonic polypectomy larger than around one centimeter uses one. The current commercial market is dominated by Boston Scientific's O-R-I-S-E Gel, with Eleview, EverLift, BlueBoost, and EndoClot S-I-S as the second-tier competitors. The pain point is durability — the cushion collapses within twenty or thirty minutes for the commercial agents, which becomes a real problem in the longer, tunneling E-S-D procedures where the endoscopist is dissecting for an hour-plus.

The Hoag lab formulation is a lyophilized sodium alginate hydrogel with methylene blue incorporated as a visualization dye. The rheological work in the paper is the interesting bit — the hydrogel has a shear-thinning profile with viscosity in the forty-four to one-hundred-ninety milli-Pascal-second range at low shear, which is roughly a threefold to hundredfold higher than the commercial agents at forty-four milli-Pascal-seconds at the top end. Higher viscosity at rest translates directly into longer cushion durability once injected. Ex vivo, the lift persisted sixty minutes; in vivo, in swine, cushion retention was maintained for forty-five minutes with clean injectability and no adverse tissue reaction. Reconstitution time is sixty to ninety seconds. Physiological p-H and osmolality. The paper does not report a head-to-head clinical trial, but the ex vivo and swine data are enough to support a 510-K device pathway conversation with F-D-A.

Third item, briefer, and it is more of a landscape note than a sourcing lead. Sean Prigge at Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health, Feinstone Department of Molecular Microbiology and Immunology, co-senior with Paul Sigala at Utah, has a bioRxiv preprint that identifies a new essential antimalarial target hub in the plasmodium apicoplast — the vestigial plastid organelle in the parasite. The claim is that acyl carrier protein — A-C-P, canonically part of type-two fatty acid synthesis — is essential in blood-stage plasmodium falciparum not because of its fatty-acid synthesis role, which is dispensable in blood stage, but because A-C-P physically binds and stabilizes apicoplast pyruvate kinase two, the sole source of nucleoside triphosphates in the organelle. Loss of A-C-P collapses P-K-two, which collapses isoprenoid synthesis, which collapses apicoplast biogenesis, which kills the parasite. It is a genuinely new mechanistic target hub. The reason it is a landscape note rather than a full sourcing lead is that Blackbird's translational thesis lives in higher-margin indications than antimalarials, and the Prigge lab has historically moved discoveries through global-health partnerships — Medicines for Malaria Venture, Gates Foundation, that ecosystem — rather than through venture-backed startups. But it is a striking piece of biology, and if any of the medicinal-chemistry-heavy neglected-disease vehicles come across our BD desk, this is a target hub worth flagging.

The lead today is the Slusher G-C-P-two postoperative-cognitive-dysfunction indication expansion, which is not new sourcing but is a natural pipeline expansion of an existing Blackbird position, on a target where Slusher's group holds the world's deepest medicinal-chemistry stack, in an indication with no approved therapy and a biomarker-selectable Phase two design. The Hoag lab and Hopkins G-I team's submucosal lifting hydrogel is a real product-shaped device story on the U-M Ventures × Blackbird surface.
