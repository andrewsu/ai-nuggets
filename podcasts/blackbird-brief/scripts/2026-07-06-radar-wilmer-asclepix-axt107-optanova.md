### The Wilmer / AsclepiX AXT107 suprachoroidal microparticle paper — what it actually is

- **The asset.** AXT107 (gersizangitide) is an anti-angiogenic peptide derived from collagen IV that disrupts α5β1 integrin and potentiates Ang2/Tie2 signaling. Discovered in the Popel Systems Biology Lab at JHU BME in the mid-2010s, licensed out to AsclepiX Therapeutics on JHU IP, and now in a completed-enrollment Phase 1/2a suprachoroidal-injection trial (DISCOVER, NCT registration public; 15 patients across 125/250/500 μg dose arms; enrollment complete May 2024; topline read expected Q2 2025 per company disclosure). AsclepiX raised $10M in July 2023 to advance the Phase 1/2a.

- **What this paper adds.** The paper is the **9-month GLP toxicology study in Göttingen minipigs** supporting the suprachoroidal microparticle formulation. Also includes: (1) rat laser-induced choroidal neovascularization (CNV) model showing ~60% reduction of neovascular area vs. vehicle at the suprachoroidal MP-AXT107 dose; (2) 9-month refrigerated stability of the microparticle formulation; (3) minipig 9-month single-dose safety with no treatment-related ocular findings, transient IOP + mild inflammation resolving quickly, NOAEL at the top dose of 1.25 mg/eye; (4) bioanalysis showing persistent AXT107 localization in choroid/RPE + scleral tissues with no systemic exposure. Importantly, the paper explicitly notes: **"the original, soluble AXT107 formulation was ineffective at inhibiting laser-induced CNV in our rat model and was consequently reformulated as microparticles."** That is a real scientific admission — the first-generation soluble AXT107 did not work efficacy-wise in the standard preclinical AMD model, and the entire microparticle reformulation is what enabled the drug to move forward. This is IND-enabling maturity, not proof-of-concept.

- **Why this matters commercially.** The paper legitimizes and de-risks the current DISCOVER Phase 1/2a trial. The suprachoroidal delivery route + microparticle formulation now has publishable, GLP-quality preclinical support — the kind of data a strategic acquirer or Series B investor wants to see before writing a check. AsclepiX has been reasonably quiet since its 2023 $10M raise + 2024 enrollment completion, and this preprint is the first fresh scientific data disclosure from the company on this modality since then.

### The Eberhart / Peterson MYC Meibomian-gland tumor paper — watch-item

- **What it shows.** MYC is functionally required for both normal Meibomian-gland (MG) proliferation + differentiation AND for viability of ocular adnexal sebaceous carcinoma (SebCA), the aggressive eyelid tumor that most often arises from the MG. The paper's key data: (a) MG-specific MYC inhibition in mice reduces cytoplasmic volume + proliferation + lipid droplet formation + increases apoptosis; (b) three different MYC pharmacological tool compounds — MYCMI6 (MYC-MAX heterodimer disruptor), MYCi361 (MYC transcriptional activity inhibitor), and 10074-G5 (MYC-MAX binder) — all suppress viability + clonogenicity of SebCA lines, with MYCMI6 giving the most robust suppression of canonical MYC transcriptional targets.

- **Why anyone should care.** Ocular adnexal SebCA is an aggressive, high-mortality eyelid malignancy that lacks well-defined oncogenic drivers. High MYC expression has been documented in SebCA tumors and cell lines but had not been mechanistically nominated as a functional dependency. This paper closes that loop and validates MYC as a druggable-in-principle target in this rare tumor. Given the ocular adnexa is Wilmer's clinical franchise and Blackbird's Wilmer thesis already exists, this is portfolio-adjacent target biology worth memory-mapping.

- **Where this sits on the sourceable-vs-watch spectrum.** Watch-item, not lead. Reasons: (1) MYC has been notoriously undruggable — every direct-MYC campaign for 30 years has stalled at chemistry; the three tool compounds cited (MYCMI6, MYCi361, 10074-G5) are all research-grade with no clinical development trajectory. (2) The paper does not disclose new IP, patent applications, or industrial partnerships. (3) The addressable population for ocular adnexal SebCA is small (rare-disease scale). (4) The MYC-targeting field has moved toward MYC-degrader (PROTAC) strategies rather than direct inhibition — those are being pursued by other labs and companies with more mature chemistry stacks. That said, if Wilmer-anchored SebCA validation supports rare-disease designations, the story becomes more interesting.

- **Blackbird action.** No immediate sourcing routing. Add JHU Pathology + Wilmer collaboration on ocular sebaceous carcinoma as a memory-mapped Wilmer-anchored oncology angle. If a MYC-degrader campaign for ocular sebaceous carcinoma emerges at Wilmer in the next 12-18 months, that becomes portfolio-adjacent — the Wilmer clinical franchise + orphan-tumor + degrader-chemistry combination is a plausible Blackbird pattern.
