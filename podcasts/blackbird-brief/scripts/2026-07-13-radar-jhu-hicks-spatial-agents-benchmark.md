The first pick is a Stephanie Hicks lab preprint from Johns Hopkins Biostatistics posted late last week — first author Yiqun Chen out of Stanford, senior corresponding Hicks. The title is "Mind the alignment gap," and the subject is benchmarking A-I coding agents on spatial transcriptomics tasks. This is not a therapeutic. It is not a sourceable asset. But it matters for two reasons that are directly Blackbird-adjacent, and the finding is genuinely counterintuitive, so it's worth two minutes.

Hicks is one half of the computational partnership behind the Lieber Institute matched-donor spatial transcriptomics program. Her lab has co-authored the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex atlas in twenty twenty-four and the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex atlas that Lieber press-released last week. She is also the co-developer of the spatialLIBD stack — the open computational infrastructure that a lot of academic and industry spatial-transcriptomics analysis now runs on. So when the Hicks lab publishes on how well A-I coding agents handle spatial-transcriptomics workflows, it's not a peripheral question. It's the group that produces the datasets telling you what happens when you try to point an autonomous agent at their own analysis pipeline.

The setup: they take a recent spatial-alignment benchmark called SABench and repurpose it as forty executable tasks for A-I coding agents. Each task hands the agent a pair of tissue slices with gene-expression matrices and coordinates, and asks the agent to align them — a real analysis task the Lieber-Hopkins atlases actually depend on. They run the same forty tasks under three conditions: a bare-bones prompt, a prompt that names the specialized alignment packages, and a full prompt with a pre-built virtual environment where those packages are already installed. Same agent — Codex-C-L-I on a G-P-T five point three variant at high reasoning effort — same tasks, three levels of scaffolding.

The intuition an operator would bring to this: more context, more tools, more specialized packages should produce better results. That's the whole thesis of investing in agent scaffolding.

The finding is the opposite. Agents given the richer scaffolding used the specialized packages more — PASTE, Spateo, S-P-A-C-E-L — as expected. But the mean alignment score actually dropped from about zero-point-four-three in the bare-bones condition to zero-point-three-six with the full environment. That's a real gap, with a confidence interval that doesn't cross zero. When they inspected the traces to figure out why, the pattern was: the added specialized tooling induced unnecessary coordinate transformations, brittle package-first workflows where the agent stopped thinking and started plumbing, and infrastructure failures where an installed package didn't quite behave and the agent didn't recover. In the basic condition, the agent had to reason about the problem from first principles and often landed on a reasonable heuristic. In the full condition, it deferred to specialized tools and lost the plot.

Second and last pick — landscape context for a sourcing thesis already in the file.

Nature Medicine dropped Michael Lim's anti-L-A-G-three plus-or-minus anti-P-D-one Phase one in recurrent glioblastoma last Thursday. Lim was chair of neurosurgery at Johns Hopkins for years and is now at Stanford; the trial itself — Adult Brain Tumor Consortium fifteen-oh-one — was coordinated out of Hopkins with Stuart Grossman and Xiaobu Ye on the paper. Forty-six patients with recurrent glioblastoma, randomized between relatlimab monotherapy — that's the B-M-S anti-L-A-G-three inside Opdualag — and relatlimab plus nivolumab. Enrolled twenty sixteen to twenty twenty. Long-standing trial, finally reading out.

The result: safety endpoint met — no dose-limiting toxicities on relatlimab monotherapy, twenty-six percent D-L-T rate on the combination. Efficacy: descriptive, non-randomized, and cautious. Twelve-month overall survival was thirty-five percent in the mono arm and fifty-two percent in the combo arm. No confirmed radiographic responses — twenty-five patients had pseudoprogression. About eleven percent of the whole cohort became long-term survivors past two years, and the survivors had baseline signatures of type-one interferon activity, higher T-cell clonality, and more myeloid antigen-presenting cells. So: encouraging shape, but forty-six patients, no confirmed R-A-N-O responses, and the signal is entirely correlative.

The so-what for Blackbird. Recurrent glioblastoma has been the graveyard of checkpoint-inhibitor programs for a decade — Bristol-Myers-Squibb's own nivolumab failed in Checkmate one-four-three; pembrolizumab, cemiplimab all disappointing. What Lim shows is that L-A-G-three engagement doesn't reverse that, but it also doesn't collapse it — you get a modest, ambiguous, biomarker-consistent signal from a B-M-S combination that a large company can either invest into a Phase two or park.
