# DSR — dual-stimulatory receptor arms TCR-T, bispecific TCE-secreting T, and chimeric-TCR cells with signal 2 plus signal 3

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.12.738084v1

## Script

One of the persistent problems in engineered T cell therapies — TCR-transgenic T cells, T cell engagers, chimeric TCRs, all of them — is that the antigen recognition part works fine, but the T cell runs out of gas because it never gets the follow-through it would get if it were a normal, endogenous T cell. Signal one, antigen through the TCR, is delivered. Signal two, costimulation from something like CD28 or 4-1BB, and signal three, cytokines from an activated antigen-presenting cell, are largely missing. Clinicians have papered over this with exogenous IL-2, with lymphodepletion, with repeat dosing — none of it elegant. A preprint out this week from Baylor's Center for Cell and Gene Therapy, from Mansi Narula in the Mamonkin and Watanabe labs, proposes a strikingly modular fix.

They call it DSR, a dual stimulatory receptor. The design is one sentence. Take the ectodomain of 4-1BB ligand, fuse it through a CD28 transmembrane segment to the intracellular endodomain of cMPL — the thrombopoietin receptor, the one that normally lives on hematopoietic stem cells and is absent from mature T cells. Both halves are things that oligomerize on their own, so the fusion behaves as a stable multimer. That's the whole construct — one gene, no secondary trigger, no external ligand needed.

The clever part is what happens when a DSR-armed T cell recognizes its antigen. Antigen ligation transiently upregulates endogenous 4-1BB on that same T cell. The 4-1BB-ligand end of DSR then reaches out and engages that endogenous 4-1BB in cis, right there on the same cell surface. That engagement does two things at once. It transmits classical 4-1BB costimulation through the endogenous receptor — that's signal two. And because DSR has cMPL hanging off the intracellular side, the same engagement triggers cMPL-driven STAT3 and STAT5 phosphorylation — a cytokine-like signal three, analogous to what a common gamma-chain cytokine would give you. Both signals are gated on antigen recognition. Withdraw antigen, everything shuts off. That's the safety story for anyone worried about the systemic toxicity you get from constitutively active cytokine circuits like the constitutive IL-7 receptor.

In vitro they showed DSR-modified T cells keep expanding through six rounds of repeated antigen stimulation, while wild-type 4-1BB-ligand controls and a signaling-dead DSR control both collapse after three. Take the antigen away and DSR cells stop growing within days — so the tonic signal really is antigen-gated. Transcriptionally, DSR cells hold onto an effector-associated program — TBX21, EOMES, CXCR6, granzyme B — and stay lower on the exhaustion markers TOX and ID3, exactly the pattern you'd want.

The reason this is worth Justice's attention rather than being one more preclinical costim gadget is that they took DSR into three different TCR-based platforms in mice and it worked in all of them. First, a transgenic TCR against a survivin peptide on HLA-A2, tested against BV173 leukemia — DSR-armed T cells expanded, persisted, and roughly doubled median survival. Second — this is the interesting one — a bispecific T cell engager–secreting T cell platform, CD123 by CD3, for AML. A single infusion of DSR-armed TCE-secreting T cells cleared MOLM-13 tumors for around 90 days with no exogenous cytokine at all, while the TCE-secreting T cells without DSR could not control the tumor. Third, a CD19 chimeric TCR — a scFv fused into TCR alpha and beta constants — got durable tumor control against NALM6 with DSR, and critically, when they knocked out endogenous 4-1BB, the DSR benefit disappeared. That last experiment closes the causal loop — the 4-1BB in cis engagement is what's driving the effect, not some off-target cMPL signal.

The take. Every TCE platform in the clinic is fighting the same problem — the antigen recognition works and the initial kill works, but the cell burns out because it never gets the follow-through. The DSR framing is the tightest solution I've seen. Everything is gated on antigen, so you don't get systemic activation. It's one construct, one gene, small enough to sit in a standard lentivirus alongside the actual TCR or the engager cassette. And it's compatible with three very different antigen-recognition strategies — transgenic TCR, T cell engager secretion, and scFv-based chimeric TCR — which suggests it'll bolt onto trispecifics and beyond without a redesign.

The obvious caveats. This is preclinical, xenograft, mouse only. There is a small background STAT phosphorylation signal in resting DSR cells that they attribute to spontaneous multimerization — small in a three-week mouse study, but the durability arithmetic in a human over years is not something a mouse can answer. They did not run DSR against a constitutive cMPL alone as a head-to-head, which would help isolate how much of the benefit is really the coordination with signal 2 versus just cMPL signal by itself. And the bispecific TCE data is TCE-secreting T cells, not off-the-shelf antibody plus separately armed T cells — the latter is the real trispecific clinical configuration that would need its own experiment.

Still, if you spend your time thinking about how to make T cell engagers durable, this is a preprint to pull down. The design principle — use an antigen-induced endogenous receptor as the trigger, and hijack the resulting engagement to deliver a second signal — generalizes well beyond the specific 4-1BBL–cMPL construct they built. That's the piece I'd expect to see other groups extend first.
