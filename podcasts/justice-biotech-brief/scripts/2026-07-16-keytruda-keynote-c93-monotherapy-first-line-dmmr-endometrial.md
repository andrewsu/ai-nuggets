# KEYTRUDA — first Phase 3 PD-1 monotherapy to beat platinum doublet chemotherapy in first-line dMMR advanced or recurrent endometrial cancer (Merck KEYNOTE-C93 topline, July 15 2026), positioning pembrolizumab for a chemo-free label in the mismatch-repair-deficient subset and opening the wider question of when a biomarker-defined subgroup earns the right to drop the platinum backbone entirely

Paper link: https://www.merck.com/news/keytruda-pembrolizumab-as-monotherapy-significantly-improved-progression-free-survival-pfs-in-certain-patients-with-advanced-or-recurrent-endometrial-cancer-with-mismatch-repair-deficient-d/

## Script

Good morning, Justice. Today's item is a Phase 3 topline that dropped from Merck yesterday, July fifteenth twenty-twenty-six. The KEYNOTE-C-ninety-three study of pembrolizumab — Keytruda — as monotherapy versus platinum doublet chemotherapy, in first-line advanced or recurrent endometrial cancer with mismatch-repair-deficient tumors, hit its progression-free survival endpoint. Overall survival showed a trend at interim but the data are immature. This is being framed by Merck, correctly in my view, as the first Phase 3 in any tumor type to show a PD-one inhibitor as monotherapy beating platinum doublet chemotherapy in the frontline setting. The trial number to remember is C-ninety-three, and it sits inside a really specific and interesting policy question, which is when a biomarker-defined subset earns the right to drop the chemotherapy backbone entirely. Let me walk you through it.

The trial mechanics first. Two hundred and ninety-nine patients randomized to pembrolizumab four hundred milligrams intravenous every six weeks for up to eighteen cycles, versus paclitaxel one hundred and seventy-five per square meter plus carboplatin at an A-U-C of five or six, given every three weeks for six cycles. Population is adults with mismatch-repair-deficient advanced or recurrent endometrial cancer who are chemo-naive, or whose recurrence came more than six months after completing prior adjuvant therapy. So this is a true first-line population in the biomarker subset. Progression-free survival is the primary endpoint, assessed by blinded independent central review under RECIST version one point one. Overall survival is the other primary endpoint. The interim readout showed PFS hit and an OS trend that Merck is holding until maturity. No hazard ratio, no confidence interval, no exact median PFS in the press release — those will come at a medical meeting, most likely a fall gynecologic oncology or European society oncology congress. Response rate, complete response rate, and duration of response were also improved and described as clinically meaningful, again without numbers. Safety was consistent with prior pembrolizumab studies and no new signals emerged.

Now the historical context, because that is where the significance of C-ninety-three actually lives. Mismatch-repair-deficient tumors are the poster child for checkpoint inhibitor sensitivity — the loss of D-N-A repair floods the tumor with neoantigens from replication errors, T cells recognize those neoantigens, and PD-one blockade unleashes them. Merck got a tissue-agnostic accelerated approval on that biology back in twenty-seventeen. In endometrial cancer specifically, the trajectory has been progressive expansion — first the tissue-agnostic monotherapy label in progressive M-S-I-high or dMMR tumors after prior systemic therapy, then the Keytruda plus lenvatinib combination for the mismatch-repair-proficient subset based on KEYNOTE-seven-seventy-five, and then in the last two years the addition of pembrolizumab to carboplatin-paclitaxel in the primary advanced and recurrent setting on the strength of the N-R-G-G-Y-oh-one-eight trial, KEYNOTE-eight-sixty-eight. That last approval, and the parallel dostarlimab label from RUBY, established chemo-plus-checkpoint as first-line standard of care regardless of M-M-R status. Both trials showed enormous benefit in the dMMR subgroups — in N-R-G-G-Y-oh-one-eight the dMMR arm cut the risk of progression or death by roughly seventy percent versus chemo alone. Which is exactly why the C-ninety-three question was worth asking. If the checkpoint is doing that much of the work in the dMMR patients, do you actually need the platinum doublet at all.

C-ninety-three says the answer is no. Or more precisely, C-ninety-three says pembrolizumab as monotherapy is superior to platinum doublet as monotherapy, in this biomarker-defined subset, on progression-free survival, with an interim overall survival signal in the right direction. What it does not yet tell you is whether pembrolizumab monotherapy is as good as, or noninferior to, pembrolizumab plus chemo. That is a different trial, and it is not what was run. So the immediate regulatory question — and I would put a lot of weight on this — is whether the label ends up being pembrolizumab as an alternative option, or as a preferred option, or as a replacement in the dMMR subset. My reading is that the practice pattern will shift toward monotherapy for dMMR patients who are chemo-averse or platinum-vulnerable — which is a real subset, older patients with comorbidities and prior pelvic radiation are common in endometrial cancer — and the combination label will hold for younger fit patients who can tolerate it. That is a real change in how first-line dMMR endometrial cancer gets treated. It also puts real pressure on G-S-K's dostarlimab franchise, which won its endometrial label in RUBY with a chemo backbone and does not have an equivalent chemo-free readout to point to.

Now the read-across for your beat, which is where this actually matters beyond gyn-onc.

First — the biomarker-defined-monotherapy paradigm. The generalizable insight in C-ninety-three is that when the underlying biology delivers a large enough neoantigen or immune-priming signal, single-agent checkpoint blockade can carry the frontline label without a cytotoxic partner. Mismatch-repair deficiency is one such biomarker. Others that behave similarly on the response-rate side are high T-M-B, high P-D-L-one C-P-S in specific histologies, and E-B-V-positive gastric tumors. The regulatory precedent set here — that a fifteen-hundred-patient trial is not necessary, that a two-hundred-and-ninety-nine-patient study in a defined biomarker subgroup can carry a first-line label — is going to be cited in the design memos for a lot of upcoming trials. For your work, this matters most where you are thinking about combinations of a T-cell engager or a V-E-G-F-P-D-one bispecific with a chemo backbone in a defined subset. If the on-target immune biology is strong enough, the chemo add-on becomes optional rather than mandatory, and the design question becomes what defines "strong enough."

Second — the P-D-one-V-E-G-F bispecific story specifically. Ivonescimab is running its major front-line lung cancer trials, HARMONi-two and HARMONi-three, as combinations with platinum doublet, and Summit and Akeso are betting the franchise on chemo-plus-bispecific beating pembrolizumab-plus-chemo. C-ninety-three does not directly threaten that thesis, but it does raise the question of whether a P-D-one-V-E-G-F bispecific in a subset that is highly checkpoint-responsive — say an M-S-I-high or high-T-M-B N-S-C-L-C subset, or hepatocellular where V-E-G-F blockade already carries clinical weight — could earn a chemo-free label the way Keytruda just did. If it can, the commercial positioning of the bispecific class shifts from replacing pembrolizumab in the chemo combination to owning the chemo-free monotherapy lane, which is a much bigger economic prize.

Third — the trispecific T-cell engager angle. For engagers targeting solid tumors, the standard combo playbook is engager plus chemo plus checkpoint. C-ninety-three is a data point that in the right biomarker subset, chemo can be dropped. That reduces the tolerability burden of the trial regimen, which for a T-cell engager with its own cytokine release and on-target-off-tumor concerns, matters a lot. It also suggests that biomarker-selected Phase two studies of a trispecific — say, in dMMR solid tumors or high-T-M-B tumors — with engager-monotherapy versus engager-plus-chemo arms might be a more informative design than the more common engager-plus-chemo-versus-chemo comparator.

Fourth — the C-A-R-T-plus-lymphodepletion analogue. This one is more speculative, but there is a structural echo here. In autologous and allogeneic C-A-R-T for solid tumors, lymphodepletion with fludarabine and cyclophosphamide is the platinum-analogue backbone. If future data show that in a T-cell-inflamed subset the C-A-R product can engraft and function without the standard lymphodepletion — because endogenous T cells are not competing for cytokines and space — the same logic that dropped chemo from front-line dMMR endometrial cancer would drop lymphodepletion from that subset of C-A-R-T. That is a research question, not a clinical one, but the C-ninety-three framing is the kind of framing that ports over.

Now the caveats.

First, no numbers. Until we see the hazard ratio, the median P-F-S curves, the crossover rate — because you can bet that patients on the chemo arm crossed over to Keytruda at progression — and the O-S maturity, we cannot judge the magnitude of benefit. The press-release language of "clinically meaningful" is doing a lot of work. Expect the full readout at a fall meeting.

Second, no monotherapy-versus-combination arm. C-ninety-three was designed against chemo, not against the current standard of care of chemo-plus-pembrolizumab. So the label will land as an additional option, not as a replacement. Physicians will pick between the two based on tolerability and patient preference, and the trial data alone will not settle which is better.

Third, the O-S trend is not yet significant. If it does not mature into a positive readout, the story becomes P-F-S-only in a first-line indication where O-S has now been shown to be reachable — that would be a soft finish and would tilt the practice pattern back toward the chemo combination.

Fourth, indication-specific. Endometrial cancer with dMMR is one of the most checkpoint-sensitive tumor types known. Do not extrapolate directly to other dMMR indications — colorectal cancer is the closest analogue and does behave similarly, but pancreatic dMMR, prostate dMMR, and other rare dMMR tumors have not shown the same magnitude of monotherapy benefit.

Bottom line. This is a small trial with a big regulatory implication. Two hundred and ninety-nine patients showed pembrolizumab monotherapy beats platinum doublet in first-line dMMR endometrial cancer, positioning it for a chemo-free frontline label in a biomarker-defined subset that comprises roughly a quarter of endometrial cancer. The mechanistic story — mismatch-repair-deficient tumors are neoantigen-rich and checkpoint-sensitive enough that the cytotoxic partner is optional — is not surprising in retrospect but has never been demonstrated at Phase three scale in first-line before. The read-across for your beat is the biomarker-defined-monotherapy paradigm — the idea that when the on-target biology is strong enough, the chemo backbone can be dropped, and that this creates real design and commercial optionality for P-D-one-V-E-G-F bispecifics, trispecific T-cell engagers, and other next-generation checkpoint-adjacent modalities that have defaulted to running with chemo. The number to watch is the P-F-S hazard ratio when the full readout drops, and the number to hope for is a mature O-S signal at follow-up. That is the brief.
