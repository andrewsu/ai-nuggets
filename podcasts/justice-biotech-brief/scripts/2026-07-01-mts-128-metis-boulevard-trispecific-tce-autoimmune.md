# MTS-128 — Metis TechBio out-licenses a preclinical AI-designed trispecific T cell engager for autoimmune disease to Deerfield-backed Boulevard Bio for twenty million dollars upfront and up to one-point-six billion in milestones

Paper link: https://www1.hkexnews.hk/listedco/listconews/sehk/2026/0630/2026063000679.pdf

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, and it lands directly on two of your three explicit interest areas at once — trispecific T cell engagers, and autoimmune biologics. Metis TechBio, a Hong Kong-listed A-I-native drug design shop, and Boulevard Bio, a low-profile U-S vehicle backed by Deerfield Management, announced yesterday a global exclusive license for M-T-S-one-twenty-eight — a preclinical trispecific T cell engager for an undisclosed autoimmune indication. Twenty million dollars upfront, up to one-point-six billion dollars in development, regulatory, and commercial milestones, tiered royalties on sales. It's the largest disclosed preclinical trispecific T-C-E out-license on record, and it's the first out-licensing of any asset from the Metis platform since the company went public on the Hong Kong exchange forty-seven days ago.

Three threads worth pulling for you specifically. What the asset actually is. Why the autoimmune indication is the interesting part. And what the deal structure signals about where trispecific T-C-Es are going.

First, the asset. M-T-S-one-twenty-eight is a trispecific T cell engager — one C-D-three arm to recruit cytotoxic T cells, plus two other target-binding arms whose antigen identities Metis is not disclosing. What Metis says publicly is that the molecule was designed and optimized end-to-end on their NanoForge platform's AiProtein engine, which is their in-house A-I-driven protein-and-antibody design system. No preclinical data have been released. The indication is listed on their pipeline page under Immunology, Autoimmune, which is the material fact for this call — the F-D-A-relevant framing is not "solid tumor T-C-E" but "autoimmune T-C-E." That's the point of the pick.

Second, why autoimmune trispecific T-C-E is the interesting story. The last three years have re-framed T-C-Es as more than a cancer modality. C-D-nineteen CAR-T off-label experience in refractory lupus starting with the Erlangen cases has shown that deep B-cell depletion resets pathogenic autoreactivity in a way that the anti-C-D-twenty class — rituximab, obinutuzumab — cannot reliably do. That was proof-of-concept for the "kill the autoreactive B cell compartment" thesis. T cell engagers are the outpatient, off-the-shelf, no-manufacturing version of that thesis — no autologous manufacturing, no lymphodepleting chemotherapy, dose it and go. Cullinan Therapeutics showed a clinical signal for its own C-D-nineteen-by-C-D-three T-C-E in systemic lupus earlier this month, which is the reference case the Fierce coverage is anchoring the Metis deal to. That's the class as it stands — bispecific, single B-cell antigen, deep pan-B-cell depletion.

A trispecific for autoimmune is the next design lever, and the mechanistic bet is worth flagging clearly. If you want to kill a pathogenic subset — say, autoreactive plasmablasts, or auto-antigen-reactive memory B cells, or the T-follicular-helper cells that support them — without wiping the entire B or T cell compartment, you need an A-N-D gate. Two tumor-antigen arms on a trispecific give you exactly that: the molecule only synapses productively when both antigens are co-expressed on the same target cell, and cells with only one antigen escape. That's dual-antigen gating for selectivity, which is the same design principle that has been proposed and worked on in trispecific solid-tumor T-C-Es for years to escape single-antigen-driven on-target off-tumor toxicity. Applied to autoimmune, the same trick lets you carve off, at least in principle, a specific autoreactive lymphocyte subpopulation while sparing the polyclonal compartment that supports normal humoral and cellular immunity. That is the therapeutic-window and safety-profile argument that Metis is making in the press language, and it is real — trispecific selectivity is the design frontier for cytotoxic engagers, and the autoimmune setting is where the selectivity premium is highest, because you are targeting your own cells in your own patient at prolonged exposure.

Third, the structural read. Boulevard Bio is a Deerfield asset vehicle — no other disclosed programs, no independent clinical infrastructure, not yet on Deerfield's portfolio page. This is the same pattern you saw with Talawar Therapeutics on the Khanda I-L-thirteen-by-I-L-eighteen bispecific for atopic dermatitis two weeks ago, and with Beeline Medicines on the B-M-S-carveout autoimmune assets this morning. Platform sells one asset preclinical, at a nine-figure headline milestone value, into a purpose-built vehicle. Vehicle carries development through Phase two on outside capital. Pharma buyer or specialist strategic is the exit. The through-line for you is that the autoimmune-T-C-E class has apparently reached the point where a Deerfield vehicle will underwrite one-point-six billion dollars of milestones against a preclinical asset with no released data and undisclosed targets, on the strength of the platform design pedigree and the class-level thesis. That's a market-structure signal — buy-side conviction on autoimmune T-C-Es is a lot higher than the current clinical data package would justify on a first-principles basis.

The caveats to hold. Trispecific T-C-Es have no clinical proof of concept yet as a class — not in oncology, not in autoimmune. Every trispecific T-C-E in the pipeline right now is preclinical or very early Phase one. Target identities and format details for M-T-S-one-twenty-eight are undisclosed, so you cannot yet evaluate the design decisions Metis actually made — which C-D-three affinity band, which two co-antigens, format geometry, half-life. And A-I-designed does not equal A-I-de-risked; the physical-property design bar being higher upfront is the pitch, but the clinic is where that bar gets tested.

Net take. The Metis-Boulevard deal is a datapoint that the autoimmune T-C-E class is now attracting oncology-scale asset-vehicle capitalization, and that trispecific format engineering has become the specific design front where sophisticated buy-side money is being placed. Given your own work on T cell engagers and autoimmune biologics, this is the deal in your field for the week. The M-T-S-one-twenty-eight target disclosures — whenever Boulevard elects to make them — will be the moment to actually re-evaluate whether the design bet is credible or whether Deerfield is paying platform premium for what turns out to be a C-D-nineteen-adjacent asset that a bispecific could have done for less.
