## Script

Good morning, Justice. Today's brief is one deal — Novartis paying up to one point five billion dollars for Myricx Bio, announced Monday morning U-S time. One point one billion cash up front, four hundred million in milestones. Not a big number by 2026 oncology-dealmaking standards, but the reason it matters is what Novartis is buying — a new class of A-D-C payload, not a specific asset.

Myricx is U-K based, spun out of Imperial College London and the Francis Crick Institute in 2019 by Ed Tate, Roberto Solari, and Andrew Bell. They closed a series A of about a hundred and fourteen million dollars in mid-2024, led by Novo Holdings and Abingworth with Eli Lilly participating. Two lead A-D-C programs, both preclinical, both targeting antigens that are extremely well-traveled in this field — B seven H three and HER two. What is not well-traveled is the payload. Myricx's payload class is an N-myristoyltransferase inhibitor. Call it an N-M-T inhibitor.

Quick refresher on why that matters. Nearly every A-D-C in late-stage development right now — Enhertu, Datroway, Trodelvy, sigvotatug vedotin, Padcev, and most of the assets in phase one and phase two that we talk about weekly on this show — converges onto two payload chemistries. Topoisomerase-one inhibitors, mostly deruxtecan or exatecan derivatives. And microtubule-binding auristatins, M-M-A-E or M-M-A-F. That is essentially it. Everything else approved is a rounding error. The knock-on consequence is that as HER two, TROP two, HER three, B seven H three, Nectin four, and other targets all get deruxtecan-class A-D-Cs in parallel, the resistance mechanisms — topoisomerase-one downregulation, M-D-R-one efflux, D-N-A damage response upregulation — become shared. Lose one, you eventually lose the class.

An N-M-T inhibitor does not touch any of that. N-myristoyltransferase catalyzes co-translational addition of a fourteen-carbon myristoyl lipid to the N-terminus of about a hundred and fifty human proteins, and that lipid tag is what anchors many of them to membrane compartments. Take it away and Src-family kinases, A-D-P-ribosylation factor GTPases, and a set of pro-survival and mitochondrial substrates all fail to localize correctly. It is a co-translational modification, so blocking it works upstream of anything the standard resistance machinery targets on the payload side. The company's preclinical claim, which is the load-bearing scientific claim in this deal, is complete tumor regression in solid-tumor models that are poorly responsive to standard payload classes — topo-one-resistant, they specifically call out. Fiona Marshall, president of biomedical research at Novartis, echoes this framing in the release — clear need for new payload mechanisms to overcome resistance.

Reasons to be skeptical before we get too far. First, N-M-T inhibitors as free molecules have a long history in oncology, and none of them made it past early clinical development without either a narrow index or off-target problems. That does bolster the case for delivering it via an A-D-C with a tumor-selective antibody, which is the pitch — but it also confirms that on the safety side this payload is not free. Second, efficacy in topo-one-resistant models is a preclinical statement that has been made before by other novel-payload sponsors and has repeatedly not translated when the cross-resistance study is finally run in patients. Third, both lead assets are on the two most crowded A-D-C targets on the planet — HER two has trastuzumab deruxtecan owning it and multiple bispecific A-D-Cs coming, B seven H three has ifinatamab deruxtecan and several other programs in phase-three-ish territory. Myricx's differentiation has to come from the payload alone, not from the antibody-target choice.

Two threads that matter for how to think about this past the headline. Thread one — Novartis is paying a preclinical-stage multiple. One point one billion up front for two preclinical A-D-Cs is not a bet on those two A-D-Cs. It is a bet on establishing N-M-T inhibitors as a payload category applicable across many future targets, exactly the way Novartis positioned radioligand therapy after the Endocyte acquisition. Fiona Marshall's quote leans on this framing explicitly. Read this deal as Novartis buying a platform, and the discount you should apply to the translation risk on the two lead assets is very different.

Thread two — this reshapes the payload-diversification conversation for everyone building in the A-D-C space. If N-M-T inhibitor payloads validate clinically, every A-D-C developer now has to ask whether their next-generation asset should carry a payload orthogonal to what their current asset carries. Duality's exatecan-based platform, ProfoundBio, and the rest all face that question. One more thing worth noting — Ed Tate has been the leading academic in N-M-T drug discovery for two decades, and this is a fundamental-biology company that spent years de-risking the payload chemistry, not a platform-hop asset assemblage. That matters for whether the mechanistic claims hold up when the field starts kicking the tires.

Bottom line. Small-ish deal by 2026 standards, but the first meaningful validation in years of a fundamentally new A-D-C payload class — a targeted-therapy-style mechanism delivered inside an A-D-C, potentially orthogonal to deruxtecan and auristatin cross-resistance. Two preclinical assets to watch, both on maximally crowded targets, so the differentiation lever is the payload itself. Watch for the first-in-human trial designs, for whether Novartis extends the N-M-T inhibitor payload onto radioligand or T-cell-engager backbones, and for a rush of novel-payload platform deals in the next two quarters. That is it for today.
