# LGR5 and MET ADCs — colorectal tumors escape an LGR5-targeting ADC by switching on MET, and a reciprocally-reinforcing dual-ADC combination delays relapse in patient-derived xenografts

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.18.739355v1

## Script

Today's pick is a preclinical paper out of a National Cancer Institute–funded group, posted to bioRxiv yesterday, and it's a nice piece of ADC resistance biology with a combination strategy bolted onto it. The setting is colorectal cancer, and the starting point is L-G-R-5 — a receptor that marks cancer stem-like cells, sits high on colorectal tumors and low on normal tissue, which is exactly the expression profile you want for an antibody-drug conjugate.

Here's the problem they set out to solve. An L-G-R-5 ADC — in their hands, an L-G-R-5 antibody carrying a topoisomerase-1 payload — knocks tumors down hard at first, but they come back. And when the group looked at why, they found two things happening together. Under treatment with either chemotherapy or the L-G-R-5 ADC, the tumor cells turn L-G-R-5 down, so the antigen the drug depends on gets scarcer, and at the same time they turn the MET receptor up and switch it on. So the escape route isn't just losing the target. It's losing the target while pivoting onto a completely different receptor for survival.

That reciprocal wiring is the actual insight here, and it points to an obvious move: hit MET too. So they built a MET ADC — telisotuzumab, the same anti-MET antibody that's already an approved drug in c-Met-high lung cancer, but conjugated to a pyrrolobenzodiazepine dimer, a DNA-crosslinking payload, a P-B-D. And this is where I want to slow down, because they make a strong claim. Their P-B-D version was more potent and more effective in colorectal models than the two clinical-stage MET ADCs built on that exact same telisotuzumab antibody — one carrying an MMAE payload, which is the approved drug, and one carrying a camptothecin-class topoisomerase-1 payload, which is AbbVie's next-generation version. Same antibody, three payloads, and the P-B-D dimer won on potency.

Then the loop closes. The MET ADC does the mirror image of the L-G-R-5 ADC — killing MET-high cells pushes the survivors back toward L-G-R-5. So each drug drives the tumor toward the other one's target. Give them together, and in patient-derived xenografts the combination markedly delayed relapse and extended survival versus either single agent. That's a genuinely elegant design — not two random ADCs stacked for additive killing, but two ADCs whose resistance mechanisms feed each other's target, so co-treatment traps the tumor in a corner it can't escape by antigen switching.

Now the opinionated part, because the potency claim is the weakest link in an otherwise clean story. Potency in a mouse is not the thing that has killed P-B-D-dimer ADCs in the clinic — the therapeutic window is. The entire reason AbbVie's own program moved from an MMAE payload to a topoisomerase-1 payload on this exact antibody is tolerability, not potency. P-B-D dimers as a payload class have a brutal clinical history — most of them washed out on toxicity, and only one P-B-D ADC is approved anywhere. So "our P-B-D version is more potent than the topo-1 version" is, read carefully, running the field's decision backwards. The interesting result isn't the payload — it's the reciprocal L-G-R-5–MET biology and the combination logic, which would hold regardless of which payloads you hang on the two antibodies. I'd want to see this combination run with clinically tolerable payloads before reading much into the P-B-D data.

Why this matters for your world. The reciprocal-antigen-regulation finding is the generalizable part. We spend a lot of time on antigen loss as an ADC and T-cell-engager resistance mechanism — the tumor drops the target and goes dark. This is a more specific and more actionable version. The tumor doesn't just lose the target, it predictably pivots onto a named second receptor, and that second receptor is itself druggable with an ADC you can pair up front. If that reciprocal wiring generalizes beyond L-G-R-5 and MET — and the group frames it as a broader principle — then the design move is to map the compensatory antigen your first ADC drives cells toward, and build the combination before resistance shows up, rather than chasing it after. That's a cleaner rationale for rational ADC combinations than the "two active drugs, hope for additivity" logic most combination trials run on.

The caveats are the usual preclinical ones — colorectal cell lines and xenografts, no primary human data, no read on whether the combination's tolerability survives contact with a real patient, and the P-B-D question hanging over the MET arm. But as a piece of mechanism, the reciprocal L-G-R-5–MET switch and the self-reinforcing combination it motivates is exactly the kind of resistance-biology-driven combination design worth having in your head. What to watch is whether the group takes this toward the clinic with a more forgiving MET payload, and whether the reciprocal-regulation principle shows up for other stem-cell-marker-plus-receptor-tyrosine-kinase pairings.
