# AK146D1 — first-in-class TROP2 × Nectin-4 bispecific ADC starts Phase 2 in combination with ivonescimab in first-line NSCLC

Paper link: https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/akeso-doses-first-patient-in-phase-ii-study-of-novel-trop2nectin-4-bispecific-adc-ak146d1-in-combination-with-ivonescimab-for-advanced-nsclc-302829383.html

## Script

Akeso announced this morning that it dosed the first patient in a Phase 2 study called AK146D1 dash 201. The regimen is AK146D1, a bispecific antibody-drug conjugate that targets both TROP2 and Nectin-4, given in combination with ivonescimab, their PD-1 by VEGF bispecific antibody. The population is advanced non-small cell lung cancer, with a particular emphasis on the first-line setting. On its face this is a Phase 2 initiation announcement — no data, no patients treated yet, just a first-patient-dosed press release. But the design of the regimen is the story, because it stacks two bispecific molecules on top of each other, and that combination geometry is the direction the whole first-line lung cancer field has been moving toward.

Let me lay out the pieces. Ivonescimab is the PD-1 by VEGF bispecific that beat Keytruda head-to-head in first-line PD-L1-positive lung cancer in the HARMONi-2 trial last year — that was the moment the VEGF-plus-checkpoint hypothesis stopped being a design speculation and became a Phase 3 fact. Summit Therapeutics has the ex-China rights and every pharma in the space is now either building their own PD-1 by VEGF bispecific or licensing one. So ivonescimab is the anchor asset — Akeso is combining everything else in their portfolio with it, because in first-line lung cancer that is the highest-value backbone they can plausibly build a combo around.

AK146D1 is the new part. It is a bispecific ADC — one antibody, two targets on the same molecule, with a cytotoxic payload conjugated to the antibody. The two targets are TROP2 and Nectin-4. Both of those are single-target ADC targets that already have approved drugs. TROP2 is what sacituzumab govitecan and datopotamab deruxtecan and Kelun's sac-TMT all hit. Nectin-4 is what enfortumab vedotin — Padcev — hits in bladder cancer. The logic of putting the two on one bispecific ADC is that TROP2 is broadly expressed across many epithelial tumors including normal epithelium, and Nectin-4 is more selective but expressed on a narrower slice of tumors. A single-target ADC has to compromise — you either dose to hit tumors that express one antigen at low levels and accept the tolerability hit, or you underdose. A bispecific ADC in principle lets you require both antigens for the highest-avidity binding, so the tumor cells that co-express get the full dose and normal tissues that express only one get less. Akeso frames this as ADC 2.0 — meaning wider therapeutic window than the first generation.

I want to be honest about how much of that mechanism is proven and how much is design bet. The bispecific-ADC selectivity story is beautifully clean on a slide. Whether it actually delivers in patients depends on things like the affinity balance between the two arms, whether obligate co-engagement really happens or whether monovalent binding is enough at high tumor antigen density, and whether TROP2 and Nectin-4 co-express on the tumor cells that matter or on separate subpopulations. That's what Phase 1 was for and what Phase 2 will start to answer. What we have today is that AK146D1 got through Phase 1 well enough to graduate into a combination Phase 2 with the company's flagship asset, which is a modest but real vote of confidence in the safety profile.

The combination logic with ivonescimab is where this gets interesting for your world. In first-line non-small cell lung cancer, the standard of care right now for the driver-negative population is chemotherapy plus a PD-1 inhibitor — the KEYNOTE-189 regimen with pembrolizumab plus pemetrexed plus platinum. Every next-generation player is trying to displace part of that. Kelun-Biotech showed a few days ago that sac-TMT plus Keytruda beat that regimen in PD-L1-negative patients — the first Phase 3 win for an ADC-plus-checkpoint doublet against the chemo-plus-checkpoint standard. Akeso's regimen is the more ambitious version of the same idea. Instead of a single-target ADC plus a single-target checkpoint mAb, it's a bispecific ADC plus a bispecific PD-1-by-VEGF antibody. Two modalities, two targets each, four target engagements in the regimen. If it works, the chemo backbone disappears from first-line non-small cell lung cancer entirely, and the winning combination is two bispecifics.

The read-through for someone thinking about T cell engagers is worth pausing on. Everything you learn about how a bispecific ADC works in combination with a bispecific checkpoint is design intelligence for the trispecific TCE playbook. The core question is the same — when you have two engineered antibody modalities stacked, do their pharmacology and safety compose the way you'd hope, or does the additive on-target-off-tumor toxicity from four target engagements collapse the window. You'd rather learn the answer from an ADC plus checkpoint combination first than from a trispecific TCE plus a checkpoint. So this is a data source worth tracking not because it's directly your modality but because it's a leading indicator.

The take. This is not a data announcement — it's a program announcement. The reason to spend attention on it anyway is that Akeso is signaling how the entire first-line non-small cell lung cancer battlefield is going to be structured over the next two years. The play is two bispecifics on top of each other, no chemo, wider therapeutic window from co-engagement logic on the ADC side, VEGF-plus-checkpoint on the IO side. Ivonescimab plus a bispecific ADC in first-line NSCLC is going to be the design pattern people benchmark against. The first Phase 2 efficacy signal from AK146D1 dash 201 will come sometime in 2027, probably at ASCO or WCLC. What to watch in the meantime — the specific Phase 2 endpoints Akeso registers, the biomarker strategy (do they require dual TROP2 slash Nectin-4 co-expression, or all-comers), and whether Summit Therapeutics ends up with the ex-China rights on AK146D1 the way they have ivonescimab. If Summit picks up the bispecific ADC too, the pairing becomes a global platform play, and the trispecific TCE field should be paying close attention to what the combination geometry can and cannot do.
