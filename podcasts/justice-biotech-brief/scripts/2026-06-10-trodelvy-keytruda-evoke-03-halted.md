# Trodelvy + Keytruda — Phase 3 EVOKE-03 halted in first-line PD-L1-high NSCLC

Paper link: https://www.merck.com/news/merck-and-gilead-provide-update-on-phase-3-keynote-d46-evoke-03-study/

## Script

Welcome to Justice's Biotech Brief for Wednesday, June 10th. Today's pick is the Phase 3 combination trial that Merck and Gilead jointly pulled the plug on Monday — Trodelvy plus Keytruda in first-line metastatic non-small cell lung cancer with high PD-L1 expression. It's the kind of failure that doesn't just close one program. It reshapes the competitive set in this exact setting for the next twelve months, and that's why it's worth the full episode.

The trial is the one Merck called KEYNOTE-D46 and Gilead called EVOKE-03 — same study, two sponsor names, about six hundred and twenty patients. The design was Trodelvy on top of Keytruda versus Keytruda alone, in previously untreated metastatic non-small cell lung cancer with PD-L1 expression at or above fifty percent and no targetable driver mutation. Dual primary endpoints — progression-free survival and overall survival. On Monday, the independent data monitoring committee told both companies that the pre-specified PFS analysis came in numerically better for the combination but missed statistical significance, and that the chance of hitting overall survival at the planned final analysis was low. Both companies took the recommendation and stopped the trial. Safety was unremarkable. This is a pure efficacy miss.

Here's the part that should land. Trodelvy is sacituzumab govitecan — a TROP-2-directed antibody-drug conjugate with an SN-38 payload and a hydrolyzable linker. It's the established TROP-2 ADC, with a real franchise in triple-negative and HR-positive breast cancer. But TROP-2 as a target has produced very different results across different ADC chemistries. At ASCO this year, Kelun and Merck reported that sac-TMT — sacituzumab tirumotecan, a different TROP-2 ADC with a different payload class and a different linker — cut the risk of progression by roughly sixty-five percent in first-line non-small cell lung cancer. Same target. Very different drug. Very different outcome. The honest read on Monday's news is that this is a sacituzumab govitecan failure, not a TROP-2 failure. The ADC chemistry mattered more than the antigen choice.

The second read-through hits the VEGF and PD-L1 wheelhouse directly. The competitive set for first-line PD-L1-high non-small cell lung cancer is moving fast. Ivonescimab — Akeso and Summit's PD-1-by-VEGF bispecific — beat tislelizumab plus chemotherapy in the HARMONi-6 squamous trial at the ASCO plenary, with a 4.2-month overall survival improvement and a thirty-four percent reduction in the risk of death. That's the moving target any ADC-plus-checkpoint combination now has to clear. And there's the parallel TROP-2 ADC-plus-checkpoint program — datopotamab deruxtecan from AstraZeneca and Daiichi Sankyo, paired with Imfinzi — still working its way through Phase 3 in this same setting. Monday's failure raises the bar for what datopotamab needs to show. And it puts pressure on the broader thesis that TROP-2 ADC plus PD-1 is a combinable strategy in first-line lung at all. Where the strategy probably still works is in the PD-L1-low population — where checkpoint monotherapy underperforms and there's more room for an ADC to add value. The PD-L1-high setting may already be too saturated at the top for an ADC partner to move the needle.

The bottom line for the day. Trodelvy as a single agent in breast cancer is fine. Trodelvy as a combination expansion play in PD-L1-high lung is now off the table. The next dataset to watch is whatever AstraZeneca says next about datopotamab plus durvalumab in this setting — that's the program now carrying the TROP-2-ADC-plus-checkpoint thesis. The broader picture is that ivonescimab and the PD-1-by-VEGF bispecific class are reshaping the first-line non-small cell lung market faster than ADC combinations can catch up. Worth keeping pumitamig in mind too — BioNTech and BMS's PD-L1-by-VEGF bispecific has a Phase 3 reading into the same competitive set. That's the day. See you tomorrow.
