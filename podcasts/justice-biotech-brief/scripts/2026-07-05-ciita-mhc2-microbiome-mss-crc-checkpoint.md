## Script

Good morning, Justice. Today is Sunday, July 5th, 2026 — Justice's Biotech Brief, one nugget from the last two days on the biotech and pharma beat you care about.

Today's pick is a bioRxiv preprint that landed yesterday from the Beyaz lab at Cold Spring Harbor, with microbiome collaborators from MIT and the University of Pittsburgh. It is a mechanistic paper, not a clinical readout, but the mechanism is one you should file away — because it names a way to convert microsatellite-stable colorectal cancer into a tumor that actually responds to checkpoint blockade. That is the piece of the oncology market that has been essentially blank since Keytruda's MSI-high approval. If this holds up, it changes how you think about the microbiome — not as a modifier of immunotherapy, but as an actual pharmacology target upstream of it.

Here is the one-line take. A specific Helicobacter-containing community in the mouse gut induces the transcription factor CIITA in the colon cancer cells themselves, which turns on MHC class II on the surface of the tumor, and that tumor-cell-intrinsic MHC class II is enough to convert combined PD-1 plus CTLA-4 blockade from a 10 percent complete rejection rate to a 71 percent complete rejection rate in a hard, immune-evasive mouse model of MSS colorectal cancer. Cancer-cell-intrinsic MHC class II — the same target melanoma correlative work has been quietly circling for the last five years — turns out to be both necessary and, for the practical purpose of sensitizing the tumor, sufficient.

Here is how they got there. They ran four orthotopic colorectal models in the mouse — the two classical syngeneic lines, and more importantly, AKP and AKPS organoids implanted endoscopically into the colon. The AKPS model — Apc-null, Kras-mutant, Trp53-null, Smad4-null — is the aggressive metastatic MSS model, and that is why this work matters translationally. It is the setting where checkpoint inhibitors do essentially nothing in humans. In this system, animals raised with a specific Helicobacter-containing community — Helicobacter typhlonius and Helicobacter mastomyrinus in particular — got smaller primary tumors, fewer metastases, more intratumoral T cell infiltration, and stronger effector programs. Single-species colonization only partially rescues the phenotype, whole-flora transfer gives the maximum effect, and three other Helicobacter species do nothing. So this is not a generic gut-microbiome effect — it is a specific bacterial signature, and the field has been circling something like this in melanoma for years without a mechanistic anchor.

Now, why cancer-cell MHC class II? Antigen presentation on tumor cells is supposed to be an MHC class I business — that is the CD8 loop, the classical cross-presentation story. Class II is supposed to sit on professional antigen-presenting cells, which then talk to CD4 helpers. But in a subset of solid tumors — melanoma, lung, and increasingly colorectal — the cancer cells themselves can be induced to express class II, and when they do, CD4 T cells appear to see the tumor directly. This paper closes the causality loop cleanly. Knock out CIITA — the master transcription factor for class II — in the AKPS organoid, and the microbiome protection collapses. Tumors grow, metastases return, T cell infiltration drops, and MHC-I stays on. So the effect is class-II-specific, not a generic antigen presentation collapse.

Force CIITA expression instead — that is the gain-of-function arm — and even in mice without the protective Helicobacter community, tumors shrink and metastasis incidence drops. And here is the pharmacology-relevant piece. Layer PD-1 plus CTLA-4 blockade onto that CIITA-overexpressing tumor, and 71 percent of the mice completely reject the tumor. The Helicobacter-negative-plus-checkpoint control is 10 percent. That is a seven-fold shift in the rejection rate driven by a single transcription factor turned on in the tumor cell. And critically, in patient-derived organoids from human MSS colorectal cancer, CIITA overexpression drives more autologous T cell colocalization with the organoid and more organoid apoptosis in co-culture. The mouse story translates in a dish with human cells.

Two important open questions. The first is what peptides the tumor MHC class II is actually presenting — the authors flag this as unresolved, and it is the piece that will determine whether the CD4 response is driven by microbial mimics, by shared self-antigens, or by tumor neoantigens. The second is the exact molecular path from Helicobacter to CIITA induction in the epithelial cell. The paper does not resolve whether it is direct microbial signaling on the cancer cell, or whether it goes through an interferon-gamma loop via a myeloid intermediary — which is the classical CIITA driver. That matters, because if the effect is interferon-gamma-mediated, you have a small-molecule handle for pharmacologically inducing tumor MHC class II without needing to reshape the gut microbiome at all.

The translational read-through for the work you care about. This is a rational combination hypothesis for the MSS colorectal setting, which is where every checkpoint program has been stuck for a decade — that is about eighty percent of colorectal cancer, and it currently gets nothing from a checkpoint inhibitor. It is not a drug yet — it is a target thesis with three possible arms. One, a live biotherapeutic — reconstitute the protective Helicobacter community as a pre-treatment. Two, a small-molecule or biologic CIITA inducer, or a tumor-cell-targeted MHC class II construct — the more elegant and drug-developable path. Three, a companion diagnostic — biopsy tumor MHC class II expression in MSS colorectal patients as a checkpoint-blockade enrichment marker. Whichever arm gets picked first, the mechanism is a lot more actionable than it was a week ago. And for your own beat on trispecific T cell engagers and CD3-engaging modalities in solid tumors, this is a reminder that tumor MHC class II engagement is the pathway that gets CD4 help back into the fight — a design lever the TCE field has largely left on the table.

Caveats. Preprint, mouse-dominant, single group, and the human organoid work is elegant but ex vivo. The upstream signaling from microbe to CIITA is still unresolved. The AKPS orthotopic model is the right mouse model for MSS colorectal cancer, but it is not a patient. What I would watch next — replication in a second independent MSS model, an interferon-gamma-versus-direct-microbe pathway dissection, and any translational group picking up the CIITA-induction pharmacology angle. If a company files an IND on a tumor-MHC-class-II induction program in the next twelve months, it will be because of this paper.

That's your brief for today. Have a good Sunday.
