# viroVbot — a computationally-screened Piry-virus envelope plus layered safety switches for redosable in-vivo CAR-T across BCMA/CD19 myeloma and Claudin-18.2 gastric cancer

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.30.735484v1

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, and it lands squarely in your CAR T lane — but from a preprint side of the field rather than a company press release. A group led out of Jamia Millia Islamia in New Delhi, with collaborators at the Institute of Genomics and Integrative Biology, posted a paper to bio-archive on Tuesday describing what they call viroVbot — a next-generation in vivo CAR T platform. Standard note upfront: I am going off the abstract here. The preprint went up 48 hours ago and bio-archive has not yet rendered the full body H-T-M-L, so the numbers and controls I would normally lean on are not readable yet. But the framing and the design decisions are worth walking you through today, because if any fraction of what they claim replicates, this is a template that competes head-on with what Umoja, Interius, Capstan, and Orbital are trying to build.

Three threads. What the platform actually does differently. Why the envelope choice is the interesting part. And how the safety layering stacks up against the current in-vivo CAR T bar.

First, what viroVbot is. In-vivo CAR T is the field's escape hatch from autologous manufacturing — you inject a T-cell-tropic viral vector into the patient, the vector fuses with circulating T cells, and the patient's own body becomes the manufacturing line. The two chronic problems are envelope immunogenicity, which limits redosing because the patient's B cells make anti-envelope antibodies after the first exposure, and off-target transduction, because whatever tropism your envelope has for T cells it usually also has for something else — hepatocytes, B cells, macrophages. Every in-vivo CAR T program in the clinic right now is fighting one or both of these battles. viroVbot's thesis is that you can fix both at the same time if you start upstream, at envelope selection, with a computational-immunogenicity-first design filter rather than a tropism-first one.

Concretely, they screened twenty-two thousand five hundred and sixty-two glycoprotein sequences with an in-house immunogenicity prediction stack, narrowing down to six hundred and forty-one vesiculovirus homologs. Vesiculoviruses are the family V-S-V — vesicular stomatitis virus — belongs to; the standard V-S-V-G envelope has been the workhorse for lentiviral transduction for two decades but it is immunogenic in humans and has broad tropism through the L-D-L receptor. Out of that vesiculovirus shortlist they picked the Piry virus glycoprotein — Piry is a rare Brazilian vesiculovirus, sero-negative in almost the entire human population, and by their prediction it presents fewer M-H-C class one and class two epitopes than V-S-V-G does. In their hands the Piry envelope also drove less T-cell activation in vitro compared to V-S-V-G controls. So the core swap is a low-seroprevalence, low-M-H-C-epitope-density envelope in the same viral family that the field already knows how to package and produce.

Second, and this is the design move you should sit with, they did not just swap the envelope — they broke it and then rebuilt it for T-cell targeting. They engineered a receptor-binding-deficient Piry glycoprotein — meaning the native tropism is dead — and then displayed C-D-three and C-D-seven single-domain antibodies on the surface of that broken envelope. That is the same retargeting concept Umoja and Interius are using, but layered on a starting envelope that was picked for immune-invisibility rather than one that was picked for potency and then dragged into acceptable safety with rerouting later. Order of operations matters here. Retargeting a low-immunogenic envelope keeps the immunogenicity gains; retargeting V-S-V-G still leaves you with V-S-V-G's underlying M-H-C profile.

Third, the safety layering. They stack three additional locks on top of the retargeted envelope. One, engineered producer cells that eliminate residual B-cell transduction — because a C-D-three-plus-C-D-seven combined targeting is not sufficient on its own to avoid some B-cell fusion, and B-cell transduction of a CAR construct is exactly the failure mode you never want repeated after the Kymriah lymphoma-transformation cases. Two, machine-learning-optimized T-cell-specific promoters driving CAR expression, so that even if a hepatocyte or a macrophage does take up the vector, the CAR does not turn on because the promoter is inactive outside T cells. Three, additional modifications explicitly aimed at suppressing hepatocyte expression and reducing phagocytic uptake. Three-layer safety with orthogonal mechanisms — targeting, promoter, and biodistribution modifiers — is the standard the field is converging on, and this is a fully-articulated version of it in one platform.

The claims that would justify the noise, if the numbers hold up. First, viroVbot-three — their lead construct — generated potent B-C-M-A-and-C-D-nineteen dual-specific CAR T responses against multiple myeloma in humanized xenografts. Dual-antigen CAR against B-C-M-A and C-D-nineteen in one hit is a plausible response-durability play in myeloma given the antigen-escape data from single-target B-C-M-A CAR programs. Second — and this is the one that matters most for your interest area — they show activity in claudin eighteen point two-targeting CAR T against gastric cancer, in the same platform. Solid-tumor CAR T is where in-vivo CAR is supposed to be the enabling technology, because ex-vivo autologous solid-tumor CAR programs have been strangled by manufacturing time, cost, and tumor-microenvironment infiltration barriers. If a single in-vivo delivery platform reproducibly generates functional claudin eighteen point two CAR T in vivo, that is a genuinely new tool in the solid-tumor cell-therapy toolbox.

Third, and the piece that binds the whole story together — they demonstrate sequential redosing with alternative envelopes. That is the point of doing the twenty-two-thousand-glycoprotein screen. You get one envelope, then you get a second envelope with a different M-H-C footprint but the same C-D-three-slash-C-D-seven retargeting apparatus, and you dose that one when anti-first-envelope antibodies have made round one obsolete. Redosability is the single biggest structural limitation of the current in-vivo CAR T generation, and this preprint is the first I have seen that frames the redosing problem as an envelope-library problem rather than as an ADA-suppression problem.

The caveats to hold. Preprint, not peer-reviewed, single group's data, humanized xenograft not patient. No release yet on whether the redosing experiments were done in the same animal or in parallel cohorts. The machine-learning-optimized promoter claim requires seeing the actual promoter panel and the leakiness data — promoter engineering has burned this field before. And the low human seroprevalence claim for Piry rests on prior serosurveys in South America that were not powered for global generalizability. If a fraction of humans have pre-existing anti-Piry immunity, the "invisible envelope" story is more limited than the abstract implies.

Net take. viroVbot is the first in-vivo CAR T proposal I have seen that treats envelope immunogenicity as a design variable rather than a problem to route around, and that treats redosing as a portfolio problem — one platform, many envelopes — rather than as a single-molecule-can-be-redosed problem. If it holds up on peer review it becomes a template rather than a product, which is arguably the more interesting outcome for your field. Watch for the body to come off the bio-archive render queue in the next 24 to 48 hours; the quantitative controls on the immunogenicity screen and the redosing curves are what will actually decide whether this is a real advance or a well-composed abstract.
