# Teclistamab plus daratumumab — BCMA T-cell engager plus CD38 monoclonal earns CHMP positive opinion for second-line myeloma on a MajesTEC-3 PFS hazard ratio of 0.17, eighty-three percent complete response, and CRS that never broke grade two

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, and it lands squarely on your B-C-M-A T-cell engager interest from the regulatory angle that closes the loop on the most important phase three readout in myeloma this cycle. On Friday, June twenty-sixth, the European Medicines Agency's Committee for Medicinal Products for Human Use issued a positive opinion recommending that teclistamab in combination with subcutaneous daratumumab be approved for relapsed or refractory multiple myeloma in patients who have received at least one prior line of therapy. The F-D-A approved the same combination in March under the same indication, so the CHMP move is the E-U catching up — but the MajesTEC-3 data underneath it is the real story, and the load-bearing implication is that the combination architecture — B-C-M-A bispecific plus C-D-thirty-eight monoclonal — has now produced the deepest phase three data set in myeloma history.

The setup is what makes the numbers interpretable. Teclistamab as a monotherapy was approved by the F-D-A and E-M-A back in late twenty-twenty-two, but only for patients who had failed at least four prior lines of therapy — a triple-class-refractory population where the bar for any active agent is low and the median overall survival is single-digit months. The MajesTEC-3 question was whether you could move the same molecule — teclistamab, a B-C-M-A by C-D-three bispecific T-cell engager — earlier into the disease course, into the second-, third-, or fourth-line setting, where the standard of care is daratumumab plus pomalidomide and dexamethasone or daratumumab plus bortezomib and dexamethasone. Both of those triplets are themselves competitive, with median progression-free survivals in the twelve-to-eighteen month range. To beat them, you need a regimen that pushes the T-cell engager's depth-of-response advantage without paying back too much in toxicity. The combination logic — pair the bispecific with daratumumab rather than with another I-MID or proteasome inhibitor — is the design choice that turned out to matter.

Now the data. Five-hundred-eighty-seven patients with one to three prior lines of therapy, randomized two-ninety-one to two-ninety-six between teclistamab plus subcutaneous daratumumab and the investigator's choice of standard-of-care daratumumab triplet. Primary endpoint progression-free survival. The hazard ratio is zero-point-one-seven, ninety-five-percent confidence interval zero-point-one-two to zero-point-two-three, p-value less than zero-point-zero-zero-zero-one. An eighty-three-percent reduction in the risk of disease progression or death. The median progression-free survival in the control arm was eighteen-point-one months. The median in the combination arm has not been reached. The three-year progression-free survival estimate is eighty-three-point-four percent in the combination arm versus twenty-nine-point-seven percent with the standard daratumumab triplet. Overall survival hazard ratio zero-point-four-six, ninety-five-percent confidence interval zero-point-three-two to zero-point-six-five, p-value less than zero-point-zero-zero-zero-one — a fifty-four-percent reduction in mortality, three-year overall survival eighty-three-point-three percent versus sixty-five percent. The overall response rate was eighty-nine percent versus seventy-five-point-three percent. The complete-response-or-better rate was eighty-one-point-eight percent versus thirty-two-point-one percent — an odds ratio of nine-point-five-six. And the M-R-D negativity rate at ten-to-the-minus-five sensitivity was fifty-eight-point-four percent versus seventeen-point-one percent. At ten-to-the-minus-six sensitivity, in patients who achieved complete response or better, M-R-D negativity was eighty-seven-point-five percent versus forty-one-point-eight. That eighty-seven-percent-deep-M-R-D-clearance number is the deepest measurable-residual-disease readout the myeloma field has from any phase three trial.

The intuition is what should land for your work. The pharmacology of why teclistamab plus daratumumab works at this depth is the load-bearing piece. Teclistamab does what every B-C-M-A bispecific does — it bridges a T cell to a plasma cell via simultaneous binding of B-C-M-A on the tumor and C-D-three on the effector, redirecting cytotoxic killing. The problem with B-C-M-A bispecifics as monotherapy is that the tumor microenvironment in myeloma is heavily immunosuppressive — high regulatory T cells, high myeloid-derived suppressor cells, exhausted effector T cells — and the redirection lands into a setting where the T cells can't sustain their kill. Daratumumab is the answer to that problem, and it's not the obvious one. The standard read on daratumumab is that it's a tumor-cell-directed antibody — C-D-thirty-eight is expressed on plasma cells, daratumumab binds, complement and A-D-C-C kill the tumor cell. That mechanism is real but it's secondary. The dominant mechanism in this combination is that daratumumab also depletes C-D-thirty-eight-high regulatory T cells and a subset of immunosuppressive myeloid cells. You are giving the daratumumab arm a head start to clear the suppressive cells out of the bone marrow microenvironment, and then the teclistamab arm engages the cleaned-up effector compartment against the plasma cell. The combination is stacking three mechanisms — C-D-thirty-eight-mediated tumor killing, immune-microenvironment conditioning by Treg and M-D-S-C depletion, and T-cell redirection by the bispecific — and the synergy shows up in both the response depth and, more surprisingly, the safety profile.

The safety is the part you might have to read twice. Cytokine release syndrome occurred in sixty-point-one percent of patients in the combination arm — but every single case was grade one or grade two. No grade three or higher C-R-S. None. Immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity occurred in one-point-one percent of patients — rare and mild. Treatment discontinuations due to adverse events were four-point-six percent in the combination arm versus five-point-five percent in the standard-of-care arm — meaning the bispecific-containing combination was actually better tolerated than the standard triplet. The trade-off is infection rate — grade three or four infections occurred in fifty-four-point-one percent of combination patients versus forty-three-point-four percent on the control arm, a real but expected delta given that you are running a B-C-M-A bispecific that depletes normal plasma cells alongside a C-D-thirty-eight antibody that hits T cell and N-K cell subsets. The C-R-S taming is the technical lesson — the immune pre-conditioning by daratumumab appears to reduce the cytokine storm that you would otherwise see with bispecific T-cell engagers, because Tregs that suppress over-activation are depleted before the bispecific arrives, but more importantly the M-D-S-C and exhausted effector populations that drive aberrant inflammatory signaling have been thinned out. The combination doesn't just amplify efficacy — it also smooths the safety profile.

What this means for the broader B-C-M-A and trispecific T-cell engager design space is the bigger take. The MajesTEC-3 template is now the new bar for any bispecific T-cell engager moving into earlier-line myeloma — pair it with daratumumab, target the bone-marrow microenvironment in parallel with the tumor cell, and you should get response depth and safety in the same package. The MonumenTAL-3 readout for talquetamab, which Justice's brief covered ten days back, is the G-P-R-C-5-D version of the same playbook, and the data was directionally similar — talquetamab plus daratumumab held its own head-to-head against the standard triplets at first relapse, with response depth and safety profiles that justified the move out of late-line. Cevostamab, the F-c-R-H-5 bispecific from Genentech, has phase one and phase two combination data with daratumumab that have been promising enough to set up the analogous phase three. The trispecific T-cell engagers — Moderna's mRNA-2808 from the Science Day readout on Thursday, J-N-J's J-N-J-five-zero-zero-three, the third-generation single-chain trispecifics — all sit in the next-generation pipeline behind this combination architecture. The question MajesTEC-3 resolves is not whether B-C-M-A bispecifics work in earlier lines — it's whether the combination architecture with C-D-thirty-eight monoclonal is the right one, and the answer is unambiguously yes. The template — bispecific T-cell engager plus tumor-cell-directed immune-modulating monoclonal — is now portable to every other myeloma target and, importantly, to other heme malignancies. The same logic applies to bispecifics targeting C-D-twenty in lymphoma plus rituximab or obinutuzumab, to C-D-thirty bispecifics in Hodgkin disease, and is starting to be tested in C-D-three by C-D-nineteen-by-C-D-twenty trispecifics where the architectural question is whether the redirection should be paired with C-D-thirty-eight or with C-D-twenty depletion.

The caveats are real and worth holding. MajesTEC-3 is a phase three in a population that has had one-to-three prior lines, which means most patients are first or second relapse. The fold-out into the first-line setting — induction therapy in newly diagnosed myeloma — is the next regulatory frontier, and the relevant trials are MajesTEC-7 and similar quadruplet studies running bispecific plus daratumumab plus an I-MID or proteasome inhibitor against the V-R-d or daratumumab-V-R-d standard. Those readouts will be the real test of whether teclistamab displaces the lenalidomide-based triplets as the cornerstone of induction. The infection rate also matters in community practice — fifty-four percent grade three or four infections is a number that requires institutional support for antimicrobial prophylaxis and aggressive monitoring, and the community-practice tolerability of teclistamab plus daratumumab outside academic centers is the operational question community oncologists are now figuring out. The cost structure of running a B-C-M-A bispecific plus a C-D-thirty-eight monoclonal — both biologics with monthly continuing therapy — is also non-trivial, especially with M-R-D-driven treatment-duration decisions still being worked out. And the F-D-A approval in March used the C-D-3-8-hyaluronidase formulation; the C-H-M-P recommendation specifies the subcutaneous daratumumab formulation. Both are consistent — the bispecific is the active ingredient — but the formulation pairing is part of why the combination tolerability is what it is, because subcutaneous administration smooths the infusion-reaction profile.

Net take. The MajesTEC-3 readout is now the reference data set for B-C-M-A T-cell engagers in earlier-line myeloma, and the C-H-M-P recommendation closes the global regulatory loop after the F-D-A March approval. For your work on next-generation T-cell engagers and the broader Calibr immunology floor, the load-bearing implication is twofold. First — the depth of response signature is real, and the response rates are not the headline. The headline is that the combination architecture pulls eighty-seven percent M-R-D negativity at ten-to-the-minus-six in complete responders, which is the depth of clearance that, if durable, starts to look like cure-equivalent biology in a disease that has historically been incurable. Second — the C-R-S taming is the technical insight worth pulling forward into next-generation T-cell engager design. Pair the bispecific with an upstream immune-modulator that conditions the microenvironment, and you appear to get both efficacy and safety wins from the same combination move. The Padcev-Keytruda story from yesterday was the A-D-C-plus-checkpoint version of the same logic in a curative-intent perioperative setting. The MajesTEC-3 story is the T-cell-engager-plus-C-D-thirty-eight-monoclonal version in a chronic-disease earlier-line setting. Both are pointing at the same architectural lesson — the single-agent era of B-C-M-A bispecifics is closing, and the combination era, with the right immune-modulating partner, is where the next decade of myeloma and other heme-malignancy therapy gets built. Worth flagging across the floor.
