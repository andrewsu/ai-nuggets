## Script

Good morning, Justice. Today's Biotech Brief is one preprint out of the National Cancer Institute's Center for Cancer Research — Tim Greten's group — that fixes a specific translational deadlock in the CAR T cell space. The problem is this. Hepatocellular carcinoma is treated in a big fraction of eligible patients by liver transplantation, and roughly one in five of those patients relapses with recurrent HCC in the transplanted liver or elsewhere. When they relapse, they are on lifelong tacrolimus — or one of the FKBP-twelve-binding mTOR inhibitors, sirolimus or everolimus — because the graft won't survive without it. Options for recurrence today are tyrosine kinase inhibitors and not much else. CAR T against Glypican-three, or G-P-C-three, has been the obvious tumor-directed play — G-P-C-three is a cell-surface oncofetal antigen expressed on the majority of HCCs and largely absent from healthy adult tissue — but every autologous or allogeneic CAR T candidate walks straight into tacrolimus, which shuts T cell activation off at the calcineurin step. You can't take the patient off tacrolimus without losing the graft. So the CAR T doesn't work.

The Greten group's fix is a two-cut CRISPR edit. First cut, F-K-B-P-one-A — the gene for FKBP-twelve, the immunophilin that tacrolimus, sirolimus, and everolimus all have to bind to before they can form the inhibitory complex that blocks calcineurin or mTOR. Delete F-K-B-P-one-A in the CAR T and tacrolimus loses its adapter. The drug is still in the patient at therapeutic troughs, the graft is still being protected — the drug just can't touch the engineered T cell. The clever safety-switch corollary is that cyclosporine still works on these cells, because cyclosporine binds cyclophilin, not FKBP-twelve. So if you had to shut the CAR T down for cytokine release or a rejection episode, you swap tacrolimus for cyclosporine and you have pharmacologic control back. Second cut, T-R-A-C — the T cell receptor alpha constant gene — which knocks out the endogenous T C R and cuts alloreactivity against the transplanted liver. Standard allogeneic-CAR T move here, but essential in the transplant setting.

The results carry the concept. Wild-type G-P-C-three CAR T is suppressed by tacrolimus in vitro — cytokine production and cytotoxicity both drop. The F-K-B-P-one-A slash T-R-A-C double-knockout CAR T holds full cytokine and cytotoxic function in the presence of tacrolimus, and shows in-vivo antitumor activity in a Hep G-two xenograft model with the mice on tacrolimus. In the graft-versus-host disease model the T-R-A-C knockout knocks alloreactivity down. And, importantly for how this actually reaches a patient, they show they can generate functional double-knockout CAR T from the P B M Cs of liver transplant recipients drawn thirty days post-transplant, which is the window where you would want to be manufacturing if you're going to have a product ready if and when the patient relapses.

The selection piece is worth flagging on its own. To get a pure double-knockout population out of a mixed edit, they used mTOR inhibitor selection — everolimus in the media kills any cell that still has functional F-K-B-P-one-A — combined with a C-D-three-based M-A-C-S depletion to strip out any cell that still has functional T C R. That combination pushes the double-knockout enrichment above ninety-eight percent with cell viability intact. That is a scalable manufacturing protocol, not a bench trick. If you extend this framework to any other transplant setting where a patient is on FKBP-twelve-binding immunosuppression — kidney, heart, lung — the same F-K-B-P-one-A knockout would carry over to a different CAR-target module. The paper points at this explicitly as a platform, and I think that read is right.

Three quick threads on why this matters for the broader engager and cell-therapy program. First, this is the first published CAR T design that solves the drug-drug-interaction problem with standard-of-care solid-organ-transplant immunosuppression using a genetic-resistance approach rather than a suicide-switch or a temporal-holiday approach. That opens post-transplant recurrent cancers as a distinct addressable segment for cell therapy. Second, F-K-B-P-one-A is a small gene and the edit is one guide — this is a straightforward add to existing allogeneic CAR platforms without extending manufacturing timelines. Third, the same F-K-B-P-one-A knockout logic ports directly to bispecific-T-cell-engager platforms if you go the ex-vivo redirected T cell route — take patient T cells, edit them, activate them with the bispecific, infuse. For a T-cell-engager or trispecific program looking at solid-tumor targets in post-transplant patients, the F-K-B-P-one-A resistance module is now on the table.

Bottom line. A cellular-engineering fix — one small CRISPR edit at F-K-B-P-one-A, layered on a T-R-A-C knockout — turns the pharmacologically-dead post-liver-transplant recurrent HCC segment into a real CAR T opportunity, with cyclosporine retained as an off-switch. Preclinical, xenograft-stage, but the manufacturing feasibility from real patient P B M Cs is already shown. Watch for the first-in-human trial design out of the N-C-I intramural program and for the pattern spreading to kidney and heart transplant recurrence indications. That's it for today.
