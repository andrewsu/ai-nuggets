# PD-1-directed CAR-T — synovium-restricted, TNFRII-armored T cell that hunts synovial disease-associated T cells and resolves experimental rheumatoid arthritis, opening a T-cell-directed lane to complement the B-cell CAR-T wave in autoimmune disease

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.09.737520v1

## Script

Good morning, Justice. Today's item is a bioRxiv preprint posted yesterday from Ido Amit's Systems Immunology group at Weizmann, with rheumatology co-authors from Hadassah and Rambam — a PD-1-directed CAR-T cell for rheumatoid arthritis. The design is worth walking through in detail because it lands in three of your active interest areas at once — CAR-T cell therapy, autoimmune biologics, and the growing effort to move engineered T cell products beyond CD-nineteen and B-C-M-A into new disease-associated targets. It is preclinical, and I am going to flag up front — the preprint has been up for about a day and the full-body H-T-M-L has not rendered yet, so today's read is off the abstract and figure legends only. Effect sizes and toxicity numbers will have to wait for the full paper. But the design itself is enough to justify covering it, and I will explain why.

The one-sentence version. The Amit group used single-cell multi-omics of human rheumatoid arthritis synovium and mouse arthritis models to identify P-D-C-D-1 — the gene that encodes P-D-1 — as a selective marker of synovial disease-associated T cells. Then they built a CAR-T cell that targets P-D-1 as an antigen — not the checkpoint pathway, the antigen — so the CAR kills the pathogenic T cells. Then they added two layers of engineering on top of that basic CAR — a tissue-restriction biosensor and a tissue-repair-promoting armor cassette. That is the paper in one breath.

Now the significance. The autoimmune CAR-T field for the last two years has been almost entirely a B-cell-depletion story. Anti-C-D-nineteen CAR-T in lupus, myasthenia, and systemic sclerosis — the Georg Schett-and-followers wave — has now been reproduced across several groups, several targets, several diseases, and the deep-B-cell-reset thesis is now field-consensus. What has been missing from that consensus is a specific therapeutic story for the diseases where the T cell compartment is doing the driving. Rheumatoid arthritis is the canonical example. The synovial T cells that infiltrate the joint and organize the ectopic-lymphoid-tissue architecture are transcriptionally distinct from circulating T cells, and depleting them with a non-selective T-cell-depleting agent has never been a viable strategy because the resulting global immunosuppression is intolerable. What Amit's group is arguing here is that if you can find a surface marker that segregates the pathogenic synovial T cells from the systemic T cell pool, you can build a CAR-T against that marker and do to R-A what anti-C-D-nineteen has been doing to lupus.

The claim of the paper is that P-D-1 is that marker. In the synovium of R-A patients and in the mouse models, P-D-C-D-1 came out of their single-cell multi-omics analysis as selectively upregulated on the disease-associated T cell subset. That is the load-bearing claim — that P-D-1-high T cells in the joint are the pathogenic population, that P-D-1-low T cells elsewhere in the body are not, and that a CAR against P-D-1 will therefore selectively hit the disease-associated compartment. Their in vitro and in vivo data — again, off the abstract — show that the CAR-T potently eliminates the synovial disease-associated T cells and produces marked attenuation of synovitis in mouse R-A models. That is the therapeutic proof-of-concept.

Now — three layers of engineering worth calling out, because each one maps to a design pattern the field has been converging on.

First — the CAR target itself is a pivot on P-D-1 that is worth appreciating. Everyone in oncology thinks of P-D-1 as a checkpoint that you block with an antibody to release T cells. Amit's group is instead treating P-D-1 as a tumor-associated-antigen-analog for autoimmune disease — a marker that happens to be selectively expressed on the pathogenic cell subset in this tissue, which the CAR can be pointed at. This is exactly the mental model shift that has been happening across the CAR-T-for-autoimmune field. In lupus, anti-C-D-nineteen is not aiming at C-D-nineteen for its biology, it is aiming at it because it is a lineage marker. Here, anti-P-D-1 is not aiming at P-D-1 for its checkpoint biology, it is aiming at it as a disease-state marker of activated exhausted T cells at the pathology site. The generalization is that any surface molecule enriched on the disease-associated cell in the affected tissue is a candidate CAR target — regardless of whether that molecule has a well-known signaling role or not.

Second — the synovium-restricted biosensor. Amit's team wired an N-R-four-A-two-driven CAR-responsive circuit onto the T cell. N-R-four-A-two — the orphan nuclear receptor N-U-R-seven-seven-eight-one — is upregulated in T cells in response to strong T-C-R signaling, and it is one of the transcriptional readouts of chronic antigen exposure in the tumor microenvironment as well. The design uses N-R-four-A-two activity as a proxy for "T cell has been chronically stimulated by antigen in the target tissue," and that signal then licenses the CAR to be active. Outside the synovium — where the T cell has not been chronically re-stimulating on target antigen — the CAR is transcriptionally kept in a lower-activity state. The point is to reduce off-tissue toxicity, because P-D-1 is not synovium-exclusive at the protein level; it comes on transiently on activated T cells everywhere. The N-R-four-A-two circuit is meant to add a second, tissue-context filter on top of the antigen-recognition filter, so that the CAR only truly engages when the local environment already tells it the T cell is chronically stimulated at that site. This is a synthetic-biology idea that has been circulating for several years — the Wendell Lim group's synNotch circuits are the direct intellectual parent — and it is now being deployed as production-grade CAR engineering rather than as a proof-of-concept.

Third — the armoring cassette. The CAR-T is engineered to secrete soluble T-N-F receptor two — sTNFRII — as a decoy for T-N-F-alpha in the local tissue environment. This does two things in one move. It provides ongoing anti-inflammatory tone in the joint, which is the same pharmacology that Enbrel — etanercept, marketed as the fusion of T-N-F-R-II to I-g-G-one Fc — has been providing for R-A patients for twenty-five years. And it counteracts the interferon and T-N-F response that the CAR-T itself induces when it kills its targets in situ. The abstract also states that the sTNFRII secretion drives a tissue-reparative myeloid state — the macrophages in the joint move toward a repair, not an inflammatory, phenotype in response to the modified cytokine environment. So the same molecule is doing therapeutic anti-inflammatory work and shaping the local myeloid response to the CAR-T's killing activity, and both effects converge on tissue healing rather than tissue damage.

Now three threads for your work.

First — for the T-cell-engager and CAR-T dual-track in your lab. The disease-associated-T-cell-target thesis, if it holds up in more organs, is a real expansion of the therapeutic addressable market for engineered T cell products. Every autoimmune disease with an identifiable T-cell-driven pathology and an identifiable tissue-restricted marker becomes a target. Multiple sclerosis, type 1 diabetes, primary Sjögren's, autoimmune myocarditis — all diseases where the pathogenic T cell population has been described in some detail by single-cell atlases in the last five years and where a P-D-1-analog surface marker may exist. The relevance to a T-cell-engager platform is direct — the same target-selection logic works for bispecifics too. If P-D-1 is the CAR target for R-A synovial T cells, a P-D-1 x C-D-three T-cell engager pointed at the same cells is a shorter-development-cycle version of the same idea, and worth benchmarking as a pharmacology candidate against the CAR-T route.

Second — for the safety-engineering thesis. The N-R-four-A-two-gated design is the general answer to the problem you and the field have been circling on for two years — you cannot use most autoimmune-relevant markers as CAR targets because they are not tissue-exclusive at the protein level. Logic-gated engineering is the way through that constraint, and the field is now producing real papers with real logic-gated products, not just synthetic-biology conference abstracts. This preprint is one of the concrete examples to point to.

Third — for the armoring paradigm. Combining a killing-modality CAR-T with a local biologic-secretion cassette is going to become standard practice. sTNFRII in R-A, potentially I-L-ten in inflammatory bowel disease, potentially I-L-two mutein for tolerogenic contexts. Every engineered-T-cell company with a broad autoimmune platform is going to need to have an opinion on which armoring cargo is right for which indication. The specific choice of sTNFRII here — because it recapitulates the Enbrel mechanism that we already know works clinically in R-A — is a nice example of the pharmacology-plus-cell-therapy stacking mindset. You are not just deleting the pathogenic cell, you are also administering a proven biologic to the target tissue continuously from a cell you have already put there.

Now the caveats, in the interest of honesty. This is preclinical, and one paper. The single-cell case for P-D-1 as a selectivity-conferring marker in R-A synovium is going to need to hold up against the objection that P-D-1 is transiently upregulated on essentially any activated T cell — the argument here is quantitative and depends on the biosensor plus the antigen filter working in concert. The mouse arthritis models — I do not have the specific model information off the abstract, but the standard set is collagen-induced arthritis and the K-B-x-N serum transfer model, and neither is a perfect human R-A analog. And the toxicity picture — off-target killing of activated P-D-1-positive T cells in secondary lymphoid organs, for example — is exactly the question I would want to see figures on when the full body renders. All of that will need to be re-read when the paper drops in full over the next couple of days.

Bottom line. This is a well-conceived preclinical CAR-T design for a disease that has been off the CAR-T table because it is T-cell-driven and needs tissue-selective targeting. It uses a T cell surface marker — P-D-1 — as a disease-state antigen rather than a checkpoint, adds an N-R-four-A-two synovium-restriction biosensor, and armors the cell with sTNFRII that both dampens inflammation and shifts local myeloid cells toward tissue repair. It opens a T-cell-directed lane in autoimmune CAR-T alongside the existing B-cell-depletion wave. And structurally, the design pattern — disease-state antigen plus tissue-context filter plus therapeutic-armor cassette — is a template that generalizes. Watch the peer-reviewed follow-up, and watch for other groups reporting the same disease-associated-T-cell-marker approach in other autoimmune indications over the next twelve months. That is the brief for today.
