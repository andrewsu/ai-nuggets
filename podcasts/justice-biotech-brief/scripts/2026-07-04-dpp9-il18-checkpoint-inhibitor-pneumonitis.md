## Script

Good morning, Justice. Today is Saturday, July 4th, 2026 — Justice's Biotech Brief, one nugget from the last two days on the biotech and pharma beat you care about.

Today's pick is a bioRxiv preprint out yesterday from Richard Flavell's lab at Yale, with clinical genomics driven by Toni Choueiri at Dana-Farber and Alex Gusev — and it lands on a piece of the immunotherapy stack that has been essentially blank for a decade. The one-line take is this. Checkpoint inhibitor pneumonitis is the number one cause of immunotherapy-related death, with a case fatality rate approaching 10 percent, and until now, no genetic risk factor for it had ever been described. This paper names the first one — the gene DPP9 — and then walks the mechanism all the way from a monocyte inflammasome to a rational, druggable therapeutic hypothesis. If it holds up, it's a two-for-one — a predictive biomarker and a targeted therapy against the toxicity that is most likely to kill your patient on a checkpoint inhibitor.

Here is how it fits together. The clinical anchor is a clinico-genomics cohort of 4,397 patients treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors — that is a very large cohort by irAE-genetics standards, big enough to actually run a statistically defensible association study for a low-incidence phenotype. In that cohort, common variation at the DPP9 locus popped out as the first genome-wide susceptibility signal for checkpoint inhibitor pneumonitis specifically — not the toxicity grab-bag, but the pulmonary event. That is the paper's core discovery.

Now, why would DPP9 sit at the top of the list? DPP9 is a cytosolic serine peptidase whose day job is proteolytic repression of the CARD8 inflammasome. When DPP9 activity is reduced, CARD8 is released from that brake, the inflammasome fires, caspase-1 gets active, and the monocyte pumps out a burst of IL-18 — that is the pathway the paper reconstructs biochemically in primary human monocytes. And critically, they show that plasma IL-18 is already elevated in the patients who go on to develop pneumonitis, before the pneumonitis is clinically apparent. That means IL-18 is not just a downstream footprint of established lung inflammation — it is a candidate predictive biomarker, running ahead of the diagnosis.

The mouse leg of the paper is the piece that really nails the causality. They generate a myeloid-restricted knockout of DPP8 and DPP9, which removes the brake specifically in monocytes and macrophages — and those mice, when challenged, recapitulate the pulmonary histopathology and immunology of human CIP. Granuloma formation in the lung. Accumulation of IFN-gamma-producing T cells. Expansion of monocyte-derived macrophages. And when they neutralize IL-18 in that system, every one of those phenotypes collapses. So the pathway isn't just correlative — it's genetically necessary, and it's pharmacologically reversible at IL-18.

That last point is where this crosses from an immunology paper into a translational program. IL-18 blockade is a druggable class that is already partway into humans. Recombinant IL-18 binding protein — tadekinig alfa — has been through clinical work in NLRC4 and other inflammasomopathies. Anti-IL-18 antibodies exist. CARD8 small-molecule inhibitors are moving toward the clinic in other indications. Any one of those tools, layered on the checkpoint inhibitor backbone or given as rescue at the first sign of an IL-18 spike, becomes mechanistically rational rather than empirical. Compare that to the current standard of care for high-grade CIP, which is steroids, more steroids, and eventually the anti-checkpoint that saved the tumor is discontinued and the tumor comes back.

A few important caveats. This is a preprint — not peer-reviewed yet, and 4,397 patients is a good cohort but not a genome-wide replication. The DPP9 signal is at a locus that is broadly pleiotropic in inflammation, so fine-mapping to a single causal variant will matter for whether this becomes a clinical screen. And the mouse recapitulation is DPP8-DPP9 double knockout, which is a stronger perturbation than the human common-variant effect size. The therapeutic hypothesis is clean, but a first clinical readout would probably not be an IL-18 blocker as prophylaxis — it would be as rescue in a patient with a rising IL-18 signal on treatment. That is the trial you would design first.

Zooming out, this is the second time in three days that a Flavell-lab DPP9-CARD8 axis paper has hit bioRxiv, after the humanized-mouse pancytopenia mechanism earlier this week. The Yale group is very clearly staking a claim that the CARD8-IL-18 branch of the inflammasome family is the branch that matters in a specific set of clinical presentations, and that the peptidase brake — DPP9 — is where the pharmacology will land. If you had to bet on which piece of the inflammasome family produces a marketed drug in the next five years, that bet is looking a lot sharper this week than it did last week.

For the immunotherapy pipeline you and your colleagues work on directly, the read-through is this. As trispecific TCEs, ADCs with cytokine-releasing linkers, and armored CAR-Ts push the field toward more powerful — but also more inflammatory — modalities, we need an actual mechanistic handle on the toxicities. This paper is the cleanest example I have seen this year of that handle being closed all the way from a human GWAS locus to a druggable target, in a single preprint. Worth watching where the Yale-Dana-Farber group publishes this next and who runs the first prospective IL-18 biomarker trial in checkpoint patients — that is where this becomes real for the clinic.

That's your brief for today. Have a great Fourth of July.
