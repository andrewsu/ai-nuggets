# AJ1-11095 — Lilly/Ajax Type II JAK2 inhibitor posts 70% SVR35 and 70% TSS50 in post-JAK myelofibrosis at EHA, with mutant allele burden falling in 21 of 23 patients

Paper link: https://investor.lilly.com/news-releases/news-release-details/lilly-present-initial-clinical-data-first-class-type-ii-jak2

## Script

Today's pick is the first clinical data for Ajax Therapeutics' lead candidate, A-J-1 dash 1-1-0-9-5, presented yesterday in an oral session at the European Hematology Association meeting in Stockholm. This is the asset Lilly bought Ajax to get earlier this year in a deal worth up to two-point-three billion dollars, and the question hanging over that deal was whether a Type two JAK two inhibitor actually does something different from ruxolitinib in patients. The phase one readout in previously-treated myelofibrosis suggests it does, and the numbers are larger than the JAK-inhibitor literature would lead you to expect.

Headline numbers, twenty-three patients, all of them previously treated with a Type one JAK inhibitor, median of two prior lines of therapy. Across five dose levels from twenty-five to one hundred twenty-five milligrams once daily, seventy percent achieved a spleen volume reduction of at least thirty-five percent as best response. Seventy percent achieved a total symptom score reduction of at least fifty percent at week twelve. And — this is the part that matters most for the disease-modifying story — reductions in driver mutation variant allele frequency were observed in twenty-one of twenty-three patients, with fifty-nine percent showing at least a twenty percent drop in mutant JAK two variant allele frequency by week twenty-four.

For context, the historical benchmark in post-JAK myelofibrosis is grim. After patients lose response to ruxolitinib, the available second-line options — pacritinib, fedratinib, momelotinib — deliver spleen response rates somewhere between zero and the low thirties depending on the trial. Hitting seventy percent in a heavily pretreated population, even acknowledging this is a small phase one, is a step change. And ruxolitinib essentially never moves the mutant allele burden. It controls symptoms by blocking downstream signaling. A drug that's also shrinking the malignant clone is doing something the current standard of care does not.

The mechanistic intuition is where this gets interesting, because Type two versus Type one is not a marketing distinction — it's a real conformational story. The existing JAK inhibitors are Type one, meaning they bind the kinase in its active, A-T-P-competitive conformation. They block activity while the kinase is on, but JAK two cycles back into the active state freely, and the A-T-P pocket is highly conserved across the JAK family, so Type one inhibitors hit JAK one and TYK two as well as JAK two. That promiscuity is part of why ruxolitinib causes cytopenias and immunosuppression — you're hitting cytokine signaling everywhere.

Type two inhibitors bind the inactive conformation, the off state, and lock the kinase out. That pocket is more sequence-divergent across the JAK family, which means you can be much more selective for JAK two specifically. The translational bet is that selective JAK two inhibition with prolonged off-state lock gives you deeper, more durable target engagement against the malignant clone while sparing JAK one and the rest of the cytokine signaling network. That's the story Ajax has been telling since 2020. The phase one data is the first patient-level evidence that the story holds up.

Safety, briefly. No dose-limiting toxicities across the dose escalation. Grade three or higher anemia in fifty-two percent, thrombocytopenia in thirty percent — which is consistent with on-target JAK two inhibition and the disease itself rather than off-target promiscuity. Transient liver enzyme elevations at the top dose, and no discontinuations attributed to drug toxicity. Lilly has selected seventy-five milligrams for dose expansion and has said they want to move into phase three in second-line myelofibrosis as quickly as possible, with parallel studies in high-risk polycythemia vera and in JAK-naive disease.

The take. If the seventy percent spleen response and the allele-burden reductions hold up in a larger expansion cohort, this is not just another me-too JAK inhibitor — it's a credible first disease-modifying agent in the myeloproliferative neoplasm space, and it validates a structural mechanism, off-state kinase locking, that's been theoretical for fifteen years. For a translational immunologist, the broader signal worth tracking is whether Type two kinase inhibitor design starts showing up in other targets where selectivity has been the limiting factor — the TYK two and JAK one programs in autoimmune disease are the obvious next dominoes. The competitive question for Lilly is whether AJ-1-1-1-0-9-5 reads out in the JAK-naive frontline setting strongly enough to displace ruxolitinib as standard of care, not just as a salvage option. That's a much higher bar, and the phase three readout in second-line myelofibrosis is the first checkpoint for it.
