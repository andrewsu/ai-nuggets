## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, and it lands directly on the bispecific-architecture-meets-autoimmune-biologic intersection that sits at the center of your interest list. This morning, June twenty-ninth, Talawar Therapeutics and JATT-two Acquisition Corp announced a definitive business combination agreement that takes Talawar public via SPAC merger. The combined company will trade on the Nasdaq Capital Market under the ticker T-L-W-R. Total proceeds at closing roughly two hundred eighty-five million dollars — sixty million from JATT-two's trust account, two hundred twenty-five million from a concurrent and oversubscribed private investment in public equity at ten dollars a share. The PIPE is led by Access Biotechnology — the founding investor — with participation from Bain Capital Life Sciences, Deep Track Capital, RA Capital, Janus Henderson, Vianti Capital, Farallon, and other healthcare specialists. The deal funds the company through a phase two-b proof-of-concept readout in the second half of twenty-twenty-eight. CEO is Marc Schegerin, previously chief operating officer and chief financial officer at Morphic Therapeutic; chief medical officer is Fabio Nunes, who came from Johnson and Johnson where he ran global phase two and phase three programs in dermatology and respiratory. Praveen Tipirneni, the former Morphic and Caldera Therapeutics chief executive, joins the board.

The asset is what matters for your work. TALA-one-twenty-five is an anti-I-L-thirteen by anti-I-L-eighteen bispecific antibody, lead indication atopic dermatitis, clinical entry first quarter of twenty-twenty-seven, interim phase one data expected fourth quarter twenty-twenty-seven, phase two-b proof-of-concept readout second half twenty-twenty-eight. There are two discovery-stage programs behind it — TALA-three-oh-seven and TALA-seven-eleven — in other immunology and inflammation indications, targets not yet disclosed. The company is the first formed out of Khanda Therapeutics, a biotech company-builder that articulates an explicit thesis — unite two orthogonal, clinically validated pathways into a single bispecific molecule, with the goal of pushing past the monotherapy efficacy ceiling that has constrained the I and I biologic landscape for the last decade.

The biology underneath the target choice is the part that's worth pausing on. I-L-thirteen is the most clinically validated Type-two cytokine in atopic dermatitis. Dupixent — dupilumab — hits the I-L-four receptor alpha and blocks both I-L-four and I-L-thirteen signaling. Adbry — tralokinumab — and Ebglyss — lebrikizumab — both directly bind I-L-thirteen. The Type-two axis is the de facto cornerstone of modern atopic dermatitis biologic therapy. But the efficacy ceiling is real and well-quantified. With Dupixent, roughly forty percent of patients hit EASI-seventy-five at week sixteen. With the more potent I-L-thirteen monoclonals, you get into the high forties. A majority of moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis patients never achieve a clean clinical response on a monotherapy Type-two blockade, and the field has spent five years asking why. The answer, increasingly, is that there is a non-Type-two channel of skin inflammation that I-L-four and I-L-thirteen blockade simply does not address.

That channel is where I-L-eighteen comes in. I-L-eighteen is an I-L-one-family cytokine produced primarily by keratinocytes — and to a lesser extent by Langerhans cells — as an inactive precursor that gets cleaved by the NLRP-one inflammasome upon activation. The major activator in atopic dermatitis skin is Staphylococcus aureus, which colonizes lesional skin at densities ten to a hundred times higher than control skin and triggers the canonical keratinocyte NLRP-one inflammasome to release mature I-L-eighteen. Once released, I-L-eighteen is context-dependent. In a Type-one polarized environment, it drives interferon-gamma. In a Type-two-dominant environment like atopic dermatitis lesional skin, it does something different — it sensitizes memory Th-two cells via I-L-nine-driven upregulation of the I-L-eighteen receptor, and then induces those Th-two cells to secrete more I-L-thirteen. So I-L-eighteen is an upstream alarmin that amplifies the same downstream I-L-thirteen signal that the Type-two-blocking monoclonals are trying to suppress, and it's also independently capable of activating Th-one, Th-seventeen, and Th-twenty-two pathways that are largely orthogonal to the I-L-four-I-L-thirteen axis. That is the mechanistic basis for the bispecific — you are blocking the proximal Type-two effector with the I-L-thirteen arm, and you are simultaneously blocking the alarmin amplifier and the non-Type-two driver with the I-L-eighteen arm, in a single molecule, with one pharmacokinetic profile and one dosing regimen.

The clinical validation for the I-L-eighteen arm is the new piece that makes this bispecific architecture actually fundable in twenty-twenty-six, and it's worth holding alongside the deal. Apollo Therapeutics' camoteskimab, an anti-I-L-eighteen monoclonal engineered for antibody recycling and a three-month dosing interval, reported positive phase two-a data in moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis last September in the CHAMELEON study. Sixty-two patients randomized, the primary endpoint of EASI reduction at week sixteen was statistically significant versus placebo, and — the most interesting subset — efficacy held up in patients who had previously failed anti-I-L-thirteen or anti-I-L-four-receptor-alpha biologics. That is the proof point. I-L-eighteen blockade captures a slice of atopic dermatitis biology that Type-two blockade misses, and in the worst-prognosis post-Dupixent population, it appears to deliver clinical benefit on its own. The Khanda thesis on TALA-one-twenty-five is that doing both simultaneously, with full target-engagement at both arms, in a single molecule that hits the same patient at the same time, should produce a deeper-and-broader response than either monotherapy. The dose is fixed, the pharmacology is co-localized, and the patient does not have to fail a Type-two biologic before getting the I-L-eighteen arm. Aletekitug, the second anti-I-L-eighteen monoclonal that posted phase two data in atopic dermatitis earlier this year in Allergy, replicates the camoteskimab signal independently. Two phase-two-stage I-L-eighteen antibodies plus three approved I-L-thirteen-axis biologics equals the "two clinically validated, complementary mechanisms" language that Talawar's CEO used in the press release. That language is technically accurate.

The competitive context for bispecific architecture in atopic dermatitis is the part where the field has already learned what does not work. Sanofi's lunsekimig — a five-domain nanobody bispecific targeting T-S-L-P and I-L-thirteen — missed its primary endpoint in the VELVET phase two-b atopic dermatitis study in April this year, though it hit on key secondary endpoints, and the same molecule hit primary endpoints in asthma and chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps in the same readout. Keymed Biotech's CM-five-twelve, also T-S-L-P by I-L-thirteen, showed a strong EASI-seventy-five signal in phase one-b in China — fifty percent versus seven percent at week six — but the patient numbers are small. Innovent's IBI-three-oh-oh-two — I-L-four-receptor-alpha by T-S-L-P — is in phase two readout. The pattern that emerges is that pairing T-S-L-P with a Type-two cytokine arm is partly redundant — T-S-L-P sits upstream of I-L-four and I-L-thirteen in the same alarmin-Type-two axis, so the second arm is providing pathway depth more than orthogonality. The I-L-thirteen by I-L-eighteen pairing is mechanistically distinct from that — I-L-eighteen sits on a separate non-Type-two amplifier track. If the Khanda thesis is that orthogonality is what matters for breaking the monotherapy ceiling, the lunsekimig miss in atopic dermatitis is actually data in favor of the I-L-eighteen choice over the T-S-L-P choice, and Talawar's pre-clinical positioning takes that exact line.

The caveats are real and worth holding. TALA-one-twenty-five is preclinical. No human data. No first-in-human dosing until first quarter twenty-twenty-seven. The phase two-b proof-of-concept readout that the cash runway is sized to is two and a half years away. The bispecific format itself is not disclosed in the deal materials — whether this is an I-g-G-like dual-Fab, a knob-into-hole asymmetric, a tandem-scFv, a nanobody construct — none of that is public. The pharmacokinetic question for an I-L-thirteen by I-L-eighteen bispecific is non-trivial because I-L-thirteen monoclonals are administered every two to four weeks while camoteskimab is engineered for a three-month dosing interval; the bispecific will have one half-life and one dosing schedule, and which target's pharmacology dominates matters for patient experience and for the depth of target engagement. The atopic dermatitis bispecific space already has graveyards — lunsekimig missing primary, Johnson and Johnson's Tremfya icotrokinra eczema readout, multiple TSLP failures in atopic dermatitis — so the I and I biologic ceiling is real, but it has not been kind to bispecifics that picked the wrong two arms. Talawar is asking the field to bet that I-L-thirteen plus I-L-eighteen is the right two arms, on the basis of mechanism plus separate phase two monotherapy data on both arms, and we will not know for two and a half years whether the integrated bispecific actually replicates that signal in one molecule.

The bigger architectural lesson — and this is where the story crosses out of dermatology and into your daily work — is what the Khanda model is doing at the company-building level. The thesis is "pair two orthogonal, clinically validated pathways in a single molecule and break through the monotherapy efficacy ceiling," and it is being applied first in I and I — Talawar in atopic dermatitis — but it is the same architectural logic that the next generation of T-cell engagers is supposed to deliver in oncology. The Padcev plus Keytruda story you got on Saturday was the same logic in an A-D-C plus checkpoint pairing. The teclistamab plus daratumumab story you got yesterday was the same logic in a B-C-M-A bispecific plus a C-D-thirty-eight monoclonal. The mRNA-twenty-eight-oh-eight Moderna readout from last week was the same logic baked into the molecule itself as a multiplexed trispecific T-cell engager. The Khanda thesis differs in one important way — it is preselecting the architectural format as a bispecific and then identifying the orthogonal target pair, rather than starting from a clinical combination signal and engineering it down into one molecule. The two strategies converge — and the empirical question that the I and I and the oncology fields are both being asked right now is whether the bispecific-as-combination thesis pulls efficacy and safety in the same direction. The MajesTEC-three data argues yes in oncology. The lunsekimig miss argues partly no in atopic dermatitis with the wrong target pair. TALA-one-twenty-five will be the next data point. The phase one readout in late twenty-twenty-seven and the phase two-b in late twenty-twenty-eight are the readouts that will tell us whether the I-L-thirteen by I-L-eighteen pairing is the one that makes the bispecific atopic dermatitis architecture finally cash.

Net take. Today's news is that bispecific architecture is now financeable at the preclinical stage in autoimmune disease at scales — two hundred eighty-five million dollars — that previously required a phase two clinical signal. The investor syndicate is heavyweight — Access, Bain, Deep Track, RA Capital, Janus, Farallon — which is the signature of a thesis-driven I and I round, not a data-driven round. The selection of I-L-thirteen by I-L-eighteen as the bispecific pair is technically defensible because both arms have monotherapy phase two evidence in the target indication, and the mechanism for combinatorial value is more orthogonal than the T-S-L-P-Type-two pairings the field has already tried. The Khanda company-builder model is worth tracking — Talawar is the first of what's positioned as a portfolio of bispecific companies, each built around an orthogonal-pathway thesis in a different indication. And the architectural lesson is portable into your trispecific T-cell engager and your A-D-C work — the bispecific-as-combination thesis is now being capitalized in both autoimmune and oncology with similar logic and similar discount rates. The data point we are waiting for is whether the integrated bispecific actually replicates the sum-of-the-monotherapy signals in one molecule. Late twenty-twenty-seven and twenty-twenty-eight are the diagnostic years.
