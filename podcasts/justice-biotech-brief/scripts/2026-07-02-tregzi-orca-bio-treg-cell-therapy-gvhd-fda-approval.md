# Tregzi — Orca Bio's Treg-enriched allograft becomes the first regulatory T cell therapy FDA-approved in the United States, cutting chronic GvHD-free survival at one year from 38 percent to 78 percent in the Precision-T Phase 3 across AML, ALL, MDS, and mixed-phenotype acute leukemia at a WAC of four hundred and twenty-eight thousand dollars

Paper link: https://medcitynews.com/2026/07/orca-bio-treg-cell-therapy-tregzi-blood-cancer-leukemia-graft-versus-host-disease-gvhd/

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, and it is the biggest cell-therapy story of the week — arguably the biggest immunotherapy approval of the summer so far. Yesterday, Tuesday July first, the F-D-A approved Orca Bio's Tregzi, formerly Orca-T, for prevention of graft-versus-host disease in adults undergoing matched-donor allogeneic stem cell transplantation for acute myeloid leukemia, acute lymphoblastic leukemia, myelodysplastic syndromes, and mixed-phenotype acute leukemia. This is the first regulatory T cell therapy — a Treg-based product — ever approved by any regulator, in any indication. That is a genuine field-defining first, and the mechanism is worth unpacking carefully, because it is much closer to a manufacturing-precision play than to a classical drug approval.

Three threads to pull for you. What Tregzi actually is. What Precision-T showed. And what the approval opens up for the Treg field — including autoimmune disease, which Orca has now explicitly telegraphed as the next indication vector.

First, what Tregzi is — and what it is not. Tregzi is not a genetically engineered T cell, not a chimeric antigen receptor construct, not a bispecific engager, and not an off-the-shelf allogeneic cell product. It is a compositionally defined allograft. Orca takes a matched donor's mobilized peripheral blood, and instead of infusing the raw graft the way conventional allo-H-S-C-T does, they isolate three components separately — the C-D-thirty-four-positive hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells that will engraft the marrow, the natural regulatory T cells, and the conventional effector T cells — and reconstitute those three components in a defined ratio before infusion. Tregs are only about one percent of the T cell pool in a standard graft, so the enrichment is significant; the infused product is Treg-dominant against a controlled Tconv background sitting on top of a full C-D-thirty-four dose. Nate Fernhoff, the C-E-O, has been describing this for years as sculpting by subtraction — the art is what you remove, not what you add — and after almost a decade of B-L-A review turbulence including a formal F-D-A review extension, the label finally puts that framing to work. No cells are engineered. No lymphodepleting chemotherapy is added to the standard conditioning. The therapeutic effect is entirely a function of the composition Orca ships.

One manufacturing constraint that matters. Tregs cannot be cryopreserved without losing suppressive function, so Orca is running a seventy-two-hour vein-to-vein window: donor apheresis, cell sort at the Sacramento facility or the newly announced Princeton, New Jersey facility, ship fresh, infuse. That is the same fresh-product problem that constrained early C-A-R-T manufacturing before Novartis's cryo protocol matured for Kymriah, and Orca is choosing to eat it because cryo would compromise the biology. Whether the four-hundred-and-twenty-eight-thousand-dollar wholesale acquisition cost prices that logistics network in economically depends on how many transplant centers can integrate a fresh-cell workflow, and how many payers see the acute-GvHD cost avoidance transplanters have been quoting.

Second, the Precision-T pivotal — which is where the approval story actually earns its keep. One hundred and eighty-seven patients with A-M-L, A-L-L, M-D-S, and mixed-phenotype acute leukemia were randomized to Tregzi versus an unmanipulated matched-donor allograft with standard-of-care GvHD prophylaxis. The composite primary endpoint was chronic-GvHD-free, relapse-free survival at one year, and Tregzi hit seventy-eight percent versus thirty-eight point four percent in the control arm. That is not a five-point delta on a soft endpoint — that is double the fraction of patients still alive, still cancer-free, and without the debilitating chronic skin, gut, or hepatic GvHD that follows a standard graft. It is the largest reported randomized delta on chronic-GvHD-free survival in any allo-H-S-C-T trial in the modern era.

Overall survival came in at ninety-four percent versus eighty-three percent at one year. Orca calls that hypothesis-generating because Precision-T was not powered to show overall survival superiority formally, but a ten-plus-point O-S delta at one year in matched-donor allo is the kind of number that reshapes transplanter behavior even without a p-value attached. The safety profile — oral and G-I inflammation, diarrhea, rash, viral infections — is consistent with expectations for the conditioning regimen and the transplant setting itself; nothing new-signal from the Treg composition specifically. Fernhoff is describing the survival delta as "an extremely important hypothesis-generating endpoint" for future trials, which is code for a Phase four confirmatory O-S-powered study that Orca will almost certainly run.

The competitive framing is worth naming clearly. The current standard-of-care for GvHD prophylaxis in matched-donor allo is post-transplant cyclophosphamide — P-T-C-y — which became the default over the last decade because it is cheap, off-the-shelf, and works reasonably well across matched and mismatched donor settings. Tregzi has to justify a four-hundred-and-twenty-eight-thousand-dollar price and a seventy-two-hour fresh-product supply chain against a generic small molecule that transplanters are already comfortable with. Precision-T did not run a head-to-head against P-T-C-y — the comparator was standard tacrolimus-plus-methotrexate — and that is the debate the K-O-L panels will have next. The composite endpoint delta is large enough that it should hold up against P-T-C-y even without a direct comparison, but the community will want that data eventually.

Third, and this is the part that matters most for what you and the Rodgers lab are building at Calibr. Two forward-looking read-throughs.

Read-through one — regulatory precedent for the Treg cell therapy class. The Treg field has been promising for two decades. Polyclonal Treg infusions in type-one diabetes. Chimeric antigen receptor Tregs in transplant tolerance. Engineered Foxp3 stability constructs. Sonoma, Abata, Quell, C-A-R-T-h-e-r-a, GentiBio — the whole cohort. Until this week, nothing in the class had crossed the bar of a randomized F-D-A-approvable pivotal. Now one has, and it is the least engineered version of the concept — just cell isolation with tight release specs and a rigorous compositional-analysis assay. That means the next generation of Treg programs, including the C-A-R-Tregs and the engineered-stability constructs, now have a first-in-class approval to point at when they ask the agency for accelerated pathways. The F-D-A has now been forced to build the review infrastructure for a Treg-based product, and that infrastructure is portable to the more sophisticated engineered constructs coming behind Orca.

Read-through two — autoimmune disease. Fernhoff was explicit on the earnings call and in the launch materials that Orca is evaluating conditioning-reduced regimens for autoimmune indications, and the sister program Orca-Q — further sculpted Tconv against more disparate haploidentical donors — is being framed as the tool for that expansion. This is a much bigger commercial story than GvHD prevention. If Orca can extend Treg-enriched cell therapy from the transplant setting into refractory autoimmune indications — the same lupus, myositis, systemic sclerosis population that the C-D-nineteen C-A-R-T-in-autoimmune companies are attacking, and that the C-D-nineteen-by-C-D-three T cell engagers we covered on the Metis-Boulevard deal yesterday and the Cullinan bispecific update earlier this month are also attacking — they are competing directly against the pathogenic-lymphocyte-depletion thesis with a mechanism that suppresses rather than eliminates. It is the opposite arrow. Tregs damp the autoreactive response; C-D-nineteen C-A-R-T deletes the autoreactive compartment. Both can plausibly work, and the mechanistic bets are different enough that both approaches will get funded and both will get tested.

The relevance to your beat. First, if you are thinking about the T cell engager and C-A-R-T franchise as the offensive weapons for autoimmune, Treg cell therapy is now credibly the flanking maneuver — and the flanking maneuver has an F-D-A precedent, which the depletion approaches do not yet have outside of oncology labels. Second, Orca's compositional-precision manufacturing template is portable to any allogeneic engineered T cell product; watching whether they release release-spec technology to the T-C-E and C-A-R-T community, or hold it as competitive moat, is a signal. Third, the deep-response-with-clean-safety profile Precision-T delivered — seventy-eight versus thirty-eight percent on chronic-GvHD-free survival, ninety-four versus eighty-three percent on O-S at one year, no new safety signals — will pressure the C-A-R-T-in-autoimmune field to match not just the efficacy of Tregs but the tolerability profile. That is a genuine bar-raise for the whole class.

Net take. The Tregzi approval is a genuine first-in-class regulatory event in cellular immunotherapy, not an incremental line addition. It validates precision-composition allografts as a modality distinct from engineered cell therapy. It gives the Treg field its first randomized pivotal win. And Orca's autoimmune expansion signal means the Treg competitive set is about to shift from a scientific curiosity to a real commercial vector inside the next twelve to eighteen months — competing directly against the depletion-based approaches the Calibr autoimmune team is watching. This is the approval of the summer for cellular immunotherapy. Worth a Slack thread with the Rodgers group today.
