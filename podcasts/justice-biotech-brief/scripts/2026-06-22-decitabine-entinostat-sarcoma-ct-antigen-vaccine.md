# Decitabine + entinostat — epigenetic priming surfaces a shared cancer-testis antigen panel in low-mutation sarcomas

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.18.733244v1

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, fresh on bioRxiv overnight from Brian Ladle's group at Johns Hopkins — a translational immunotherapy paper that should land squarely on your radar because it solves a target-nomination problem the field has been working around rather than through. The claim, in one sentence — you can pharmacologically create the antigen repertoire that low-mutation tumors don't have, and the antigens you create are shared across tumors. If that holds, it changes how you think about cell-therapy targets in checkpoint-refractory cancers. Heads up — the preprint posted last night, the body hasn't rendered yet on bioRxiv, so this take is built off the abstract; I'll flag where that limits me.

Set up the problem first. Pediatric sarcomas — rhabdomyosarcoma, osteosarcoma, Ewing's, synovial — have been a graveyard for checkpoint blockade. The dominant explanation is mutational. Low tumor-mutation burden, few neoantigens, no foreign peptides for T cells to see, so anti-P-D-one has nothing to unleash. The workarounds have been adoptive cell therapy against tissue-restricted antigens — Adaptimmune's N-Y E-S-O one T-C-R-T in synovial sarcoma is the canonical example, real responses but a tiny addressable population — plus checkpoint combinations that don't generalize. What this paper does instead is the move the field hasn't tried at scale. Stop accepting the cancer's antigen profile as given, and force an antigen repertoire by de-silencing the right genes.

The mechanism is decitabine plus entinostat. Decitabine is a D-N-A methyltransferase inhibitor — hypomethylates promoter C-p-G islands, restores expression of genes the tumor's epigenetic program has shut down. Entinostat is a histone-deacetylase inhibitor — relaxes the chromatin around those genes so the transcription decitabine enables actually proceeds. Both are clinically used — decitabine in M-D-S and A-M-L, entinostat in solid-tumor trials — and both are weak alone as immunotherapy primers. The abstract says sequential combination is what does the work. In the K-RAS-driven K-P Sarc murine model, the combination significantly upregulates cancer-testis antigens — genes whose normal expression is testis or placenta restricted, so when you turn them back on in tumor cells they look foreign to peripheral T cells because central tolerance never saw them. The combination also pushes M-H-C class one expression up, which solves the second half of the antigen problem — peptides need to be presented to be useful.

The therapeutic test is a whole-cell vaccine. Take the epigenetically treated tumor cells, irradiate them so they can't proliferate, engineer them to secrete G-M C-S-F for dendritic-cell recruitment, deliver. Vaccination produced T cell immunity against matched tumor challenge. Then the cascade that actually confirms mechanism rather than coincidence. One — the response was directed toward the epigenetically upregulated antigens. They ran the antigen-specificity experiment, the response is on the new antigens not on baseline tumor signals. Two — the response was T-cell dependent. Three — it was potentiated by checkpoint blockade on top, meaning the epigenetic vaccine and checkpoint blockade are synergistic, not redundant; that's the right clinical-regimen prediction. Four — it conferred immunologic memory, durable.

Here's the part that should catch your attention as a translational immunologist. The epigenetically upregulated antigens were shared between two different murine sarcoma lines — K-P Sarc, and a second model called M-three-nine-M with different mutational drivers — and cross-protection emerged. Vaccinate against one, get protection against the other. That turns the paper from a vaccine that works in one mouse model into a generalizable target-nomination strategy. The implication is that there is a relatively conserved cancer-testis antigen panel that emerges when you epigenetically prime mutationally diverse sarcomas, meaning the same antigens are tractable across subtypes. Then the human translation. Same combination on human sarcoma cell lines. Same gene-expression changes, shared antigen targets, increased M-H-C class one. That bridges the mouse-to-human gap on the load-bearing finding.

The translational read for your group is about target nomination. The cell-therapy target menu in sarcoma is bounded by what's natively expressed — G-D-two on osteosarcoma, her-two variable, B-seven-H-three more recent, C-D fifty-six in rhabdo via Adaptimmune-style approaches. The cancer-testis menu — N-Y E-S-O one, MAGE-A-four, K-K L-C one — has been small and expression-heterogeneous, which is why it's been a T-C-R-T niche rather than a CAR-T or T cell engager space. This paper says, if you pharmacologically force expression of a shared cancer-testis antigen panel across sarcoma subtypes, you've created a multiplexed target landscape that didn't exist. The deployable program is epigenetic priming as the upstream regimen step, then off-the-shelf T-C-R-T, T-C-R-mimic antibody, or H-L-A-restricted T cell engager against the induced antigens. That's a regimen design, not a single asset, and it's a regimen design where the priming step is two F-D-A-approved drugs you can order tomorrow.

A few caveats worth saying out loud. One, mouse plus human cell line — the human side is convergent gene expression, not a vaccine read in humans. Two, the abstract doesn't name the specific cancer-testis antigens upregulated. The thing I'd most want from the body is which antigens are on the shared panel, and whether any of them are surface tractable for classical CAR-T or T cell engagers, because most cancer-testis antigens are intracellular and require peptide-M-H-C modalities. Three, decitabine plus entinostat as a combination is doable in adults but the chronic-tolerability profile in pediatric patients isn't established — a Phase one would need to nail dose intensity, schedule, and whether the epigenetic effect on the tumor is durable enough to support a vaccine cycle. Four, the whole-cell vaccine modality is operationally heavy and patient-specific — fine as proof of mechanism, but the real-world deployment is to identify the antigens, build off-the-shelf cell therapy or T cell engager against them, deploy after the priming. Five, two co-authors have moved to Nkarta and Merck — declared unrelated, but it's a note that the cell-therapy and epigenetic-modulator industries are paying attention here.

Net take. Mouse plus convergent human cell line, abstract only, but the conceptual contribution is sharp — pharmacologically induce a shared antigen repertoire across mutationally heterogeneous tumors and you've solved the target-nomination problem for cell therapies in low-mutation cancers. Worth tracking when the body lands. The specific antigens in the shared panel are what'll determine whether this becomes a Phase one in two years or stays a model-system curiosity.
