## Script

Good morning, Justice. Today's item lives directly on your beat — cell therapy, autoimmune, and the design choices that will decide whether allogeneic CAR-T becomes a scalable modality outside oncology. Fate Therapeutics presented emerging data yesterday at the International Society for Stem Cell Research meeting in Montreal on FT819, their off-the-shelf, iPSC-derived, anti-CD-nineteen CAR-T, in treatment-resistant systemic sclerosis. Four evaluable patients, all four with a meaningful clinical response at three months, no cytokine release syndrome, no ICANS, no graft-versus-host disease, no hypogammaglobulinemia, and — most importantly — one of those four dosed with no lymphoconditioning at all. The headline is not that CAR-T works in scleroderma; that thesis has been building since the Erlangen data three years ago. The headline is that Fate is showing you can walk back essentially every heavy piece of the CAR-T bedside protocol and still see clinical activity.

Start with the molecule. FT819 is generated from a clonal induced pluripotent stem cell master cell bank, so every dose is manufactured from the same starting genetic material and can be stored on the shelf, thawed, and given on demand. The CAR itself uses the one-X-X design out of Sadelain's group at Memorial Sloan Kettering — a CD-twenty-eight costimulatory domain paired with a modified CD-three-zeta signaling tail that attenuates ITAM strength. The point is to reduce activation-induced exhaustion without giving up cytotoxicity. The CAR transgene is inserted directly into the T-cell receptor alpha constant locus — the TRAC locus — so it borrows the endogenous TCR promoter for physiologic expression, and at the same time complete bi-allelic knockout of TRAC ablates surface TCR, which is what makes this allogeneic construct safe from graft-versus-host disease. That combination — clonal iPSC, TRAC-integrated one-X-X CAR-nineteen, TCR-null — is what turns a bespoke autologous product into a bank of frozen vials you can give in an infusion clinic.

The trial. FT819-one-oh-two is a Phase 1 single-arm basket study across four B-cell-driven autoimmune diseases — ANCA-associated vasculitis, systemic lupus, systemic sclerosis, and idiopathic inflammatory myopathies. Thirty patients dosed across baskets at twenty-one sites, sixteen US and five outside. Two dose levels — three-point-six times ten-to-the-eighth and nine times ten-to-the-eighth viable cells. And two conditioning regimens that carry the design lesson. Regimen A is standard lymphodepletion — cyclophosphamide or bendamustine, no fludarabine. Regimen B is no lymphoconditioning at all — patients continue their maintenance immunosuppressive, stop five days before infusion, then get FT819. That Regimen B arm is the design-critical arm for the field.

Five scleroderma patients dosed, four evaluable at data cut. All four met the moderate-to-severe treatment-resistant inclusion criteria — failed or intolerant to at least two prior immunosuppressives for at least three months. Importantly, two of them had disease duration well over five years, which is a population many CAR-T-in-autoimmune trials specifically exclude because established fibrosis is presumed less reversible. Baseline modified Rodnan skin scores ranged from eighteen to thirty-four, disease duration from one-and-a-half to nine-and-a-half years, and four of five had interstitial lung disease. This is not the easiest cohort in the field, and Fate went into it on purpose.

Efficacy at three months. All four patients hit at least a twenty-five percent revised Composite Responder Index in Systemic Sclerosis — the rCRISS-twenty-five endpoint — and one patient hit rCRISS one hundred, which is the top of the composite scale. Skin scores moved. Patient A-one went from twenty-two to fifteen at three months and stayed at fifteen at six months. Patient A-two moved thirty-two to twenty-three. Patient A-three moved eighteen to ten. Patient B-one — the no-lymphoconditioning arm — moved thirty-four to thirty, a real change, just a smaller one, which is honest given a smaller pharmacologic push. Clinician and patient global assessments improved across the board, HAQ Disability Index came down in three of four, and forced vital capacity was preserved or improved. Deep B-cell depletion was clean in peripheral blood in the conditioned patients, and Fate also showed nasopharyngeal adenoid tissue B-cell depletion, meaning the CAR-T is reaching lymphoid compartments and not just clearing circulating B cells.

Safety in these first four is the standout piece. Zero cytokine release syndrome. Zero neurotoxicity. Zero graft-versus-host disease. Zero hypogammaglobulinemia at three months. Zero dose-limiting toxicities. Two of four had a grade three adverse event — one migraine and one immune thrombocytopenia, both in the same patient, who separately had a suspected inflammatory myopathy overlap flare and started mycophenolate for that around month six. Three of five dosed as outpatient or discharged inside twenty-four hours. This is a walk-into-clinic, get-infused, go-home profile — not what CAR-T looks like in oncology, and the whole reason the field is even trying to run this modality in non-malignant disease.

Where to hold the enthusiasm. Depth of response is not yet at the level the autologous data have set. The Erlangen group's autologous CAR-nineteen scleroderma cases, and Cabaletta's CABA-two-oh-one, are producing mRSS drops in the double digits with essentially normalization of skin at twelve to twenty-four months. Fate's three-month numbers are meaningful but modest by comparison — call it a seven-to-nine-point drop in mRSS for the conditioned patients, four points for the no-lymphoconditioning patient. It's plausible those numbers deepen with time and it's plausible they plateau there. The no-lymphoconditioning arm is where the design-critical answer lives — the field needs to see whether that arm converges on the conditioned response over six to twelve months, or whether it stalls out at a shallower plateau. Immune thrombocytopenia in a CAR-T-treated scleroderma patient — even one flagged as an overlap syndrome — is a signal to watch across the basket. Persistence of an off-the-shelf CAR-T in a lymphoreplete host is also a real question; peripheral B cells started returning in some patients by month one to three, which for treatment-resistant autoimmune disease may or may not be a problem depending on whether the immune reset that occurred during the depletion window is durable.

Read-through for your program. The load-bearing scientific claim here is that a CAR-nineteen-driven immune reset in autoimmune disease may not need fludarabine — or, in the extreme, any lymphoconditioning at all — if the CAR-T is potent enough. If that holds, the CAR-T-in-autoimmune modality goes from a specialty hospital procedure to something that can be run in a community rheumatology infusion clinic, which is the difference between a boutique salvage therapy and a real second-line replacement for rituximab plus mycophenolate. The same design lesson generalizes to trispecific T-cell engager and bispecific B-cell-directed platforms — if the immune-engagement pharmacology is strong enough, the pre-conditioning story softens. That is the strategic point for the platform side of your work.

Watch for. B-cell recovery kinetics at six and twelve months in the no-lymphoconditioning arm — the single most informative Fate data drop of the next twelve months. Any partnership signal on FT819 in autoimmune indications; Fate has an ongoing Ono Pharmaceutical collaboration referenced in the forward-looking statements. Data updates at ACR Convergence in November and any regulatory alignment on a registrational path for scleroderma or lupus. Peer-reviewed publication of the combined basket safety and efficacy dataset.

Bottom line. Off-the-shelf, iPSC-derived, TRAC-integrated one-X-X CAR-nineteen with complete TCR knockout, given as outpatient without fludarabine — and in one case without any lymphoconditioning — is producing meaningful clinical responses at three months in treatment-resistant systemic sclerosis with zero cytokine release, zero neurotoxicity, and zero graft-versus-host disease. The depth versus autologous CAR-T is the open question. The accessibility upside is the point. Worth tracking. That's it for today.
