# Lasme-cel — first allogeneic CAR-T with FDA RMAT designation and final Phase 1 readout in r/r B-ALL

Paper link: https://www.cellectis.com/en/press/cellectis-presents-final-phase-1-results-of-lasme-cel-and-preliminary-results-on-eti-cel-at-eha-2026-congress/

## Script

Welcome to Justice's Biotech Brief for Thursday, June eleventh. Today's pick is allogeneic CAR-T — the modality that the rest of the field has been writing off as autologous matures and in-vivo CAR-T gathers momentum. Cellectis just put down two pieces of news in twenty-four hours that drag the off-the-shelf thesis back into serious play. Tuesday afternoon the F-D-A granted Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy designation to lasme-cel — the first allogeneic CAR-T product ever to hold R-M-A-T status with a pivotal trial enrolling. And this morning, at the European Hematology Association meeting in Stockholm, Cellectis released the final Phase one BALLI-oh-one data that underwrote the designation, alongside preliminary data on a second allogeneic product. The numbers are good. The competitive read is more interesting.

Lasme-cel is the new generic name for what used to be UCART-twenty-two — a CD-twenty-two-directed CAR-T made from healthy-donor T cells, edited with Cellectis's TALEN platform to knock out two genes. The T-cell receptor knockout prevents graft-versus-host disease, which is the threshold problem for allogeneic cell therapy. The CD-fifty-two knockout lets the product survive lymphodepletion with alemtuzumab — and that matters, because off-the-shelf CAR-T needs deeper, longer lymphodepletion than autologous products to take root, and you need the infused cells to be invisible to that wipe. The indication is relapsed and refractory B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia — the same setting where Kymriah and tecartus already run as autologous CAR-T, except those products take three to four weeks to manufacture, and these patients don't always have three weeks to spare.

Here's the final Phase one readout. Forty-five patients across dose-finding, all in third line or beyond. In the seven patients who matched the Phase two pivotal target population — heavily pretreated, median five prior lines of therapy — overall response rate was a hundred percent, complete-remission-or-incomplete-recovery rate was fifty-seven percent, and minimal-residual-disease-negative status was achieved in three-quarters of responders. Safety has historically been the part that dragged allogeneic CAR-T down, and this profile was clean. Severe cytokine release syndrome in four percent of patients. Severe ICANS in four percent. Severe immune-effector-cell-associated hemophagocytic syndrome in two percent. Every toxicity event resolved. The pivotal Phase two arm of BALLI-oh-one is enrolling, with the first interim analysis on the books for the fourth quarter of this year.

Two reads. First, on the competitive set. Allogeneic CAR-T is the field that the rest of biotech walked away from. Allogene is fighting for relevance. Caribou and Atara are both under pressure. And the in-vivo CAR-T programs — Legend's L-B-twenty-five-oh-one with a hundred percent overall response rate in CD-nineteen-and-CD-twenty non-Hodgkin lymphoma at this same EHA meeting, plus Eli Lilly's two-point-four-billion-dollar Orna acquisition built around circular-RNA-driven in-vivo CAR-T for autoimmune disease — are the modality the market has decided is going to eat allogeneic's lunch. So Cellectis getting first into a pivotal trial, with R-M-A-T behind it, in the population that needs an off-the-shelf product the most, is the milestone that decides whether the allogeneic thesis survives or gets retired. The seven-patient Phase two target subset is a small denominator, and the bar at the Q-four interim is real, but a hundred percent response with seventy-five percent of responders MRD-negative is the right shape of signal for B-ALL — where deep remission, not just any response, is what predicts durability.

Second, the read-through to the rest of the allogeneic and in-vivo space. Cellectis also dropped preliminary data on eti-cel — a CD-twenty-by-CD-twenty-two dual-targeting allogeneic CAR-T, in relapsed or refractory B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma. Fourteen patients, median three prior lines, and importantly, ninety-three percent had already failed on autologous CD-nineteen CAR-T. At the optimal dose, eighty-eight percent overall response rate with sixty-three percent complete responses. That post-CD-nineteen-relapse population is exactly the gap the autologous franchise can't close — re-treating with the same target after failure rarely works, and a second round of manufacturing burns weeks the patient doesn't have. Allogeneic with a dual-antigen target is a real answer to that gap if the durability holds. The full Phase one for eti-cel reads in the fourth quarter, alongside the BALLI-oh-one Phase two interim.

The bottom line: Cellectis has converted a long-running platform bet into the first allogeneic CAR-T in a pivotal trial, with R-M-A-T behind it and a clean Phase one in hand. Q-four becomes a real catalyst for the entire off-the-shelf thesis. And the most interesting head-to-head over the next twelve months isn't allogeneic against autologous anymore — it's allogeneic against in-vivo, two completely different answers to the same logistics problem, reading data into overlapping patient populations. Cellectis just put down the marker. See you tomorrow.
