## Script

Good morning, Justice. Today's item is a corporate announcement from yesterday, July fourteenth twenty-twenty-six — NextCure and Avere Therapeutics filed a definitive merger agreement, alongside a Hansoh Pharmaceutical in-license and a three-hundred-and-twenty-million-dollar private placement, to advance a once-weekly oral I-L-twenty-three receptor antagonist called A-V-R-oh-oh-one. I want to spend the episode on it because it sits at the intersection of three things you care about — an autoimmune biologic-like modality, cross-Pacific asset flow out of a Chinese pharma company you already know for its A-D-C partnerships, and the question of how much room is left in a mature autoimmune target class for a differentiated follow-on. This one deal touches all three.

Here is the short version. Avere is a new NASDAQ-listed company — ticker A-V-R-X — being formed by absorbing NextCure through an all-stock reverse merger, in which existing Avere holders end up with roughly ninety-nine percent of the pro-forma equity. The purpose of the vehicle is to develop A-V-R-oh-oh-one, a cyclic peptide I-L-twenty-three receptor antagonist that Avere has just exclusively licensed from Hansoh Pharmaceutical for ex-Greater-China rights — one hundred and twenty million dollars upfront, up to two-point-one-eight billion in milestones, plus royalties in the mid-single to low-double digits. The transaction is being funded by a three-hundred-and-twenty-million-dollar private investment led by Fairmount and Hansoh itself, with a syndicate that includes Venrock, General Atlantic, Wellington, T. Rowe Price, Redmile, Boyu, R-T-W, Balyasny, Janus Henderson, and roughly a dozen other health-focused funds. The stated use of proceeds is a U-S Phase 2b psoriasis study starting early twenty-twenty-seven with readout in the first half of twenty-twenty-eight, plus initiation of a Phase 2b ulcerative colitis program and preparation for Phase 3 in psoriasis.

Now the molecule, because that is where the therapeutic story sits. A-V-R-oh-oh-one is a cyclic peptide antagonist of the I-L-twenty-three receptor, engineered for oral bioavailability and a plasma half-life of about a hundred hours — that is what makes once-weekly oral dosing possible. To orient you against the current landscape — the injectable I-L-twenty-three antibodies are guselkumab, that's Tremfya, and risankizumab, that's Skyrizi. Both bind the p-nineteen subunit of the interleukin-twenty-three cytokine itself and shut down downstream signaling. They deliver deep P-A-S-I responses in psoriasis and are now the gold standard of care, but they are large monoclonal antibodies given by subcutaneous injection every eight to twelve weeks. The oral small molecule in the space is deucravacitinib — Sotyktu — a T-Y-K-two allosteric inhibitor that blocks signaling downstream of the receptor. And then in the last twelve months, the whole category shifted with icotrokinra — J-N-J-two-one-one-three, sold by Johnson and Johnson as I-C-O-T-Y-D-E — which became the first oral peptide antagonist of the I-L-twenty-three receptor itself to reach F-D-A approval. Phase 3 data in moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis showed roughly sixty-five percent of patients achieving I-G-A zero or one and around fifty percent hitting P-A-S-I ninety at sixteen weeks. Icotrokinra is dosed once daily — that is the "first-generation once-daily oral inhibitor" that the Avere press release keeps referring to.

A-V-R-oh-oh-one is the second-generation entry in that same target class. Same target — I-L-twenty-three receptor — same peptide-antagonist modality, same oral route. What is different is the pharmacokinetic engineering — the roughly hundred-hour half-life that supports once-weekly dosing instead of once-daily. That is a meaningful convenience differentiator on a chronic autoimmune indication where compliance is real, and it is also a marketing-and-adherence story that carries directly into ulcerative colitis and Crohn's, where the daily-dosing burden gets amplified across a patient's life.

Now the clinical data — and this is where I have to be careful, because what the release actually said is thinner than the headline. Hansoh has completed a Phase 1b in Chinese patients with moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis and has initiated a Phase 2b in China with a twenty-twenty-seven readout. The Phase 1b language in the release is that once-weekly A-V-R-oh-oh-one achieved P-A-S-I and P-A-S-I seventy-five responses at week four and week eight that were, quote, "comparable on a cross-trial basis to the first-generation once-daily oral inhibitor, despite only four weeks of dosing." No exact P-A-S-I seventy-five percentage. No sample size. No dose levels. No head-to-head against icotrokinra — this is a cross-trial comparison drawn out of matched-benchmark reasoning against the icotrokinra Phase 1b, which as best I can reconstruct from J-and-J's own historical disclosure landed in the neighborhood of thirty to forty percent P-A-S-I seventy-five at week eight with sixteen weeks of daily dosing. So the load-bearing claim here is that A-V-R-oh-oh-one matched that number at week eight with only four weeks of drug exposure. That is a genuinely interesting pharmacodynamic assertion — it says the hundred-hour half-life is producing enough sustained receptor occupancy on the tissue-resident cell population that response continues to accumulate even after dosing has stopped — but it is being asserted against a benchmark, not against a randomized comparator, and the actual numbers are being held back for a future medical meeting. The safety readout was described as generally well tolerated with an adverse event profile supporting continued development. That is standard biotech-press-release language.

Now the read-across for your beat, which is where I think this deal matters more than the specific molecule.

First — the modality-shift story in autoimmune. What you are watching is the migration of biologic-like target engagement onto oral routes via engineered peptide chemistry. Cyclic peptides — stabilized by intramolecular constraints against G-I protease degradation, engineered for oral absorption and long systemic half-life — are graduating out of interesting-tool status and into approved drugs. Icotrokinra was the beachhead. A-V-R-oh-oh-one is the follow-on. The generalization that matters is that any autoimmune target where injectable monoclonal antibodies dominate — I-L-seventeen, I-L-thirteen, T-N-F, integrins, complement factor B, and probably eventually B-cell-directed depletion — is a target where the oral cyclic peptide competitor will get built. The T-cell engager work and the C-A-R-T-plus-armoring work you are doing operates in the same design space — the question of what modality delivers the best therapeutic index for a given target — and the peptide route is a lane you should have some opinion on.

Second — this is the same JAK-alternative pressure I flagged for you yesterday on bempikibart, but from a completely different direction. The oral JAK inhibitors — baricitinib, upadacitinib, ritlecitinib, deuruxolitinib — cover psoriasis, atopic dermatitis, ulcerative colitis, and rheumatoid arthritis with a class black-box for infection, cardiovascular events, malignancy, and thrombosis. The JAK A-D-C route you are watching addresses that safety problem by restricting where the JAK inhibitor distributes. Icotrokinra and now A-V-R-oh-oh-one address the same problem by not being JAK inhibitors at all — they are oral targeted immunomodulators at a single upstream receptor, and their safety profile is likely to be cleaner than the JAK class by virtue of narrower mechanism. If the oral I-L-twenty-three-receptor class continues to deliver competitive efficacy across autoimmune indications, the ceiling for oral small molecules in autoimmune shifts, and the market opening for a JAK A-D-C narrows. Both bempikibart yesterday and A-V-R-oh-oh-one today are data points against a JAK-owned future.

Third — the Chinese asset flow. Hansoh Pharmaceutical is the same company that has been an active A-D-C partner with G-S-K, most notably licensing G-S-K the B-seven-H-three A-D-C H-S-twenty-oh-nine-three that just delivered the positive artemis-oh-oh-eight Phase 3 readout in small cell lung cancer earlier this month — one of your prior episodes covered that. Hansoh is now not just a licensor but a strategic investor in the U-S vehicle that carries their asset — they are participating in the P-I-P-E alongside Fairmount. That is a different structural template than the classic in-license — it is Chinese pharma taking equity in the U-S entity that is going to run the North American development and commercialization program for their asset. Expect more of this pattern. The two-point-three-billion-dollar total deal value tag on this transaction, mostly milestones, is aggressive but not out of line with recent Chinese-origin A-D-C and biologic deals — that pricing is now the market.

Fourth — the reverse-merger-plus-P-I-P-E capital structure. NextCure was originally an immune-oncology biotech running L-A-I-R-two antibodies and various tumor-microenvironment targets — its programs were not producing the outcomes needed to justify the standalone equity, and the company had cash but no path. Absorbing it as a public vehicle for a new PIPE-funded asset lets Avere skip the traditional I-P-O process and hit NASDAQ pre-Phase-2b with more than three hundred million in cash. Legacy NextCure holders end up with about one percent and a contingent-value right on any residual asset monetization. This capital-formation pattern — reverse-merge a mid-cap biotech with a stalled pipeline, in-license a mid-to-late-stage asset from a Chinese pharma, land a syndicate PIPE at closing — is now the fastest route to a public company running Phase 2b in autoimmune. Watch for the pattern; you will see it again.

Now some caveats.

The Phase 1b data are not disclosed at the level required to independently reproduce the comparability claim. Cross-trial comparisons across ethnic populations, endpoints scored by different investigators, and different disease-severity baselines are the softest kind of comparator argument you can make in dermatology. The full Chinese Phase 1b readout — dose levels, sample size, P-A-S-I seventy-five percentages, and safety database — needs to be presented before that "comparable to icotrokinra" claim can be evaluated. Expect it at a fall dermatology congress, most likely European Academy of Dermatology and Venereology or American Academy of Dermatology.

The U-S Phase 2b will not start until early twenty-twenty-seven, so the timing gap between deal-announcement excitement and next data point is about eighteen months, and the Chinese Phase 2b readout in twenty-twenty-seven will land first — that becomes the intervening catalyst. If the Chinese readout underperforms icotrokinra's Phase 2b, the story compresses hard.

Ulcerative colitis remains speculative. There is no Phase 1b U-C data in the release. The I-L-twenty-three axis works in U-C — that is the mechanistic basis of guselkumab and risankizumab in inflammatory bowel — but converting a psoriasis oral peptide into a U-C-effective program is not automatic. Systemic exposure required to reach gut tissue is different from what is required to reach skin, and the T-cell subsets driving U-C differ from those driving plaque psoriasis. Watch what dose escalation and biomarker work Avere runs before the U-C Phase 2b starts.

Finally — Hansoh's strategic-investor role creates some ambiguity around governance and pricing that will need to be watched. Whether Hansoh's participation in the P-I-P-E is priced at the same terms as the outside syndicate, whether the milestones on the underlying license are truly at arm's length, and whether the ex-Greater-China commercial coordination with Hansoh's China entity creates friction — those are all questions that the S-one filing and the ten-K coming out of Avere over the next year will start to answer.

Bottom line. This is not a clinical readout — it is a strategic transaction — but it is a well-structured transaction that puts a real second-generation oral I-L-twenty-three-receptor peptide antagonist into a fresh public company with more than three hundred million dollars, a Chinese pharma strategic investor, and a differentiated once-weekly dosing pitch against a first-generation drug that only just cleared approval. It is a data point on where autoimmune biologics are headed structurally — modality-shift into oral peptides at established targets, cross-Pacific asset flow with Chinese-pharma equity participation, and reverse-merger vehicles as the preferred route to a Phase 2b-ready public company. The scientific question — whether the hundred-hour half-life and once-weekly oral dosing actually delivers clinical differentiation from icotrokinra in a head-to-head — will not be answered before twenty-twenty-eight. The strategic pattern is worth understanding today. That is the brief.
