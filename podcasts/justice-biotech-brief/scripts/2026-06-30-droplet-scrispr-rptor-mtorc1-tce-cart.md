# Droplet single-cell CRISPR screen of per-cell T cell killing — RPTOR / mTORC1 emerges as a brake across blinatumomab, CAR-T, and 1G4 TCR settings; transient rapamycin during ex vivo expansion improves adoptive transfer

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.23.734054v1

## Script

Good morning, Justice. One pick today, a bioRxiv preprint posted yesterday out of Roger Geiger's lab in Bellinzona that lands directly on the bispecific T cell engager and CAR-T work that sits at the center of your interest list. The headline finding is unexpected: knocking out Raptor — the defining component of m-tor complex one — accelerates per-cell killing by cytotoxic T cells across both a CD-nineteen by CD-three bispecific and a CAR-T setting, even though those same cells lose fitness for long-term expansion. The way they got to that finding is the other half of the story, and it's the part that's actually portable to other labs.

The methodological problem is that pooled CRISPR screens have been the workhorse for finding gene knockouts that change T cell behavior, but killing is not a cell-autonomous phenotype. The single-guide R-N-A lives in the T cell. The functional readout is whether the tumor cell it engaged actually died. In bulk co-culture, that signal gets washed out by proliferation, persistence, and longer-term outgrowth — you end up reading out fitness, not killing kinetics. Arrayed screens preserve the linkage but cap throughput in the tens to low hundreds of perturbations. The team's solution is droplet microfluidics. They co-encapsulate single primary human CD-eight T cells with single CD-nineteen-positive lymphoma cells and the bispecific engager blinatumomab in picoliter water-in-oil droplets, let killing happen over ninety minutes, convert to double emulsions so that conventional flow cytometry can read them out, sort dead-target droplets from live-target droplets, and recover the guide R-N-As from each fraction. Each screen yields roughly twenty million droplets, fifteen percent of which contain a T-cell-and-target pair, and about a quarter of those show killing inside the ninety-minute window — so each screen reads out three quarters of a million informative single-T-cell encounters per donor. They run a focused library of four-and-a-half thousand guides against seven hundred ninety-one genes covering T cell signaling, differentiation, and trafficking, across at least five donors per batch.

The screen recovers what it should: T-cell-receptor-proximal signaling — C-D-three-D, C-D-two-forty-seven, L-C-K, Z-A-P-seventy, L-A-T, V-A-V-one, I-T-K, C-D-twenty-eight — plus the granule machinery — granzyme B, U-N-C-thirteen-D, R-A-B-twenty-seven-A, V-A-M-P-eight — the synapse cytoskeleton, E-O-M-E-S, N-F-kappa-B. As negative regulators of killing — that is, knockouts that *accelerate* target-cell death — it picks up the established hits: P-T-E-N, R-A-S-A-two, S-O-C-S-one, F-O-X-O-one, and Helios. So the canonical biology is intact, which is the right baseline for trusting the new hits. There are two unexpected ones. The first is A-F-A-P-one-L-two, a signaling adapter previously flagged in dysfunctional tumor-infiltrating T cells. The second — the one with the legs — is R-P-T-O-R, which encodes Raptor.

Raptor as a *negative* regulator of killing is the surprise, because m-tor complex one has been a posterchild for *supporting* T cell growth and effector differentiation. So the authors pulled the thread carefully. Knocking out R-H-E-B, the upstream activator of m-tor complex one, phenocopies the R-P-T-O-R effect — confirming it's the m-tor complex one output that matters, not some m-tor-independent function of Raptor. The signaling state these cells enter has reduced phospho-S-six-kinase, *increased* AKT serine-four-seventy-three phosphorylation — consistent with release of the known S-six-kinase negative-feedback loop on upstream PI-three-kinase-AKT — lower CD-sixty-two-L, higher LAG-three, PD-one, CD-twenty-five, and CD-sixty-nine. So a more differentiated, more effector-like surface phenotype. Proteomics shows perforin and granzyme B abundance actually go *down* in the resting Raptor-knockout cells, which is the key control: the faster killing is not about more loaded granules, it's about a rewired execution state. And the anabolic programs collapse — amino-acid biosynthesis enzymes A-S-N-S, G-L-U-L, C-T-H drop; lipogenesis enzymes F-A-S-N, A-C-A-C-A, H-M-G-C-S-one, F-A-D-S-one, F-A-D-S-two, S-C-D drop; amino acids and glycolytic intermediates pile up unused.

That gives you a clean conceptual handle. Cytotoxic execution and anabolic growth are dissociable programs in the same T cell, and m-tor complex one is the dial that picks between them. Crank it down and you get faster per-cell killing but you trade away the metabolic substrate for sustained proliferation. They confirm this isn't a blinatumomab-specific or a JeKo-1-specific result by transducing primary T cells with the one-G-four T-cell receptor against N-Y-E-S-O-one and testing against Huh-seven liver tumor spheroids and A-three-seventy-five melanoma. Same enhancement of early killing across both solid tumor models. So it generalizes across CD-three bispecific engagement, CAR-T recognition, and engineered T-cell-receptor settings, which is exactly the range of recognition modalities you work across.

Now the translational hook, which is the part to actually take away. Permanent R-P-T-O-R or R-H-E-B knockouts *do not* improve tumor control in vivo, because the proliferation defect catches up with them once you transfer the cells into a tumor-bearing animal. But low-dose rapamycin during the eight-day ex vivo expansion of one-G-four-TCR-transduced T cells reproduces the fast-killing state and *improves* tumor control and survival in A-three-seventy-five-bearing immunodeficient mice. So the recipe isn't to permanently engineer m-tor complex one out — it's to transiently bias the manufacturing window with a drug that's already on the shelf at every cell therapy facility in the country. That manipulation fits cleanly into existing CAR-T, T-cell-receptor-T, and adoptive-transfer protocols. It doesn't require a new viral construct. It doesn't require a new edit. It's a media additive during expansion.

A couple of caveats worth holding. The bulk of the screen and the rapid-killing claims rest on a ninety-minute window — orthogonal eight-hour bulk assays back it up, but this is fast-killing biology in vitro, not durable tumor-control biology. The in vivo evidence is one immunodeficient mouse model with one tumor cell line, and the durable-response question against an immune-competent system is open. And rapamycin's effects on memory-versus-effector T cell differentiation have been studied for two decades, including by Pearce, Powell, and the Restifo lab — what's new here is the per-cell killing kinetics angle and the screen that uncovered it, not the underlying observation that m-tor modulation is biologically actionable.

Net take. The droplet single-cell CRISPR platform turns T-cell killing into a real pooled screening phenotype with kinetic resolution, and it surfaces a clean, transferable lever — a low-dose rapamycin preconditioning step during ex vivo expansion — that improves rapid per-cell execution across blinatumomab, CAR-T, and TCR-T modalities without requiring genetic engineering of the m-tor pathway. The conceptual reframe — execution versus anabolic fitness as dissociable T cell programs — is portable to the trispecific T-cell engager and ADC-plus-T-cell-engager combination work, because the question of how to dial up per-cell killing during the encounter window without burning the T cell's persistence budget shows up in every one of those modalities.
