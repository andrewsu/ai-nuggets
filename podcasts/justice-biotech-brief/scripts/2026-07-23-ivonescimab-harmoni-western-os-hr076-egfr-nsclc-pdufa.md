# Ivonescimab — Western overall-survival hazard ratio in HARMONi catches up to Asia at 0.76, de-risking the November FDA decision

Paper link: https://smmttx.com/news/press-releases/news-details/2026/Ivonescimab-Plus-Chemotherapy-Shows-Consistent-Favorable-Overall-Survival-Results-in-Western-and-Asian-Patients-in-Updated-Analysis-from-Global-Phase-III-HARMONi-Study/default.aspx

## Script

Today's story is the one you've probably been waiting for on the P-D-1 times VEGF front. On July 22nd, Summit Therapeutics put out an updated overall-survival analysis from the global phase three HARMONi study of ivonescimab, and it lands right on the single question that has hung over this entire drug class in Western patients.

Quick refresher on why this matters. Ivonescimab is the tetravalent bispecific that blocks P-D-1 and VEGF in one molecule — the Akeso-originated, Summit-partnered asset that became the poster child for the argument that a China-born bispecific could go head-to-head with the checkpoint standard of care. HARMONi is the trial that tests it in a genuinely hard setting: E-G-F-R-mutated, non-squamous non-small-cell lung cancer, in patients who have already progressed on a third-generation E-G-F-R inhibitor. Ivonescimab plus platinum chemo versus placebo plus chemo, with progression-free survival and overall survival as co-primary endpoints.

Here's the tension the field has been sitting with. At the primary analysis back in April 2025, progression-free survival was a clean, statistically significant win. Overall survival was not — the global hazard ratio was 0.79, but the p-value came in at 0.057, just over the line. And when you split it by geography, the story looked worse for the West: the Western subgroup hazard ratio was basically 0.98, essentially no survival separation, while the Asian patients were already showing a robust benefit. That split fueled a very specific worry — that the survival signal was an Asia phenomenon and might not translate to a U.S. label.

The update Summit just released is essentially a follow-up-maturation story, and it's a clean one. Across three data cutoffs, the Western hazard ratio has walked steadily in the right direction: 0.98 at the primary analysis, then 0.84 in September 2025, and now 0.76 at the June 2026 cutoff — which is the same 0.76 as the overall trial population. In other words, the Western curve has caught up to the Asian curve. And the mechanism here isn't spin: at the primary analysis the Western patients had only about nine months of median follow-up because they enrolled later, versus roughly 32 months for the Asian patients. Now the West is out to 23 months, and with mature data the benefit looks the same on both sides of the world. No new safety signals, profile described as manageable and consistent with everything prior.

Now the opinionated part, because you should not read this as a fresh positive trial. This is a descriptive, updated analysis of a secondary look at overall survival — it is not a newly powered, alpha-controlled result that flips the primary miss into a win. The nominal p-value crossed 0.05 at the September cutoff, but that's nominal, not a pre-specified success. What actually changed is the interpretation risk: the single most plausible bear case against ivonescimab in the West — "PFS wins but survival doesn't follow" — just got a lot harder to argue when the Western hazard ratio has converged to 0.76 with two years of follow-up. Summit says the data are already with the F-D-A, and the target action date on the biologics license application is November 14th. So this is a de-risking readout ahead of a real decision, not the decision itself.

Why should you care beyond this one asset. If you're working anywhere near VEGF-times-P-D-1 or VEGF-times-P-D-L1 bispecifics, HARMONi has been the reference case for the whole "does it translate to Western patients" debate — the thing skeptics point to. This update reframes that skepticism as a follow-up-immaturity artifact rather than a biology-doesn't-travel problem. That read-through matters for everyone chasing this mechanism, from Summit's own broader program to the Merck and Pfizer P-D-1-times-VEGF entrants and the trispecific players stacking CTLA-4 on top. The class's credibility in the West has been partly hostage to this one survival curve, and today that curve moved the right way.

The caveat to keep in your back pocket: a hazard ratio of 0.76 in a post-progression, E-G-F-R-mutant population is meaningful but not overwhelming, the confidence intervals and updated median survival numbers are being held for an upcoming medical meeting, and none of this substitutes for the front-line, checkpoint-naive settings where the bigger commercial question actually lives. But for a class that has been defined as much by doubt as by data, watching the Western survival benefit fully materialize with time is exactly the kind of maturation you want to see before a November approval call.
