## Script

Today's nugget is a fresh bioRxiv preprint from a group at the University of Electronic Science and Technology of China describing NEURA, a system for running neuroimaging analyses with large language model agents in a way that's designed from the ground up to be hallucination-resistant. The reason it's worth a look is that NEURA takes the central credibility problem for agentic biomedical AI — that the model sometimes confidently asserts things that aren't true — and reframes it as a structural engineering problem rather than a model-quality problem. The structural fix it proposes is borrowed almost directly from formal methods, and it lands on something the authors call a proof-carrying framework.

Here's the setup. Neuroimaging is a domain where automation is genuinely valuable. You have heterogeneous software stacks — FSL, FreeSurfer, AFNI, ANTs, SPM — multimodal data, multistage statistical workflows, and a tradition of long, brittle pipelines that take a domain expert weeks to assemble correctly. An LLM agent that could read a research question, plan the analysis, drive the tools, and write up a structured report would save a lot of time. People have built such agents. The catch is that LLMs hallucinate, and a hallucinated effect in a clinical-adjacent context is much worse than a hallucinated email draft. So the question is not whether to use agents — it's how to make their outputs trustworthy enough to publish from.

NEURA's answer has two pieces. The first is disease-aware and tool-aware planning. Free-text research questions and neuroimaging datasets get converted into executable analysis plans. Important here is that the planner knows about specific diseases and specific tools, so it isn't generating generic Python that happens to call FSL — it's generating analyses with the structure that field uses. The second piece is the part the paper actually leans on, which is a deterministic verification layer inspired by formal proof. Before any claim is retained for reporting, that claim has to be checked against tool-derived evidence and domain axioms. Tool-derived evidence means the actual output the analysis software emitted. Domain axioms means a curated bank of things that are known to be true about brains, regions, statistical procedures, and so on. The check is deterministic — symbolic rules and constraint satisfaction, not another LLM grading its own homework. If a claim can't be derived from the evidence under the axioms, it doesn't make it into the report.

The empirical results are persuasive. On NeuroEval, an expert-curated benchmark of 110 neuroimaging tasks, NEURA hit 89.5 percent planning accuracy, which is a 30.5-percentage-point improvement over directly querying a frontier LLM. More interesting than the headline accuracy is the controlled hallucination injection experiment. The authors deliberately introduced errors into intermediate outputs and asked whether the verification layer would catch them. Under the specified axiom bank and trust assumptions, all the injected error classes were detected — with no false positives. That kind of clean result tells you the verification layer is actually checking something specific and tractable, rather than doing soft pattern matching. There's also a case study on spinocerebellar ataxia type 3 where NEURA reproduced cerebellar atrophy and abnormal diffusion patterns consistent with established pathology and independent expert analyses. So beyond the controlled tests it's also recovering known biology end-to-end.

The reason this matters outside neuroimaging is the general pattern. Most agentic biomedical AI systems today defend against hallucination with what you might call probabilistic self-checking — the agent asks itself or another LLM whether the answer looks right, or it cross-checks with retrieval. That works some of the time but doesn't get you to scientific reliability, because the checker is just as liable to hallucinate as the original. NEURA's proof-carrying angle says you can do better if you constrain the output space with explicit domain axioms and require every claim to trace back to tool-produced evidence. The price you pay is that someone has to maintain the axiom bank, and the axiom bank only covers the disease and tool combinations you've encoded. The payoff is that within that scope, the system stops being probabilistic about whether it's making things up. It becomes auditable.

A couple of caveats worth keeping in mind. The axiom bank in this paper is curated for the specific subset of neuroimaging tasks in their benchmark, so the proof-carrying guarantees are scoped to that bank — generalizing the approach to a new biomedical domain means doing the axiom curation work over again. And while planning accuracy is up sharply, the paper doesn't quantify how much of that gain is from disease-aware planning versus the verification layer specifically; it would be useful to see those two contributions separated. Both are addressable in follow-ups, not flaws in the core idea.

The takeaway worth keeping is the framing. Coupling domain-grounded agency with proof-carrying verification turns LLM-driven workflow automation from probabilistic self-checking into auditable scientific computation. That's the right ambition for biomedical agents, and this preprint is one of the cleaner demonstrations that the ambition is reachable in 2026, not five years out.
