# Multi-agent psychiatric medication KG — patient forums lead the FDA on antidepressant side effects by months

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26205

## Script

Today's pick is a fresh arXiv preprint from a group at the Chinese University of Hong Kong's Department of Psychiatry, in collaboration with Stanford, Penn, Vanderbilt, Shandong, and Arizona. The title is "Knowledge-augmented Agentic AI for Mental Health Medication Information Seeking," and it's doing several things at once that I think are worth pulling apart carefully.

Setup. Psychiatric medications are prescribed long-term, individualized, and frequently adjusted. After a clinical encounter — which is usually short — patients routinely go online to fill in what they didn't get answered in the room. That online information environment is split between two completely different epistemic worlds. On one side, you have the FDA's Adverse Event Reporting System, two decades of postmarketing surveillance data, authoritative but abstract — the kind of language that lists "nausea," "insomnia," and "suicidal ideation" in the same bullet-point format regardless of frequency, severity, or context. On the other side, you have Reddit and WebMD consumer reviews, written in natural language by people actually on the drugs, experience-near and specific but completely unvalidated. The authors make the argument that naively combining these two — serving the patient a mix of regulatory fact and peer narrative without distinguishing which is which — is actively dangerous, because in psychiatry the expectation of a side effect can induce that side effect. Nocebo is real. If you tell someone sertraline causes weight gain without contextualizing the frequency and the forum provenance, you may create what you're predicting.

Their answer is a knowledge-graph-grounded, source-aware multi-agent system that keeps these two worlds structurally separate while making them jointly queryable.

What they built. The pipeline starts with data. They collected 466,525 Reddit posts over twenty years, 60,782 WebMD medication reviews, and the full FDA Adverse Event Reporting System quarterly releases from 2005 through early 2025 — all filtered to nine antidepressants from the WHO's Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical classification for the nervous system. Those drug terms come with their brand-name synonyms, derived from a constrained LLM prompt, to handle the way patients actually write about these drugs in forums.

On top of that corpus, they ran an LLM-based named entity recognition pipeline benchmarked against physician-annotated gold standard data. Nine models competed. GPT-4.1-mini won on the combined accuracy-throughput-cost tradeoff, hitting F1 scores of 0.969 for medication name extraction and 0.973 for primary condition extraction — physician-level on those tasks. Side effect name extraction was harder, topping out at 0.912 with Deepseek. Attribute-level extraction — severity, timing, frequency of a side effect — was much harder still, with most models in the 0.2 to 0.5 range, which the authors correctly read as patients describing adverse events qualitatively rather than with precise temporal structure.

The extracted entities — medications, conditions, side effects, and their relations — are then mapped to controlled biomedical vocabularies. Drugs to ATC-N identifiers. Conditions to ICD-10. Side effects to the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities, known as MedDRA. The mapping uses embedding-based nearest-neighbor retrieval with cosine similarity thresholds calibrated on the physician-annotated standard. The result is a Neo4j knowledge graph with four main node types — Post, Medication, Condition, SideEffect — connected by four edge classes: treats, causes, causes-by-withdrawal, and comorbid-with. Critically, every relationship carries a provenance link back to the source posts that support it, so you can always trace any claim in the graph to the specific documents that generated it.

There are three separate knowledge graphs: one for Reddit, one for WebMD, one for FDA FAERS. They are not merged.

On top of those three graphs, six agents handle user queries. A NER agent extracts what medication the user is asking about and maps it to canonical identifiers. A User Intent agent classifies the clinical question type — is this a general side-effect inquiry, a demographic question, a longitudinal question about how risks change over time? The outputs of those two agents converge at a KG Query Generation agent that selects which graphs to query and formulates the actual Cypher queries. Demographic and epidemiological questions get routed primarily to the FAERS and WebMD graphs, which carry structured age and sex metadata. Experiential, context-rich questions get routed to the Reddit graph. Retrieved evidence goes to a Summarization agent that produces independent source-specific summaries, always preserving which graph each claim came from. A Comparison agent synthesizes those into a cross-source comparison that explicitly marks where the three sources agree and where they diverge. Finally, a Validation agent checks the synthesized response against a safety ruleset before the answer goes to the user.

The key findings. The authors did a multi-source comparative analysis across the nine antidepressants. The headline result is that Reddit and WebMD are highly concordant with each other — Jaccard similarity up to 0.905 — but both differ substantially from the FDA data. This is not surprising once you think about it, but it's now quantified: patient communities form a partly independent safety signal from the regulatory channel. What makes this concrete and important is the temporal analysis. For sertraline — that's the generic name for Zoloft, one of the most commonly prescribed antidepressants — the authors found that many adverse events appeared in community sources hundreds of days before the corresponding FDA date. The community was reporting what patients actually experience before those experiences had worked their way through the pharmacovigilance reporting chain into regulatory records.

That's a surveillance lead time with real implications. The authors are careful to frame this as a supplementary signal that requires careful validation, not as a replacement for regulatory data. The two channels capture different things: regulatory reports are structured and nominally verified; community reports are representative of lived experience but noisy and uncontrolled. Source-aware integration is exactly the right frame — keep them epistemically distinct, make both queryable, let the patient and clinician see where the two worlds agree and where they diverge.

The editorial take. Three things stand out here.

First, the architecture is correct for the problem. The multi-agent design with source-aware routing is not a gimmick — intent-based graph selection is the right way to handle a system where some questions genuinely need regulatory metadata about age and sex distributions, and others need peer community experience about what discontinuation actually feels like. Flattening these into a single retrieval index would lose the provenance contract that makes the system safe.

Second, the controlled vocabulary mapping is the backbone. ATC-N, ICD-10, and MedDRA are the same vocabularies that show up in clinical genomics, drug interaction databases, Wikidata, and knowledge graphs across the biomedical space. The fact that this system grounds its nodes in those shared namespaces means it's not a one-off application — it's potentially pluggable into any data integration architecture that speaks those vocabularies. That's a meaningful design choice and it's why the system can in principle run cross-drug comparisons cleanly rather than getting tangled in naming variants.

Third, the temporal lead-time finding is a genuine scientific result, not just an engineering demonstration. If patient communities are reliably signaling adverse events months before the regulatory record catches up, then there's a real argument for building community pharmacovigilance infrastructure that feeds into regulatory review pipelines, not just into patient chatbots. The authors don't make that argument explicitly — they're careful to limit their claims to what the data support — but it's where this line of work points.

Caveats. This is a preprint. The multi-agent chatbot is described architecturally but evaluated primarily through qualitative examples and the NER benchmark; there's no end-to-end evaluation of whether the chatbot actually helps patients understand their medications more safely or accurately compared to a baseline. That's the next study. The authors acknowledge it explicitly. The temporal lead-time analysis is also observational — earlier community reporting doesn't prove those reports are correct, and some fraction of what showed up on Reddit before the FDA may be anecdote rather than signal. The provenance-aware design is precisely the right way to hedge against that, but the calibration question requires prospective clinical study.

The deeper take for this audience: this is what data integration looks like when you're serious about it. Not one knowledge graph, but three — one per source — connected by shared vocabulary nodes. Not one question-answering agent, but six with distinct roles and source-aware routing. Not "here's what the internet says about sertraline," but "here's what the FDA record says, here's what WebMD reviewers report, here's where those two agree, here's where they diverge, and here is why that distinction matters for your decision." That framing is both the technical contribution and the right public health message about how to deploy AI in a domain where credibility is everything and nocebo is real.
