# BioPathfinder — a multi-agent CAR-T evidence engine takes its own hypothesis into a mouse

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.15.738646v1

## Script

Today's episode is about a preprint that pushes agentic biomedical AI past the point where most of these systems politely stop. It's called BioPathfinder, out of Song Li and Lili Yang's groups at UCLA together with Cedars-Sinai, posted to bioRxiv on the sixteenth. It's a multi-agent workflow for CAR-T cell therapy research, and what makes it interesting is not the multi-agent scaffolding — that part is by now familiar — but the fact that the authors let it generate a hypothesis, then went into the wet lab and tested that hypothesis in a mouse model, and it worked.

Let me set up the problem first. CAR-T cell therapies produce astonishing responses in some patients and fail in others, and the field has spent years generating single-cell RNA-seq datasets from treated patients to figure out why. The trouble is that this evidence is fragmented across primary papers, GEO accessions, supplementary tables, patient metadata, and study-specific annotations. The same biological claim means completely different things depending on whether it comes from the infusion product, an early post-infusion sample, an endogenous bystander T cell, or a relapse biopsy. Existing LLM-based approaches in this area have mostly ignored that structure — they run retrieval over abstracts and hope the sample context is implied.

BioPathfinder's central design choice is to separate evidence construction from hypothesis generation, and to make everything provenance-tracked. The architecture is four linked agentic components. A Curator agent scans Europe PMC — starting from about sixteen thousand publication records going back to 2017 — extracts dataset accessions, resolves paper-to-dataset links, and normalizes patient sample context. The output is a frozen bundle of fifty-seven strictly eligible CAR-T patient scRNA-seq studies. Against an expert ground truth, the Curator hit 90.5 percent recall and 82.6 percent precision, which is notably better than comparable single-cell resources like scBaseCount or CZ CELLxGENE, which only overlapped with two or three of these studies.

Then a Planner agent takes the frozen evidence base plus an exploration question and generates structured hypothesis cards. Each card has a bounded claim, a proposed dry-lab analysis, a wet-lab test, a claim boundary, and a strongest alternative — deliberately shaped to be falsifiable and dataset-aware, not the usual wall of speculative prose. Reviewer agents then score those cards. There are three review routes: a minimal rubric-only route, a source-grounded route, and an agent-based route that adds live literature search and network checks. Each route rates the same hypotheses on the same seven scientific-merit dimensions.

The methodology paper move that I want to flag here is that the authors treat reproducibility of the agent as a first-class metric. They run the Planner three times on the same questions and the Reviewer three times on the same fifty hypotheses. They report Spearman correlations, Kendall tau, and intraclass correlation coefficients across those repeats. This is not standard practice in agentic-AI papers, and it should be. If your automated hypothesis prioritizer isn't rank-consistent under resampling, you don't really have a prioritizer.

Now the biological punchline. The top-scored hypothesis from expert selection was that post-infusion CAR-T cells acquire an NK-like transition program that drives exhaustion, and that perturbing genes in that program could improve persistence. Fine — that's the hypothesis-generation step everyone shows. Where it gets interesting is what the system does next. The Bioinfo Analyst agent orchestrates local tools — scVI, Monocle3, scTenifoldKnk — over a dataset the hypothesis card explicitly nominated, in this case GSE125881. Virtual knockout on the KLR receptor family points at KLRC1, KLRD1, and KLRG1. Human experts then pick KLRC1 — which encodes NKG2A — for wet-lab work.

And this is where the paper leaves the pack. They do repeated in vitro Raji tumor cell stimulation of CD19 CAR-T cells and confirm that NKG2A gets progressively enriched in CD8 CAR-T cells with each round of antigen exposure. They then do an NSG mouse Raji-FG xenograft model, treat with CD8 CAR-T alone or with monalizumab, which is the clinical anti-NKG2A antibody. The combination reduces tumor burden by bioluminescence, extends survival, and increases serum IFN-gamma, TNF-alpha, and IL-2, plus recovery of CAR-T cells from the liver. The AI-nominated target survives contact with a mouse.

Two caveats matter for how you interpret this. The chosen target isn't strictly novel — prior CAR-T single-cell work had noted KLRC1 in NK-like post-infusion signatures, and monalizumab is already in cancer trials. What's genuinely new is the functional perturbation. So this paper is less "AI discovered a new target" and more "structured multi-agent evidence handling can be defensibly hooked to a wet-lab decision." The other caveat is scope — the corpus is deliberately narrow, only CAR-T-treated patient studies, which limits the mechanistic search space.

The signal I take away is that the value here is in the plumbing, not the reasoning. Provenance-tracked evidence bases, frozen upstream artifacts, reviewer reproducibility scoring, and executable dry-lab tool registries — these are the boring engineering choices that let an agentic biomedical system make it into a mouse instead of a slide deck. That's the template worth stealing.
