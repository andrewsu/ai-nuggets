# HypoAgent — agentic abductive hypothesis generation over biomedical knowledge graphs

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.31370

## Script

Today's nugget is HypoAgent, an agentic framework for interactive abductive hypothesis generation over knowledge graphs, posted to arXiv late last week out of HKUST and tested on a biomedical knowledge graph among others. This one is interesting because it pushes against a pattern that has become a little stale — KG question answering bolted onto a single large language model with retrieval — and instead treats hypothesis generation as a multi-agent loop with a real diagnosis step.

Start with the setup. Abductive reasoning over a knowledge graph means: given some observed entities or facts in the graph, generate the logical hypotheses — combinations of relations and intermediate nodes — that would explain them. It is the bread-and-butter shape of a lot of biomedical questions. You see a phenotype, a drug response, a gene-disease association, and you want the machine to enumerate plausible mechanistic explanations grounded in what the graph actually contains. Existing controllable methods let the user supply conditions to steer the generation, and that works in single-turn settings, but as soon as the interaction becomes a real dialogue with the system, two things break. First, the model loses track of evolving intent across turns — the user refines, reframes, narrows, and the system either ignores the history or over-fits to the last utterance. Second, when a generated hypothesis is wrong or partially wrong, the system gives almost no fine-grained signal about which part of it failed and why, which is exactly the information a scientist needs to push the next iteration.

HypoAgent splits the problem across three cooperating agents and that division is the contribution worth dwelling on. The first is an Intent Recognition Agent, whose job is to take the current user utterance plus the dialogue history and ground it into executable conditions over the knowledge graph — concrete constraints that the downstream generator can actually consume. The second is a Hypothesis Generation Agent, which produces candidate hypotheses under those conditions in a controllable way. The third, and the one that does the most distinctive work, is a Root Cause Analysis Agent. When a hypothesis is judged unreliable, this agent diagnoses which fragments of the candidate are responsible, then probes the local neighborhood in the knowledge graph to identify substitutions or refinements that the graph actually supports. The end-to-end effect is a system that does not just produce a hypothesis and stop — it produces a hypothesis, localizes its weak link, and proposes a graph-grounded fix.

On the benchmarks the authors run two flavors of knowledge graph — commonsense and biomedical — and three evaluation regimes: single-turn, multi-turn, and unconditional. HypoAgent posts state-of-the-art semantic similarity to gold hypotheses across all three. The single-turn number says the baseline architecture is sound. The multi-turn number is the one that matters more, because it is the regime that proves the intent recognition agent is actually carrying user state across turns rather than treating each utterance independently. The unconditional setting tests whether the generation agent can produce reasonable hypotheses without explicit guiding conditions, which is closer to the early-exploration phase of real biomedical work where the user does not yet know what to ask.

A few read-throughs that matter for this audience. First, the architectural pattern — intent grounding, controllable generation, root cause diagnosis — is genuinely transferable beyond hypothesis generation. The same triad applies to almost any agentic task over a structured knowledge resource: turning fuzzy natural-language asks into concrete graph queries, executing them in a controllable way, and offering structured feedback when the execution fails. If you are building anything that lets a researcher converse with a biomedical knowledge graph, the diagnosis loop is the part most worth stealing. Second, the biomedical evaluation is on a domain-specific knowledge graph rather than a toy academic one, which matters because the hard part of abductive reasoning in biology is often not the reasoning, it is whether the graph actually contains the entities and edges the hypothesis would need. The Root Cause Analysis Agent's neighborhood probing is implicitly a coverage-aware operator — it can only refine into what the graph supports — and that is the right design choice for messy real-world knowledge graphs where coverage is uneven. Third, and this is the speculative read, the same diagnosis agent could in principle drive a curation loop. If it consistently flags the same kinds of missing-edge failures across many queries, that is a structured signal about where the underlying knowledge graph should be expanded next. Whether the authors take that step or someone else does, it is the natural next move.

One caveat worth naming. The paper reports semantic similarity to gold hypotheses, which is a sensible automatic metric for benchmarking but not a substitute for biological correctness. In the biomedical setting in particular, a hypothesis that is semantically close to a known answer can still be wrong in subtle but important ways. The next test is a human-expert evaluation on novel queries where there is no gold hypothesis at all, only a working scientist who can tell you whether the candidate is plausible and worth following up. That is where this class of system will either earn its keep or stall.

That is today's nugget.
