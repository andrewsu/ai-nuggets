# GEOAgent — autonomous retrieval and standardized preprocessing across NCBI GEO

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.06.730646v1

## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI. Today's pick is from a group at the Guangzhou Institute of Biomedicine and Health, part of the Chinese Academy of Sciences, and it tackles a problem that has frustrated computational biologists for roughly two decades — making the Gene Expression Omnibus actually reusable at scale. The tool is called GEOAgent, and the title of the paper, which went up on bioRxiv yesterday, is "GEOAgent: An AI-driven Autonomous Framework for Intelligent GEO Data Retrieval and Standardized Preprocessing."

Let me set up why this matters. The Gene Expression Omnibus, run by NCBI, is the largest public repository of functional genomics data — somewhere north of a hundred and eighty thousand sequencing series at this point, covering bulk RNA-seq, single-cell, ATAC-seq, ChIP-seq, you name it. In principle, that's an extraordinary scientific commons. In practice, anyone who has tried to reuse GEO data knows the catch. The sample annotations are heterogeneous text fields written by whoever uploaded the study, so finding the right datasets requires reading a lot of free-form descriptions. And once you have the right series, the raw sequencing files need assay-specific preprocessing — a different pipeline for bulk versus single-cell, different again for chromatin accessibility, with experimental design pairings, controls, and replicate relationships that have to be reconstructed by hand. It is the kind of toil that eats weeks of a graduate student's life and produces nothing publishable.

GEOAgent's argument is that essentially all of that toil is now automatable, and the right architecture is an agent on top of a real workflow engine. There are two coupled components. First, autonomous semantic governance — the metadata for those one hundred and eighty thousand series, plus eighty-four thousand associated PubMed records, are organized into a relational database and a semantic index that supports natural-language retrieval. So instead of writing a query against GEO's brittle field structure, a user asks for, say, single-cell RNA-seq of human pancreatic islets from type two diabetes patients, and the agent returns the matching series. Second, an automated Nextflow pipeline they're calling bioStream that picks up where retrieval ends. The agent reads the parsed metadata, decides which assay modality each sample belongs to, resolves the experimental design — which samples are treatment versus control, which are technical replicates, which are biological replicates — standardizes the sample naming, and then generates a deployment-ready manifest that bioStream executes in containers. Bulk omics, single-cell omics, both handled.

The benchmark numbers are striking. In expert-curated evaluations, the retrieval step hit ninety-six percent precision. Assay classification — figuring out from messy metadata whether a series is bulk RNA-seq or single-cell or ATAC-seq — was one hundred percent accurate. Sample relationship resolution, also one hundred percent. Those are the two steps that historically need the most human curation, and the agent is essentially solving them.

A few reasons this is the right kind of work, and a few honest caveats. On the positive side — first, this is the right altitude for an agentic system. The agent isn't trying to do the science; it's doing the data-engineering preamble that sits between the science and the data. That's the layer where automation pays off massively, because the science varies by lab but the preamble is genuinely shared infrastructure. Second, they didn't reinvent execution — they wrote the semantic and planning layer on top of Nextflow, which is the right call. Nextflow has containerization, provenance, and reproducibility solved; layering an agent over it gives you a system whose outputs another lab can actually trust and rerun. And third, the whole thing is open. The web platform is publicly accessible, the source code is on GitHub, and the databases are deposited on Zenodo. For a community that has spent decades trying to make GEO data more reusable, that combination — open code, open data, open service — is what you want.

The honest caveats. Ninety-six percent retrieval precision is excellent, but precision is not recall, and the paper is the place to look for whether queries about niche assays or rare cell types still surface the right studies. And the assay coverage in bioStream is bulk and single-cell omics — important and broad, but spatial transcriptomics, long-read sequencing, and the multi-modal assays now flooding into GEO are next on the list, not solved. The agent is also evaluated on its own preprocessing outputs, not on whether a downstream biological conclusion drawn from those outputs replicates — which is a harder question that the next paper from this group should probably take on.

But this is exactly the unglamorous, high-leverage agentic-AI work the biomedical community needs more of. A lot of agentic-biology papers are still in the demo phase. GEOAgent is a public service. If the precision holds up on community use, the cost of doing a meta-analysis across hundreds of GEO studies just dropped by an order of magnitude, and that changes what questions are tractable for individual labs.
