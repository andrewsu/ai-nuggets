# Tolkach's SPARK: An Agent That Writes Its Own Pathology Tools and Tests Them on 5,400 Cancer Patients

Paper link: https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04357-y

## Summary

Yuri Tolkach's group at University Hospital Cologne, with collaborators, published SPARK in Nature Medicine on May fifth — a System of Pathology Agents for Research and Knowledge that uses a language model as the controller, a stable of pre-existing specialized vision algorithms as tools, and pathology images as the substrate, and that autonomously generates biological hypotheses, builds analytical workflows for them, runs them, and refines them — all without retraining a single underlying model. They evaluated it on more than five thousand four hundred patients across eighteen independent cohorts and five cancer types, and the news-and-views companion piece in Nature Medicine has already framed it as one of the first end-to-end demonstrations of autonomous scientific discovery in clinical oncology. The interesting move is that SPARK inverts the foundation-model paradigm — it does not train a bigger image encoder, it composes existing encoders, segmenters, and classifiers into a coordinated agent that uses language as the universal interface between tools, hypotheses, and the pathologist driving it. For an audience that builds open biomedical infrastructure, the lesson is that the next wave of clinical AI may not be more weights, it may be tool-use orchestration over the weights we already have.

## Script

Today's nugget is the lead Nature Medicine paper from yesterday, May fifth. The title is An Agentic Framework for Autonomous Scientific Discovery in Cancer Pathology, the system is called SPARK — System of Pathology Agents for Research and Knowledge — and the senior author is Yuri Tolkach at the Institute of Pathology at University Hospital Cologne, with Florian Trost as first author and a list of collaborators that includes Reinhard Buettner. Nature Medicine ran a companion news-and-views piece on the same day, which is a signal the editors thought this was a milestone paper rather than a routine methods contribution.

Let me describe what SPARK actually is, because the framing is the whole point.

The conventional way to do AI in pathology, for the last three or four years, has been to train a foundation model on millions of histology image tiles, and then fine-tune it for a downstream task — tumor detection, grading, biomarker prediction. That has worked. We have UNI, CONCH, Virchow, Prov-GigaPath, and a handful of clinical-grade pathology foundation models that are now genuinely useful. But every new question requires you to either fine-tune the model or train a downstream head, and the loop from biological hypothesis to running analysis takes weeks if you are fast and months if you are normal.

SPARK rewrites that loop. The system has a language model as the controller. It has a library of pre-existing specialized vision tools — tile-level encoders, cell segmenters, tissue classifiers, the kind of components a pathology AI lab already has on a shelf. And it has access to whole-slide images plus clinical metadata. The pathologist asks SPARK a question in natural language — for example, do tumors with this morphological pattern respond differently to immunotherapy. SPARK decomposes that question into sub-hypotheses, picks the right combination of tools to test each one, generates the analytical workflow itself, runs it on the slides, looks at the results, and either reports back or proposes a refinement and goes again.

Crucially, SPARK does not retrain anything. The underlying tools are frozen. The intelligence is in the orchestration — which tool to call, in what order, on which region, with what threshold, and how to chain the outputs into a hypothesis test. Language is the universal interface between the agent, the tools, and the human.

The validation. They ran SPARK on more than five thousand four hundred patients across eighteen independent cohorts and five cancer types — lung adenocarcinoma, lung squamous cell carcinoma, colorectal cancer, breast cancer, and oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma. The agent autonomously identified clinically relevant tissue markers — features tied to disease progression, to established pathology parameters, and to treatment response. The companion news-and-views piece characterizes it as autonomous scientific discovery in oncology, which is a strong claim, and the paper does enough to support it that Nature Medicine put its name behind the framing.

Three things to say about why this matters for the kind of work this audience cares about.

First — this is the strongest published demonstration so far that the foundation-model era and the agentic-AI era are not in competition. They are stacked. SPARK takes the pathology foundation models we already have, and the specialized segmenters and classifiers we already have, and treats them as tools an agent can compose. The performance ceiling is not set by the size of the largest image encoder anyone has trained — it is set by how well the orchestration layer can pick and combine those encoders for a given biological question. That is a meaningfully different bottleneck. It means the rate of clinical discovery may be limited less by GPU budgets and more by how good our agent scaffolding gets.

Second — language as the universal interface is the load-bearing design choice. The pathologist does not write Python. The pathologist writes a question. The agent writes the workflow. That is the same architectural pattern we are seeing in agentic systems for genomics, for knowledge-graph reasoning, for drug-target validation. SPARK is one more data point that the unifying interface across biomedical AI is going to be natural language plus a tool-calling layer, and that domain experts will interact with their own data through a language model rather than through a notebook. Good news for accessibility, mixed news for reproducibility, hard news for anyone in the lab whose job is to be the in-house scripting layer between the biologist and the data.

Third — for the open-science side of this audience, SPARK is a quiet argument for open biomedical infrastructure. The paper works because there is a deep stack of open pathology foundation models, open histology datasets, and open downstream tools that the agent can compose. If those underlying components were locked behind APIs, SPARK as described could not exist in academia. The next version of this story — agent in oncology, agent in genomics, agent in drug discovery — will be told inside the institutions that kept their substrate open. That is a continuation of the through-line from the Huang ADA paper yesterday, the OptimusKG and Zitnik biomedical-knowledge-graph work two days ago, and the broader pattern this show keeps surfacing — the agentic AI era runs on top of curated open biomedical resources, and the value of those resources is going up, not down.
