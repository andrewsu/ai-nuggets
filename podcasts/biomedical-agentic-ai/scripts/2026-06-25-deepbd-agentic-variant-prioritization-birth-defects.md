# DeepBD — agentic variant prioritization that beats Exomiser on birth-defect genomes

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24779

## Script

Today's pick is a fresh arXiv preprint from a group at Zhejiang University. It's called DeepBD, and the framing is, in the authors' own words, a grounded agentic workflow for variant prioritization and diagnostic interpretation of genetic birth defects. The reason I'm picking it for the show is that it does something this field has been promising for two years and rarely delivering — it puts a real agentic layer on top of clinical-grade variant interpretation, evaluates it at scale, and beats the de facto standard tool on the held-out benchmark. It's also a worked example of what knowledge-graph and ontology grounding actually buys you when you wire it into an agent the right way.

Setup. Once a child with a suspected genetic birth defect has been exome- or genome-sequenced, the bottleneck is no longer the sequencing. It's the post-sequencing interpretation. A clinical geneticist now has tens to hundreds of candidate variants per case and has to rank them under incomplete fetal or infant phenotypes — the baby hasn't lived long enough to express the full phenotype yet — and synthesize evidence across population allele frequencies, gene-disease validity, variant-effect predictors, phenotype ontologies, pathway context, protein structure, and the clinical literature. That synthesis is exactly the kind of long-tail evidence integration where the current dominant tool, Exomiser, is the workhorse, but where Exomiser was built around a fixed rule-and-score pipeline rather than a flexible reasoning loop. DeepBD's bet is that you can do better by separating the work into three layers: a pretrained scoring engine that produces calibrated patient-specific variant scores, a set of specialist tool modules that refine those scores using gold-standard external tools, and an agentic review layer that sits on top, reorders the candidates, and writes the diagnostic synthesis.

What the engine actually does. The pretrained evidence engine fuses three streams. First, structured rule evidence — allele frequency, predicted impact, the ClinGen gene-disease validity and dosage-sensitivity calls, ClinVar and HGMD pathogenicity assertions, inheritance pattern. Second, a variant-intrinsic representation that combines sequence embeddings from a genomic foundation model — they cite Evo — with functional predictors REVEL, PrimateAI, SpliceAI, and AlphaMissense. Third, and this is the part that should make a Su Lab listener sit up — a heterogeneous biological knowledge graph spanning the candidate gene, the patient's Human Phenotype Ontology terms, cellular and anatomical context, and pathway nodes, with what they call knowledge-guided graph attention that carries learned trust coefficients over individual evidence sources. The three streams are concatenated, gated, cross-multiplied, and squashed through a sigmoid to produce the engine's variant score. So the engine is not an LLM. It's a graph-attention model with learned per-source trust coefficients that produces a calibrated score, and the LLM never gets to override that score with free-form reasoning.

What the agent does. The agentic layer sits above the engine and never overwrites the engine's evidence — it operates over a reviewable workspace. Each candidate variant arrives with its full evidence trace: the population-frequency signal, the consequence annotation, the variant-effect predictions, the gene-disease validity, the ClinVar or HGMD evidence, the phenotype-linked graph paths the engine used, the high-attention cellular or pathway nodes, and the external tool signals. Specialist modules are agent-callable — there's a SHEPHERD phenotype-driven track, an Exomiser candidate-pool calibration, an LLM-assisted reranking step, and a protein-structure refinement step that pulls from AlphaFold DB and uses Rosetta-style modeling when the protein modeling is biologically meaningful. The agent's job is constrained top-K reranking and diagnostic synthesis with reflection — it can reorder the candidates and write the diagnosis, but every claim it makes resolves back to a numbered entry in the evidence workspace. The grounding is structural — the LLM never gets to assert a biological fact it can't trace to the engine, the KG, or a specialist tool.

The numbers. The system was developed and held-out tested on an in-house fetal and infant cohort, eighteen thousand six hundred and twenty-two cases total. The held-out solved-case benchmark is five hundred forty-nine probands and one thousand three hundred and seven curated causal variants. On that benchmark, DeepBD achieved Recall at one of 0.658, Recall at three of 0.882, Recall at five of 0.912, Recall at ten of 0.929 — meaning the curated causal variant lands as the top candidate in roughly two thirds of solved cases, and inside the top three in nearly nine out of ten. That beats standalone Exomiser, standalone DeepRare, and prompted LLM reranking on Exomiser's top-twenty candidate list, evaluated head-to-head. The clinical relevance test is the Recall at three number. In a real interpretation workflow a geneticist reviews the top handful of candidates, and getting the right answer into that top three in eighty-eight percent of solved cases is the kind of recall that changes how much manual work the second-look phase actually requires.

The Su Lab read. Three things.

First, the architectural pattern is the right one, and it lines up exactly with the deterministic-orchestrator argument we talked about yesterday on biomeStat. The agent is not the source of truth. The truth lives in the evidence engine's calibrated scores, in ClinGen and ClinVar and HGMD assertions, in the Human Phenotype Ontology graph paths, and in AlphaFold structures. The agent's job is structured review and synthesis — reorder, justify, write — over a workspace where every cell is provenanced. That keeps the hallucination surface bounded to the reranking and writing layer rather than letting the LLM invent variant pathogenicity from the latent space.

Second, the knowledge-guided graph attention with learned per-source trust coefficients is the bit of mechanism design that should land hardest on this audience. You can read it as the system explicitly learning how much to weight ClinVar versus HGMD versus AlphaMissense versus the HPO path on a per-prediction basis, rather than baking those weights into hand-coded rules the way Exomiser does. That is exactly the right way for a knowledge-graph-grounded biomedical model to handle source heterogeneity. And it's the kind of thing that lets you swap in a better predictor or a richer ontology later without rewriting the scoring logic.

Third, the caveats. This is single-institution. The 18,622-case cohort is one fetal-and-infant cohort, the held-out test is a 549-proband internal split, and the authors explicitly call out that prospective workflow validation across sequencing pipelines, hospitals, and ancestry groups has not been done. The benchmark also evaluates against Exomiser's top-twenty candidate pool, so any case where Exomiser drops the truth out of the top twenty becomes invisible to the comparison — DeepBD doesn't rescue Exomiser's recall failures at the front of the funnel, only its ranking failures inside the top twenty. The right read of the number is that DeepBD is a method paper benchmarked retrospectively, not a deployed clinical tool. The architectural lesson generalizes. The specific Recall-at-one figure needs prospective replication.

The deeper signal is that two papers in two days have now argued the same thing from two different angles. biomeStat said the agent should drive deterministic gold-standard tools without inventing biology. DeepBD says the agent should reorder and synthesize over a calibrated evidence engine without overwriting it. Different domains, same architectural pattern — keep the LLM out of the inference path, put it in the orchestration and review path. That convergence is worth noticing.
