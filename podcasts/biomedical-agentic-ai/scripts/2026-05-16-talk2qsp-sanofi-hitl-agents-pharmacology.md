# Talk2QSP — Sanofi Wires Human-in-the-Loop Agents Into Quantitative Systems Pharmacology

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.06.723244v2

## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI. Today's nugget comes from inside Sanofi, which is a useful change of pace, because most of what we cover on this feed is academic preprints with hosted demos and good intentions. This one is a pharma-grade agentic system, written up by Tommaso Andreani's group at Sanofi and posted to bioRxiv on the 15th in a substantive version 2 revision. The paper is called Talk2QSP. The setup, the architecture, and the framing are all worth working through.

First, what is QSP. Quantitative Systems Pharmacology is the part of computational pharmacology that builds ordinary differential equation models of how a drug moves through the body and engages its targets. These are mechanistic, executable models — pharma companies use them to plan dose-finding studies, to design clinical trials, to compare candidates in silico before committing to expensive in vivo work, and increasingly to replace certain clinical experiments outright. The models are real research infrastructure. And the deployment bottleneck, the authors argue, is not the modeling itself — it's the translation step. A clinical or preclinical scenario lives in a paper as free text. To run that scenario through an existing QSP model, somebody has to read the paper, work out which model entities are being manipulated, convert dose-and-units phrasings into parameter changes, identify the right intervention window, and then translate all of that into simulation-ready inputs. That's a substantial expert task, and it's the part that doesn't scale.

So Talk2QSP is an agent-based framework that automates that translation. The pitch is to turn QSP from a slow, expert-bottlenecked modeling shop into what the authors call an experimental planning engine — point it at literature, and it derives executable scenarios. The interesting design choice is what they don't do. They do not hand the whole problem to a single reasoning LLM and ask it to think about SBML model structure end to end. They tried that. It failed unreliably. Instead, they decompose the task. There's a semantic grounding layer that resolves natural-language references to model entities — the proteins, compartments, and parameters in the underlying ODE model. Then a Scenario Extractor, driven by a large language model, reads the literature passage and produces a structured description of what intervention is being asked for. Then a dual-agent Scenario Mapper takes that structured description and converts it into precise parameter configurations, broken into what they call discrete verifiable work orders rather than one opaque single-shot output.

That phrase — discrete verifiable work orders — is the design principle that ought to travel. The agentic-biology literature is full of pipelines where an LLM is asked to do a long chain of reasoning over some complex biological substrate and emit an answer. The reliability story for those pipelines is hard. What Talk2QSP does instead is break the work into small steps where each step's output is a checkable, structured artifact — a parameter change in canonical form, a unit conversion with the conversion factor explicit, a no-op declaration that this scenario doesn't actually change the model. Each piece can be inspected and verified before the simulation runs. It's the equivalent of moving from a long chain of thought to a sequence of small typed function calls.

The other design choice worth naming is the Human-in-the-Loop strategy. When the agents hit a genuine biological ambiguity — a literature description that could plausibly map to multiple model interventions — the system does not guess. It surfaces the ambiguity to a domain expert, lets the modeler resolve it interactively, and proceeds. The framing here is genuinely useful, because the agentic-AI conversation has been pulled toward a binary — either the agent is autonomous and replaces the expert, or it's not really agentic. Talk2QSP picks neither pole. It runs end to end where it can, and when it can't, it asks. The interactive resolution becomes part of the trajectory rather than a failure mode.

The evaluation is on the small side but reads as honest. Four kinetic ODE and QSP models, seven scenarios curated by subject matter experts at Sanofi from literature descriptions, covering multi-dose interventions, unit conversions, no-op scenarios where the system has to correctly recognize that the described change doesn't apply to the model, and ambiguity-triggered cases that exercise the human-in-the-loop path. The system resolves all seven correctly. The authors are explicit that single-shot reasoning over the raw SBML by the same underlying language model handles these cases unreliably — that's the comparison point that makes the decomposed-agent architecture earn its keep.

Two things to take from this one. One is the design pattern. If you're building agents that operate over a structured biological model — metabolic, signaling, pharmacological, anything with executable semantics — Talk2QSP is the template to study. Semantic grounding upfront, decompose the task into verifiable work orders, hand the math to deterministic infrastructure underneath, surface ambiguity to a human instead of confabulating. The other is the deployment signal. Sanofi shipping this kind of system from the inside is the part of the agentic-pharma conversation we hear less of than the press-release announcements. The framing — agents as scenario translators that feed deterministic models, not autonomous decision makers — is the version of agentic pharma that's actually load-bearing right now.
