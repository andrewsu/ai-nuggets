# scBench-Long — single-cell biology agents pass one task in four, and fail the same four ways

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.26563

## Script

Today's pick is a brand-new arXiv preprint out of LatchBio, called scBench-Long. The full title is "Verifiable Benchmarking of Long-Horizon Single-Cell Biology", and it's the latest entry in a series this group has been running — first SpatialBench, then scBench, and now scBench-Long. The reason I'm pulling it for the show is that I think it's the cleanest piece of work I've seen on what it actually means to evaluate an agent doing real single-cell biology, end to end, against a deterministic ground truth. And the headline numbers, plus the named failure-mode taxonomy, are both more sobering and more useful than the usual benchmark write-up.

Setup. There's been a wave of "AI for biology" benchmarks this past year — LAB-Bench, BixBench, BioMnistery, GeneBench, the various agentic biology evals — and most of them measure either broad biology knowledge, isolated tool execution, or scripted analysis steps. None of them really test whether an agent can take raw or near-raw single-cell data and recover a complex scientific claim, which is the thing a working computational biologist actually does. scBench-Long's bet is that the right test is a long-horizon one: stage the experimental context exactly as a scientist would see it, hand the agent the raw matrices and the auxiliary evidence files, ask one compact scientific question, and grade only whether the final structured answer is correct, deterministically, over a controlled vocabulary.

What's in the benchmark. Twenty-one evaluations grouped under five study systems. Melanoma CD8 T-cell tumor reactivity from paired single-cell RNA and TCR sequencing. CD8 multiome regulatory inference from paired RNA and ATAC. Human-monkey chimeric embryo development from Smart-seq2 cross-species transcriptomes. Age-dependent KRAS-driven lung tumorigenesis from combinatorial single-cell RNA. And lethal COVID-19 lung pathology from single-nucleus RNA. Each cluster of tasks revisits the same experimental system with different scientific questions — about cell identity, donor composition, regulator direction, clonotype, ligand-receptor interpretation, mechanism — which mirrors how a real single-cell dataset actually gets interrogated in practice. The evidence layers that each task may require include immune-repertoire data, chromatin accessibility, ortholog maps, ligand-receptor databases, developmental references, and orthogonal validation experiments. So it's not "answer this multiple-choice gene question." It's "here are the raw outputs of a real experiment, plus the auxiliary resources you'd actually need; recover the scientific conclusion."

How the benchmark was built — and this matters. Paper claims from the source studies are used as candidate evaluations, not as automatic ground truth. A task gets included only after the authors independently reproduce the target conclusion from the same data the agent will be given, then harden it through review, distractor design, and inspection of trajectories from multiple model families. The grading is deterministic pass/fail over a controlled answer surface — typed JSON, controlled vocabularies, hard-fail conditions for biologically incompatible answers, and distractors specifically chosen to separate data-supported conclusions from common shortcuts. They also run an LLM-judge rubric over trajectories as a companion diagnostic, but they explicitly refuse to use rubric scores as the benchmark number. The benchmark number is the deterministic endpoint grade. That decision is correct, and it's why this benchmark is worth taking seriously.

The numbers. They ran 17 model-harness pairs — Claude, Gemini, OpenAI GPT, Grok, Kimi, and GLM, across the PI, Claude Code, and OpenAI Codex harnesses — three replicates per evaluation where runs completed, for 1,068 total trajectories. The top pair was Claude Opus 4.8 with Claude Code, at 16 passes out of 63 runs — that's a 25.4 percent pass rate. Gemini 3.5 Flash with PI came in second at 22.2 percent. But the more honest number is the task-level robustness. The top pair passed at least one replicate on 8 of 21 evaluations, a majority of replicates on 6 of 21, and all three replicates on only 2 of 21. Five of the 21 evaluations had zero passing trajectories across every model and every harness. So the frontier today is: agents can sometimes get the right answer on a complex single-cell analysis, but they rarely do it reliably, and there's a hard subset of tasks where they essentially never succeed.

The failure modes — and this is the contribution I think will outlive the leaderboard. The authors identify four recurring ways that long-horizon single-cell agents fail, and every one of them is bio-specific, not a generic tool-use bug.

First, familiar biological priors masking task-specific evidence. The melanoma CD8 task asks the agent to identify the tumor-reactive T-cell population, where reactivity is defined by physical tumor-T-cell engagement combined with paired TCR clonal expansion. Agents routinely default to the literature-canonical CD39-high, PD-1-high, TOX-high exhausted-phenotype signature, even after running the right analysis steps that would have shown the relevant signal lives elsewhere. They knew the textbook answer, so they submitted the textbook answer. In one task the authors deliberately swapped the singlet and tumor-cluster metadata labels while leaving the expression matrix and TCR data untouched. Many models either silently relabeled the result back to the expected biology, or partially followed the data and then overgeneralized to the prior. The agent could see that the data didn't support the prior, and it picked the prior anyway.

Second, raw abundance mistaken for biological importance. The chimeric-embryo task asks whether ligand-receptor communication between human and monkey epiblast cells supports a single dominant signaling family, after accounting for direction and database composition. Agents routinely picked the largest raw interaction class as the answer. The data, in fact, did not show any single family dominating once you corrected for database imbalance and bidirectionality. Two of fifty trajectories passed. GPT-5.5 and Opus 4.8 went zero for six across their paired harnesses on this one.

Third, association mistaken for mechanism. The tumor-conjugate task gives the agent malignant cells sorted from physical tumor-T-cell conjugates, plus matched malignant singlets. The data are cross-sectional, sampled after physical pairing — so any program enriched in the conjugate population could be tumor-intrinsic, induced by contact, selected for by the sort, or even bleed-through from the co-sorted T cell. The right answer requires distinguishing those, and acknowledging which programs the data can and can't direction-resolve. Agents reliably treated enrichment in the conjugate population as direct evidence of a directional causal mechanism. No model-harness pair passed this evaluation.

Fourth, failing to integrate multiple modalities. The CD8 multiome tasks require recognizing regulatory patterns across paired RNA and chromatin accessibility. Models often followed RNA differential expression alone and nominated genes that the enhancer data didn't support. In one case a model recovered that KLF2 was acting as a repressor — but it inferred that from KLF2's own RNA expression, not from the coordinated behavior of its targets in the multiome data. The right answer required reading two modalities together. The model read one.

The Su Lab read. Three things.

First, this benchmark is the right reply to the past month's stream of papers — including the ones we've covered this week — that demonstrate agentic biomedical AI working on a single curated task. It's easy to ship a paper where an agent does one thing well. It's much harder to ship a benchmark where the agent has to recover a real scientific conclusion that needed donor-aware reasoning, immune-repertoire integration, chromatin support, cross-species orthology, and orthogonal validation — and to grade only the endpoint. scBench-Long does that, and the result is that even the best current systems are passing roughly a quarter of attempts and only nailing two out of twenty-one tasks reliably. That's the actual frontier, and it's important to anchor to it rather than to the SOTA result of any individual demonstration paper.

Second, the four failure modes are an extremely useful checklist for anyone wiring up a biomedical agent right now. They're not "the agent picked the wrong tool" or "the agent's context window overflowed." They're "the agent substituted a literature prior for the evidence", "treated a large number as a meaningful number", "called an association a mechanism", and "ignored a modality." Those four failure modes recur in non-agent computational biology too. They're a recognizable taxonomy of how a hurried analyst makes claims the data don't support. The point is that the agents inherit them — which means the way you wire your agent has to actively defend against them, the same way you'd teach a new graduate student to defend against them. Force the agent to consult both modalities. Force it to enumerate what direction the data can and can't resolve. Force it to check against orthogonal validation evidence before submitting. The benchmark is essentially saying: these are the cells where your agent will lose recall, and you should design accordingly.

Third, a note on the methodology that should land especially hard on this audience. The authors use published paper claims as candidate evaluations but explicitly refuse to treat them as automatic ground truth. A task only enters the benchmark if they reproduce the target conclusion themselves from the same data the agent will be given. That's the right call given the literature's reproducibility track record, and it's the right call given that we want benchmarks whose graded answers are actually defensible. It also means scBench-Long has a finite scaling property that future versions are going to have to grapple with — every new task requires real reproduction work. But the design is correct, and it matches the general pattern across the high-quality biology benchmarks of the past year: claim-conditioned, deterministic-endpoint, reproducible.

One last note, and it's a fun one. The strongest model-harness combination on the benchmark is Claude Opus 4.8 with Claude Code — which is, as it happens, the very setup writing this script for me. The win there is narrow — 16 of 63 versus Gemini 3.5 Flash with PI at 14 of 63 — and the authors point out that harness choice changes the picture: Opus 4.8 is stronger with Claude Code than with PI, but the same models can flip ordering under different harnesses. Read that as the headline lesson on system design, not on model ranking. Long-horizon biological agents are evaluated as model-plus-harness pairs, not as models alone. The harness is part of the agent.

That's it for today. The deeper signal is that the field now has a credible verifiable benchmark for end-to-end single-cell biology, with a 25 percent ceiling and a four-named-failure-mode taxonomy that any working biomedical-agent team should be reading. Talk to you tomorrow.
