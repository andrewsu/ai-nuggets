# NAIS — a governed AI scientist runs a real hospital GWAS, and phenotype design is where the agent earns its keep

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.11084

## Script

Today's pick is NAIS — the NVIDIA A-I Technology Center A-I Scientist — an arXiv paper posted on July thirteenth from NVIDIA's A-I Technology Center and China Medical University Hospital in Taichung, Taiwan. It is one of the first agentic-A-I systems built to sit inside a real hospital, run a real genomics study on protected patient data, and not touch a single row of that data directly.

Framing. The last year of A-I-scientist papers has been dominated by systems that impress on benchmarks and public datasets. Co-Scientist, Kosmos, the original A-I Scientist, AutoScientists, Feynman — the pattern is a language model in some sandbox with unlimited access to open corpora, generating hypotheses, writing code, drafting papers. That is not what a research hospital can deploy. Hospitals have protected health information, they have institutional review boards, they have data warehouses that must not become exfiltration channels. Handing a frontier model raw access to an E-H-R database is not a scientific problem, it is a compliance non-starter. NAIS is what an agentic A-I scientist looks like when you write the governance layer first and design the reasoning layer to fit inside it.

Architecture. There are three components. First, an optional proposal-readiness pipeline that ingests a research proposal PDF, retrieves related work, evaluates it, and rewrites it into the institution's template. Second, NemoClaw, the agent, running on a Nemotron-3 Super model — a hundred and twenty billion parameter reasoner deployed on-premise. Third, and this is the load-bearing piece, a broker service. The agent never touches protected data. The agent drafts an analysis specification in natural language, submits it to the broker, waits for a human to approve, and then the broker materializes the cohort inside a locked-down Kubernetes environment, launches PLINK 2 in an isolated container, and returns only aggregate summaries — quality-control metrics, allele frequency tables, Manhattan plots — through a manifest U-R-L. Raw genotypes and identifiable patient records stay in the vault. External network access is blocked by default; the agent has to ask for specific outbound connections. This is not "agent with permissions"; it is "agent without access, brokered through a compliance boundary."

Case study. The primary validation is a hypertension genome-wide association study on two hundred eighty-six thousand four hundred and twenty-two individuals in the hospital's genomic biobank. The team interacts with NemoClaw through a Telegram workspace. Natural-language goal in, analysis specification out, approval, brokered execution. The agent drafts the S-Q-L cohort extraction, orchestrates the PLINK 2 logistic regression with age, sex, and the first ten principal components as covariates, and drafts the methods and results sections. On the final Manhattan plot, five genome-wide-significant loci at hypertension canonicals — F-G-F-5, A-T-P-2-B-1, C-N-N-M-2, F-T-O, and G-R-B-1-4. F-G-F-5 hits negative log ten p around seventy. As a secondary demonstration, a drug-induced liver injury workflow on the same platform reaches a multimodal graph-neural-network A-U-C of zero point eight four two after the agent iteratively fuses molecular graphs, Morgan fingerprints, physicochemical descriptors, and DiffDock binding scores.

The interesting story. This paper's most important contribution is not the loci. Those are known. The contribution is where the human-A-I loop actually earned its keep. The agent's first pass at defining hypertension used laboratory blood-pressure thresholds — anyone above one-forty-over-ninety is a case. The clinical genomics team defined cases using I-C-D-ten diagnosis codes and antihypertensive medication prescriptions. Those two definitions disagreed on three thousand nine hundred and fifty subjects. So the team ran the discordance analysis. Of those three thousand nine hundred and fifty subjects the agent had called hypertensive but the expert phenotype had called normotensive, only one hundred and twenty-five had any antihypertensive prescription on record. The other three thousand eight hundred and twenty-five had elevated pressure at a lab visit but no diagnosis, no drug — patients whose blood pressure spiked on the day of the draw. The agent-labeled cases were mostly noise. Team-directed a rerun with the reconciled phenotype, and the GWAS reproduced the expert result. The paper's line is worth quoting — PLINK 2 execution is commodity, deciding whether hypertension means I-C-D codes or medications or lab thresholds or their combination is not. That is where the agent value lives.

There is also a small anecdote in the methods that I loved. An earlier version of the phenotype file encoded cases and controls as zero and one. PLINK 2 interprets zero as a missing value, so it silently excluded every sample from the analysis. The agent noticed and switched to one and two. The failure mode is small; the fact that a hundred-and-twenty-billion-parameter agent had to trip over the same footgun a graduate student trips over is a healthy reminder about where the current generation of research agents actually is.

Editorial reads. First, this is the "governed execution" niche for A-I scientists, and it is going to be a big one. Every other system in this space — Co-Scientist, Kosmos, AutoScientists — either operates on public data or doesn't publish its governance story. NAIS makes the compliance layer the frontier claim. That framing is going to matter more, not less, over the next two years, and hospitals will adopt the systems that treat privacy as a design axis rather than an afterthought.

Second, the paper is disciplined about what it did not do. It did not discover new loci. It replicated known ones. It did not run in fully autonomous mode. Every S-Q-L plan and every analysis launch required team approval. The N-A-I-S authors call this "scientist-in-the-loop," and their line is that as foundation models get smarter, the binding constraint on autonomous research shifts from language-model reasoning to A-I research infrastructure — persistent broker state, containerized execution, workflow orchestration, reproducible environments. Infrastructure, not reasoning, is where the next few years of A-I scientist progress will be built.

Third caveat. Single institution, small drug-induced-liver-injury dataset, and the "expert-comparable" claim is a replication claim, not a discovery claim. There is no reported case where NAIS surfaced a novel signal the human team had not already found. That is fine for a first paper; it is not the final story. The interesting follow-ups are what happens when a governed agent is turned loose on a phenotype the domain experts have not curated yet, and whether the phenotype-design-versus-P-L-I-N-K-2-execution asymmetry generalizes beyond hypertension.
