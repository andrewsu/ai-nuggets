# BioDesignBench — frontier LLM agents pick the right protein design tools but evaluate them shallowly; forced multi-metric depth closes most of the gap to the human expert

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.06.723381v2

## Script

Today's paper is on BioDesignBench from the Romero lab at Duke, which is the cleanest verdict yet on frontier LLM agents in protein design — and it lands on the same behavioral picture scBench-Long drew for single-cell biology agents three days ago. The strongest agents now beat a deterministic hardcoded pipeline but still trail the human expert; the gap is not which tools they call, but how shallowly they evaluate what those tools produce. Across all eight hundred and thirty-six task-condition observations the agents ran, not one ever discarded a generated candidate. A prompting intervention that forces multi-metric evaluation closes most of the gap to the expert, which says the deficit is behavioral, not capability-limited.

Setup. There has been a wave of demonstrations of LLM agents driving protein design pipelines — natural-language task in, designed protein out, with the agent orchestrating tools like RFdiffusion, ProteinMPNN, AlphaFold, and Rosetta along the way. The demonstrations are real. What the field has been missing is a principled way to ask not just "did it work" but "how did it work" — which tools the agent reached for, in what order, how many candidates it generated, how thoroughly it evaluated them before submitting. That is the question this paper sets up to answer.

What they built. BioDesignBench is seventy-six expert-curated protein design tasks across five molecular subjects — antibodies, enzymes, miniprotein binders, scaffolds, and fluorescent proteins — split between de novo design and redesign. Every task is drawn from publications dated after every evaluated model's training cutoff, and there are twelve decoy tasks with fabricated targets to catch contamination. The agent gets the tools through a single Model Context Protocol surface that exposes seventeen tools in four categories — generative, structure prediction, physics-based scoring, and analysis. Two presentation modes are compared. Unguided mode lists atomic tools with no orchestration hints. Guided mode groups tools by function and packages common sub-pipelines as composite workflows. Same backends, different metadata.

The scoring is a hundred-point rubric across six components — Approach, Orchestration, Quality, Feasibility, Novelty, and Diversity. Where there are reliable quantitative proxies, scoring is algorithmic. Quality is thirty-five points and comes entirely from Boltz-2 re-prediction metrics — so the part of the score that depends on the protein actually being good is grounded in re-running structure prediction, not on an LLM judge's opinion. The subjective dimensions — strategic appropriateness of tool choice, adaptiveness of the orchestration — go to a panel of LLM judges, with the evaluated agent's own model family excluded to prevent self-preference bias.

Four frontier agents — DeepSeek V3, GPT-5, Claude Sonnet four-point-five, Gemini two-point-five Pro — each under both modes. Three mode-agnostic baselines anchor the scale: a deterministic hardcoded pipeline at fifty-four-point-five, a human expert using the same seventeen tools at sixty-one-point-seven, and a human oracle executing the expert-curated optimal tool sequence at seventy-five-point-two.

The headline numbers. Two of the four LLMs beat the hardcoded pipeline in both modes. DeepSeek V3 in unguided mode scored sixty-point-six. GPT-5 unguided fifty-five-point-eight. Sonnet four-point-five sat below the hardcoded baseline in both modes. Gemini two-point-five Pro scored under nine — a complete failure mode, not a quality problem. The paper diagnoses Gemini's failure as a systematic inability to invoke the tools through the MCP interface, which is worth noting separately because it isn't poor scientific reasoning; it is the tool-calling layer itself breaking. The other three agents all called tools just fine. None of them reached the human expert.

Now the core result. The authors decompose tool use into two things. Coverage — do you invoke the right stages of the pipeline at all. And evaluation depth — once you've generated a candidate, how thoroughly do you evaluate it before submitting. Guided mode improved coverage for everyone. Guided mode did almost nothing for depth. Even when an agent picked the right workflow, it generated a few candidates, scored them once, and submitted. The asymmetry is sharp when tool calls are broken down by pipeline stage. At backbone generation, agents match or exceed the human expert. At scoring and evaluation — physics-based, interface, stability — agents call those tools at about fourteen percent of expert intensity.

And the most striking single statistic in the paper. Across all eight hundred and thirty-six task-condition observations, no LLM condition ever discarded a generated candidate. Not once. Whatever came out of the stochastic generative tool got submitted. The agent treated a sample from a stochastic process as if it were a deterministic answer.

That observation is the explanatory engine of the paper. Correlationally, the number of distinct evaluation metric categories applied per candidate predicts total score with a Spearman rho of zero point six eight across eight hundred and thirty-six observations. The relationship holds when the human expert is excluded, which rules out the expert as a leverage point. Shallow evaluation, not poor tool selection, is the dominant gap.

The intervention. On a stratified eighteen-task subset they ran three conditions per task. Baseline. Forced-depth — instruct the agent to generate multiple candidates, evaluate each across multiple complementary metrics, rank, submit only the top performers. And a compute-matched low-variety control where the agent did roughly the same amount of evaluation overall but using only a narrow range of metrics — same tool-call budget, same wall-clock, same token cost as forced-depth, but the evaluation is shallow in variety, not in volume.

The result is clean. DeepSeek V3 went from fifty-eight-point-seven to sixty-eight-point-one — plus nine-point-three points. GPT-5 went from forty-six-point-eight to sixty-two-point-seven — plus fifteen-point-nine points. Both Wilcoxon-significant. Improvements distributed across most tasks, not concentrated on a few. The gains land in Approach and Orchestration, not in structural Quality — meaning the intervention changed how the agent used the pipeline, not the quality of the underlying tools.

The compute-matched control is the key piece. For DeepSeek V3, low-variety scored about the same as baseline despite using nearly identical tool calls, tokens, and wall-clock to forced-depth. The gain isn't more evaluation. It is broader evaluation across complementary metrics before selection. And the hardcoded pipeline — which already has multi-metric evaluation baked into its fixed workflow — gets no benefit from the same intervention. The intervention is remediating a behavior that exists in LLM agents and is absent in systems with hardcoded evaluation loops.

Now the editorial reads. This one matters.

First. The strongest LLM agents already match the underlying tools' ceiling on Quality once they execute a valid pipeline. The bottleneck is not whether they can call AlphaFold or RFdiffusion. The bottleneck is that they call the scoring tools too few times and never throw a candidate away. That is a much more tractable problem than "improve the model." The fact that a prompting intervention closes most of the gap to the expert — fifteen-point-nine points for GPT-5, on a benchmark where the expert sits at sixty-one-point-seven — says the deficit is behavioral, not capability-limited. Agent scaffolding, not better models, is the next frontier for tool-driven scientific workflows that rest on stochastic generative tools.

Second. The two-axis decomposition — coverage versus depth — is the contribution that travels furthest. Coverage is what guided MCP modes, tool documentation, and orchestration hints fix. Depth is what nothing on the metadata side fixes. If you are wiring a biomedical agent right now and your strategy is "write better tool descriptions, hint at composite workflows" — that buys you coverage. It does not buy you depth. Depth requires you to actively structure the agent to generate multiple candidates, score them on orthogonal metrics, and discard the losers. That is a scaffolding choice, not a documentation choice.

Third. The "never discarded a candidate" statistic is the most diagnostic single observation in the paper, and it is a behavior pattern that almost certainly generalizes well beyond protein design. Drug discovery agents, single-cell agents, molecular dynamics agents — anywhere the underlying tool is stochastic and the right move is generate many, score across complementary metrics, keep the best — the failure mode in scBench-Long where agents take the largest raw ligand-receptor abundance as the answer, the failure mode here where agents submit the first stochastic backbone they generated, the failure mode in the multiple recent benchmark papers where agents stop exploring after one or two iterations — they are all the same mode. Trained to produce a concise, high-probability answer in a few interaction steps. Deployed against a workflow where the right answer is a population to be filtered, not a single sample to be reported. The mismatch is structural and recognizable.

Fourth. The forced-depth result moves agents along the same evaluation-variety-versus-score relationship the correlational analysis identified, not off-axis. That is a real demonstration that the lever they identified is the operative one — not just an arbitrary prompt change that happened to help. The compute-matched low-variety control is what gives that claim weight; without it, "more eval improves performance" reads as either tautological or "more compute helps." With it, the claim sharpens to "broader, multi-metric evaluation under fixed compute improves performance" — which is the actionable form.

Caveats. The intervention experiments are on an eighteen-task subset, not the full seventy-six. A single human expert anchors the baseline. Everything is in silico — Boltz-2 re-prediction and Rosetta scoring, no wet-lab characterization. The authors are clear on all of this. The architectural claim is more general than any one of the experiments; the experiments exist to make the architectural claim concrete.

The take. Two papers in one week — scBench-Long and now BioDesignBench — converge on the same behavioral picture for biomedical agents wrapping stochastic computational tools. Agents pick reasonable tools. Agents fail to explore the output space those tools generate. The remediation is not bigger models. The remediation is scaffolding the agent to do the generate-evaluate-filter loop the field of computational biology has been doing on stochastic tools for a decade. The forced-depth intervention here is the cleanest demonstration so far that this is a behavior, not a capability, and that prompting alone can move the needle by ten to sixteen points on a hard benchmark. If you are building an agentic biomedical tool today, the design lesson is to bake the generate-evaluate-filter loop into the agent's scaffold rather than trusting the model to discover it on its own. That is the actionable read.
