# GeneBench-Pro — a 129-problem agentic benchmark for genomics and translational biomedicine exposes the notice-act gap in frontier models

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.29.735386v3

## Script

Today's pick is GeneBench-Pro, a bioRxiv preprint from OpenAI posted July second — an expanded and significantly hardened successor to GeneBench, the agentic-genomics benchmark that showed up in the funnel a couple months ago. The GeneBench-Pro suite now runs a hundred and twenty-nine multi-stage scientific analysis problems across ten primary domains — population genetics, statistical and quantitative genetics, regulatory and molecular omics, clinical genetics, pharmacogenomics, somatic cancer genomics and liquid biopsy, functional perturbation, proteomics, microbial genomics, forensic genetics — with a genomics-centered core and adjacent coverage into translational biomedicine.

The setup is worth understanding, because the design choices are what make the benchmark load-bearing. Each problem drops an agent into a sandboxed workspace with a minimum viable prompt, a set of realistically messy staged data files, and a standard scientific Python stack plus common genomics tooling. The prompt states a scientific question and a target estimand — the quantity to be estimated — but says almost nothing about how to get there. The agent has to figure out what analysis path the data can support, choose the estimator, execute it, and emit a single JSON answer for deterministic grading.

The key methodological move is that the data is not real. It is constructively simulated end to end, so the authors know the full causal structure and the exact answer that the correct workflow would recover. The reason is subtle but important. If you build a benchmark on top of real historical datasets, at every step in a multi-stage analysis there is usually a defensible alternative choice that a competent analyst might make, and the benchmark ends up grading agents against the specific path the designer happened to pick. That leads to a decaying pass rate as chain length grows, for reasons that have nothing to do with the agent's actual scientific reasoning. Simulation lets the authors tune the data-generating process so that the endpoint is genuinely identifiable, plausible wrong analyses fail for substantive reasons, and small researcher-choice variation does not flip the graded answer. That is the whole game — a benchmark that punishes real scientific failure and not the exercise of judgment.

The other key move is external review. Eighty-two of the hundred and twenty-nine problems were sent to a pool of eleven domain experts — graduate students, postdocs, industry scientists, professors — with full access to the design rationale, the data-generating process, and the analysis walkthrough, and asked to look for hidden ambiguity in the estimand, errors in the reference implementation, or contrived setups. Ten of the reviewed problems are open-sourced on Hugging Face. Fifty go to Artificial Analysis for third-party model benchmarking. The remaining sixty-nine are held out internally to reduce contamination.

Now the numbers. Sixty model configurations were evaluated. The best mainline row, GPT-5.6 Sol at max reasoning, hits an eval-level pass rate of twenty-eight point seven percent. The separately reported Pro variant reaches thirty-one point five. GPT-5.5 lands at twelve, GPT-5.2 at five. The strongest non-GPT baseline is Claude Opus four point eight, at sixteen. Reasoning level matters a lot within a family — GPT-5.6 Sol rises from three point seven with no reasoning to twenty-eight point seven at max. And the unsolved tail is substantial. Across the best mainline row, the fraction of problems that clear a fifty-percent pass rate only reaches thirty percent, and forty-six percent never pass any run. This is not a saturated benchmark. It is measuring something frontier agents are visibly getting better at, but nowhere near reliable on.

The most interesting qualitative finding, and the one that generalizes past this specific benchmark, is what the authors call the notice-act gap. When you read the model traces on failed problems, the agent very often does notice the diagnostic signal that would have led to the correct analysis path. It sees the QC anomaly, spots the ascertainment issue, flags the outlier — and then it treats that observation as a local data-cleaning matter rather than as evidence that should change the downstream statistical method. The stronger models are not necessarily better at noticing. They are better at propagating what they noticed forward into a different estimator or a different pipeline. Weaker models identify the same local signal but persist on the initially plausible but wrong analysis path. That expert-versus-novice pattern is a real thing in the human cognitive-science literature, and seeing it show up cleanly in frontier-model traces is diagnostic. If you are building agents for this class of work, the bottleneck is not knowledge and not local pattern recognition. It is the ability to let a diagnostic observation actually restructure the plan.

Why this matters for a computational biologist who does this work every day. Two things. First, the authors quote the concrete labor economics: a typical GeneBench-Pro problem takes a human expert on the order of ten to forty hours end to end, which at conservative rates is a few thousand dollars of labor per problem. Even partial reliable automation of this class of analysis, if it closes the inferential loop, is not a marginal productivity gain — it materially changes the throughput of target follow-up, hypothesis triage, and the iteration cycle between data generation and decision-making. Second, this is the direction of travel for a serious biomedical-agentic-AI benchmark. Real, messy staged data. Minimum-viable prompt. Multi-step contingent decisions. Deterministic graded endpoint. External expert review baked in. Tiered release to reduce contamination. This is the shape.

One caveat worth naming. Simulation makes grading interpretable but does not fully reproduce the documentation gaps, the study-specific weirdness, or the scale of a real cohort analysis. And the binary all-or-nothing grading collapses partial progress into failure — an agent that resolves five of six decision points but blows the last one scores the same as one that fails on step one. That mirrors the real-world consequence of a wrong final answer, but it does hide useful stage-level signal.

The take. GeneBench-Pro is the current best measurement of the multi-stage scientific reasoning that genomics-and-translational-biomedicine analysts actually do, and the gap between best-model performance and useful reliability is still large. The notice-act framing is the concrete lens to bring to your own agent traces — when a run fails, ask whether the model saw the diagnostic signal and failed to propagate it, or genuinely missed it. Those two failure modes need different fixes.
