# Nekrutenko Penn State — A Free Open-Weight Implementer Matches Frontier Opus on Agentic Variant Calling

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.13.724985v1

## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI. Today's paper posted to bioRxiv yesterday from Anton Nekrutenko at Penn State — the Galaxy project, for listeners who place the name there — and it is, I think, the most empirically grounded answer I have seen this year to a question every compbio group is quietly wrestling with right now. The question is this. If you are going to wire up an agent that plans a biomedical analysis, calls tools, executes code, inspects results, and iterates, do you actually need to be hitting a frontier model on every single step? Or is the planning side the only place where frontier reasoning earns its keep, and can the repetitive execution side be handed off to something free and local without losing any accuracy?

The setup is unusually clean. Nekrutenko fixes the planner role to Claude's Opus and uses it to author plans of increasing detail for a per-sample variant-calling workflow — a workhorse bioinformatics task that almost every genomics lab runs constantly and that has a well-defined notion of correctness, because you can compare the called variants against a truth set. The plans range from terse, one-paragraph descriptions to fully spelled-out, step-by-step recipes. Those plans then get handed to six open-weight implementer language models, all released in 2026, running locally on desktop hardware. The implementer is the part of the loop that takes the plan, writes the commands, runs them, reads the output, fixes its own errors, and produces the result. The planner stays the same across the whole experiment so the comparison is apples to apples on just the implementer.

The headline number is that the qwen three-point-six twenty-seven-billion-parameter model — qwen three point six, twenty-seven B, written that way in the paper — reproduced frontier accuracy on every plan, at every level of detail. Not approximately. Every plan. Then, to put real pressure on the comparison, Nekrutenko built a thirty-six-cell error-injection matrix — six categories of induced failure crossed with six perturbation severities — and asked whether the open-weight implementer would recover from each injected fault the same way Opus did when handed the identical broken state. Qwen three-point-six twenty-seven-B matched Opus cell-for-cell across all thirty-six cells. Same failure modes, same recovery patterns, same successes, no asymmetric blind spots. And the hardware to run the implementer side cost under two thousand dollars — either a Jetson box or an Apple Mac Mini was enough.

The reason this matters, and the reason I think this paper deserves a real read rather than a glance, is the economic structure it implies for agentic biomedical analysis. The per-call inference cost on a frontier model adds up very fast when you are letting an agent iterate freely against tool outputs — every retry, every error inspection, every minor command tweak is another paid call. If the implementer steps are the loop-heavy part, and the implementer steps can be run for free on a thousand-dollar box that lives under your desk, then the planner-frontier and implementer-local split is not a nice-to-have, it is the obvious architecture. You pay frontier prices on the small number of high-leverage planning calls. The thousands of execute-inspect-retry cycles that follow run on metal you already own. The Galaxy lineage of the lab, which has been thinking about routine bioinformatics workflows at scale longer than almost anyone, makes the choice of variant calling as the test workload a serious one rather than an arbitrary one.

A few things to flag honestly. The first is that the test workload is one workflow — admittedly an important one — and not, for example, single-cell analysis, or assembly-graph navigation, or anything involving heavy multi-modal reasoning. Whether the planner-frontier, implementer-local split holds when the implementer steps stop being script-and-tool-driven and start needing genuine reasoning is an open question, and the paper does not claim to have answered it. The second is that Nekrutenko is explicit that the specific open-weight implementer he is recommending today will be superseded in months — the open-weight landscape evolves that fast. To his credit, the artifacts are released as a re-evaluation framework, not as a static result. The plans, the harness, the scoring code, and the per-cell error-injection artifacts are all in his github repository. The point of the paper is the methodology and the architectural lesson, not the specific model name.

The architectural lesson is what I want to leave listeners with. The agentic-AI conversation around biomedical research has, for the last six months, mostly assumed that better agents mean bigger and more expensive models running at every step. This paper says that for a real, useful, repetitive bioinformatics workload, the planner is where the frontier money should go, and the implementer is a commodity. If that generalizes — and I suspect for any tool-heavy, well-specified workflow it will — then the right thing for an academic biomedical lab to do right now is not to budget for another frontier API tier. It is to put a Jetson or a Mac Mini on a shelf, point a local open-weight implementer at it, and let the frontier planner orchestrate from above.

That is a pretty significant cost-curve flip, and the empirical case for it is now on the table. Back tomorrow.
