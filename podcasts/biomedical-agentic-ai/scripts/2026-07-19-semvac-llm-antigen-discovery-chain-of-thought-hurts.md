# SemVac — LLMs read the vaccine literature to nominate antigens, and chain-of-thought reasoning hurts

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.07.13.737696v1

## Script

Today's paper is one I think is actually useful to have in your head if you are shipping any LLM-over-literature workflow. It's called SemVac, out of the Academy of Military Medical Sciences in Beijing together with the National University of Defense Technology in Changsha, posted to bioRxiv on the seventeenth. It benchmarks 14 frontier language models on protective-antigen prediction from PubMed-derived text, and it has one striking, counterintuitive finding that I want to spend real time on.

Let me start with the setup. Reverse vaccinology, as a field, took off in the early 2000s. The idea is to skip growing the pathogen and screen its proteome in silico for antigen candidates using sequence features, transmembrane helices, subcellular localization, that kind of thing. Recent flagship methods, like PLGDL from earlier this year, add protein language models and geometric deep learning on structure. What all of this misses is that a good vaccine antigen isn't just a sequence feature. It's a protein whose biology, its role in adhesion, invasion, toxin secretion, immune evasion, has been characterized over decades of experimental work, and human vaccinologists read that literature and use it. The claim in this paper is that if you feed the same literature to a language model and prompt it to act as an expert immunologist, it can do that reading for you.

The mechanism is deliberately unfancy. For each candidate protein, they run PaperBLAST, which is a sequence search that returns publications where the protein or its homologs are mentioned. That pulls on average 268 papers per protein. They extract text snippets from those papers, drop them into a structured JSON prompt along with homology statistics, and ask the model to return a single floating-point probability that the protein is a protective antigen. No fine-tuning, no bespoke architecture, just prompted retrieval-augmented reasoning.

They then benchmark this on a curated set of 246 bacterial antigens with wet-lab-confirmed protective status. Fourteen models — GPT-5.2 Pro, GPT-5.2, Claude 4.5 Opus, Gemini 3 Pro and Flash, Grok-4 and Grok-4.1 Fast, Qwen 3 Max on the closed side; Kimi K2, DeepSeek V3.2, MiniMax M2.1, Mistral Large 3, Qwen 3.5 Plus, GLM-5 on the open-weight side. Precision is the primary metric, on the reasonable grounds that a false-positive vaccine candidate costs you months of wet-lab time. GPT-5.2 Pro leads at 0.897 precision. Claude 4.5 Opus and GPT-5.2 are second tier around 0.85 to 0.88. The interesting number is Kimi K2, an open-weight model, at 0.838 — beating PLGDL, the specialized protein-language plus geometry pipeline, at 0.798. General-purpose LLMs equipped with structured literature evidence rival or exceed dedicated computational tools without ever being trained for the task.

Now the paranoia check. If the model has an easy time, maybe it is just picking up on the word "vaccine" or "protective" appearing near the protein in the retrieved papers. The authors run the obvious control — strip every paper mentioning those keywords from the input and re-run Kimi K2. Precision drops from 0.838 to 0.836. A rounding error. The model is genuinely integrating properties like localization, immunogenicity, host interaction, and pathogenicity, not skimming for the word "vaccine."

Now the finding I actually want to flag, because it changes how I would design any biology-facing LLM pipeline. They compare each model in two modes — standard inference and explicit reasoning, meaning chain-of-thought, step-by-step deduction, the whole thinking-mode apparatus. The result is unequivocal and it goes the wrong way. Reasoning consistently increases recall — by up to about eight percent — and consistently decreases precision, by as much as seven-and-a-half. That trade goes the wrong way for antigen discovery, where a false positive costs you a wet-lab campaign. Their explanation is that verbose reasoning traces manufacture biologically plausible but unfounded stories — hallucinated mechanisms, speculative pathways, overinterpreted homologies — that shift borderline candidates confidently into the positive class. Biology, they argue, is governed by evolutionary constraint and physicochemical pattern, which is not a chain of deductions; forcing the model to lay one out amplifies its tendency to invent one. Kimi K2, which does not lean on long explicit reasoning traces, wins on precision at a fraction of the cost. If your prompt says "think step by step" on a biology discovery task, you might be optimizing against yourself.

Real-world test. They point SemVac at the 187-protein Mpox proteome. Top candidates include M1R, A35R, A29L, H3L, and L1R, all of which are known validated antigens for orthopoxviruses. Then it flags B20R, a viral TNF-receptor homolog that acts as a secreted decoy for host TNF-alpha — an immune-evasion factor, not a canonical antigen target — and rates it 0.89 based on immune-modulation and structural-exposure evidence in the retrieved papers. The model's own reasoning trace cites the C19L knockout literature in vaccinia, which is a defensible chain of evidence a wet-lab vaccinologist would find worth chasing.

Two takeaways. First, when the retrieval substrate is real — PaperBLAST plus 268 papers per protein — general LLMs match specialized biology models on a real biological task, and an open-weight model at a fraction of the API cost is genuinely competitive. Second, the chain-of-thought pitfall is real and it argues against turning reasoning mode on by default for biology-discovery pipelines that care more about precision than recall. Representation-level reasoning in the weights beats explicit language-level deduction here — an empirical claim, backed by numbers, that the field should stop tacitly assuming the opposite.
