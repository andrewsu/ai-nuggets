## Summary

A bioRxiv preprint posted yesterday from Saman Halgamuge's group at the University of Melbourne — co-authored with Australian epilepsy genetics heavyweights Bahlo, Berkovic, Scheffer, and Gecz — argues that the standing methodological choice in disease gene prioritisation is wrong. Most ML pipelines either lean on sample-level experimental data (gene expression, PPI networks) and overfit to dataset-specific noise, or lean on curated knowledge (ontologies, pathways) and lose gene-level resolution. KIML, Knowledge Inclusive Machine Learning, integrates both within a single pipeline — experimental signal plus PubMed-derived literature representations plus structured biomedical knowledge graphs — and benchmarks the result on Developmental and Epileptic Encephalopathy with a temporal split, then generalises across six additional diseases. It beats existing methods on accuracy, holds up under ontology-enrichment biological evaluation, and produces interpretable per-gene explanations. The framing matters more than the marginal performance gain — this is the Su-Lab thesis written down in a benchmarked paper: structured biomedical knowledge isn't a nice-to-have on top of ML, it's where gene-level discrimination actually comes from.

## Script

Today's nugget is the bioRxiv preprint that posted yesterday afternoon from Saman Halgamuge's group at the University of Melbourne. The paper's title is "Knowledge Inclusive Machine Learning for Disease Gene Prioritisation." Sixteen authors, posted on May second. The corresponding institution is Melbourne. The clinical co-authors include some of the biggest names in epilepsy genetics — Sam Berkovic, Ingrid Scheffer, Jozef Gecz, Melanie Bahlo, Mark Bennett. That co-author list matters because it tells you this is not a methods paper that happens to gesture at a disease. It's a methods paper written together with the people who diagnose the disease.

The framing is the most important thing in this paper, and it's the framing I want this audience to hear. Halgamuge writes that most disease-gene-prioritisation ML pipelines have, for years, made what he calls a false choice. Choice one — train on sample-level experimental data. Gene expression, protein-protein interaction networks, perturbation screens. The advantage is resolution at the level of individual genes. The cost is overfitting to dataset-specific noise and learning correlations that don't reflect biology. Choice two — train on curated knowledge. Ontologies, pathways, structured biomedical knowledge graphs. The advantage is biological grounding. The cost is that ontologies don't discriminate well between similar genes — they tell you a gene is in a process, not whether it's the right gene in that process. The argument of the paper is that both are necessary. The framework, KIML, K-I-M-L, integrates three context streams. Experimental data, the sample-level signal. Literature representations from PubMed embeddings. And structured biomedical knowledge graphs. All in a single pipeline.

I want to flag, before I get into results, that this is not a wholly novel concept. Multi-modal gene prioritisation has been around for a while. KGML, Open Targets' L2G, several knowledge-graph plus expression methods. What is novel here is the explicit framing, the temporal-split evaluation, and — for this audience specifically — the cleanness of the message. The paper's thesis is, quote, neither is sufficient alone, end quote. That's the line.

The benchmark. The team picked Developmental and Epileptic Encephalopathy as the lead disease. DEE is a hard test case for two reasons. First, the clinical phenotype is overlapping and severe — children present early, the genetic causes are heterogeneous, and gene-level precision genuinely changes treatment decisions. Second, the gene list keeps growing. New DEE-associated genes are reported every quarter. That's exactly the situation a temporal split is designed to evaluate. They train on genes known to be associated with DEE before some cutoff date, they test on the genes that were nominated as DEE-associated after that cutoff date. That's the closest you can get to a prospective evaluation in a retrospective benchmark, and it's a much harder evaluation than a random-split cross-validation.

KIML wins. The paper reports consistent improvement over existing methods on the temporal-split DEE benchmark, additional support from ontology-enrichment biological evaluation — meaning the genes the model prioritises tend to fall in pathways the literature already associates with the disease — and generalisation across six additional diseases. The paper also generates per-gene interpretable explanations for the prioritisation. That last piece is the bit I'd press them hardest on if I were reviewing — interpretable explanations from a multi-modal pipeline are notoriously easy to make plausible-sounding and hard to make actually faithful — but the fact that they're attempting it at all, on a clinically-loaded disease, is the right move.

Three things to take from this paper for our audience. First, the framing is correct and worth quoting in your next grant. Disease-gene-prioritisation is not an experimental-data problem or a knowledge-graph problem. It is a problem where the two layers are mutually irreducible, and the field has been picking one or the other for a decade because the engineering is hard. KIML is one of several pieces of evidence this year that the engineering is now tractable.

Second, the literature-representation channel from PubMed sits squarely in the territory this audience cares about. Su Lab and adjacent groups have spent years shipping the ingestion infrastructure that feeds these channels — Wikidata-bound biomedical entities, MyGene, MyDisease, Open Targets adapters, the entire ecosystem of identifier mapping. Every multi-modal gene-prioritisation pipeline that integrates a PubMed channel — from KIML to whatever lands tomorrow — relies, downstream, on an entity-disambiguation layer that the curated open-data community built. The methods paper gets cited. The disambiguation layer doesn't. We should be talking about that more.

Third, I want to set this against today's "More headlines." AutoResearchBench, posted last Tuesday, dropped a sobering number on the literature-search-as-agent narrative. Frontier agents over a three-million-paper arXiv corpus score around nine percent — on both Deep Research, find a specific paper, and Wide Research, retrieve all matching papers. Nine. The conjunction of these two papers, on a quiet Sunday morning, tells you the actual shape of the field right now. Off-the-shelf agents are bad at scientific literature retrieval as a free-form discovery problem. Methods that bake structured biomedical knowledge into the pipeline are increasingly competitive at the focused, clinically-loaded prediction problems. The conclusion that follows is the one I keep landing on this week — the agentic-AI win in biomedicine, when it lands, is going to come from agents-plus-curated-resources, not agents alone. That's the through-line for today.
