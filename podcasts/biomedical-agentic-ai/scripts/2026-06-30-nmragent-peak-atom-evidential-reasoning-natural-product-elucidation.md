# NMRAgent — peak-atom evidential reasoning over chemical knowledge graphs triples NMR structure-elucidation accuracy and resolves novel natural products

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.29776

## Script

Today's paper is NMRAgent, an LLM-powered agent for nuclear magnetic resonance structure elucidation, from Jun Xia's group at Hong Kong University of Science and Technology Guangzhou, with collaborators across USTC, Peking Union Medical College, and Shanghai AI Lab. It is the strongest single demonstration this month that evidential reasoning — making the agent produce explicit, atom-level evidence for its predictions rather than a black-box output — is the right form for scientific agents wrapping specialised tools. On a held-out scaffold-split benchmark with novel chemical scaffolds, it nearly doubles the previous state of the art. Then it elucidates two unknown natural products and corrects four published structural misassignments.

The problem. Working out the structure of an unknown molecule from its NMR spectrum is foundational to natural-product chemistry and early drug discovery, and AI is stuck between two approaches that miss each other. Database retrieval works well when the target sits inside the library but cannot propose a novel scaffold. De novo prediction models — encode the spectrum, decode a SMILES — can propose any structure but operate as black boxes; the peak-to-atom assignment a chemist actually uses to verify a result is bypassed entirely. NMRAgent bridges that gap.

The architecture is three levels, and the design choice at each level is worth following.

The Knowledge-Level. An LLM planner queries a chemical knowledge graph using retrieval-augmented generation to formulate an elucidation plan. The knowledge graph integrates several open natural-product resources — LOTUS, COCONUT, the Natural Products Atlas — plus ChEMBL for bioactivity context, plus Marinka Zitnik's precision medicine knowledge graph for broader biomedical grounding. Alongside the external graph, the agent maintains a private NMR evidence memory of previously verified peak-atom assignments, so the system can reuse prior spectroscopic reasoning the way a human chemist accumulates spectral intuition. The planner outputs an executable workflow — which tools to call, in what order, against what evidence — rather than enumerating molecular structures blindly.

The Molecular-Level. Two candidate sources operate in parallel. A retrieval branch searches a 158-million-entry spectrum-structure database — an expansion of Pubchem-NMRNet — using a self-supervised NMR encoder called UltraNMR. Coarse retrieval pulls global-spectrum neighbours, then dense embeddings re-rank by molecular similarity. A de novo branch invokes NMR-conditioned generators like ChefNMR and NMRMind to propose structures outside the database. Crucially, every candidate — retrieved or generated — is then re-simulated using a fast NMR predictor and sent to an LLM-powered Peak-Atom Verifier. The verifier produces explicit assignments — this experimental peak maps to that atom — a consistency score, and an unresolved-mismatch set. That mismatch set is what drives the next level.

The Fragment-Level. When candidates do not fully explain the observed spectrum, the verifier-flagged mismatch regions become the optimisation target. Candidates are decomposed into chemically meaningful fragments under BRICS cleavage rules. The LLM reads the peak-atom mismatch as a refinement hypothesis — preserve these fragments that already explain the spectrum, repair these fragments where the prediction disagrees with experiment — and recombines fragments under a hard molecular-formula constraint. Refined structures go back to the verifier in an iterative loop.

The numbers. On NMRGym — the scaffold-split benchmark, where test molecules are topologically distinct from anything seen during training or retrieval — NMRAgent reaches a Top-1 accuracy of forty-six-point-eight percent and a Top-10 of sixty-seven-point-one. The strongest task-specific baseline, NMRSolver with formula constraints, hits a Top-10 of thirty-six-point-five — a thirty-percentage-point absolute gap. Tanimoto similarity to ground truth: zero point seven-nine versus zero point six-four. On nmrshiftdb the Top-10 is eighty-one percent, against thirty-eight for NMRSolver. On Exp450, an experimentally curated benchmark, seventy percent. The robustness across data sources is the point — this is not a single-benchmark trick.

And the most interesting baseline result. General-purpose, reasoning-oriented, and science-oriented LLMs — GPT-4o, Claude Opus four-point-seven, Gemini 2.5 Flash with thinking, DeepSeek V3, o3-mini, Intern-S1, Intern-Pro — all score at or near zero on the scaffold-split benchmark when asked to solve it directly. Frontier reasoning models, no matter how capable, lack the spectral grounding required to recover exact molecular structures from NMR peaks. The agent works not because the underlying LLM is smarter, but because the agent grounds the LLM through specialised tools, retrieval over a chemistry knowledge graph, and an explicit verifier loop. The authors are direct about this in the discussion — the LLM does not intrinsically understand NMR; its effectiveness comes from tool grounding and verifier-provided evidence. That is a useful piece of honesty to put in a paper.

The wet-lab validation. Beyond the benchmark, the team applied the agent to two genuinely novel natural products provided by collaborating chemistry groups — a coumarin dimer from Hydrangea davidii and a compound from Vitex trifolia. NMRAgent recovered structures consistent with the experimentally validated solutions in both cases, with Tanimoto similarities of one-point-zero. They also re-analysed four published structural revision cases, including Altechromone A and Samoquasine A. Given only the originally reported 1H and 13C shifts — no synthetic, crystallographic, or DFT evidence — the agent recovered the revised structures, not the original incorrect assignments. The agent works as a real-world spectroscopic auditor.

Editorial reads. First, evidential reasoning is the right pattern for scientific agents. The verifier produces atom-level evidence for every claim, which means a human chemist can audit the prediction the same way they would audit a colleague — peak by peak, atom by atom — rather than trust an opaque score. That is the form of agentic AI that survives review in a high-stakes scientific workflow. Second, the three-level architecture is templatable beyond NMR. Knowledge-Level retrieval over a curated KG, Molecular-Level dual candidate construction with verifier-grounded scoring, Fragment-Level evidence-driven refinement — replace "molecule" with "hypothesis," "fragment" with "sub-claim," "peak-atom" with whatever the domain's atomic evidence is, and the same loop applies to other inverse-problem scientific tasks. Third, the dependency on integrated chemistry KGs is worth highlighting for an open-science audience. LOTUS, COCONUT, Natural Products Atlas, ChEMBL — these are community-curated open databases, and the agent's reach is bounded by their coverage. Investments in those resources cash out as direct capability gains in downstream agentic systems. Fourth — and this is the cleanest finding for scientific-agent design — frontier LLMs alone are not enough. The verifier loop and the specialised tools are what produce the result. The agent is the scaffolding, not the model.

Caveats. One-dimensional 1H and 13C only — no 2D NMR, no IR, no UV, no stereochemistry. Database completeness still bounds the reachable structures. And the system is still below expert-chemist reliability on the hardest natural-product cases.

The take. NMRAgent is the strongest argument this month that the next leap for biomedical and chemical agents is not a bigger model but a better verifier — make the agent produce atomic-level evidence for its claims, then build the rest of the architecture around supporting and refining that evidence. Code, dataset, and weights are all open on GitHub and Hugging Face.
