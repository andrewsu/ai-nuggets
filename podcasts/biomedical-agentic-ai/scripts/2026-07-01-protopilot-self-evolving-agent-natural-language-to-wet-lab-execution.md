# ProtoPilot — self-evolving multi-agent system that converts natural-language biology intent into validated SOPs, SDK-compliant robot code, and feedback-corrected wet-lab execution

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.31763

## Script

Today's paper is ProtoPilot, a self-evolving multi-agent system for automating life-science experiments from natural-language intent all the way to physically executed and feedback-corrected wet-lab work. It comes out of Shanghai AI Lab and Genoria AI. It is the strongest single demonstration this month that the interesting frontier for biomedical agents is not writing better protocol text but keeping biological intent, operational constraints, device SDK, executable code, and empirical readouts coherent across a stateful, closed-loop workflow.

The problem. A wave of biology agents — Co-Scientist, Virtual Lab, PrimeGen, BioProAgent — has shown that language models can generate protocols and plan experiments. But the handoff to a physically executable experiment is fragile. A protocol has to express biological logic. An SOP has to ground that logic in feasible volumes, concentrations, and labware. Device code has to bind the procedure to deck layout, well mapping, timing, and vendor-specific SDK commands. A workflow that looks fine as text can break on any of those transitions. ProtoPilot's argument is that the workflow itself, across all those layers plus the wet-lab feedback that follows, is the object that has to stay aligned.

The framework. Alongside the system the authors built BioLab bench, an expert-grounded benchmark of two hundred ninety-four synthetic-biology and molecular-biology tasks derived from ninety-eight gold-standard protocols, spanning three difficulty tiers. Every task carries wet-lab expert rubrics along seven quality dimensions, three-layer device-level validity gates covering Python syntax, parameter alignment, and SDK export against slot conflicts and pipetting bounds, and — for the marquee cases — real wet-lab tests on physical liquid-handling instruments.

The architecture rests on two mechanisms. The first is hierarchical multi-agent orchestration. An Orchestrator Agent maintains stateful workflow state, decomposes intent into modules, and coordinates a Protocol Expert Agent trained with reinforcement learning for step-level SOP generation and a Coding Agent that translates validated protocols into instrument-executable code. A multilayer memory bank holds domain knowledge, retrievable protocol documents, working plan, and a task-experience base of completed trajectories and feedback. Rubric validators check every module for biological plausibility, parameter consistency, safety, and protocol-code alignment before integration.

The second mechanism is the one worth staring at. Rather than generating code as a direct translation from protocol text to API calls, the Coding Agent uses a runtime-updated skill library organised into three hierarchical classes: parameter-confirmation skills that resolve underspecified qualitative descriptions into executable values, deck-layout skills that determine physical configurations and well-position maps, and instrument-operation skills that emit SDK-compliant executable code. Skills are created from expert rules, protocol context, SDK information, and prior trajectories; evaluated by rubric validators, code checks, and execution feedback; and revised through a create-evaluate-revise loop. The bet is that stable expert operational principles — the rules a human experimenter carries in their head but that are absent from both protocol text and SDK docs — are what actually transfer across tasks and devices.

The numbers. On the seven-dimension rubric across all two hundred ninety-four tasks, ProtoPilot scored ninety-four-point-seven, three points above the next-best specialised system and more than six above every general-purpose LLM. In blind evaluation by three wet-lab scientists across thirty-four queries, ProtoPilot took the top slot in seventy-point-six percent of queries and appeared in the top three in ninety-point-two percent, with automated rubric and expert rankings correlating at Spearman zero-point-nine-three. On protocol-to-code, overall gate pass rate was ninety-six-point-six percent, against a ceiling of thirty-five percent for general-purpose LLMs. And the cross-device evaluation is the cleanest architectural signal: across AlphaTool MGI, Hamilton STAR, Opentrons OT-2, and Tecan EVO, ProtoPilot's gate pass rate ranged from eighty-five to ninety-one — a spread of six points. LabScript-AI ranged from nine to fifty-six — a spread of forty-seven points. On the Opentrons OT-2 specifically, ProtoPilot hit eighty-eight percent; the purpose-built, single-platform OpenTrons-AI hit thirty-two.

The wet-lab experiments. Foundational operations first — ninety-six-well E. coli inoculation, ordered tenfold serial dilutions, colony PCR on twenty-four clones with all twenty-four producing the expected amplicon. Then multi-step cloning — pET-21b GLuc and RLuc wild-type plasmids constructed across three replicates, all Sanger-confirmed. Then parallel site-directed mutagenesis of sixteen luciferase mutants — all eight RLuc and seven of eight GLuc sequence-confirmed. And then the marquee experiment. PCA-based DNA assembly of four fragments — EGFP plus three regions of a bacterial CPS gene — with two iterative optimisation cycles inside a single run. First: vector-backbone PCR produced a prominent nonspecific band; ProtoPilot analysed the failure mode, redesigned the primers, and the second preparation produced a clean single band. Second: the initial transformation showed colonies on the negative control and lawn-like growth on samples; ProtoPilot attributed the failure to inactivated ampicillin and excessive plating volume, regenerated the protocol with fresh plates and reduced plating volume, and re-executed. All four target sequences ended with at least one Sanger-confirmed correct clone.

Editorial reads. First, the closed-loop revision is the capability that moves this out of the demo category. The field already has systems that write protocols. The interesting move is that when the wet-lab reports a failure, the Orchestrator updates workflow state, invokes the relevant expert agent, produces a revised protocol grounded in the specific failure mode, and re-executes. Ampicillin degradation and excessive plating volume are exactly the kind of unglamorous operational knowledge that lives in a graduate student's head, not in the protocol text — and the skill library is the mechanism that lets a system encode and reuse it.

Second, the skill-library factoring is the templatable pattern. Parameter-confirmation, layout planning, code-specification — three classes of reusable operational skill, each refined through create-evaluate-revise loops against rubric validators and execution feedback. That maps to any domain where an agent has to convert underspecified natural-language intent into executable actions on a physical or software instrument. Encode expert operational rules, not surface syntax of a specific SDK.

Third, general-purpose LLMs reach Protocol Scores of seventy-seven to eighty-six on this benchmark yet fall below thirty-five percent on gate pass. Surface-level code quality does not imply physical executability without coherent cross-layer alignment. This is the same finding last week's arc — BioDesignBench, scBench-Long, Clair3-Connect — kept converging on. The bottleneck is not the model. It is the scaffolding around the model that forces cross-layer consistency and closed-loop revision.

Caveats. Wet-lab validation is limited to synthetic biology and molecular biology — no cell culture, no biochemical assay, no in vivo work. The dry-lab side of the loop — hypothesis generation, sequence design, computational modelling upstream of the experimental request — is deliberately out of scope. And the benchmark, though carefully constructed, is not yet community-standard.

The take. If you are building agentic infrastructure for biology today, ProtoPilot is the paper to compare against on the workflow-scaffolding axis. The next unit of progress in this space will not be a better protocol writer. It will be systems that can look at a lawn of colonies on a negative-control plate and say, out loud, "the ampicillin was probably bad — let me try again."
