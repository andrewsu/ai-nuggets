# biomeStat — agentic AI as deterministic orchestrator, dengue phylodynamics on 1,000 Asian genomes in under a day

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.10.731380v1

## Script

Today's pick is a bioRxiv preprint posted yesterday from a Sri Lanka-based group — the company Advanced Molecular Works together with the University of Sri Jayewardenepura. The system is called biomeStat, and the framing they put on it is the framing I want to spend the episode on, because it's the right framing for the show. They call it agentic AI as a deterministic orchestrator. The claim is that you can compress a full genomic-epidemiology workflow — sequence retrieval, alignment, maximum-likelihood phylogenetics, Bayesian phylodynamics, selection-pressure analysis, structural mapping — from weeks of expert bioinformatician time down to a single twenty-hour analysis session, and you do it without ever letting the model invent biology.

Setup. Genomic epidemiology is an orchestration problem more than a modeling problem. You need to pull complete genomes out of GenBank with the right filters, run a multiple sequence alignment, build a maximum-likelihood tree with bootstrap support, fit a molecular clock, run BEAST2 phylodynamics with the right substitution model and chain length, run HyPhy for selection, calculate Tajima's D per protein per serotype, predict B-cell and T-cell epitopes, map population entropy onto a crystal structure, and pull it all together. Each of those steps has its own parameter set, its own quirks, its own version-specific bugs, and its own compute profile — most of it is happy on CPU, but the BEAST2 chains want GPU acceleration through the BEAGLE library or you're waiting for weeks. The traditional approach is an expert bioinformatician at a workstation. The naive AI approach is to point a large language model at the data and ask it to do the analysis end-to-end. That's the version that hallucinates. The biomeStat version is the third option.

What they built. biomeStat is an agent whose job is to drive established gold-standard tools, not to replace them. The LLM writes Python code that calls IQ-TREE, BEAST2, MAFFT L-INS-i, HyPhy, TreeTime, PyMOL, and the NCBI Entrez API inside containerized sandboxes. It picks the substitution model. It configures the MCMC chain. When it hits a version-specific BEAST2 bug — deprecated variable type, mathematical constraint inside an operator — it diagnoses and patches the XML in real time rather than halting. When it needs the BEAST2 phylodynamics to actually finish, it autonomously escalates from the CPU tier to an H200 GPU and configures BEAGLE for CUDA parallelization. The underlying math stays deterministic. The natural-language layer translates intent — "human host, Asian origin, 2000 to 2025, complete genomes" — into deterministic API queries, not into a generative guess at what the answer might look like.

The demonstration. One thousand complete dengue genomes from sixteen Asian countries spanning twenty-five years. The agent ran the full pipeline — data retrieval, alignment, ML tree, time-scaled phylogeny, four per-serotype Bayesian Skyline Plots, a Birth-Death Skyline model, selection analysis across six viral proteins, codon-adaptation analysis, epitope mapping, and structural visualization on the envelope-EDE1-antibody complex and the NS5 polymerase — in nineteen and a half hours of wall-clock time. The biology came out roughly where you'd expect dengue biology to come out. Effective reproductive number hovers right around one across all five time intervals, which is what endemic dengue looks like. Tajima's D is strongly negative across every protein-serotype combination, consistent with population expansion and purifying selection.

The interesting numbers are the ones the orchestrated pipeline produced as integrated outputs. One thousand eight hundred and sixty-nine candidate immune escape sites, identified by joint criteria — high Shannon entropy at an epitope position with evidence of diversifying selection. The agent then cross-referenced those candidates against the Immune Epitope Database. Seventy-two and a half percent of the computational candidates landed inside a sequence-matched, experimentally validated IEDB epitope. That cross-validation step, done inside the same agent session, is what turns the surrogate epitope-prediction pipeline into a believable result rather than a list of computational artifacts. The other concrete output is one hundred and seventy-six positions exceeding ninety-nine point five percent within-serotype conservation, including the NS3 protease catalytic triad, the NS5 polymerase GDD motif, the NS3-NS4B interface, and the cytosolic loop drug-binding region. Those last two domains are the binding sites of mosnodenvir and the Novartis compound currently in Phase 2 trials, and the known resistance positions for both were absent from all nine hundred and ninety-two intact NS4B sequences in the dataset — which is a useful piece of pre-deployment surveillance for two antivirals that haven't seen clinical pressure yet.

The Su Lab read. Three things.

First, the deterministic-orchestrator framing is the right architectural lesson, and it generalizes well beyond viral genomics. The agent is not the source of truth and is not asked to be. The agent is the operator. The truth lives in IQ-TREE's likelihood surface, in BEAST2's posterior, in the IEDB. The hallucination surface area collapses because the model never gets to assert a biological fact — it gets to choose which deterministic tool runs next and how it's parameterized. Compare this to the dominant alternative, which is fine-tune a domain language model on a corpus and ask it to be both interface and inference engine. That collapses curation and inference into the same set of weights, and you lose updatability and you lose verifiability. biomeStat is on the right side of that line.

Second, the seventy-two percent IEDB hit rate is the more interesting validation number in the paper than the twenty-hour runtime. It is the agent grading its own work against an external curated reference, in the same session, without being prompted to do so as a separate evaluation experiment. That self-validation loop — run the analysis, then cross-reference the output against a domain-standard knowledge resource — is the loop you want any agent in this space to be running. It's also where ontology- and knowledge-graph-grounded biomedical resources earn their keep, because they are the things the agent can defer to.

Third, the caveats. This is a company preprint — the first two authors are the founders of Advanced Molecular Works which built biomeStat — and it is not peer reviewed. The MCMC chains are ten million states, shorter than the fifty to a hundred million states commonly recommended for Birth-Death Skyline analyses. The epitope predictions use surrogate algorithms — Hopp-Woods, Emini fractional probability, the NetMHCpan rule set — rather than calling the neural-network predictors directly. The authors note that the agent could integrate BepiPred 3.0 and NetMHCpan 4.1 as additional containerized modules; they just didn't, for runtime reasons. Take the architectural pattern as the lesson and treat the specific dengue numbers as a system demonstration rather than as settled epidemiology.

The deeper signal is what this paper says about where the field is heading. The argument is not that agentic AI replaces the expert bioinformatician. It is that the agent absorbs the orchestration and the parameter tuning and the version-specific bug fixing — the parts that have nothing to do with biological judgment and everything to do with making the tools cooperate — while the underlying mathematics stays in the hands of tools the field has spent decades verifying. That trade is exactly what should make agentic AI usable for global-health surveillance in places where you can't always find an expert phylogeneticist in the building.
