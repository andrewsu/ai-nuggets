# VISTA Architect — precompute the patient as a knowledge graph, then let agents query it for tumor boards

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22692

## Script

Today's pick is an arXiv preprint that went up over the weekend from Stanford Medicine's Department of Biomedical Data Science, with Manuel Rivas as senior author. The system is called VISTA Architect. The one-line take is that this is the cleanest argument I have seen yet for treating a patient's electronic health record not as a long document you reprocess on every query, and not as a corpus you retrieve over with RAG, but as a precomputed knowledge graph an agent can traverse. The headline number is ninety-six point four percent accuracy on tumor board variables across more than a thousand patients, and the comparison they ran against retrieval-augmented generation is the part that should change how anyone in this space designs their next clinical pipeline.

Setup. The problem is multidisciplinary tumor boards, where a thoracic oncology team has to reconstruct a patient's history before the meeting — what's the diagnosis, what's the histology, what genetic testing was done, what therapy is the patient on right now, when was the last CT, are there toxicities, what surgeries have happened. Fifteen variables in the salient set in this paper. Each one of those is in the EHR somewhere — across discharge summaries, radiology reports, pathology reports, medication lists, structured fields — and the human workflow is a clinician scrolling through documents the morning of the meeting trying to assemble a timeline. The natural LLM move is one of two things. Either you stuff the long context window with the patient's record and ask, or you index the documents and do RAG. The authors show, with a matched comparison, that both of those approaches lose to a third option in a way that has structural reasons, not just engineering ones.

What they built. Two layers, and the design is the read. The bottom layer is something they call the MEDS Graph — source-faithful, every event in the EHR represented as a node with full provenance back to the originating document, no abstraction yet. The top layer is the Timeline Object Architecture, the TOA. The TOA is built by a graph-guided LLM extraction step that walks the source-faithful graph and synthesizes it into a deduplicated, temporally coherent timeline of clinical events. Same diagnosis mentioned in twelve documents collapses to one node with twelve provenance links. A treatment start, a CT date, a pathology result — each gets an event object with a time stamp and a pointer back to the document. The crucial property is that this synthesis happens once, at ingestion, not at query time. Downstream, when an agent needs to answer a tumor board question, it does not reread the documents. It traverses the patient-state graph and only falls back to source text when something needs verification.

The numbers. Eleven hundred and eighty patients in the thoracic oncology cohort. Fifteen variables. Seventeen thousand seven hundred individual evaluations. Ninety-six point four percent accuracy, with a tight confidence interval. The matched BM25 retrieval-augmented baseline runs at around sixty-seven percent. That's a thirty-point gap, which is large for a clinical extraction task. And the breakdown by variable is where this gets interesting. Demographics — date of birth, sex — both systems hit a hundred percent because the information is unambiguous and in one place. Where VISTA opens up the gap is on the time-varying state variables. Previous surgery, metastasis status, radiation therapy — the RAG baseline drops into the thirty-three to fifty percent range on these, and VISTA stays above ninety-five. The reason is direct: those are the variables where the right answer depends on assembling a temporal sequence of events, not on finding one passage. Retrieval pulls one chunk and the model paraphrases it. The graph pre-encodes the temporal order, so the agent gets the resolved state.

The agentic interface. They put a Claude Opus orchestrator on top of the graph with tool calls into the TOA, run an out-of-sample thirty-patient cohort, and clock the agentic preparation at about two point two minutes per board. Two point two minutes for what is currently a hour-plus of manual chart review. Whether that number holds outside thoracic oncology is the limitation the authors are open about — they validated on one specialty, the modular design is meant to generalize but they have not shown the transfer yet.

The Su Lab read. Three things. First, this is the argument for graph-first clinical AI that I have been waiting for someone to actually quantify. The dominant pattern in the literature has been long-context-or-RAG-over-documents, and we have been hand-waving for two years about why structured patient state should be better. Sixty-seven versus ninety-six on time-varying variables is the receipt. The retrieval failures are not noise — they are exactly the failures the theory predicts, on exactly the variables where temporal assembly matters. Second, the right place to spend an LLM call in this stack is at ingestion, doing the extraction into the graph, not at query time. Once the synthesis is in the graph with provenance links back, query-time agents can be lighter, cheaper, faster, and auditable. That is also the design that gives you the two-point-two-minute preparation number. Third, the unsolved problem the paper is honest about: the TOA itself is LLM-extracted, which means errors at ingestion propagate. The provenance links let a clinician verify, but no one is going to manually verify eleven hundred patients. The next research question for this group, and for anyone copying this design, is how to score the TOA's reliability per node without humans in the loop. That's where I would push next.
