# Medea — a verification-first omics agent whose wins survive an unpublished yeast synthetic-lethality screen

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.01.16.696667v3

## Script

Today's paper is Medea, an AI agent for therapeutic reasoning across biological contexts, out of Marinka Zitnik's group at Harvard together with Trey Ideker's group at UCSD, third version on bioRxiv on the seventeenth. It's a rare case of a biomedical-agentic-AI paper where the load-bearing claim isn't "our LLM is smarter than yours." It's about which verification checks you put around the LLM, and whether they matter enough to change what the agent actually gets right.

Framing first. The chronic failure mode of agents in omics work isn't reasoning-in-the-abstract, it's context slip. You give the agent an objective involving a specific cell type in a specific disease, and over a long-horizon plan the agent silently swaps in a single-cell tool for a bulk-tissue question, or queries a pathway knowledge base curated for a different tissue, or fires differential expression with mismatched covariates that then propagate confidently into downstream pathway ranking. The analysis runs, no error is raised, and the answer is quietly wrong. Medea's argument is that fixing this requires verification wired into the agent's plan, not tacked on afterwards — checks during planning, checks during execution, and a consensus step that can abstain when the evidence disagrees with itself.

Architecture. Four modules. Research planning writes the analysis plan and checks its integrity — does the plan carry the specified cell type and disease context all the way through, do the chosen tools' assumptions actually match the data. Analysis executes code with pre-run validation that dataset and tool are compatible, and post-run checks that the output is statistically sensible, not just runtime-error-free. Literature reasoning retrieves publications and screens them for contextual relevance, so you don't aggregate a study on a different cell type into your evidence pool. And a multi-round discussion module reconciles evidence across tool outputs, retrieved literature, and the LLM's own parametric knowledge — and abstains when the panel disagrees, rather than picking a plausible-sounding answer. The tool space is twenty tools spanning single-cell and bulk transcriptomics, cancer vulnerability maps like DepMap, pathway and ontology resources, and foundation models like PINNACLE and TranscriptFormer.

Evaluation. Almost five thousand seven hundred open-ended analyses across three domains. First, cell-type-specific therapeutic target nomination — five diseases including rheumatoid arthritis, type 1 diabetes, and Sjögren's syndrome, twenty-nine cell type contexts, about twenty-four hundred analyses. Second, synthetic-lethality prediction in seven cancer cell lines, another twenty-four hundred analyses. Third, immunotherapy response prediction in nearly nine hundred bladder-cancer patients from multimodal profiles. Across the three, Medea improves on its own backbone LLM by up to about forty-six percent on target nomination, twenty-two on synthetic lethality, and twenty-four on immunotherapy response, with McNemar's tests in the low basis points. Same story regardless of whether the backbone is GPT-4o or Claude 3.7 Sonnet. And critically, module ablations show the wins come from the verification layer, not tool selection alone — running only literature reasoning, only planning-plus-analysis, or the full stack produces markedly different abstention profiles. The literature-only configuration abstains around seventy-seven percent of the time on cell-type-specific target nomination, precisely because the literature on that question is thin. That is the correct thing to do when you don't have evidence. A bare LLM confidently answers instead.

Now the part I want to flag, because it addresses a real skepticism about this whole line of work. If you evaluate biomedical agents on published benchmarks, the concern is always that the LLM has seen the answer during pretraining and is regurgitating it. Medea takes that concern seriously and runs the kind of control most of the field skips. The authors ran an unpublished epistatic mini-array-profiling screen in yeast — two hundred thirty-eight thousand gene-gene pairs assayed under two DNA-damaging treatments — and evaluated Medea on predicting the experimentally measured synthetic-lethal interactions. Yeast, an EMAP screen, and the actual assay result — none of which was in the literature or in any training corpus when the runs happened. Medea recovers the experimentally measured hits. That's the strongest form of anti-leakage argument you can make for an LLM-driven scientific agent, and it should be the standard the rest of this field is held to.

Two editorial takeaways. First, this paper reframes what "agentic" means when applied to biomedical research. The value isn't a bigger model or a fancier tool registry. It's verification wired into planning, execution, and evidence synthesis, plus calibrated abstention when the evidence panel disagrees. Loud abstention when the evidence isn't there is a feature, not a bug — and it is where bare LLMs and pattern-matching baselines quietly lose ground. Second, the yeast experiment is the template every follow-up paper in this space should be graded against. If your agent's wins evaporate the moment you point it at data that couldn't possibly have leaked, the wins were never real. Medea passed that test — and it makes the rest of the paper's numbers materially harder to argue with.
