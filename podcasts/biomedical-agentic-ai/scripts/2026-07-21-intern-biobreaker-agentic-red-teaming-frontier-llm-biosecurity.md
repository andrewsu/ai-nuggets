# Intern-BioBreaker — an agentic red-teamer jailbreaks frontier LLMs into hazardous biology, and where the guardrail really has to live

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.18056

## Script

Today's paper is one of those "the field just stepped over a line" results, out of the Shanghai AI Laboratory with collaborators in biomedical engineering at Fudan. The system is called Intern-BioBreaker, and the honest one-line version is this: it's an agentic red-teaming model built specifically to find out whether frontier language models can be talked into helping with dangerous biology, and the answer turns out to be uncomfortably often yes.

Start with why this needed doing. Most biosecurity evaluations of language models are still stuck at the level of "did the model refuse, yes or no," or multiple-choice quizzes about pathogens. Those metrics saturate — every frontier model looks safe on them — and, worse, they tell you nothing about whether a jailbroken model actually produces something biologically real. Intern-BioBreaker is designed to attack that gap from both ends.

The attacker itself is the agentic part, and it's the piece worth your attention. It starts from a scientific foundation model, gets fine-tuned on general jailbreak data, then reinforcement-learned specifically against biological safety tasks, with a panel of open models playing defender and a guard model scoring each attempt. At inference time it runs as an agent: it picks an attack strategy, sends a prompt, reads the target model's response, and if the refusal holds, it adapts and tries again, up to eighteen attempts per task. It's essentially an automated adversary that probes your model's soft spots and learns them in real time.

And it works alarmingly well. Across fourteen leading models — the current GPT, Claude, Gemini, Grok, DeepSeek, and Qwen families — the task-level attack success rates are high, and on the broader misuse benchmark, ten of the fourteen hit one hundred percent. The one consistent bright spot: the Claude models were the most resistant on both benchmarks, though "most resistant" still meant a majority of tasks eventually fell. Against GPT-5.5 the attacker reached sixty percent, up from thirty-two for the base model it was built on — which tells you the vulnerability isn't about starting from a weak model, it's about training an attacker that fits its target.

Now the part that will draw the headlines, and the part I want you to be skeptical about. The authors push past "the model said something bad" to "the model produced a sequence." In case studies they induced a model to generate modified influenza hemagglutinin variants, then ran them through the actual computational stack — translate the DNA, fold the protein with AlphaFold three, dock it against human and avian receptor analogs. The generated variants folded confidently, and on docking they scored as tighter binders than the originals, which is at least a plausible signal of enhanced receptor binding. Separately, in a mock end-to-end scenario, the tested models produced DNA sequences for hazardous proteins that preserved the target's amino acid sequence while evading homology-based screening, and — in the scariest framing — walked a hypothetical novice through the whole plan over several conversational turns.

Here's my caveat, and it's a big one. The abstract talks about DNA synthesis, expression in a host, and verification by mass spectrometry — the full wet-lab pipeline. They did not run it. They stopped at the computational stage, deliberately, for biosafety, and the physical protocol is described rather than executed. So the demonstrated result is not "we built a working pathogen." It's narrower, and honestly still important: frontier models can be pushed, by an automated agent, into outputs that clear the very filters we'd use to catch danger — structure prediction says the protein is plausible, and homology search says it's novel enough to slip past screening. The material-risk claim is inferred from standard protocols, not shown. I'd want that distinction kept front and center, because "wet-lab validated" is doing a lot of work in the abstract that the experiments don't quite cash.

Two reasons this matters for us. First, this is agentic AI turned on AI safety itself. The genuinely interesting object here is the red-teaming agent, and that same recipe — an adaptive, feedback-driven attacker — generalizes to auditing any model capability, not just biology. Second, it's a concrete argument about where the guardrail actually has to live. If text-level alignment saturates and can be beaten by an adaptive agent, then the durable control point moves downstream, to nucleic-acid synthesis screening that reasons about composition rather than doing naive homology matching. That's the policy ask the authors land on, and it lines up with this year's open letter from the major labs calling for mandatory synthesis screening. The uncomfortable takeaway for anyone building scientific agents: your model's refusal behavior is not your safety story.
