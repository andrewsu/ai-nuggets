## Script

Today's pick is OmniCellAgent, a multi-agent AI scientist for omic-driven discovery from Fuhai Li's group at Washington University in St. Louis. The third revision went up on bioRxiv yesterday, June second.

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.07.31.667797v3

The premise is one that anyone who's tried to use an AI agent for transcriptomics has run into. Most of the recent omics-agent work — and there's been a lot of it over the last year — quietly assumes that the human user shows up already holding a curated, disease-specific single-cell RNA sequencing dataset. The agent then does what agents are good at: literature retrieval, downstream analysis, maybe a knowledge-graph lookup at the end. But the hard part for a non-computational researcher is not running Scanpy. The hard part is knowing which of the tens of thousands of public single-cell experiments are actually relevant to the disease they're trying to understand, and which of those can be sensibly merged with which controls. That dataset-selection-and-integration step is where the typical agent quietly punts back to the user.

OmniCellAgent is built around the claim that that step itself is what should be agentic. The framework sits on top of a large single-cell RNA sequencing corpus and treats dataset retrieval, integration, and analysis as a job for an autonomous agent, not a precondition the user has to satisfy. Once the relevant data is assembled and analyzed, a second agent, the biomedical prior-knowledge agent, takes the candidate targets that fall out and systematically annotates them by reasoning over curated databases and the literature. And then a layer of domain-specific expert agents handles interpretation of the high-priority targets — turning a ranked list of genes into a story you can act on. The evidence from each layer feeds upward, and the output is a structured analytical report plus data-driven hypotheses. The authors evaluate it across multiple disease settings and argue that it can identify relevant datasets, prioritize biologically meaningful targets, and produce evidence-supported hypotheses end to end.

Here's why it matters for this audience. The architecture is one of the cleanest expressions I've seen lately of separating the data-selection-and-integration layer from the knowledge-grounding layer from the interpretation layer, with explicit agentic responsibility for each. That's an organizational pattern that generalizes well beyond single-cell. Anywhere a biomedical workflow currently expects a human to perform the implicit curation step — selecting which Geo series to download, which proteomics datasets to harmonize, which patient cohorts to merge — there's room for a dedicated agent whose only job is that step, and whose output other agents can build on. The prior-knowledge agent piece, where curated databases and literature serve as the grounding substrate for target annotation, is also where the knowledge-graph-versus-pure-LLM tension actually shows up in practice. The paper's framing treats curated structured knowledge as a first-class input alongside literature retrieval, which is the right way around for high-stakes target prioritization. Hallucinated targets are an expensive thing to chase down a lab bench.

The honest caveats. The abstract is light on hard numbers. There's no headline F1, no count of datasets touched, no specific disease settings named — at least not in the abstract. That's a signal to read the full paper carefully before adopting the architectural claims wholesale, and to look at the version history. This is a v3 update of a manuscript that's been live since July of last year, which is either evidence of careful iteration or evidence of a paper still searching for its strongest evaluation framing. The agent-orchestration field is now busy enough that the bar for an end-to-end omics-scientist agent is no longer "does it run" — it's "does it find a hypothesis a domain expert wouldn't have found, on a benchmark the field agrees is non-trivial." The next thing I'd want to see from this group is a head-to-head against a human-curator-plus-standard-pipeline baseline on a held-out disease setting, with the hypotheses prospectively evaluated.

The structural lesson, though, is durable. The agent that does dataset selection and integration is probably the most valuable piece of infrastructure missing from current biomedical agentic AI stacks. OmniCellAgent puts a name and a working implementation on that role, and slots it into a knowledge-graph-grounded, multi-agent pipeline that maps cleanly onto how an experienced computational biologist actually works.
