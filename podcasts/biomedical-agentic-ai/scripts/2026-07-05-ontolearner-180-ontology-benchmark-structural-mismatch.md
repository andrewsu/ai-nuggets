# OntoLearner — an open-source library and 180-ontology benchmark showing LLM ontology learning is bottlenecked by structural mismatch, not model scale

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01977

## Script

Today's pick is OntoLearner — an open-source Python library and benchmarking framework for ontology learning with large language models, out of TIB Hannover with a contributor from IBM Research, posted on arXiv this week.

The pitch is straightforward. Ontology learning has been fragmented for decades. On one side is the ontology infrastructure ecosystem — BioPortal, the OBO Foundry, EBI's Ontology Lookup Service, UMLS, FAIRsharing — places you go to find and reuse ontologies. On the other side is the more recent LLM-driven ontology-learning wave — NeOn-GPT, OntoGPT, OntoChat, OLLM — every group building its own pipeline against its own gold standard. There has never been a shared substrate that says, here are the ontologies, here is how to load them, here are the train-dev-test splits, here is the evaluation. OntoLearner tries to be that substrate. One hundred and eighty machine-readable ontologies across twenty-two domains, exposed as pipeline-ready HuggingFace datasets, with standardized splits for the three canonical ontology-learning tasks: term typing, taxonomy discovery, and non-taxonomic relation extraction. Pip-install, load ChEBI or the Gene Ontology or QUDT the same way you'd load a HuggingFace text corpus, run the standardized evaluation. MIT licensed, aligned with FAIR principles.

That contribution alone is worth paying attention to for anyone who works on biomedical knowledge graphs — because it removes an excuse. If you're evaluating a new ontology-alignment method or a new KG-completion pipeline, there is now a shared benchmark that spans twenty-two domains rather than yet another ad-hoc slice. But the more interesting piece is what they do with the substrate. Because they built it, they can now ask a question nobody else could answer at scale: when large language models fail at ontology learning, is it because the models aren't big enough, or is it something structural?

They ran twenty-two retrieval models and twelve LLMs across the twenty-six most structurally diverse ontologies in the collection, from small event schemas to gigantic biomedical vocabularies. What comes back is not the story the scaling-optimist would predict. Term typing — the classification task of asking what kind of thing a word refers to — gets to somewhere between forty and eighty-three percent F1 depending on domain, and it scales cleanly with model size. Going from Qwen 3 at six hundred million parameters to fourteen billion lifts Finance term typing from twenty-two percent to seventy-two. So the standard story holds for that task. But taxonomy discovery — the task of assembling the is-a hierarchy from a set of types — is stuck in the single digits for Biology & Life Science, Agriculture, and Ecology. Scaling from six hundred million to fourteen billion parameters moves Agriculture from one point six percent to eight point five percent. Fourteen billion parameters, and the answer is essentially still noise. Non-taxonomic relation extraction is bimodal: some domains reach over ninety percent, others hover near zero.

The authors chase the obvious counter-hypothesis: maybe the strong numbers are just training-corpus memorization. And in fact, they find good evidence of that on the Units & Measurements ontology — very low perplexity, meaning the model has clearly seen it before. But then they look at GoodRelations, an e-commerce ontology in the Finance domain, where mean perplexity is above six thousand, indicating essentially no memorization signal, and the fourteen-billion-parameter model still hits seventy-two percent F1 on term typing. So it isn't just contamination. Performance actually reflects a genuine structural fit between how the model represents relational knowledge and how the ontology organizes it.

The take-home line, and this is the part that lands if you've tried to actually engineer knowledge extraction from an LLM: the bottleneck of automated ontology learning is not model size and not architecture. It is the structural mismatch between how transformers encode knowledge as flat associative sequences and how ontologies encode it as constrained hierarchies with transitive closure and subsumption. Taxonomy discovery breaks because it requires reasoning the models don't natively do at scale. Retrieval-augmented generation helps most when the model is partially informed — when it needs a nudge, not when it's guessing from scratch. And instruction-tuned variants beat reasoning-tuned variants on this task, because structured extraction rewards output discipline, not open-ended chain-of-thought.

If you work at the intersection of AI and biomedical knowledge graphs, this is worth reading in full. As a tool, it's the shared substrate the field has been missing — pushed to PyPI and HuggingFace under MIT license, with a reproducible ontology complexity scorer bundled in. As a diagnosis, it says the path from foundation models to reliable ontology automation is probably not through bigger models. It's through hybrid architectures that give the model a structural scaffold, and through evaluation frameworks that measure hierarchy quality rather than surface-level F1. That reframing — from a scaling problem to a structural-alignment problem — is the paper's real contribution.

That's OntoLearner.
