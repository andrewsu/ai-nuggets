# LongMedBench — a MIMIC-IV benchmark for long-horizon medical LLM agents, and the implicit-time collapse RAG memory does not rescue

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09322

## Script

Today's pick is LongMedBench, an arXiv paper posted July tenth from a Chinese collaboration led by teams at Tsinghua and Peking University. It is a real-EHR benchmark for medical LLM agents that measures the one thing every prior medical benchmark has refused to measure — whether a model can reason across a long clinical trajectory.

Setup. Nearly every medical LLM benchmark you have seen is a short-context test. MedQA is board-style question answering, one prompt one answer. MedAgentBench measures tool use over a couple of turns. Even the recent agentic benchmarks tend to score decisions inside a single visit or a single case description. That is not how medical care actually looks. A real patient shows up thirty times over three years, accumulates prior notes and lab trends and medication changes, and every new decision has to be read against that longitudinal record. The claim of a medical LLM agent is that it will one day help clinicians do that reading. But nobody has been measuring whether they can.

Mechanism. The benchmark is built from MIMIC-IV, the de-identified intensive care record widely used in medical AI. LongMedBench extracts three hundred thirty-five patients with a total of six thousand nine hundred ninety-nine inpatient visits — close to twenty visits per patient on average, with a median of forty-five medical events per visit. Each patient becomes a long-context evaluation instance. Over that instance, the benchmark runs three progressive task suites. First is factual QA, split into two sub-cases: explicit, meaning what was the sodium value on this date; and relative, meaning what was the sodium value at the second-to-last admission. Second is temporal reasoning, three tasks: visit cloze, where the model fills in a masked visit; visit sorting, where a shuffled sequence has to be ordered; and joint sorting, where visits and their events are ordered together. Third and hardest is long-horizon decision-making — next action prediction, argument prediction, and discharge decisions — where the model has to synthesize the entire trajectory to produce a clinically defensible next step. Every task is scored with either exact-match, F1, or Kendall's tau.

Evaluation. Four frontier models: GPT-five-mini, Qwen-turbo, DeepSeek-v-three-point-two, and DeepSeek-v-three-point-two in thinking mode. On explicit-timestamp QA the top model hits a Kendall's tau of about zero-point-nine-two-five, which sounds reassuring. On the relative version — the exact same underlying question, phrased in terms of visit ordering rather than calendar dates — performance collapses to zero-point-four-two-three. Visit sorting tops out at zero-point-four-two-four for the best model. Joint sorting tops out at zero-point-three-three. Long-horizon decision-making averages around zero-point-four-four-five for the best model across three subtasks. The pattern is the same everywhere. The model can pull a fact if you tell it exactly where the fact is. It cannot pull the same fact if you ask it to first figure out where the fact is.

The other headline finding is the memory-augmentation ablation. The authors bolt a retrieval-augmented memory system onto each model, giving it access to past visits as external context. Memory augmentation provides minimal gains on decision-making. The paper's phrasing is that decision-making is dominated by the model's immediate context, not by retrieved history. Translated — RAG did not rescue the trajectory-reasoning gap.

Editorial reads. First, the significance. This is the first benchmark that seriously measures the thing medical LLM vendors are actually claiming to do. Every pitch deck for a clinical AI agent shows a graphic of the model reading three years of chart notes and surfacing the missed diagnosis. LongMedBench is what happens when you make the vendors demonstrate that read. What they demonstrate is that state-of-the-art models handle any single timestamped fact well, and cannot integrate more than one such fact into a decision. That is not a marginal shortcoming. It is a load-bearing failure of the deployment premise.

Second, why memory did not save the day is the part worth sitting with. Retrieval-augmented memory is the default answer to context limits, and the last two years of agent research have converged on some variant of retrieve-then-reason. The LongMedBench result is that on tasks where the reasoning has to happen over the whole trajectory — not over a single retrieved snippet — retrieval helps very little. If your medical agent needs to know that a patient's creatinine has been trending up over four admissions, please-retrieve-the-relevant-labs is not the intervention. The intervention is something that treats the trajectory as a first-class structured object rather than a bag of documents. That is a research direction the benchmark points at without prescribing.

Third, one methodological note that matters. The benchmark tests text prompts over serialized EHR. It is not tool use over a live FHIR server, and it is not an agent with autonomy to call sub-systems. It measures reading comprehension over a very long clinical document, not agentic behavior in the tool-calling sense. Future work in this line should push toward the agentic version, where the model has to decide what to retrieve and what to ignore. But even the reading-comprehension version is a load-bearing capability, and the fact that the field has been quietly not measuring it is the finding beneath the finding.

Caveats. The model panel is small — four models, no open-source seven-billion or thirteen-billion baselines — so the state-of-the-art-fails framing is really the-four-large-models-the-authors-could-afford-to-run failed. And all patients here are ICU patients from MIMIC-IV, which biases the trajectories toward acute critical cases rather than the routine primary-care trajectories where longitudinal reasoning arguably matters most. But the framework is public, the task suites are well-specified, and the finding replicates across the models tested. This is the medical agent benchmark to beat.
