# PreprintPaperTracker — an LLM claim-audit of 72,644 bioRxiv preprints against their peer-reviewed publications

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.06.30.735556v1

## Script

Today's pick is PreprintPaperTracker, a bioRxiv preprint posted July 1 from Ruslan Rust's group at USC's Zilkha Neurogenetic Institute with Hao Yin at Robarts Research. It runs a claim-level audit at whole-corpus scale to answer an old, unresolved question — how much do biomedical preprints actually change in peer review, and are they reliable enough to cite before that happens?

The setup is straightforward. Match every bioRxiv preprint posted 2018 through 2025 to its peer-reviewed publication by DOI. That yields seventy-two thousand six hundred forty-four abstract pairs, covering thirty-four hundred journals and twenty-five bioRxiv subject categories. Then use Claude Sonnet 4.6, temperature zero, structured JSON output, to extract one primary and two secondary claims from each preprint abstract and its published counterpart, and classify the pair for content change — unchanged, minor, or major — and hedging shift — more cautious, unchanged, or more confident.

Prior work has been small, COVID-specific, or measured textual similarity instead of the claim itself. What is new here is running a claim-level audit at the scale of the whole corpus, using an LLM whose agreement with two domain experts matches how well the experts agree with each other. Cohen's kappa in the low sixties for both. Sonnet self-agreement across three replicate runs sits at 0.75. That calibration is what makes the rest of the paper load-bearing.

The headline numbers. The primary claim is unchanged in about forty percent of pairs and only minorly revised in another fifty percent. Only about ten percent see substantial revision. Hedging language is unchanged in eighty-six percent; when it does shift, weakening beats strengthening two to one. Method claims are the most stable, revised substantially only five percent of the time. Descriptive, association, and mechanistic claims sit at around twelve percent. Across seventeen fields with at least fifteen hundred pairs each, weakened claims outnumber strengthened claims in every single one — no exceptions. Major revision rises with journal impact and with time in peer review — longer review, higher-impact journal, more claim-level change. And the rate of major revision has declined monotonically from seventeen percent in 2019 to under six percent in 2024, even after adjusting for review duration.

Then the striking secondary finding. Papers that were never posted as preprints are retracted at roughly twice the rate of preprinted papers — eighteen versus eight per ten thousand papers, rate ratio 2.3, confidence interval 1.2 to 4.5. Observational, based on few retractions, cannot fully rule out confounding. But the direction cuts against the intuition that preprinting is a shortcut around quality control. If anything, it runs the other way.

Why this matters for us — three reasons.

First, this is an LLM-based measurement instrument being validated the way you have to validate one at this scale. The authors panelized three models — Sonnet, Opus, and Haiku — on a stratified subsample of a hundred and twenty pairs, ran two independent human experts against the same set, and checked whether model-human agreement matched human-human. It did. They locked a version 7.1 codebook in April 2026 before running the full corpus. They report the exact model identifier, temperature, and output-token limit, following TRIPOD-LLM reporting standards. If you are going to build an LLM classifier on biomedical text, this is roughly the shape of the validation that makes the number defensible.

Second, this is what open science plus LLM at scale looks like when it works. The pipeline is on GitHub. The labeled dataset is searchable through a public dashboard. The input corpus is a public preprint server, the target is publicly retrievable published abstracts, the model calls run against a public commercial API. Nothing about this required proprietary data or institutional gatekeeping. It is the kind of study that would have taken a graduate student a year of manual annotation a decade ago. The LLM makes the labor tractable and the scale is what makes the answer definitive rather than field-specific.

Third, the authors flag the obvious next move themselves. Peer review currently supplies about seven months of median latency before a reader learns that a claim has been softened. If an LLM can label claim strength as well as an expert, why not attach that annotation at posting time on the preprint itself? That is a concrete downstream application for the same instrument they just validated — putting calibrated claim-confidence directly on the preprint and closing the gap that peer review currently fills. For open-science tooling this is the natural pipeline: LLM annotator on ingestion, human-agreement calibration in the background, structured claim metadata that downstream services can consume.

The obvious limitations, since the discussion is unusually candid. Abstracts, not full text — changes in methods, figures, and results are invisible. Only the first preprint version was compared to the published version, so author self-revision, reviewer influence, and journal production are bundled together as "peer review." Preprint deposition is selective, so the corpus describes what got preprinted and published rather than all submitted work. The retraction analysis is observational and not adjusted for differential time at risk. And claim stability between versions is a necessary but not sufficient condition for correctness — the study explicitly does not audit whether the claims are right, only whether they are stable.

The net take. For anyone who has spent the last few years defending preprints to more conservative colleagues, this is now the paper you cite. And for anyone building LLM-as-annotator pipelines on biomedical corpora, it is a rare example of one calibrated properly — locked codebook, human-agreement validation set, expert kappa reproduced by the model, results released as an open dataset with a searchable interface. The claim-level audit is a template. The preprint-reliability finding is the immediate payoff. The claim-strength annotator at posting time is the actual product.
