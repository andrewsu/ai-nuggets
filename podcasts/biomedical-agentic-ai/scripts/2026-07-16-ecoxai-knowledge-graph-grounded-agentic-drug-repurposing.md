## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI for Thursday, July 16, 2026. Today's pick is a fresh bioRxiv preprint out of Jason Moore's group at Cedars-Sinai, titled "EcoXAI: Autonomous Agentic Ecosystem for Explainable Artificial Intelligence and Biomedical Discovery." It landed as version one this week, and it is worth attention for a specific reason. This is one of the cleanest concrete demonstrations I have seen of an agentic system that is genuinely grounded in a biomedical knowledge graph, runs end-to-end without a human sitting in the loop, and produces a repurposing hypothesis you can actually check against the literature. That last part is the hard part, and they cleared it.

Let me set the scene. The recurring problem with LLM-driven biomedical discovery is that if you hand a raw language model a data question, it will happily generate biologically plausible-sounding but ungrounded output. The community's answer, over the last year and a half, has largely been retrieval-augmented generation — pipe some structured context into the prompt and hope that anchors the reasoning. EcoXAI takes a firmer stance. Rather than treating knowledge graph access as one more retrieval trick decorating a monolithic chat interface, it decomposes the analytical workflow into explicit, auditable pipeline stages. Ingest the dataset. Run exploratory analysis. Query the knowledge graph. Generate candidate hypotheses. Test them with methodology appropriate to the hypothesis type. Store the outcome in what the authors call discovery memory. Then loop.

The architectural choice here is the thing to notice. Each stage is a Docker microservice with its own logs, scripts, and outputs. The orchestration layer routes tasks to specialized agents equipped with what the paper calls skills — data normalization, exploratory analysis, hypothesis generation and testing, and knowledge graph interaction. The framework runs on top of modern agent harnesses like Claude Code, Codex, and OpenCode, and it is model-agnostic — you can point it at commercial APIs or a local model. That last option matters practically, because the authors report they did the entire study on a local Qwen 3.6 27B model running continuously for about two days, which they estimate would have cost over three hundred dollars in Sonnet 4.6 tokens. This is the shape of agentic biomedical AI that a mid-sized academic group can actually deploy — reproducibly, in a container, on their own hardware, against their own knowledge graph.

Now the concrete demonstration. They pointed EcoXAI at AlzKB, an Alzheimer's-focused knowledge graph maintained by the same group, and gave it a drug repurposing task. Two constraints made this interesting. First, EcoXAI was explicitly forbidden from using drugs already known to be associated with Alzheimer's. Second — and this is the sharper edge — it was forbidden from using any gene connected to Alzheimer's in the graph for feature selection. That is a strong constraint. It forces the agent to reason outward across the network rather than pattern-match on known disease genes. The workflow was to construct, for each candidate drug, a feature set derived from the graph — direct targets, first- and second-order interactors, shared processes, pathway-restricted subnetworks — train an XGBoost model on those features against ADSP genetic data, and compare its cross-validated performance to a randomized baseline built from one thousand models trained on random gene sets.

Of 103 drug candidates evaluated autonomously, 79 produced models that beat the randomized baseline. The pattern that emerged is genuinely useful. Direct drug targets alone frequently performed at or below the randomized baseline — the target list by itself was not informative. The expanded biological interaction networks, in contrast, substantially improved performance across immune modulators, kinase inhibitors, metabolic drugs, calcium channel blockers, and GPCR-targeting compounds. This is a coherent methodological finding: Alzheimer's risk is captured better by the neighborhood of a molecular target than by the target itself. And it emerged from the agent's own exploration, not a preplanned experimental design.

The headline hit is Maraviroc, a CCR5 antagonist that is a clinically approved HIV antiretroviral. EcoXAI autonomously constructed a seventy-seven-gene interaction network around it, hit a cross-validated AUC of 0.60 on the initial network, then, without prompting, expanded the network with second-order bridge genes and pushed AUC to 0.61 — comparable to the best human-curated models in prior AD repurposing studies. Notably, CCR5 sits three hops from Alzheimer's disease in AlzKB and all AD-associated genes were excluded during hypothesis generation. Yet the subsequent literature check found multiple independent lines of evidence linking CCR5 to AD — elevated expression in AD brains, involvement of the CCL5 axis in microglial amyloid-beta and tau signaling, and amyloid-induced CCR5 upregulation in brain endothelial cells. In other words, the agent recovered a biologically real mechanism through pure graph reasoning, without being told the answer.

The reason this paper matters is not the specific AD result — it is the shape. EcoXAI is a runnable existence proof that a containerized, KG-grounded, memory-augmented multi-agent system can do autonomous scientific hypothesis generation on standard hardware, at an academic cost point, and produce hypotheses that hold up against the literature. That is the direction the field needs to head.
