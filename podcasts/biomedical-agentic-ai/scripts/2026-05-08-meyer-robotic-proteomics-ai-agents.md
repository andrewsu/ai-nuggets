# 2026-05-08 — Robotic perturbation proteomics, with AI agents reading the read-out

Paper link: https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.64898/2026.05.04.722718v1

## Script

Welcome back to Biomedical Agentic AI for Friday, May 8, 2026.

Today's nugget comes from Jesse Meyer's group at Cedars-Sinai, posted on bioRxiv yesterday. The title is "Robotic perturbation proteomics and AI agents enable scalable drug mechanism discovery." It is one of the cleanest demonstrations I've seen this year of agentic AI doing real work on the analysis side of a high-throughput biology pipeline — not just chatting about data, but turning a pile of mass-spec measurements into mechanism-oriented hypotheses that then survive wet-lab and electronic-health-record validation. That's the part that makes this paper worth your five minutes.

Here's the setup. Most drug screens read out one or two phenotypes — viability, a reporter, a target-engagement assay. Meyer's lab wanted the systems-level view, which means proteomics on every condition. They built an end-to-end platform: semi-automated sample preparation, fast LC-MS-MS, and then — and this is the new part — an agentic AI layer that does the analysis and writes structured mechanism summaries. They ran 172 compounds through HepG2 cells, generated 1,232 proteomes, and quantified more than 8,700 proteins. Total wall-clock time, about three weeks. For perspective, the human-driven analysis of a single one of those datasets used to be a week's project on its own.

The agentic part is what I want to emphasize, because there's a real risk in this space of papers waving the word "agent" at what is really a fancy plot generator. That isn't what's happening here. The system takes the high-dimensional perturbation signature and produces what they call structured, mechanism-oriented summaries — meaning the agent is reading the proteomic delta, looking up what those proteins do, proposing which pathways are most consistent with the perturbation, and then — critically — generating experimentally testable hypotheses with enough specificity that someone can go to the bench and check them. Analysis time falls from days to under one day per compound. That's the kind of throughput change that turns a one-off biology paper into a platform.

And then they actually checked the agent's work. Two of the agent-generated hypotheses got promoted to wet-lab validation. The first: methylene blue — yes, the old blue dye that turns up in everything from antimalarial therapy to surgical staining — was flagged as a likely cholesterol-lowering agent based on the perturbation signature alone. They went and tested that in vitro and confirmed it. The second is more striking, because it left the lab entirely. The agent flagged a potential link between loratadine — the over-the-counter antihistamine — and increased circulating iron. They then went to a matched electronic-health-record cohort and found an association consistent with the in-vitro signature. That is an agentic AI proposing a real-world pharmacology hypothesis from proteomic data, and then a translational team confirming it in patient records. That is the loop everybody in this field is trying to close.

Let me say what I think is significant and what I think is still open. On the significant side: this is exactly the workflow Andrew, you and a lot of other computational biologists keep arguing should exist. Proteomics is finally cheap enough to run as a phenotypic screen. The bottleneck has shifted from data generation to interpretation. And interpretation is the place where an agent — armed with literature retrieval, pathway databases, and a willingness to write down its reasoning — is actually well-suited to add value. Methylene blue and loratadine both have decades of clinical history, but neither of these specific mechanistic links is in any standard textbook. The agent surfaced them in hours.

On the open side: 172 compounds is a screen, not a library. The cell line is HepG2, which is a hepatoma model with all the metabolic quirks that implies. The two validated hypotheses are exciting, but the paper doesn't tell us the false-discovery rate of the agent's mechanism summaries — what fraction of the agent's other hypotheses would also have validated, what fraction would have been wrong but plausible-sounding, and what fraction would have been obviously wrong if a domain expert had looked. That's the question I'd want answered before I deployed this on a thousand-compound library, and it's the hardest thing to evaluate honestly. The paper is clearly the start of something rather than the finished product.

A small craft note worth flagging. This paper is one of a small but growing pattern — STAT for spatial transcriptomics earlier this week, the Bota Bioscience compiler-verified protocols paper that also went up this week, and a few others — where the agentic AI sits in front of a specific instrument or assay class, not as a general copilot. That's the right level of abstraction. A general "biology agent" is hard. An agent that understands the shape of an LC-MS-MS perturbation matrix and knows which pathway databases to pull on is much more tractable, and Meyer's group has shown that the resulting system can do something useful end to end.
