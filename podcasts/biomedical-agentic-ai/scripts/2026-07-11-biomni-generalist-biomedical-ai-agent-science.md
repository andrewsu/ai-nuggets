# Biomni — a generalist biomedical AI agent in Science, an action space mined from 25 subfields, and a wet-lab-validated CRISPR clone

Paper link: https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adz4351

## Script

Today's pick is Biomni, published in Science yesterday, from the Leskovec group at Stanford with Aviv Regev on the senior-author line and a long list of Stanford Medicine collaborators — Michael Snyder, Le Cong, Jonathan Bernstein. The paper is titled "Autonomous biomedical research with an artificial intelligence agent." There was a bioRxiv preprint over a year ago, so the ideas are not brand new — but this is the peer-reviewed, Science-anointed version, and it is the closest the field has come to a genuine general-purpose biomedical AI agent.

Setup. The last year of agentic biology has been dominated by specialist systems — one agent for single-cell annotation, another for trajectory inference, another for cloning, another for variant prioritization. Every one of them wires a fixed set of tools into a fixed workflow, tuned for a specific task. Biomni asks the harder question. Can you build one agent that handles all of them, without task-specific tuning?

The architecture is two parts. First, Biomni-E1 is the environment — the biomedical action space the agent operates in. An action-discovery agent read the hundred most recent bioRxiv papers in each of the twenty-five bioRxiv subfields, so about twenty-five hundred papers, and extracted the specialized tasks, tools, databases, and software they used. Human experts validated the extractions and implemented them. The result is a curated environment of one hundred fifty specialized biomedical tools — things like wet-lab protocols, sgRNA design, motif enrichment — one hundred five software packages, and fifty-nine databases. Databases with web APIs, like PDB and OpenTargets and ClinVar, are wrapped in unified natural-language query functions where an LLM parses the schema on the fly. Databases without APIs are pulled locally into pandas dataframes.

Second, Biomni-A1 is the agent that operates in this environment. Three design choices matter. One, the agent uses a retrieval step to pull only the relevant subset of tools and databases into context for each query — because you cannot fit a hundred and fifty tool schemas into a prompt. Two, the action interface is code, not function calls. The agent writes Python, R, or Bash, executes it, reads the observation, and revises. This is the CodeAct pattern, and it matters because biomedical workflows involve loops, conditionals, parallel calls, and glue that does not fit a rigid function-call signature. Three, planning is adaptive — the agent drafts a bullet-list plan up front and refines it as observations come in.

Now the empirical claim, and it is a strong one. On the database-question-answering subset of LAB-Bench, Biomni scored seventy-four percent, matching human expert performance, versus forty-one percent for a plain coding agent. On the sequence-question-answering subset, eighty-two percent — above the human number. On the biomedical subset of Humanity's Last Exam, roughly triple the base LLM. Then the authors curated eight held-out real-world benchmarks — variant prioritization, GWAS causal gene detection, CRISPR screen design, rare disease diagnosis, drug repurposing, single-cell annotation, microbiome analysis, patient gene prioritization — and ran Biomni zero-shot with no task-specific tuning. Average relative gain over the base LLM was four hundred percent; over a plain coding agent, forty-three percent; over a ReAct-only ablation of Biomni itself, twenty percent. That last number is the one that tells you the code-based action interface, not just the environment, is where the win lives.

The case studies are what make this a Science paper rather than a benchmark paper. A researcher handed Biomni four hundred fifty-eight messy Excel files of wearable continuous glucose and skin temperature from thirty participants, plus sleep data and multi-omics on the same cohort. Biomni built a ten-step pipeline on its own, found a consistent postprandial thermogenic response averaging over two degrees Celsius, and surfaced a sleep-timing-beats-sleep-duration finding as narrative. A separate researcher pointed it at a published multi-omics dataset of human embryonic skeletal development — three hundred thirty-six thousand nuclei of paired single-nucleus RNA and ATAC-seq — and asked for gene regulatory network analysis. Biomni ran pySCENIC plus GRNBoost2 plus cisTarget plus AUCell in the right order, in a five-hour run, recapitulated the known RUNX2 story, and nominated AUTS2, ZFHX3, and PBX1 as under-appreciated regulators of skeletal cell fate. The wet-lab study is my favorite. Twenty cloning tasks — Golden Gate, Gibson, Gateway, restriction — scored blind by an external expert. Biomni matched the senior Stanford postdoc on accuracy and completeness. Then a real Biomni-designed protocol was executed at the bench: an sgRNA against B2M cloned into lentiCRISPR v2 Blast. Colonies grew. Sanger sequencing confirmed the insertion. That is the AI-biologist story with the wet-lab step actually taken.

Editorial reads. First, the significance is not that any single component here is new — code-as-action, retrieval-based tool selection, adaptive planning are all standard agent moves — but that they are integrated into an environment that spans twenty-five biomedical subfields and generalizes zero-shot. That is the actual claim. Second, the architectural bet against fixed workflows is worth internalizing. Every specialist agent paper you have read this year hardcodes a plan. Biomni composes one per task. When the field settles, that is likely the shape of the winner. Third, the action-discovery step — mine papers to define your tool universe — is a template other groups will copy. The authors are candid that their recency bias is a bug. Mining only the last hundred papers per subfield misses foundational methods that everyone still uses. Expect versions that pull deeper. Fourth, and this is the point that matters most for open science, the platform is live and free at biomni.stanford.edu. You can put your own dataset in front of it right now. The reproducibility posture is unusually strong for a Science paper.

Two caveats. The paper is honest that Biomni still trails experts on nuanced clinical judgment and novel experimental reasoning — it is a compiler of existing methods, not a discoverer of new ones. And the environment is finite. Every task you throw at it succeeds or fails based on whether the tool it needs was in the mine. But if you have been waiting for the flagship general-purpose biomedical AI agent to point at, this is the one.
