# Self-expanding reaction ontology — a multi-agent LLM writes verifiable SMIRKS rules, grows 68 classes to 14,073, and matches the proprietary standard across 666,000 patent reactions

Paper link: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.01061

## Script

Today's paper is a pretty striking demonstration of turning multi-agent large language models into a reliable engine for symbolic knowledge — specifically, using them to build and expand a chemistry ontology at a scale that manual curation could never reach. The work comes from Philippe Schwaller's group at EPFL, the same group behind the Molecular Transformer and RXN Mapper, and it targets one of the oldest bottlenecks in computer-assisted synthesis planning: the rulebook.

Here is the setup. If you want a computer to plan the synthesis of a target molecule, you need a catalog of reaction rules — patterns that say "any molecule with this shape can be transformed into that shape by this class of reaction". The commercial gold standard, called NameRxn, has been manually curated for years and contains a few thousand classes. The leading open-source alternative, called Reaction Insight, is smaller. Chemistry, though, follows a long-tail distribution. A handful of reaction families dominate; the tail of rare transformations is essentially endless, and each one demands the same careful hand-encoding as a boring amide coupling. Manual curation cannot keep up. And when your generative-chemistry model is forced to sample only from historically documented rules, it can never explore anywhere new either.

The pipeline in this paper attacks both problems — classification and rule encoding — with a five-agent LLM architecture running over roughly 666,000 reactions extracted from US patents. The key move is to reject any single-prompt approach. Long-context language models degrade in a U-shaped curve when you ask them to reason over a huge taxonomy in one pass, so the authors decompose the job. A coarse-hierarchy agent maps each reaction cohort to one of 68 seed classes drawn from the Royal Society of Chemistry's ontology of named reactions. A detailed classification agent refines the assignment down through the tree. A verifier agent independently audits the label against the reaction structure and either accepts or rejects it. When the verifier rejects — meaning the existing taxonomy has no good home for this reaction — a generator agent proposes a new class, and an aggregator agent atomically merges the proposal into the living hierarchy, deduplicating and resolving conflicts as it goes.

Run corpus-wide, that loop expanded the taxonomy from 68 seed classes to just over 14,000 distinct labels, entirely from the data with no human in the loop. And the labels are not hand-wavy. Using a confident-learning framework, with a lightweight fingerprint classifier trained on the LLM's own labels via cross-validation, the estimated noise rate for the expanded taxonomy is about 2.2 percent, versus 0.6 percent for NameRxn. Most of that noise turns out to be boundary ambiguity — legitimate cases where a reaction sits between two mechanistically overlapping classes, like amination of heteroaryl halides versus nucleophilic aromatic substitution. After adjudicating with a separate LLM-as-judge over just the flagged conflicts, the true mislabelling rate drops to roughly 0.3 percent, essentially comparable to the human-curated proprietary tool.

The second half of the pipeline is what makes this genuinely useful. For every class in that expanded taxonomy, the LLM writes an actual reaction template — a SMIRKS pattern that encodes the graph transformation at the reaction center. That is deterministic and computable: the pattern either matches a molecule or it does not. But language models are famously bad at SMILES and SMIRKS syntax, so the authors close the loop with iterative refinement. The model drafts a broad, high-recall SMIRKS pattern, an automated validator tests it against the corpus, and the model then applies chemically grounded mutations to prune false positives while keeping true-positive recall. What comes out the other side is a library of validated SMIRKS templates that a fast fingerprint classifier can match against unseen reactions at 97.7 percent strict-match accuracy at the third level of the hierarchy — matching the proprietary NameRxn and beating the open-source Reaction Insight, and doing it with an ontology that resolves chemistry more finely at the deeper levels.

The architecture has a nice practical property beyond the raw numbers. The deterministic SMIRKS layer is fast enough to sit inside a real-time synthesis-planning workflow. The slow language-model layer only fires on demand, when a reaction falls outside the current template coverage, and when it does, the system automatically proposes new taxonomy entries and keeps growing. It is a living reactivity database, not a static file.

Two things make this stand out for me. First, this is a really concrete example of using large language models as generators of symbolic artifacts you can inspect, edit, and match deterministically, rather than as end-to-end predictors whose errors propagate silently downstream. That is exactly the direction responsible AI in chemistry needs to head, and the multi-agent verification loop — draft a label, verify it, propose a new label if verification fails, atomically merge into a living hierarchy — is a clean template that generalizes well beyond reaction rules to any structured-knowledge domain where you want the LLM to grow an ontology from data. Second, the calibration is honest. The authors do not claim the language-model pipeline beat NameRxn outright. They report the noise breakdown, decompose it into boundary ambiguity versus genuine error, and only then argue for parity with the proprietary tool at matched resolution. That kind of care is what turns a demo into an infrastructure contribution.

The downstream that matters most, at least for this audience, is generative chemistry with a synthesizability constraint — which is essentially drug discovery. Right now, if you ask a generative model to design molecules that can actually be made, it is implicitly boxed in by whatever templates the curated rulebook happens to contain. A dynamically expandable, corpus-derived rule library changes that constraint: you can generate into previously inaccessible regions of chemical space with the guarantee that the transformation has a validated, executable rule to match. That is the piece I think will get picked up first by other groups.
