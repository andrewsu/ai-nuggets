## Summary

OptimusKG is a new multimodal biomedical labeled-property graph, posted to arXiv on April 29 by Lucas Vittor, Ayush Noori, Iñaki Arango, Joaquín Polonuer, Sam Rodriques, Andrew White, David Clifton, and Marinka Zitnik — that author list pulls together FutureHouse, the Zitnik lab at Harvard, and Clifton at Oxford. The graph holds 190 thousand nodes, 21.8 million edges, and 67 million property instances drawn from 18 ontologies and controlled vocabularies, and the design choice that matters is that it is a labeled property graph rather than a triple-store — schema is enforced at the top level, but type-specific properties, cross-references, and provenance are kept on the edges and nodes themselves. The validation is the part that lands hardest for this audience: the team used PaperQA3, FutureHouse's literature-grounded multimodal agent, to check whether sampled edges had supporting evidence in the published literature. Seventy percent of sampled real edges were supported, 83.4 percent of synthetic false edges were correctly rejected, and the unsupported real edges concentrated in experimental and functional-genomics resources — the authors' read is that the graph captures knowledge that may precede synthesis in the secondary literature, which is the right read. The combination is an explicit demonstration of the architectural commitment this podcast has been tracking all week — curated, schema-constrained biomedical infrastructure plus an LLM agent that reads papers, jointly, in the same pipeline.

## Script

Today's nugget is an arXiv preprint that posted last Wednesday from a notable cross-institution collaboration. The paper is OptimusKG — Unifying Biomedical Knowledge in a Modern Multimodal Graph. Eight authors. Lucas Vittor, Ayush Noori, Iñaki Arango, Joaquín Polonuer, Sam Rodriques, Andrew White, David Clifton, and Marinka Zitnik. The interesting thing about this author list, before we touch the science, is which groups it stitches together. Sam Rodriques and Andrew White and Joaquín Polonuer are FutureHouse — the AI-for-science non-profit that built PaperQA, BixBench, AviaryBench, and the agent-stack we have been arguing about all month on this show. Ayush Noori, Lucas Vittor, and Marinka Zitnik are the Zitnik lab at Harvard — TxGNN, PrimeKG, the people who have been writing the methods papers on biomedical knowledge graphs for the better part of the last five years. David Clifton is Oxford. That is FutureHouse plus Zitnik plus Clifton on a single biomedical knowledge graph paper. That kind of author list is itself a signal — the agent people and the knowledge-graph people are now publishing together, not in parallel.

Let me give you the numbers up front because they matter. OptimusKG has 190,531 nodes across 10 entity types, 21,813,816 edges across 26 relation types, 67,249,863 property instances encoding 110,276,843 values across 150 distinct property keys. The graph is built from 18 ontologies and controlled vocabularies. It spans molecular, anatomical, clinical, and environmental domains. It is bigger than PrimeKG, the Zitnik lab's previous flagship — PrimeKG had on the order of four million relationships. OptimusKG has roughly five times that. But the more important difference is not size — it is the architectural choice.

PrimeKG is conceptually a triple-store with semantics. Subject, predicate, object. OptimusKG is built as a labeled property graph — an LPG. That distinction is a few minutes of background that I want to give properly because it is the load-bearing design decision in the paper, and it is not the kind of decision that gets noticed when you skim the abstract.

In the RDF and triple-store tradition, knowledge is encoded as edges between entities. Drug X targets Protein Y. Gene G is associated with Disease D. To attach metadata to the edge — the evidence source, the experimental modality, the confidence — you have to reify the edge. You make the edge itself an entity, you give it an identifier, and you then attach properties to that entity. It works. It is also, by common consent, a pain in the neck. The edge becomes a node, the graph schema bloats, and querying degrades.

In a labeled property graph, the edge itself can carry properties. So the edge that says Drug X targets Protein Y can also carry, on the edge, the experimental assay it was derived from, the publication, the cell line, the confidence score, whatever metadata the source had. No reification. The schema stays clean. The query stays simple. This is the model that Neo4j popularized and it is the model that has been quietly winning in industry biomedical KGs for five-plus years even as academic methods papers have continued to use the triple-store framing.

Why does this matter for OptimusKG specifically. Because the paper is explicit that the graph is built from 18 ontologies and controlled vocabularies, and the things you want to keep when you import an ontology are exactly the type-specific properties — cross-references to other identifier systems, evidence codes, hierarchy positions, provenance — that get awkward in triples. The LPG choice means the harmonization step does not have to flatten the source. You can import a Gene Ontology term with its evidence codes intact, you can import a UniProt entry with its cross-references intact, you can import an OMIM entry with its modes of inheritance intact, and they sit in the same graph without losing the source-level richness. The paper frames this as resolving the historical tension between knowledge graphs derived from unstructured text — which lack schema — and graphs derived from structured resources — which are hard to harmonize. The LPG is the resolution.

Now the part of the paper that should land hardest for the audience of this show. The validation. Most KG papers stop at structural statistics and call it done. OptimusKG is validated by an LLM agent reading the literature. Specifically — PaperQA3, FutureHouse's literature-grounded multimodal agent, the descendant of the original PaperQA that Andrew White's group has been iterating on for two years. The team sampled edges from OptimusKG and asked PaperQA3 whether the published scientific literature supports each edge. PaperQA3 found supporting evidence for 70 percent of sampled real edges. The team also constructed synthetic false edges as negative controls. PaperQA3 correctly identified 83.4 percent of those synthetic false edges as unsupported. So the agent has substantial discrimination — high agreement on real edges, high rejection on false ones — and the gap between the two numbers tells you the precision-recall trade.

The interesting failure mode, and the authors flag this honestly, is what happens with the 30 percent of real edges PaperQA3 could not find support for. That set was not random. It concentrated in associations derived from experimental and functional-genomics resources — perturbation screens, expression atlases, the kinds of high-throughput data assets that release into structured databases before the systematic review and synthesis paper is written. The authors' interpretation is that OptimusKG captures biomedical knowledge that may precede synthesis in the literature. That is the right interpretation. It is also the interpretation Andrew Su has been articulating about MyGene, MyDisease, and the Wikidata-bound biomedical entity layer for years. The structured-data layer is sometimes ahead of the prose. The fact that PaperQA3 can detect this gap, on a sample of 21 million edges, is itself a useful capability — you now have a way of identifying which edges in the KG are literature-confirmed and which are downstream-of-experimental-data-but-not-yet-in-a-review.

And the team validates that KG using a literature-reading agent — which is the operationalization of the Hardi paradigm in reverse. The agent does not just consume the KG. The agent audits it.

Two cautions before I get to what this means for the Su Lab.

First, a 70 percent agent-supported edge rate is not a 70 percent KG accuracy rate. PaperQA3 has its own miss rate on retrieval. An edge that is biologically real but obscure may show up as unsupported simply because the relevant paper is not in PaperQA3's reachable corpus. So the 70 number is a lower bound on agent-confirmable evidence, not on truth. The 83.4 rejection rate on synthetic false edges is the more meaningful number — it tells you the agent is not just rubber-stamping everything you give it. But anyone who reads the paper should be careful not to over-translate the headline 70 into a calibration number for the whole graph.

Second — and this is a recurring critique I have of the labeled-property-graph literature — the schema constraints in an LPG are softer than in a strict typed RDF schema with class hierarchies and cardinality constraints. The paper says the graph enforces a top-level schema for nodes and edges. What that means in practice depends a lot on how strict the enforcement is at ingestion time. If the schema is "these are the entity types, these are the relation types," that is useful but it is not the same as a fully reasoned type system. I would want to see the schema specification in detail, and I would want to know what happens when a source resource has an entity that doesn't fit. The paper is preprint stage so this is not a fatal critique, just a flag.

Now — what this means for the Su Lab and adjacent groups, because this is the audience this podcast is for.

OptimusKG is a knowledge graph people in this audience are likely to want to sit on top of. Not necessarily as the sole substrate — Wikidata still has properties OptimusKG does not, MyGene and MyDisease are still the right shape for the use cases they were built for, Open Targets has therapeutic-area depth no general graph matches — but as a complementary, larger, schema-aware substrate that an agent can query. The 18 source ontologies are likely overlapping with the ones MyDisease and MyGene already harmonize from, but OptimusKG bundles them in a graph form that is set up for graph traversal. If you have an agent doing multi-hop reasoning over biomedical relationships, OptimusKG is now a candidate backend.

The PaperQA3 validation methodology is also portable. You can run it against any knowledge graph, including the Wikidata-bound biomedical layer. There is a publishable paper in someone running PaperQA3 against the Wikidata biomedical entity graph and reporting the agent-confirmable rate per entity type. I would read that paper. So would Andrew. The methodology of agent-validates-KG is going to become standard in the next 12 months and there is a first-mover advantage in being the people who run it on the open-data biomedical layer.
