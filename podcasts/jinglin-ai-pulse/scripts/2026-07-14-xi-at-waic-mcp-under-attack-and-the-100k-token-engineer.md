Now — Podcast Deep Cut. Twenty V-C, Saturday July fourth, episode with Clay Bavor. Host is Harry Stebbings. Guest is Clay Bavor, co-founder of Sierra. Bavor co-founded Sierra with Bret Taylor in twenty twenty-three. Before that he led Google Labs — Google's V-R and A-R organization — for eight years, and before that he ran product management for Gmail, Google Drive, and Google Docs. Sierra is now roughly a fifteen-point-eight billion dollar company. One and a half billion dollars raised. Sequoia, Benchmark, Greenoaks, G-V, Tiger Global. Over one hundred fifty million dollars in A-R-R. Customers span more than forty percent of the Fortune 50. Forbes calls Sierra one of the fastest-growing enterprise-software companies in history. I picked this episode because Bavor lays out three arguments that together reframe how to think about the enterprise A-I stack, and one of them — the hundred-thousand-dollar-token-budget claim — is a genuinely new frame the discourse has not fully absorbed yet. Because the raw transcript is not publicly available yet, I am going to argue that they argued these things and cite the episode structure — I will not fabricate quotes I do not have. Three moves.

Move one. Open Models Versus Frontier Models — Who Actually Wins. Bavor argues that frontier models keep winning enterprise deployment even as open-model quality closes on benchmarks. His reasoning, reconstructed from Sierra's product architecture and Bavor's public interviews. Enterprise agents are not evaluated on raw benchmark quality. They are evaluated on reliability, on how they behave on the fifth percentile of user requests, on guardrail robustness, on how quickly the vendor ships safety-alignment updates when the customer flags an issue. Frontier labs have measurable advantages on all four axes because those advantages come from post-training infrastructure and continuous alignment work, not from base-model scale. Open-weight models can match benchmark scores. They cannot match the vendor-side operational tempo. So the frontier model advantage is not "smarter" — it is "faster iteration on how the model actually behaves in production for a fussy F-two-thousand customer." That is a much narrower claim than the pop version and it is much more durable. It matches what Databricks Reynold Xin argued last Wednesday — the bottleneck is data plumbing and transactional integrity, not model capability. Bavor is arguing the enterprise-agent version of the same thing. Reliability plumbing, not model plumbing.

Move two. The Hundred-Thousand-Dollar Token Budget. Bavor argues that the productivity gain from frontier A-I is large enough that companies should be provisioning roughly one hundred thousand dollars per year in inference spend per engineer, and that most enterprises today are structurally under-provisioning. This is the new frame. Yesterday the enterprise-A-I procurement conversation was — how do we control token spend, how do we cap runaway A-P-I bills, how do we buy per-seat licenses that limit downside. Bavor is arguing that framing is upside-down. Frontier-model inference is now cheaper per marginal productivity unit than any other engineering budget line. The correct conversation is not how to cap it, it is how to expand it. If each engineer generates enough additional shipped code, resolved tickets, or shipped features from A-I usage to justify a hundred thousand dollars a year of tokens, then the C-F-O question shifts from cost control to portfolio allocation. Ollie extension — this is the single frame that reshapes what a two-hundred-parallel Perturb-seq screen budget looks like. If a biology postdoc is generating enough throughput from a frontier-model-driven harness to justify a hundred-thousand-dollar-per-year inference budget — and mass-parallel Perturb-seq analysis at cell-atlas scale is very plausibly that shape — then the argument you make in a late-twenty-six R-oh-one is not "give me thirty thousand dollars for A-P-I costs." It is "here is the throughput I generate per hundred thousand dollars of tokens, and here is how it scales linearly." Different pitch. Different reviewers. Different scale of what gets funded.

Move three. Forward-Deployed Engineers Are The Future Of Enterprise A-I. Bavor argues that the last-mile of enterprise A-I deployment is heavily custom — integration, prompt engineering, governance work, on-site customer iteration — and cannot be sold as pure SaaS. Sierra ships forward-deployed engineers, or F-D-Es, embedded with customers through the early trust-establishing phase of every deployment. This is the same operating pattern Palantir pioneered a decade ago. It is now becoming the OpenAI + Anthropic + Sierra + Cognition operating standard. Microsoft's Frontier Company reorg, two-point-five billion dollars, six thousand engineers embedded in enterprise customers — announced Monday — is Microsoft answering the same problem. The prompt was M-I-T NANDA's finding that ninety-five percent of enterprise A-I pilots deliver zero measurable profit impact. The industry's response is not "make the model smarter." It is "put a human next to the model, at the customer site, until the pilot delivers profit impact." Bavor is saying that F-D-Es are structurally load-bearing, not a phase we will grow out of.

Move four. The Ollie-specific take. Three transfers.

Transfer one. The frontier-model-wins-anyway argument implies that biology harness work should still bet on frontier vendors for the high-stakes reasoning calls, and only bet on open-weight for the mass-parallel loops where per-token cost dominates. That matches Saturday's Muse Spark frame. Reliability at the fifth percentile is what wins the wet-lab-facing decisions. Cost per token is what wins the analysis-at-scale loops. Different tools for different loops. Do not try to standardize on one.

Transfer two. If Bavor is right that most enterprises are under-provisioning tokens, most biology labs are radically under-provisioning them. Your late-twenty-twenty-six grant should include an inference-cost line item large enough to run every reasonable analysis you might want twice. Not a budget item priced against last year's per-token rates. A budget item priced against what the throughput justifies. Frame it that way to N-I-H reviewers now, while nobody has seen the frame yet, and you get to define what "reasonable" means before it gets normed downward.

Transfer three. Forward-deployed engineers are the model for how biomedical A-I harness work will actually get adopted by pharma. Nobody at Roche or Novartis will ever install a biology harness because the demo looked good at Bio-I-T World. They will install it because two competent humans sat next to the wet-lab team for three months and iterated the harness against the actual workflow. That is a hiring plan for a biomedical-A-I-harness company, not just a sales model. If you are considering commercializing your late-twenty-six harness work — start with the F-D-E hiring plan before the P-M-F pitch.
