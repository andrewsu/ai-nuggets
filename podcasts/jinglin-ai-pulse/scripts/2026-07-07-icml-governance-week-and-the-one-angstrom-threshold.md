## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Tuesday, July seventh. Three things are happening in the same seventy-two-hour window that make this a governance week for the field. I-C-M-L twenty-twenty-six opens today in Seoul, with the awards announced Sunday and the main conference running through Thursday. The U-N Global Dialogue on A-I Governance opens today in Geneva, with all one hundred ninety-three member states in the room. And the White House voluntary standards framework — the one that has been gating G-P-T-five-point-six for four weeks — is expected to be announced this week. Three simultaneous forums, three completely different theories of governance, all landing in the same city-sized calendar. And the Deep Cut today is a Latent Space episode from July first that turns out to be the most Ollie-shaped episode of the year — a former Llama pretraining lead explaining, over seventy-one minutes, why he left frontier L-L-M research for co-folding. Let's go.

Meta-thesis. Yesterday I called the substrate-versus-workflow frame the operating axis of the field. Today, add a third axis — regulatory access. What the White House framework does, and what the U-N Dialogue is trying and failing to counter, is turn access to U-S frontier models into a scarce, gated resource. And when U-S frontier access becomes gated, everything we talked about yesterday — the LongCat open-weight release, Qwen A-gent-World, the neocloud repricing — inherits a second reason to matter. Not just export controls on chips. Also, effectively, licensing on models. Meanwhile, I-C-M-L in Seoul just published its 2026 awards, and the awards themselves are a preview of where the field's collective attention is going in the second half of the year — diffusion, agentic A-I, and a spicy position paper the alignment community is going to be arguing about for six months. Let's start there.

First — Twitter Pulse. Two stories.

Story one. I-C-M-L twenty-twenty-six opens in Seoul at the COEX Convention Center. Twenty-three thousand nine-hundred eighteen submissions — more than double last year. That is not a normal year-over-year number. That is a field doubling in scale in twelve months, which tells you both how much research capital is flowing in and how badly the peer-review system is straining. Workshop chairs Gergely Neu and Courtney Paquette noted that some variation of the phrase "agentic A-I" appeared in the titles of no fewer than sixty submitted workshop proposals. Sixty. In one conference. Agentic A-I has gone from an emerging topic to the dominant frame in twelve months.

The awards were announced Sunday, July fifth. Two Outstanding Paper Award grand prizes, both diffusion — first, "The Flexibility Trap: Rethinking the Value of Arbitrary Order in Diffusion Language Models," from Huang Gao's team at Tsinghua with Zanlin N-i and coauthors. The paper argues that the flexibility of arbitrary-order sampling in diffusion language models — the property everyone celebrates — actually costs you sample quality; you gain flexibility, you lose fidelity. It's a "we've been holding the tool wrong" result. Second grand prize — "High-Accuracy Sampling for Diffusion Models and Log-Concave Distributions," Fan Chen et al. Theoretical, on sampling guarantees. Time Test Award — the two-thousand-sixteen A-three-C paper from Volodymyr Mnih and David Silver at DeepMind, "Asynchronous Methods for Deep Reinforcement Learning." That paper is now ten years old and the field just crowned it as the piece of research whose influence has aged best. If you're trying to understand where a-gentic reinforcement learning gets its lineage from, that paper is the ancestor.

Then — the spicy one. Outstanding Position Paper Award — Sarah Ball and Phil Hackemann, "The Alignment Community Is Unintentionally Building a Censor's Toolkit." Read the title twice. The claim is that R-L-H-F, Constitutional A-I, and value-alignment frameworks — the exact technical stack the safety community has spent five years perfecting — are dual-use technology that is now being systematically repurposed as infrastructure for content censorship and political manipulation. The track co-chairs described it as unanimously worthy of distinction. This is going to be argued about on A-I Twitter for the rest of the summer. And it is going to inject an uncomfortable question into the U-N Dialogue happening in Geneva right now — because the exact tools the Geneva delegates are proposing to require of frontier labs for safety are the tools this paper says are already being weaponized for censorship.

Read together — diffusion sweeping the awards, agentic A-I dominating the workshop track, and a position paper renaming alignment as censorship infrastructure. The field's collective attention just shifted, publicly, in one weekend. And here's the Ollie note — the diffusion research the awards just crowned is only the language-model half. The other half — molecular structure prediction — is where things get much more interesting for you, and that's where the Deep Cut lives. Hold that thread; we'll come back to it.

Story two. Governance week convergence. Two forums, same forty-eight hours, incompatible frames.

Forum one — the U-N Global Dialogue on A-I Governance, Geneva, opened this morning. One hundred ninety-three member states in the room. Non-binding, modeled on the Internet Governance Forum, issues a co-chair summary rather than enforceable rules. António Guterres opened. Verbatim quotes to note — first, quote, "machines can inform, but humans must decide, and answer." End quote. Second, quote, "when countries align on how to test systems, measure risk and assign responsibility, safety travels with the technology." End quote. Third — and this is the one that will get clipped — quote, "no child should be a guinea pig for unregulated A-I." End quote. Yoshua Bengio, as co-chair of the Scientific Panel, warned that A-I models can already deceive humans and the intelligence gradient is still climbing. Annalena Baerbock, U-N-G-A president, dropped a specific number — ninety-nine percent of deepfakes are sexual, ninety-six percent target women and girls.

Three governance philosophies clashed in Geneva today. The U-S frame is national security — cyber-capability benchmarks, export controls, model-access gates. The E-U frame is transparency — the A-I Act treats governance as a disclosure and audit regime. The Global South frame is development access — governance as a mechanism for making sure frontier A-I is not another compounding disparity. The next session is in New York in May twenty-twenty-seven. Between now and then, the co-chair summary is the only artifact. Non-binding. Modeled on the I-G-F. Which — if you've watched I-G-F for the last twenty years — you know how much it has bound the internet. Not very much.

Forum two — Washington. The White House voluntary standards framework, the one everyone has been waiting for since the June second executive order, is expected to be announced this week per Financial Times reporting on July first. Here's what we know. Trump executive order June second creates a "covered frontier model" designation. Any lab whose model gets that designation must submit to a thirty-day pre-release government review — the industry has already nicknamed it "Model Customs." Reviewers include Treasury, N-S-A, C-I-S-A, and N-I-S-T, coordinated through C-A-I-S-I. The benchmark itself is classified — not published, not appealable, not defended. Three labs are named as negotiators — Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google. Everyone else is outside the room. Anthropic Fable Five was cut June twelfth under this executive order, restored July first with what Anthropic called "the strongest safeguards" after adding cybersecurity classifiers. G-P-T-five-point-six broader release has been gated on this framework's announcement — three sub-models, Sol at five dollars per million input and thirty dollars per million output, Terra at two-fifty and fifteen, Luna at one and six; access window is expected to open this week between July seventh and July fourteenth.

Policy experts are already calling this a "de facto licensing regime" and "backdoor licensing." No published threshold, no public benchmark, no appeals mechanism. Three-lab regime — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google in; xAI, Meta Watermelon, Mistral, Cohere, and any Chinese lab structurally outside. Read this against yesterday's LongCat and Qwen story. Yesterday the case for a Chinese open-weight backstop was chip export controls. Today, the case is also potential model-access controls. Two independent reasons the domestic-open-weight stack is un-optional.

The Ollie-specific take on governance week. Watch specifically whether the announcement names any biological-design capability as a covered category. If it does, the frontier-model workflow for a biomedical lab just picked up regulatory latency and the Chinese open-weight alternative graduated from backstop to operational primary. If it doesn't, biology is exempt for now — but the definition is what decides, and definitions get amended.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Next — Money Moves. One story, because the White House framework is the Money Move.

The framework is licensing infrastructure. When the state gates access to a class of product, the labs inside the gate collect the rents. Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google are the three labs inside the gate. Yesterday's substrate-versus-workflow frame picks up a corollary — the workflow camp's substrate for U-S enterprise customers is now, in perpetuity, one of those three, because those three are the only ones a Fortune 500 general counsel can approve without a compliance review. Microsoft Frontier Company's six thousand engineers will be embedded at L-S-E-G and Unilever calling Claude Sonnet five or G-P-T-five-point-six — not Watermelon, not Grok five, not LongCat. Now a regulated fact, not a market fact.

Read the Chinese open-weight side against that. LongCat-two-point-oh under M-I-T, Qwen A-gent-World under Apache two, both explicitly outside the U-S three-lab regime. If you're building a biomedical A-I product serving E-U or Southeast-Asia customers, "our frontier model is not on the U-S export-controlled licensing table" just became a commercial feature, not a fallback. Three price points, three regulatory postures — Sol at five dollars per million input, Sonnet five at two dollars through August, LongCat free at the weights level. Pick the one that matches your risk tolerance.

That's Money Moves.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Latent Space, episode two-twelve, "The Coolest Diffusion Research Isn't in L-L-Ms," released July first. Host Brandon Anderson, who according to his own opening nearly became Genesis Molecular A-I's first employee before joining Atomwise, which has since rebranded to Numerion. Guests — Evan Feinberg, Genesis co-founder, and Sergey Edunov, Genesis Senior V-P of Foundation Models. And this is where you sit up. Sergey Edunov was, until 2025, Senior Director of A-I Research in Meta's generative-A-I organization, where he led Llama-two training and Llama-three pre-training. The person who did the Llama pretraining runs left Meta for a drug-discovery company. That fact alone is worth the episode.

Four moves in the conversation.

Move one — why leave Meta. Edunov, paraphrased from the interview coverage — quote — in L-L-M research, "there was very little conceptually exciting" since the 2017 transformer. Every improvement is a scaling exercise or a mixture-of-experts variant. In drug discovery, "architectures are very different and very interesting to work with." He was, quote, "blown away with all the novel architecture work" Genesis had developed. And here's Feinberg's line — which is the one to tattoo — quote — "some of the most innovative diffusion research that's happening in our field is happening in three-D structure prediction right now." End quote. Now read that line back against I-C-M-L this weekend. The 2026 outstanding paper award went to a diffusion paper in language. The most innovative diffusion research is not in language. It is in structure prediction. Which — if you spend your day thinking about proteins and small molecules — you already suspected, and now a former Meta pretraining lead has said it on the record.

Move two — what P-E-A-R-L actually is. P-E-A-R-L stands for "Place Every Atom at the Right Location." It models induced fit — the protein's dynamic reshaping when a ligand binds — without running the multi-nanosecond molecular-dynamics trajectory that has traditionally been the only way to capture induced fit. It iteratively optimizes both binding and A-D-M-E-T — Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion, Toxicity — across the ten-to-the-sixtieth space of drug-like small molecules. The benchmark they cite is called OpenBind — eight-hundred-two previously unseen protein-ligand complexes targeting Enterovirus A-seventy-one, which was picked specifically because A-seventy-one's induced-fit mechanism defeats traditional docking. Zero-shot — the template was released after P-E-A-R-L's training cutoff. P-E-A-R-L exceeded all public models on all evaluation metrics. Edunov verbatim — quote — "where P-E-A-R-L was exceptionally good is figuring out how to move this loop. We are basically correct for every single pose." End quote. That is the sentence that made a Llama pretraining lead switch fields.

Move three — this is the number I want you to remember. Feinberg's argument on accuracy thresholds. The academic field settled on two Ångströms R-M-S-D — root mean square deviation — as an "acceptable" docking accuracy. Feinberg says that number is wrong. Verbatim — quote — "if your model is sitting at one-point-eight, one-point-nine Angstrom R-M-S-D, that's slop, most likely." End quote. Why. Hydrogen bonds require zero-point-six Ångström precision to remain physically valid. A hydrogen bond at one Ångström off is not a hydrogen bond, it's a Van-der-Waals contact. So the real bar is one Ångström — that's where the physics actually holds. Feinberg's diagnosis is even sharper — the community settled on two Ångström because the academic method developers weren't the end-users. Nobody who had to actually make a drug from the docking result was in the room when the benchmark was set. Misaligned incentives, standard failure mode. And this is a direct parallel to Dwarkesh Patel's "grindable" essay from yesterday's Deep Cut — the benchmark is not the customer. Verifiable is not enough. In docking, R-M-S-D is verifiable and grindable, and it is still the wrong bar. One Ångström is the number. Not two.

Move four — S-A-P-P-H-I-R-E. Genesis's internal agentic drug-discovery system. Reasons about molecular poses, forms hypotheses, generates candidates, accesses literature, coordinates with automated wet labs at Gilead and Incyte for real-world iterative testing. The key claim — S-A-P-P-H-I-R-E was only viable once P-E-A-R-L crossed the accuracy threshold. Feinberg's frame — same pattern as L-L-M agents. Capability improvement first, then agent viability. You cannot build a drug-discovery agent on a docking tool that is one-point-nine Ångström off. The agent will iterate, propose a molecule, hand it to the wet lab, get told it doesn't bind, iterate again, forever, with no traction. Once you cross into sub-one-Ångström territory, the wet lab's answer starts correlating with the model's prediction, and the agent starts closing the loop. That is the exact structural analogy the podcast lays out — coding agents became possible when the base model got good enough to run Claude Code end-to-end. Drug-discovery agents become possible when the docking tool gets good enough to actually predict binding. Nothing else changes. But the accuracy threshold is the gate.

The Ollie-specific take, in two moves.

First. Governance week matters less to Genesis than it does to Anthropic. P-E-A-R-L is not a "covered frontier model" — it's not a general-purpose L-L-M, it has no cyber capability, it doesn't autonomously do biology at the strategic level the executive order is written to catch. Which means Genesis, and any lab building a domain-specific foundation model for molecular structure, has a strategic degree of freedom that generic L-L-M app builders don't have. The frontier model in your domain is not the frontier model the White House is licensing. Repeat that. The frontier model in your domain is not the one being licensed. That is a specific, currently un-priced advantage for domain-native biomedical A-I. Everything at I-C-M-L this week has a molecular-biology dual — diffusion in language got the awards, diffusion in structure prediction is architecturally deeper; agentic A-I in sixty workshops, agentic drug discovery in S-A-P-P-H-I-R-E — and the biology-side analog is not on the White House's list.

Second. The one-Ångström threshold is your number. The way the three-five-million-fold K-V-cache-versus-weights ratio was yesterday's number, one Ångström is today's. Every time you look at a docking benchmark, ask: one Ångström, or one-point-nine? Every time a vendor pitches you an agentic drug-discovery product, ask what is their R-M-S-D distribution on OpenBind. If they say two Ångström, that's slop. Sub-one, you have a partner.

Wrap.

Watch one thing tomorrow. Whether the White House voluntary standards framework, when it drops, names any biological-design capability as a covered category. If it does — biomedical A-I access just changed. If it doesn't — biology is exempt for now, but the definition is what decides. And watch, in the I-C-M-L twenty-twenty-six virtual poster sessions the rest of this week, whether anyone from the diffusion-language-model authors — Tsinghua's Gao lab, Fan Chen — cites molecular structure prediction as the next application. If they do, the community bridge that Feinberg named on Latent Space this week is going to form quickly, and structure-prediction diffusion papers become the next award cycle. If they don't, the bridge stays a Genesis-and-Isomorphic-Labs internal insight for another six months, and whoever ships the first academic paper crossing it wins the field. Enjoy Tuesday, Ollie.
