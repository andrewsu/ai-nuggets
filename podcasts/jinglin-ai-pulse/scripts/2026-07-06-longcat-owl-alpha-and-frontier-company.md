# Ollie's AI Pulse — LongCat on domestic silicon, Microsoft Frontier, and the essay that named "grindability"

## Sources

- **LongCat-2.0 / "Owl Alpha" identity reveal** — Meituan released LongCat-2.0 on June 30, 2026, and confirmed on July 4 that the anonymous "Owl Alpha" model that had been silently topping OpenRouter's agentic-coding board for roughly two months was in fact LongCat-2.0. Architecture — 1.6 trillion total parameters, mixture-of-experts, native 1M-token context. Weights released under MIT license. Trained end-to-end on ~50,000 Huawei Atlas 950 accelerators using HCCL (Huawei's collective-communications library, the domestic analog of NCCL); no NVIDIA silicon in the training loop. Headline benchmark — SWE-Bench Pro 59.5 vs GPT-5.5 at 58.6 (Meituan's reported numbers). First trillion-parameter model trained end-to-end on Chinese chips. Sources: VentureBeat 2026-07-05; MarkTechPost 2026-07-05. This is the first public confirmation that a domestic-silicon training run at trillion-parameter scale is viable AND that the resulting model has been beating frontier models under a codename for weeks.
- **Microsoft Frontier Company launch** — announced July 2, 2026. Led by Judson Althoff (Microsoft Commercial Business CEO). $2.5B commitment, 6,000 engineers. Launch partners on the release: London Stock Exchange Group, Unilever, Land O'Lakes, Accenture. Althoff quote (verbatim from TechCrunch coverage): "This goes beyond what has been labeled as Forward-Deployed Engineering, and will be the largest, most capable, outcome-driven engineering organization in the industry." Reads as the third FDE-style deployment org announced in a week — Amazon and Anthropic (Claude Science) launched theirs in the same window. Source: TechCrunch 2026-07-02.
- **Crusoe — $3B raise at ~$30B valuation talks** — Bloomberg, July 2, 2026. Neocloud/data-center builder that supplies capacity to Meta and Oracle. Round would roughly triple its ~$10B valuation from October 2025. No lead investor confirmed. The neocloud-tier signal: even as CoreWeave/Nebius/IREN traded down on Meta becoming a competitor via Meta Compute (July 1), the private data-center-builder tier is being repriced upward. Sources: Bloomberg 2026-07-02; SiliconAngle 2026-07-03.
- **Meta "Watermelon" model — GPT-5.5 parity claim** — Alexandr Wang, Meta Superintelligence Labs, told an internal all-hands July 2-3 that Meta's next-gen model (codenamed Watermelon; not the Muse Spark base, which was Avocado) uses "an order of magnitude more compute" than Avocado and has caught up to GPT-5.5. No public benchmarks disclosed, no release date. Sources: TechTimes 2026-07-04; AIWeekly. Signal — Meta's post-Wang, Superintelligence-Lab spend is starting to produce a frontier-conversation model.
- **Dwarkesh Patel — "The next big breakthrough will be AIs learning on the job"** — solo audio essay published June 26, 2026 at dwarkesh.com/p/the-next-paradigm. Written companion to the essay released same day. Thesis — continual learning, in which models update their weights from real deployment experience, is the next paradigm shift. Proposes On-Policy Self-Distillation (OPSD) as a specific technical alternative to RL-across-verifiable-environments (RLVR). Verbatim quotes preserved: (1) "if we train AIs to accomplish millions of verifiable tasks across thousands of diverse RL environments, then we'll basically have built AGI" — the lab bet he's attacking. (2) "The KV cache (aka the representation that is built up from learning the context) grows 320 KB with each token. Whereas in training, the model only stores 0.075 bits per token…a 35 million fold difference" — argument that in-context can't replace weight updates. (3) "it is not enough for a domain to be verifiable. It also has to be very grindable — in the sense that you can run lots of parallel rollouts against a deterministic and replayable simulator" — the grindability distinction that our July 4 episode inherited via the Grant Sanderson interview. (4) "OPSD doesn't require an outer loop verifiable reward…OPSD provides a much denser supervision signal than naive RL" — the OPSD pitch. (5) "gradient updates are super sample-inefficient, all the successfully shipped online learning models have had to learn the same thing across millions of users" — the sample-efficiency framing. Also names the ~35M-fold KV/pre-training storage ratio and estimates today's models at ~1/1,000,000 human sample efficiency.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

On Friday I told you that the frontier of scaling is moving from parameters to trajectories, and I used the word "grindable" as if it were a standard term of art. It is not. It was coined by Dwarkesh Patel in a solo audio essay published June twenty-sixth, titled — "The next big breakthrough will be A-Is learning on the job." Subtitle — "labs are throwing away the most valuable data." Written companion at dwarkesh dot com. I want to spend the next few minutes on the actual argument in that essay, because it is the source text for the vocabulary we've been using, and reading it directly is worth the price of admission.

The argument has four moves.

Move one. What the labs are actually betting. Verbatim from Dwarkesh — quote — "if we train A-Is to accomplish millions of verifiable tasks across thousands of diverse R-L environments, then we'll basically have built A-G-I." End quote. He calls this the R-L-V-R bet — reinforcement learning across verifiable rewards. It's the majority position at OpenAI, DeepMind, and Anthropic today. He is going to attack it.

Move two. The first attack — verifiable is not enough. Verbatim — quote — "it is not enough for a domain to be verifiable. It also has to be very grindable — in the sense that you can run lots of parallel rollouts against a deterministic and replayable simulator." End quote. Coding is grindable — Docker container, replay, thousands of parallel rollouts. Math is grindable — Lean, verified, cheap to replay. But web automation is verifiable and not grindable — you can check whether Amazon dot com processed a checkout, but you can't run a thousand agents through that flow at the same time because Amazon's fraud detection will shut you down. And wet-lab experiments are neither — the reward signal is real, but the loop takes weeks and the environment is not replayable. The grindability constraint is the constraint. Domains that are grindable make progress. Domains that are only verifiable stall. Domains that are neither — most of empirical science — sit outside the current R-L paradigm.

Move three. The second attack — in-context learning cannot replace weight updates. This is the specific technical claim I want you to hold onto. Verbatim — quote — "the K-V cache (aka the representation that is built up from learning the context) grows three hundred twenty kilobytes with each token. Whereas in training, the model only stores zero-point-zero-seven-five bits per token — a thirty-five-million-fold difference." End quote. Read that number twice. The K-V cache — which is what a long context window is, mechanically — stores something like thirty-five million times more state per token than pre-training does. Meaning — the same information, encoded as a weight update, is thirty-five million times more compact than the same information sitting in the context window. You cannot substitute context for weights. If you want the model to actually know something durably, you have to update its weights. The current architecture has the wrong ratio.

Move four. The proposed alternative — On-Policy Self-Distillation, or O-P-S-D. Which — connection back to our episode from Saturday June twenty-seventh, where we ran three O-P-D papers back to back — this is the same family, applied recursively to the model itself. Dwarkesh — verbatim — quote — "O-P-S-D doesn't require an outer loop verifiable reward…O-P-S-D provides a much denser supervision signal than naive R-L." End quote. Mechanically — the student model at deployment time predicts tokens. A teacher version of the same model, but one that has been given the accumulated session context, predicts what the tokens should have been. The student's weights get updated to match. Every session becomes training data. Every user interaction becomes a weight update. And crucially — no outer-loop reward is needed. The reward is denseness — the student wants its no-context prediction to converge to what its own with-context prediction was.

Read this as a cleaner unified version of what we've been circling all week. On-policy distillation was the June twenty-seventh papers. Horizon-scaling was the June thirtieth Agents-A-one paper. The grindability constraint was the July third Sanderson interview. All of them are downstream of the argument Dwarkesh laid out on June twenty-sixth. He named the constraint — grindable — and named the alternative — O-P-S-D. Everything since has been footnotes.

The Ollie-specific take. Substitute biology for the standard R-L domain. Wet-lab experiments are the canonical non-grindable domain. Perturb-seq is grindable, single-cell R-N-A-sequencing is grindable, imaging is grindable in silico — but the actual C-R-I-S-P-R knockout in a mouse is not grindable, and the reward for a novel drug takes ten years. If the R-L-V-R bet stalls at the grindability boundary, then biology is where it stalls hardest. Which is either bad news for biological foundation models, or — Dwarkesh's more optimistic move — argument that biology is the domain where O-P-S-D matters most. Because the biological scientist does have accumulated context — a decade of hands-on lab work, ten thousand read papers, informal intuitions about which experiments will actually work. If you can compress that context into weights the way O-P-S-D proposes, then a lab-owned biomedical agent stack starts to look qualitatively different from a generic Claude Sonnet call. The teacher is the P-I with fifteen years of context. The student is the base model. The distillation loop is what turns one into an agent that thinks like the other. That is a very specific research program, not a metaphor. And Dwarkesh, whose day job is not biology, effectively laid the framework out ten days ago.

The K-V-cache-versus-weights three-five-million-fold number is the specific mental hook to keep. Every conversation you have with Claude, every session you run in Claude Code, the model is spending three hundred twenty kilobytes per token on transient state — none of which persists. The intellectual work of a research session lives in the K-V cache and dies at the end of it. That is a design flaw of the current stack. O-P-S-D is one proposed fix. There will be others. But the number is the thing. Remember the number.
