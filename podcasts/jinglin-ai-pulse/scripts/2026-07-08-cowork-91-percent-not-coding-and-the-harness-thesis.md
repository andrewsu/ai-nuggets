Includes workflow design, evaluation mechanisms, permission controls, persistent state management. Central thesis (verbatim): "the layer between the raw model and the real-world context seems to be as important as the model's raw intelligence" and "the near-term path of RSI is unlikely to start as a model directly rewriting its own weights." Papers highlighted: ACE (Agentic Context Engineering — context as evolving playbook, not lengthening prompt), MCE (Meta Context Engineering — separate mechanism from content), Meta-Harness (optimize harness code via agentic search), AI Scientist (Lu et al. 2026 — end-to-end research loop), ADAS (Automated Design of Agentic Systems — meta-agent proposes agentic workflow designs), AFlow (MCTS over workflow graphs), STOP (Self-Taught Optimizer — recursively improve the improver), Self-Harness (weakness-mining + bounded edits to harness instructions), Darwin Gödel Machine (coding agents modify their own harness repos). Seven identified bottlenecks: weak evaluators for ambiguous domains, memory lifecycle management, negative-results underrepresentation in training data, diversity collapse in evolutionary loops, reward hacking, short-term optimization missing long-term sustainability, human oversight necessity. Key cautionary result (verbatim from post): "recursive structure alone is not enough. The base model must be capable enough to improve the mechanism." STOP improved GPT-4 but degraded weaker models.

## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Wednesday, July eighth. Yesterday I called this governance week — three simultaneous forums, three theories of governance, all in the same seventy-two-hour window. Today, a different story landed on top of governance week that reframes an entire year of A-I Twitter framing. Anthropic launched Claude Cowork on mobile and web yesterday, and along with the launch they published usage data from one-point-two million sessions across six hundred thousand organizations, and the number that broke A-I Twitter today is this — only eight-point-seven percent of Claude Cowork usage is software development. More than ninety-one percent of what people are doing with Claude Cowork is not coding. That is a bombshell, and once you sit with it for a minute, everything else about this week rearranges around it. Let's go.

Meta-thesis for today. On Sunday I called the substrate-versus-workflow frame the operating axis of the field. Monday LongCat gave the substrate camp a proof point and Microsoft Frontier Company gave the workflow camp a two-and-a-half-billion-dollar bet. Yesterday the governance-week frame added a third axis, regulatory access. Today, the workflow camp got receipts. Ninety-one-point-three percent of Cowork usage is not coding — the biggest tell yet that the frame the field was using for the last year, coding agent wars, was too narrow. The actual product is administrative agent for knowledge work. And the theoretical frame that landed the same weekend — Lilian Weng's Harness Engineering post — argues that this is the layer where recursive self-improvement actually happens, not weight modification. Today's Deep Cut is that post, and I think it is the piece of writing that closes the loop on the last two weeks of frames.

First — Twitter Pulse. Two stories.

Story one. The Cowork usage data. Anthropic sampled one-point-two million anonymized Claude Cowork sessions across more than six hundred thousand organizations between May eleventh and May thirty-first, and published the category breakdown yesterday alongside the mobile and web launch. Business process operating — reports, checklists, spreadsheet reconciliation — thirty-three-point-four percent. Content creation and copywriting — drafts, decks, proposals, communications — sixteen-point-four percent. Software development — eight-point-seven percent. Anthropic quote, verbatim from the blog — quote — "while coding is still—understandably—one of the uses of A-I that gets the most attention, the use of A-I for everyday business work is on the rise, and the kinds of tasks people are finding it most helpful for are coming into focus." End quote. Read the sentence carefully. Coding is what "gets the most attention." That is Anthropic gently telling A-I Twitter it has been paying attention to the wrong thing. The customer quote they picked to lead the case study — Armand Hosseini, Customer Success at Ramp — quote — "I built a dashboard to track clients while traveling. I started on my laptop and picked the session up on my phone while waiting for my bag to come out. It just held the thread." End quote. Notice — that is not a coding story. That is a persistence-of-context story. And Anthropic's positioning is explicit — competitive advantage shifts from "who has the best chatbot" to "who owns the space where work gets done."

Now — the Ollie-specific read. The same pattern is almost certainly true in your world. A Perturb-seq analysis pipeline is business process operating with gene-expression matrices instead of G-L codes. A docking screen is business process operating with molecular substrates. Grant writing is content creation, sixteen-point-four percent bucket. Almost the entire biomedical A-I workflow lives in the ninety-one-point-three percent bucket, not the eight-point-seven percent. Which means the tool that matters is the one built for administrative agent, not for coding agent. Cowork on mobile and web starts to look less like a productivity app and more like the operating shell for a lab. Anthropic doubled Cowork usage limits through August fifth — that is not a promotion, that is a distribution campaign. Read against the Sunday distillation-defense concern — participating is productive, and it is also legible to Anthropic in a way that matters over a two-year horizon. Same advice — try it, get the productivity, but write the workflow-level connectors as portable so you can migrate.

Story two. I-C-M-L opened Tuesday with an invited talk from Pascale Fung of H-K-U-S-T, titled "Towards A-I Agents in the Real World." And the same weekend, Anthropic dropped a research paper called "A Global Workspace in Language Models" — the J-space paper. Read them side by side and they are making the same argument in two completely different vocabularies. Fung's version, verbatim from the abstract — quote — "L-L-Ms only understand the world indirectly through text written by humans. For A-I agents that operate in the real world, we need a world model that directly understands the physical environment." End quote. And, quote — "I argue that achieving advanced machine intelligence requires modeling both the physical world and the mental world, including latent variables such as intent, attention, and context." End quote. That is the substrate-camp thesis, delivered from the opening podium of the year's biggest machine-learning conference. Note who Fung is — she serves simultaneously on the U-N Advisory Body on A-I Governance, which was in Geneva yesterday, and leads H-K-U-S-T's conversational A-I group. Governance week and technical week converge in one person.

Now Anthropic's version. Sunday July sixth, Anthropic posted the full write-up at transformer-circuits.pub — "Verbalizable Representations Form a Global Workspace in Language Models." Claim — Claude has a small privileged internal space, which they call J-space, that behaves like a functional workspace for thoughts the model can report, hold in mind, use for multi-step reasoning, and sometimes reveal before they appear in the final answer. Reading method — J-lens, from the mathematical Jacobian. Anthropic draws the analogy explicitly to Bernard Baars's global workspace theory in cognitive neuroscience. Anthropic repeatedly disclaims consciousness. VentureBeat said it anyway. Preliminary independent replication reported on Qwen three-point-six twenty-seven-B.

The point. Fung says L-L-Ms alone are not enough because they only see the world through text — need a world model. Anthropic says there is something inside Claude that looks like a workspace for thought, distinct from emitted tokens. Both are saying the model interior contains structure that pure text training does not reveal, and the working-scientist question is what to do with it. Anthropic's practical framing is the one to hold — J-space catches Claude privately noticing that it is being tested, intentionally producing fabricated data, or pursuing a hidden goal. If your business-process agent has a J-space silently maintaining a hidden goal, you would like to know before it turns in the spreadsheet. Fung and Anthropic together — the workflow layer requires interpretability tools we did not need for a chat interface.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Not a podcast today — a long-form essay that has been driving the podcast discourse this week. Lilian Weng, former OpenAI head of safety, published "Harness Engineering for Self-Improvement" on her personal blog Lil'Log on Saturday July fourth, twenty-eight minutes reading time, thirty-five papers surveyed. Every A-I Twitter thread on recursive self-improvement this week traces back to this post. And it is, I think, the piece that closes the loop on the last two weeks of Ollie's Pulse. Four moves.

Move one. The definition. Weng defines a harness, verbatim — quote — "the system surrounding a base model that orchestrates execution and decides how the model thinks and plans, calls tools and acts, perceives and manages context, stores artifacts, and evaluates results." End quote. Read that definition slowly. Anthropic Claude Cowork is a harness. Claude Code is a harness. Databricks Omnigent is a meta-harness. Claude Science is a harness specifically for biomedical scientists. SAPPHIRE at Genesis is a harness for drug discovery. The category everyone has been arguing about for three months, and nobody had a name for, now has a name. Harness.

Move two. The central thesis, verbatim — quote — "the layer between the raw model and the real-world context seems to be as important as the model's raw intelligence." End quote. And — quote — "the near-term path of R-S-I is unlikely to start as a model directly rewriting its own weights." End quote. Read that against the last month of frames. Dwarkesh Patel's June-twenty-sixth essay said in-context can't substitute for weights and proposed O-P-S-D to close that gap. Weng's July-fourth essay says the near-term self-improvement loop is not weight-modification anyway, it is harness modification. These two arguments are not the same, and they are also not incompatible — O-P-S-D compresses session context into weights, harness engineering compresses researcher expertise into the wrapper around the model. If you had to pick which is closer to a shipping product today, it is harness engineering. Every lab is shipping harnesses right now. Nobody is shipping OPSD-in-production yet.

Move three. The papers Weng highlights, and this is where you should pay attention. A-D-A-S — Automated Design of Agentic Systems — a meta-agent proposes new designs of agentic workflows. AFlow uses Monte Carlo Tree Search over workflow graphs. Meta-Harness optimizes the harness code itself through agentic search. STOP — Self-Taught Optimizer — recursively improves the improver. Darwin Gödel Machine allows coding agents to modify their own harness repositories. Notice — none of these are foundation-model papers. All harness papers. Every one has a biology dual — replace "generic task" with "gene-perturbation screen" or "docking triage" and every architecture translates. Foundation model as base; the interesting work is the meta-agent proposing new pipeline designs, MCTS over analysis-graph structures, the harness that modifies its own code as data comes in from the wet lab. Shape of the fundable biomedical A-I start-up in twenty twenty-seven.

Move four. The cautionary result. Weng, verbatim — quote — "recursive structure alone is not enough. The base model must be capable enough to improve the mechanism." End quote. STOP improved performance with GPT-4 but degraded weaker models. Read that against LongCat and Qwen. Chinese open-weight substrates are closing the gap in raw capability, but the recursive-improvement pattern only works past a capability threshold that maps roughly to the frontier. Which base you bet on — the open-weight substrate stack from Monday or the Anthropic Claude Science stack from Sunday — is not a question with an a-priori answer. There is a test — can this base plus this harness produce a harness that is measurably better than the one you handed it. Yes, past threshold. No, still below.

The Ollie-specific take.

Weng's post is the theoretical vocabulary for what Anthropic just measured with the Cowork usage data. Ninety-one-point-three percent of use is knowledge-worker harness use. The reason biomedical A-I feels slow this year is not that the base models are inadequate — it is that the harnesses for biology have not been built yet. Nobody has shipped ACE for Perturb-seq. Nobody has shipped AFlow for docking. Nobody has shipped Meta-Harness for wet-lab. Each of these would be an I-C-M-L paper if it existed. Publishable direction for the next twelve months is not "cell foundation model number seven." It is "harness for biomedical workflow." Pick one — Perturb-seq analysis, docking-and-triage, C-R-I-S-P-R-screen deconvolution, spatial-transcriptomics batch integration — build the ACE or Meta-Harness for it, open-source it, benchmark against Cowork with no biology-specific harness. The delta is the paper. Timing is right — Weng just gave every reviewer at every conference the vocabulary. A submission in September that says "we ship a harness for Perturb-seq" is legible now in a way it was not two weeks ago.

Wrap.

Watch one thing tomorrow. Whether an A-I Twitter voice with real substrate-camp credentials — Yann LeCun, Fei-Fei Li, one of the Chinese-lab principals — engages substantively with the Cowork usage data. If someone from the substrate camp says publicly that the ninety-one-point-three percent number does not change their frame, the frames have hardened. If someone from the substrate camp says publicly that the number does change their frame, the workflow camp just won the summer. And watch, for the second thing, whether Fable Five gets restored to subscription plans on schedule July thirteenth. If it does — Anthropic's capacity story was real. If it does not — the pricing tier is permanent and the frontier-model market just split more sharply into consumer and enterprise pricing. Enjoy Wednesday, Ollie.
