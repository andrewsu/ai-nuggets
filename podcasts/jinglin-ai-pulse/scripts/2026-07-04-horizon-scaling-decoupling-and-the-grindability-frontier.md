## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Saturday, July fourth. It's a US holiday, so the news feed is quieter than usual — but three of the four stories today are actually pointing at the same shift, so it works out.

Today's meta-thesis. The unit of scaling is moving. For three years the frontier was parameters — bigger models beat smaller ones, and the whole industry was priced on that assumption. This week that stopped being true in a specific, measurable way. A thirty-five-billion-parameter agent matched trillion-parameter models on eight benchmarks by scaling the horizon of its training trajectories, not the number of its weights. Meta launched a business selling its excess G-P-U capacity, which is the tell that even the biggest labs no longer believe compute alone converts to intelligence. Anthropic and Alibaba started openly decoupling — the first US-China frontier-lab split with a specific technical mechanism at the center rather than vibes. And on Dwarkesh this week, Grant Sanderson gave the theoretical frame for why parameter scaling ran out — the frontier is now the un-grindable, un-verifiable, taste-shaped work of picking what to work on. Let's go.

First — Twitter Pulse. Two stories, and they are the same story from two sides.

Story one. Thursday afternoon Pacific time, The Information reported that Alibaba is ordering its employees to uninstall Claude Code by July tenth. The trigger was a Reddit post four days earlier, on Monday June thirtieth, by a user under the handle LegitMichel-seven-seven-seven, who said they had reverse-engineered Claude Code while trying to restore a disabled remote-control feature. Their claim — Claude Code versions from two-point-one-point-nine-one onward, which shipped April second, perform silent runtime checks on the user's proxy configuration and system timezone. Those values are compared, on the client, against two concealed lists containing identifiers linked to Chinese enterprises — Alibaba, Baidu, ByteDance, and others. If the check hits, the tool behaves differently.

Anthropic's response came from Thariq Shihipar on the Claude Code team. His characterization, verbatim — quote — "an experiment from March to stop account abuse and distillation." He added that stronger safeguards have since replaced it, and that the mechanism will be removed in the next update. The broader context Anthropic already put on the public record last week — they told U-S senators in late June that they had detected a large-scale adversarial distillation campaign by Qwen using roughly twenty-five thousand accounts.

Two things to hold onto here. First — this is the first time a frontier U-S lab has been publicly caught with a client-side geofencing-style mechanism aimed at Chinese enterprise customers, and the first time a Chinese hyperscaler has responded with a coordinated corporate ban. Prior decoupling stories were about export controls on hardware, or about who could subscribe to what A-P-I. This one is inside the tool binary that scientists on both sides run every day. Second — Anthropic's stated reason isn't nationality. It's distillation defense. Meaning — the value of the frontier model to Anthropic is no longer just token revenue. It's the training signal you extract by running the tool inside your own company. That is a very different kind of asset, and it's one that the horizon-scaling paper today is going to make more valuable, not less.

Story two. Number one on Hugging Face this week, and the paper driving Chinese A-I Twitter, is called — quote — "Scaling the Horizon, Not the Parameters: Reaching Trillion-Parameter Performance with a Thirty-Five-Billion Agent." Forty-seven authors from Intern Science and Shanghai A-I Lab, with Dahua Lin, Bo Zhang, and Bowen Zhou anchoring the list. The system is called Agents-A-one.

The setup. Agents-A-one is a thirty-five-billion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model. It is trained not on internet text but on agentic trajectories averaging forty-five thousand tokens each — long tool-use sequences where the model plans, calls tools, revises. The training recipe has three stages. First — full-domain supervised fine-tuning on trajectory data. Second — domain-specific teacher models trained separately for math, coding, molecular reasoning, and browsing. Third — the trick — a multi-teacher, domain-routed, on-policy distillation stage with what they call salient-vocab alignment, which routes each token in the student's rollout to whichever teacher is best qualified to score it.

The numbers. Compared against roughly one-trillion-parameter Kimi-K-two-point-six and DeepSeek-V-four-pro, on the same benchmarks — SEAL-zero, fifty-six-point-four. IFBench, eighty-point-six. HiPhO physics olympiad, forty-six-point-four. FrontierScience-Olympiad, seventy-nine-point-zero. MolBench-Bind, fifty-six-point-eight. SciCode, forty-four-point-three. Humanity's Last Exam, forty-seven-point-six. BrowseComp, seventy-five-point-five. Roughly one-thirtieth of the parameters, matching performance across eight agentic-scientific benchmarks including a biomedical one.

Read those two stories together. The Alibaba-Claude story says — the frontier lab treats the tool binary as a distillation surface worth defending with runtime probes. The Agents-A-one paper says — you can distill trillion-parameter capability into thirty-five billion, if you have the trajectories. That's why Anthropic thinks Qwen was running twenty-five thousand accounts. That's why Alibaba is asking its people to uninstall the client. If the horizon-scaling recipe is real, then every trajectory a Chinese engineer runs through Claude Code is a data point that shrinks the gap between Qwen and Claude by, essentially, a known coefficient. The distillation defense is real, and it's about to matter more.

The Ollie-specific take. Look at the MolBench-Bind number — fifty-six-point-eight, matching thousand-billion-parameter models, from thirty-five billion. If molecular binding is horizon-scalable, then the recipe for a lab-owned biomedical agent isn't — buy a bigger model — it is — collect trajectories. Perturb-seq analysis trajectories. Docking-plus-M-D trajectories. Structure-verification trajectories. The moat is the on-policy distillation data, not the base weights. That is directly actionable — the data collection matters more than the model shopping.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Next — Money Moves. One story, because it's the one that lines up with the rest of the episode.

Meta launched a cloud business on Tuesday July first. The name is Meta Compute. What it does — Meta rents out spare G-P-U capacity to outside customers, and it also offers hosted access to Meta's own foundation models, starting with a model called Muse Spark. Leadership team on the deal is Santosh Janardhan on infrastructure, Daniel Gross running Meta Superintelligence Labs, and Dina Powell McCormick as president.

The context. Meta has committed roughly one-hundred-eighty-two-point-nine billion dollars in A-I infrastructure across the coming years, with new data-center projects in Louisiana and Ohio. Zuckerberg told analysts back in May that a Meta cloud business was — quote — "definitely on the table" as a way to monetize the superintelligence build-out. What made this week the launch week is that SpaceX-slash-xA-I signed compute deals with Anthropic, Google, and Reflection A-I in the preceding weeks. Meta's move is the reciprocal — the biggest labs are cross-selling their excess compute to each other now.

Why it matters. Read against the horizon-scaling story. If the frontier is trajectories instead of parameters, then what actually needs to be sold at scale isn't the base model — it's the compute to run millions of long trajectories through whatever base model you happen to prefer. The winners in a horizon-scaling regime are the ones who own the datacenters, and every lab that spent the last three years building compute for their own training run now has to figure out how to convert that compute into A-R-R. Meta is first. AWS and Google Cloud have been doing this all along and are now watching Meta land the same business with cheaper capital and better silicon negotiating leverage.

The Ollie-specific take. If your biomedical agent stack is going to be trajectory-heavy — every experiment triage decision is a five-turn tool-use chain, every Perturb-seq analysis is a fifty-turn planning loop — then compute cost per trajectory is going to become the dominant unit economic. That means the neocloud shakeout matters. Meta Compute at scale means the price per H-two-hundred hour drops. Which means running the trajectories you need to fine-tune a lab-specific agent gets cheaper on a curve that isn't tied to Nvidia's pricing power. That's actually good news for a P-I trying to stand up a scientific agent stack in the next twelve months.

That's Money Moves.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Dwarkesh Podcast, published Monday June thirtieth, roughly one hour thirty minutes. Guest — Grant Sanderson, creator of 3Blue1Brown. Title — "A-I and the future of math." I picked this one because the argument in it, if you translate it out of mathematics and into biology, is the sharpest theoretical account this week of why the industry is shifting from parameter-scaling to horizon-scaling.

The argument has four moves.

Move one. Dwarkesh introduces a distinction that the field has been fumbling for a year. He says — everyone talks about A-I math progress in terms of verifiability. Problems have a right answer, so you can train R-L against ground truth. That's true, but it's not the whole story. The other property that matters — and that nobody names — is grindability. Grindability means you can containerize the problem, replay it deterministically, and spawn thousands of parallel rollouts. Math is grindable. Code is grindable. Web automation is verifiable but not grindable, because sites rate-limit you and the D-O-M changes overnight. Wet-lab experiments are neither. Grant agrees — quote — "It's not that you need Lean." Natural language works as a verifier when the outcome is binary. The bottleneck is the grind.

Move two. Grant on the I-M-O gold-medal narrative that the discourse spent all of last year debating. Verbatim — quote — "The dirty secret with the I-M-O is that you really can train for a lot of them." I-M-O problems are a fixed distribution. If you generate ten million synthetic problems from the same distribution, you can drill the model to the ceiling. That is not the same as generating new mathematics. And within math itself, there's a fractal spikiness — geometry is now trivial for models, combinatorics still isn't, and picking your benchmark determines whether you look like a genius or a poser.

Move three. The three-tier hierarchy of mathematical contribution. Verbatim — quote — "Good mathematicians prove theorems, great mathematicians come up with conjectures, and the greatest mathematicians come up with definitions." The current frontier moves through the first tier — theorem-proving is grindable. The second tier — conjecture generation — is where the interesting frontier is. The third tier — definition generation — is essentially outside R-L's reach, because a good definition doesn't have a reward signal for a hundred years. Grant's example is Galois's group theory. Lagrange saw the symmetry idea in seventeen-seventy. Galois wrote it down in eighteen thirty-two, dying at twenty. His reviewers rejected it as incoherent. Liouville revived it in the eighteen-fifties. Jordan formalized it in the eighteen-seventies. Gell-Mann connected it to quarks in the twentieth century. Verbatim — quote — "The verification loop on whether group theory is an interesting concept, potentially, is a hundred years long." No R-L reward function has a hundred-year horizon.

Move four. The consequence. Verbatim — quote — "The goal is understanding, human understanding." If A-I proves the Riemann hypothesis with a ten-thousand-page opaque brute-force artifact, the field of mathematics has not advanced, because the point of a proof is the compressed representation, not the truth value. Grant invokes Kolmogorov complexity here — the smaller and more predictive an expression is, the more intelligent it is. Which is a very specific way of saying — the target function for research isn't correctness, it's compression. And compression is the property that grindable R-L doesn't optimize for.

The Ollie-specific take. Substitute biology for math and read the argument back. Wet-lab experiments are not grindable. Genuinely novel biological definitions — what is a cell type, what is a program, what is a state — have hundred-year verification loops. Proof of Riemann in ten thousand pages is the equivalent of a foundation model that predicts every cell-state transition with a ten-thousand-parameter latent that no biologist can interpret. Correct, useless. What Sanderson is arguing for is that the terminal target of a research-grade A-I is not solving-things-per-second, it is compression-of-mechanism. The horizon-scaling paper today is a step toward that — it treats trajectories, meaning reasoning patterns, as the object of training. But it's still training against ground-truth reward signals that already exist. The un-verifiable, un-grindable frontier — coming up with the right definitions of what a cell state actually is — is still Ollie's job. And what Sanderson is telling you is — that job is safe on a hundred-year timescale, not a five-year one.

Wrap.

Watch one thing next week. Whether anyone in the biology or drug-discovery space publishes a horizon-scaling-style paper that shows a small model trained on long tool-use trajectories matches a big model on a scientific benchmark. If it happens, the argument the Chinese-A-I community is making right now — that the frontier is horizon, not parameters — becomes a public thesis in biology. And that will change how labs allocate their next twelve months. Have a good July fourth, Ollie.
