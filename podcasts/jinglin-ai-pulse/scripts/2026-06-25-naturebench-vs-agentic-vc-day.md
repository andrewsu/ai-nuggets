## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Thursday, June twenty-fifth. Today was an agent-shaped day from both sides. Academia put out two papers that ask whether the agent stack is ready for serious work — one on whether coding agents can actually do Nature-family science, the other on whether agent memory has converged on an architecture yet. Spoiler — both papers are skeptical. And while those preprints were going up on archive, venture capital was wiring out about three hundred sixty million dollars across seven agent-themed rounds in a single day, plus an open-source counter-move from Databricks. We'll close with Dwarkesh Patel's recent solo essay on what he calls the data black hole, because it ties the two halves of the day together. Let's go.

First, Twitter Pulse.

The paper everyone is reading this morning is called NatureBench, out of Frontis-A-I in collaboration with Tsinghua, Peking, and one collaborator at Harvard. It is exactly what it sounds like. They built a benchmark of ninety cross-discipline tasks distilled from peer-reviewed Nature-family papers, and they ran ten frontier agent configurations on each one to see how often the agents could match or beat the published state-of-the-art result from the original paper. Six domains. Cellular omics. Protein biology. Biomedical modeling. Physical modeling. Molecular design. Relational reasoning. And on the agent side they covered the full frontier — Claude Opus four-point-six and four-point-seven, G-P-T five-point-four and five-point-five, Gemini three-point-five Flash, Kimi K-two-point-six, MiniMax M-two-point-seven, DeepSeek V-four Pro, G-L-M five-point-one, and Qwen three-point-seven Max. Each running under its native harness — Claude Code, Codex C-L-I, or Gemini C-L-I.

The headline number is brutal. The strongest model configuration in their evaluation beats the published Nature-family state-of-the-art on only seventeen-point-eight percent of the ninety tasks under their cleanly-better criterion, which they define as a ten percent or larger relative improvement on the paper's own scoring metric. Less than one in five. And the failure-mode analysis is where the paper actually earns its claim. Of the runs that fall short, forty-five-point-one percent fail because the agent picks the wrong method for the task. Twenty-four-point-four percent fail because the agent runs out of compute or wall-clock budget. Only three-point-one percent of failures are at the understanding layer — the agent did not get what the task was asking — and seven percent are at the strategy layer. So the agents aren't getting confused about the problem. They're picking the wrong tool for it.

The authors' framing on this is the line that will travel — agents succeed primarily through methodological translation, converting scientific tasks into familiar supervised prediction problems, rather than through genuine scientific invention. That phrase, methodological translation, is the take to carry. The agent's strategy on a hard Nature-family task is to find the closest neighbor in its training distribution and apply that pattern. When the published paper's actual contribution is a method that does not have a close neighbor — a new architecture, a non-standard loss, a domain-specific preprocessing step — the agent picks something familiar instead, and the published S-O-T-A holds. It's not that frontier models can't follow instructions or use tools. It's that they don't yet invent.

This matters specifically for the biomedical A-I world you work in. NatureBench covers cellular omics, protein biology, and biomedical modeling as three of its six domains. So when someone says, agentic A-I will accelerate biomedical research — this is now the benchmark that lets you ask, by how much. The answer the paper gives is, at the frontier of what the literature already reports, by less than nineteen percent of the time. That's a real number to anchor on, and it's a long way from automated science.

Companion paper from the same forty-eight hours and the same broad Tsinghua orbit — "Are We Ready For An Agent-Native Memory System?" The team benchmarks twelve memory systems on five workloads across eleven datasets. The headline is the negative result. No single memory architecture dominates across workloads. Temporal and graph-organized memories are good at cross-session aggregation and event-order reasoning. Hybrid filtering is best at exact matching in long, semantically coherent dialogue. Trace-preserving memory is required when correctness depends on intermediate state changes. Highly structured systems incur orders-of-magnitude higher index construction time and query latency than lightweight stores, yet do not consistently deliver proportional accuracy. And the most operationally useful finding — localized maintenance is more cost-efficient than global reorganization. Don't rebuild the index when one trajectory changes. Patch the part that touched.

Read those two papers together. NatureBench says the agent can't pick the right method when the method is novel. Agent-Native Memory says the agent can't even pick the right memory architecture when the workload changes. Both papers are pointing at the same gap — the scaffolding around the model has become the bottleneck, not the model itself. And the scaffolding is still very much under construction. That's the academic mood of the morning.

The contrarian read on these two papers — and you'll see this on the timeline as the cycle plays out — is that benchmarks like NatureBench are inevitably retrospective. The agents are graded against published methods. The genuinely novel agent contribution, if it exists, would not show up as a published S-O-T-A beat, because there is no S-O-T-A to compare against. So the seventeen-point-eight number is a ceiling on near-distribution invention, not a ceiling on agent capability writ large. I think that's a real caveat. I also think the failure-mode breakdown — forty-five percent wrong method choice — undermines the contrarian read pretty hard. If the agent had access to a novel method and didn't choose it, that's still a method-selection failure.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Next — Money Moves.

The interesting thing about June twenty-fourth, the same day the NatureBench paper started going viral, is what venture capital did. Seven agent-themed rounds, all closing or announcing in the same twenty-four hours, for roughly three hundred sixty-two million dollars combined.

Three of those are vertical-agent companies. Assort Health raised a hundred twenty million Series C, led by Menlo Ventures with Lightspeed participating. They build agentic A-I for healthcare administrative work — patient scheduling, intake, referrals, document processing, copilots for staff. Taktile raised a hundred ten million Series C led by Goldman Sachs Alternatives. They are agent decisioning for banks and insurers — underwriting, claims, fraud, A-M-L. Attention raised thirty million Series B led by R-T-P Global. They are an agent operating layer for revenue teams. Three vertical agents in three of the most regulated verticals in the economy. Healthcare. Finance. Sales compliance.

Then there is one agentic-codegen platform — Hang Ten Systems, a thirty-two million seed led by Mayfield, building enterprise agent code generation with reusable skills libraries. Mayfield leading a seed at that size says they think this is platform-stage, not project-stage.

And then there are three pure infrastructure rounds that are the more telling signal. Runlayer raised thirty million Series A led by Felicis to build the governance and cost-visibility layer for enterprise A-I agents — permissioning, security, multi-system control. Coval raised twenty-eight million Series A led by Norwest for testing, simulation, and quality assurance specifically for voice and chat agents. Seltz raised twelve-and-a-half million seed led by Speedinvest and B Capital to build machine-readable search infrastructure designed specifically for agent retrieval, rather than for human browsers.

Read these three together. Runlayer is the governance layer. Coval is the evaluation layer. Seltz is the retrieval layer. All three exist because enterprises are deploying frontier agents and discovering that the frontier-model A-P-I plus a system prompt is not a deployable product. The scaffolding has to exist around the model — and it doesn't, yet, in any consolidated form. So the picks-and-shovels of agent deployment are getting funded simultaneously, in the same week, by three different funds, all pointing at gaps that the model providers haven't filled.

The interesting open-source counter-move was Databricks. Matei Zaharia and Reynold Xin published an episode on Latent Space yesterday called "Why the Frontier Ecosystem must be Open" — and at the same time released Omnigient, a meta-harness for agents, under Apache two-point-oh. Their pitch is that the agent fragmentation problem starts at the harness layer. Every frontier lab ships its own harness — Claude Code, Codex C-L-I, Gemini C-L-I, your internal one — and they don't speak to each other. Xin's line is — L-L-M capabilities are wrapped into an agent harness, and these harnesses have different interfaces that make combining them or swapping them difficult. So Omnigient is a unified A-P-I and a shared session model, open-sourced, intended to be the abstraction layer that survives whichever frontier model wins. Zaharia adds the governance frame — we want to ensure that users can control their agents, enforce guardrails like cost budgets and permissions. That's the exact same pitch as Runlayer's Series A — Databricks is just betting the abstraction lives in open source, not in a startup.

The collective shape of all of this is — money is moving on the bet that the next-stage moat is the scaffolding, not the model. NatureBench is the academic data point that says the model can't carry it alone. The funding day is the operator data point that says the market has noticed.

Mistral also shipped O-C-R four on Monday, by the way, deployable as a single container on customer infrastructure, one hundred seventy languages, paragraph-level bounding boxes. Worth noting as the regulated-enterprise version of the same thesis — when the differentiator is deployment posture, not raw capability, the inference-and-infrastructure layer captures the margin. Same thread.

That's Money Moves.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Last Friday, June nineteenth, Dwarkesh Patel published a solo essay-episode on the Dwarkesh Podcast feed called "The data black hole at the center of A-I." It's a sample-efficiency argument, and it is the right frame for everything we just covered. So let's walk it.

Patel's setup is — humans hear roughly two hundred million tokens from birth to adulthood. Frontier language models train on ten to one hundred trillion tokens. That's a million-fold gap in raw exposure. And the gap shows up everywhere outside of language. A teenager learns to drive in about twenty hours of practice. Self-driving systems need millions of demonstration hours. A human can pick up a new humanoid robot in hours. Current behavioral-cloning systems need millions of demo episodes. Deaf people achieve general intelligence with whole sensory channels removed, which tells you the input volume is not the binding constraint on cognition the way it is for current models.

The conclusion he draws — and this is the line that lands — data distribution improvements, not architectural cleverness, drive recent A-I advances. Quote, open models only lag state-of-the-art closed models by four months because data can be extracted from public A-P-Is, while hyperparameters cannot. Unquote. The architecture is mostly fungible. The data is the moat. And for task-specific frontier capability — legal documents, coding examples, management consulting reports — what's actually happening behind the scenes is that the labs are paying hundreds of human experts per skill to generate trajectory data, and the model is processing each trajectory hundreds to thousands of times. That is not how humans learn anything.

Now put NatureBench next to that argument. Forty-five percent of NatureBench failures are wrong method choice. Why would an agent at the frontier of language and reasoning pick the wrong method? Because the agent has never been shown a similar enough task with the right method. Its training distribution does not cover the long tail of method-task pairings that working scientists have built up over years. The Nature-family papers represent exactly the regime where the right method is one the agent hasn't seen enough times to internalize. Patel's frame predicts exactly the NatureBench failure mode.

Now put the funding day next to that argument. Runlayer, Coval, Seltz, Assort, Taktile, Attention, Hang Ten — every one of these is implicitly a data-acquisition play. Vertical agents collect proprietary trajectory data in healthcare administration, finance decisioning, sales workflow. Infrastructure plays — governance, eval, retrieval — collect telemetry on how frontier models actually fail in enterprise settings. The companies that win this cycle are the ones whose deployment loop produces the cleanest task-specific demonstration corpus.

The Ollie-specific takeaway. Biological world models — the field you work in — sit in a strictly harder version of Patel's problem. There is no public-A-P-I source of cellular perturbation-response trajectories. There is no ten-trillion-token equivalent for cell state. The current biological foundation models are all sample-starved relative to what language models had at the equivalent stage of their development. So the bet to watch is whichever lab — academic or industrial — produces the cleanest large-scale perturbation-response trajectory corpus first. Patel's frame says that lab wins the next round. The architecture is secondary. In your domain that probably means watching Tahoe-style consortia, the next wave of Perturb-seq atlases, and whichever industrial play is buying or building proprietary in-house perturbation panels — that's where the moat is being poured, even when the headlines are about model architectures.

That's the deep cut.

Wrap.

The one thing to watch over the next few days is which of the seven agentic-A-I rounds funded this week explicitly names task-specific trajectory data as a use of proceeds, versus which one is still positioned as a model-capability play. That's the bellwether for whether Patel's sample-efficiency thesis has propagated from the X discourse into the operator class. The vertical-agent companies in regulated industries — healthcare, finance, sales — are the cleanest test case, because they already sit on a deployment loop that produces exactly the trajectory data that Patel says is the next moat. Whoever frames their next milestone around the data, not the model, is reading the room correctly. Have a good Thursday, Ollie.
