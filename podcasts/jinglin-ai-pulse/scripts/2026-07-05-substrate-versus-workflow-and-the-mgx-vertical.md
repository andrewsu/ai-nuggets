## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Sunday, July fifth. The U-S 4th-of-July weekend has kept the news cycle quiet, but the pieces that did land this week actually line up into one coherent thesis, so today's episode is going to be a bit more argumentative than usual.

Today's meta-thesis. The industry has quietly split into two camps on where the intelligence lives — the substrate camp and the workflow camp. The substrate camp says — build the world model, and the applications fall out. Qwen-AgentWorld and WorldDirector are in this camp. The workflow camp says — the base model is a commodity, wrap it in the domain-specific tools and workflows and that's the moat. Anthropic Claude Science and Databricks Omnigent are in this camp. And on Wednesday, M-G-X in Abu Dhabi closed a forty-nine-billion-dollar fund that owns pieces of both camps — under both bets, the same sovereign capital wins. The interesting question for Ollie is which camp biology sits in. And this weekend the answer stopped being obvious. Let's go.

First — Twitter Pulse. Two stories, both about world models, both showing that the field has stopped agreeing on what a world model is.

Story one. The substrate camp had two big papers in the last ten days. WorldDirector, arXiv from Wednesday July second, is a joint from Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, Ant Group, and Zhejiang University. It's a video world model with a twist — instead of learning to predict pixels end to end, the way most video-diffusion models do, WorldDirector decouples what they call semantic motion orchestration from visual generation. The L-L-M plans the three-D trajectories of every object and the camera moves. Those trajectories then act as control signals for the video generator. What that buys you — visual identities of dynamic objects are preserved even when the object leaves the scene, is out of view for a long time, and re-enters. The old failure mode of video world models is that a dog that walks behind a tree comes out as a different dog. WorldDirector fixes that by making the object identity a symbolic variable in the L-L-M plan, not an emergent property of the pixels.

Now hold that in your head and put the second paper next to it. Qwen-AgentWorld, from the Qwen team at Alibaba, released ten days ago on June twenty-fourth, still driving Chinese-A-I Twitter this weekend. Two sizes — thirty-five billion active-three-billion mixture-of-experts, and three-hundred-ninety-seven billion active-seventeen-billion. Both open-sourced Apache two-point-oh, two-hundred-fifty-six-K context. It calls itself the first language world model — it simulates agentic environments across seven domains, which are M-C-P, search, terminal, S-W-E, web, O-S, and Android. It's not predicting pixels — it's predicting the next state of a computer environment given the current state and an agent action. Training recipe — continued pre-training with state-transition dynamics data plus professional corpora, then supervised fine-tuning on next-state prediction, then reinforcement learning with a hybrid rubric-and-rule reward. More than ten million environment-interaction trajectories across the seven domains. The headline number on AgentWorldBench, the benchmark they released with it — the three-hundred-ninety-seven-billion model scores fifty-eight-point-seven-one overall, beating G-P-T-five-point-four at fifty-eight-point-two-five, Claude Opus four-point-eight at fifty-six-point-five-nine, and Gemini three-point-one Pro at fifty-four-point-five-seven.

Two things to hold onto. First — the small delta on AgentWorldBench doesn't matter — what matters is that Qwen defined the benchmark and is beating the frontier on it, open weights, Apache two-point-oh. This is the same move DeepSeek made a year ago with the R-one release. Second — put WorldDirector and Qwen-AgentWorld side by side, and you can see the field has fragmented on what a world model is. WorldDirector models the physical world in pixels with symbolic object identity. Qwen-AgentWorld models the digital environment in symbols with no pixels at all. Both call themselves world models. And a biological world model — a foundation model that predicts what a cell does when you knock out a gene — is a third thing again, closer in shape to Qwen-AgentWorld than WorldDirector, because the state is symbolic, not pixel, but with a much messier reward signal.

The Ollie-specific take on the substrate camp. If Qwen-AgentWorld is a language world model of computer environments, the direct analog for biology is a language world model of cellular environments — where a state is a gene-expression vector or a phospho-signalling snapshot, actions are perturbations, and next-state prediction is trained from Perturb-seq trajectories. Nobody has published this yet with an open Apache-two-point-oh release and a public benchmark. The Qwen team has just shown that if you have ten million real environment-interaction trajectories, next-state prediction training gets you past G-P-T-five-point-four zero-shot. Biology has the trajectories — the Perturb-seq atlases are close to ten million cells now — and doesn't have the model. That's a specific, actionable gap.

Story two. The workflow camp had a bigger week. Anthropic launched Claude Science on Monday June thirtieth. It's a beta workbench app on Mac and Linux for Claude Pro, Max, Team, and Enterprise. The key claim isn't a new base model — Claude Sonnet 5, which launched the same week, is the substrate. Claude Science bets that what's missing is the scaffolding on top of the model, not the model itself. What Claude Science is — a generalist coordinating agent with access to more than sixty curated skills and connectors, pre-configured across genomics, single-cell analysis, proteomics, structural biology, and cheminformatics. It integrates the N-vidia BioNeMo Agent Toolkit for Evo two, Boltz two, and OpenFold three. Three-D protein structures, genome-browser tracks, and chemical structures render natively in the workbench, and every artifact is reproducible and traced back to the code that generated it. Anthropic is putting up thirty thousand dollars in Claude credits and Modal is adding two thousand dollars in compute for up to fifty A-I-for-Science projects at launch.

That is a very specific bet. It says — the marginal dollar for research productivity isn't going into a better base model, it's going into the harness. A scientist doesn't type prompts, a scientist points a workbench at a data lake. Anthropic is not the first to say this — Elicit has been arguing something similar for two years, which we covered on Thursday. What's different is that Anthropic is now a workflow company as well as a model company. Which is a hedge — if the base-model gap between Claude and Qwen-AgentWorld collapses next quarter, Anthropic still has the workbench. And the workbench compounds on data connectors, not on training tokens.

Read the two stories together. Qwen and the Chinese labs are building better substrate under an open license — they are betting the workflow layer commoditizes because open substrate makes it commoditize. Anthropic is building better workflow on top of closed substrate — betting that the workflow doesn't commoditize even when the substrate does. Both bets can be right at different time horizons. But on any six-month horizon, only one of them is the moat.

The Ollie-specific take. Look at what Claude Science ships preconfigured for — genomics, single-cell, proteomics, structural biology, cheminformatics. That's your day-to-day tool surface. It also ships Evo two, Boltz two, and OpenFold three as first-class connectors, which means a Claude Science session can call a protein-language model on a residue-level query and get the answer back in-workbench without you leaving the environment. Which is genuinely useful. But it also means that if you and every other biomedical A-I researcher standardize on Claude Science as the workbench, then Anthropic is holding the log of every scientific question you ask in exactly the way that the horizon-scaling paper yesterday said mattered — as trajectories. Same distillation-defense concern as Claude Code, just applied to science instead of engineering. Worth being aware of before you commit a lab-wide subscription. The pragmatic move is — try it, get the productivity, but write your key connectors as portable M-C-P servers so you can migrate if the ecosystem shifts.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Next — Money Moves. One story, because it's the story that binds the two camps together.

Wednesday, July first, M-G-X — the Abu Dhabi A-I investment firm chaired by Sheikh Tahnoon bin Zayed — closed Fund One at forty-nine billion dollars, four billion above the original forty-five-billion target. L-Ps from institutional and private investors across the Gulf, North America, Asia, and Europe. Roughly seventy percent of the capital allocated to North America. Portfolio, so far, fourteen companies. The ones on the public record — Anthropic, where M-G-X co-led the thirty-billion Series G in February and participated in the sixty-five-billion Series H in May. OpenAI, at the three-hundred-billion-valuation round in March which was one-hundred-twenty-two billion in fresh capital. xA-I, twenty-billion Series E in January. SpaceX. Aligned Data Centres, roughly forty billion in a consortium acquisition. And, importantly for us — Isomorphic Labs, the DeepMind drug-design spinout, whose two-point-one-billion Series B closed on May twelfth was co-led by Thrive Capital with M-G-X participating alongside Alphabet, G-V, Temasek, CapitalG, and the U-K Sovereign A-I Fund. M-G-X plans to spend up to ten billion dollars a year going forward.

Why this matters. The whole substrate-versus-workflow argument depends on which camp gets to sixty percent gross margins first. And that gets decided by who has the capital to eat losses through the transition. Look at M-G-X's portfolio as a single vertical. At the top — one closed-substrate model lab, Anthropic, and one closed-substrate hyperscaler-adjacent lab, OpenAI, and one wildcard, xA-I. In the middle — the datacenters, Aligned. At the application layer — Isomorphic Labs, which is a workflow company for drug design. This is the first sovereign-capital vehicle where you can trace a paperclip from Sheikh Tahnoon, through Anthropic Claude Sonnet 5, through the Claude Science workbench, into Isomorphic Labs's IsoDDE drug-design engine, and out the other side as a molecule that goes into a Novartis or Lilly or J-and-J trial. That's vertical concentration inside a single fund.

Read against Together A-I from last week — Together closed eight-hundred million on Aramco Ventures leading. That was the first-order signal that Middle East petro-capital is now underwriting the neocloud O-S-S serving layer. M-G-X is the second-order signal — the same capital region is now underwriting the whole vertical, from model to compute to application. The winners in this A-I cycle are not the ones with the best model or the best workflow, they're the ones on the LP list. And the L-P list is increasingly Gulf sovereign capital plus North American mega-endowments.

The Ollie-specific take. Isomorphic Labs is now the reference biology bet for Gulf sovereign capital, and it's a workflow-camp bet — a drug-design workbench, not a foundation model. If you were designing a start-up thesis in biomedical A-I in the next twelve months, the M-G-X portfolio composition tells you what shape the fundable company is. It is not — build another cell foundation model — because Isomorphic already exists as the reference application and the substrate is Alphabet's problem. It is — build the workflow layer for a domain Isomorphic doesn't cover. Biobank triage. Trial-endpoint prediction. Companion-diagnostic design. The workflow layer over the substrate. That is where the check clears.

That's Money Moves.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Latent Space Podcast, published Wednesday June twenty-fourth, one hour eight minutes fifty-one seconds. Host swyx. Guests — Matei Zaharia and Reynold Xin, the Databricks co-founders, recorded at Data plus A-I Summit twenty-twenty-six. Title — "Why the Frontier Ecosystem must be Open." I picked this one because it is the workflow-camp thesis in its clearest form, and because the argument, if you read it carefully, is directly transferable to biology.

The argument has four moves.

Move one. The origin story of Omnigent, which Databricks open-sourced in mid-June under Apache two-point-oh. Reynold Xin explains it with a personal detail — he had to keep his laptop running in the car, during his commute, just to maintain the session state of an agent he was using. That is the missing primitive. Verbatim from Zaharia — quote — "you just wanna build the stuff that lets you deliver the agent, and make it portable across things." Omnigent sits on top of Claude Code, Codex, Cursor, Antigravity, Pi, and custom agents, and it provides session persistence, spend control, and security policy. Within days of open-sourcing it, they had roughly four hundred pull requests, about half from external contributors adding Kubernetes support, additional cloud sandboxes, and new agent harnesses. That is a network-effect signal.

Move two. Why open. Zaharia — quote — "If you think it's a layer that will, there'll be some network effect, it'll benefit from many people collaborating on it." Translation — Databricks does not want to own the agent harness itself. It wants to own the substrate under it — the compute, the storage, the governance, the runtime. If the harness is open source and standardizes, then the substrate under it captures the network effects.

Move three. L-T-A-P, which is Databricks's answer to the classic H-T-A-P — hybrid transactional-analytical processing — problem. Instead of collapsing the query engines, which is what H-T-A-P tried, L-T-A-P unifies the storage layer. Transactional writes go into column-oriented Parquet, which is analytics-native format, so the analytic queries can read the transactional data immediately without a change-data-capture pipeline. Why this matters for agents — a customer story from Xin — agents debugging service-level-agreement dips could only see product telemetry, not the actual database operational context. With L-T-A-P — verbatim — quote — "it would make those agents ten times more powerful if they understand what exactly are they doing." The claim is that agent-quality bottleneck is not model reasoning, it is data reachability.

Move four. The Dream Engine philosophy — Reynold's team internally calls it Reyden. It's a decade-long shift from engineering papers to data-driven design. Instead of implementing textbook algorithms and hand-tuning them, they trained an M-L model on quadrillions of trace data points across their fifty-to-sixty million daily V-M workloads, and that model predicts which algorithm and which data structure will optimize a given query at runtime. It's not one query engine — it's a runtime dispatcher over many implementations. Databricks operates roughly fifty-to-sixty million V-Ms a day, processes exabytes, and via the Neon acquisition has roughly thirteen million databases launched a day, largely driven by agents experimenting and branching workflows. So the training data for the Dream Engine is the substrate of the entire agent economy running on Databricks.

The Ollie-specific take. Substitute biology-research pipeline for enterprise data lake and read the argument back. A lab-owned biomedical agent stack has the same portability problem — session persistence across a multi-week Perturb-seq analysis, spend controls when a docking-plus-M-D loop starts burning G-P-U hours, security policy when the agent is looking at patient data, and operational context when it needs to reach into a Snowflake instance of imaging metadata to answer a question. Every one of Zaharia's problems has a mirror problem in biology. And the Databricks bet — that the harness commoditizes and the substrate under it doesn't — is exactly the bet the M-G-X portfolio is making one layer up. If you want to build the Databricks of biology, you're not building a foundation model — you're building the storage plus governance plus policy plus runtime layer that sits under whatever foundation model happens to be on top this quarter. That's the workflow-camp bet, translated. And unlike Claude Science, which is a workbench for the individual scientist, the Databricks-shape is a substrate for the institution — the university, the pharma company, the CRO. Different customer, different check size, different moat. But arguably the more durable one on a five-year view.

Wrap.

Watch one thing next week. Whether anyone — a lab, a start-up, a hyperscaler — announces an Omnigent-style meta-harness or a Claude-Science-style workbench specifically framed for biology or wet-lab experimental workflows. If it's a workbench, it's the Anthropic strategy. If it's a meta-harness, it's the Databricks strategy. Either would be a green flag that a real bio-workflow company can be funded on M-G-X-shaped capital in the next twelve months. And whoever ships first sets the vocabulary the rest of the field has to speak. Enjoy the last day of the long weekend, Ollie.
