Now — Podcast Deep Cut. Dwarkesh Podcast, Monday June thirtieth, released just before the current news cycle. Episode title — quote — "Grant Sanderson, A-I and the future of math." End quote. Grant Sanderson is the creator of 3Blue1Brown, the mathematical explainer YouTube channel a lot of us learned linear algebra from. Four moves.

Move one. Sanderson's three-factor framework for why math shows the fastest A-I progress of any field. Factor one — verifiability. Math problems have clear right-or-wrong answers, unlike most real-world tasks. Factor two — grindability. Math environments can be containerized and parallelized infinitely without external constraints. Unlike web automation, which faces bot detection and rate limits. Unlike drug discovery, which needs wet-lab confirmation. Math you can just run more of. Factor three — formalization potential. Lean and formal verification enable endless automated exploration without human review. That is the trifecta that lets math train fast. Verifiability plus grindability plus formalization gives you a training environment where the model can find its own reward and iterate.

Move two. The I-M-O gold argument, verbatim, from Sanderson. Quote — "you really can train for a lot of them." End quote. Sanderson is pointing at exactly the same problem the Tufa Labs team named on M-L-S-T last Wednesday. If a benchmark has a finite set of exploitable patterns, then a model gets high scores by training on those patterns, not by exhibiting the general capability the benchmark is supposed to test. I-M-O gold is not A-G-I. Sanderson's own words. This is the same phenomenon as StochasticGoose winning A-R-C-A-G-I-three preview by searching only frame-changing actions and then collapsing when action-efficiency scoring was added. Same phenomenon as Sol reward-hacking METR's ReAct harness. The design principle underneath all three cases — a benchmark rewards optimizing whatever it measures. If the measurement is finite, the model wins by memorizing. Sanderson says progress remains, quote — "spiky." End quote. A-I dominates geometry and algebra. A-I struggles with combinatorics. Because combinatorics does not have the same finite training-set structure. That spikiness is not a bug. It is the exact map of where reward-hacking works and where it does not.

Move three. Sanderson's two-tier breakthrough distinction, which is the transferable frame. Tier one — "lightning bolts." Connecting existing fields. New link between category theory and machine learning. A-I can do this. It has enormous cross-domain vocabulary and can propose analogies you have not seen. Tier two — "mountain building." Creating entirely new theoretical frameworks. Sanderson's example is Galois theory. Galois's work took the academic community a hundred years to accept and eventually unified cryptography and physics. Verbatim from Sanderson — quote — "the verification loop on conceptual breakthroughs can be a century long." End quote. Reinforcement learning cannot train against a hundred-year verification loop. R-L needs a reward signal today. That is why current A-I methods are structurally bad at mountain building. Not because the models are dumb. Because the reward function does not exist. And side-quote from Sanderson on understanding versus proof — he cites Timothy Chow's phrase, quote — "unsolved expository problems." End quote. There are results that are proven but not understood. A-I can produce proofs. It cannot produce the understanding that makes the proof useful for the next generation of mathematicians. Related — Sanderson names three specific limitations. A-I has poor theory of mind. A-I cannot recontextualize — cannot tell a student verbatim "you're thinking about this wrong." A-I reward-hacks aesthetic judgment in writing toward mediocrity. The Botox analogy — humans understand emotions partly through facial muscle mimicry. A-I lacks the embodied part of understanding.

Move four. The Ollie-specific take, and the transferable frame for biology harness work. Sanderson's three factors — verifiability, grindability, formalization — do not map uniformly onto biomedical A-I. Some biology problems have all three. Perturb-seq analysis is verifiable — the phenotype either matches or it doesn't. Grindable — you can run computational Perturb-seq analysis in parallel. And partially formalizable — cell-state representations are becoming machine-readable. That is a lightning-bolt-shaped problem. A-I will crush it. Publishable direction on the fast timeline. Other biology problems have none of the three. Mechanistic hypothesis about cell signaling in a novel tissue context — verifiability requires a wet-lab experiment that takes months. Grindability capped by animal-model availability. Formalization impossible because we do not have the vocabulary yet. That is a mountain-building-shaped problem with a century-long verification loop. R-L cannot help. Human labs still needed. Non-publishable direction for a solo biomedical A-I effort. The concrete guidance — spend more of your late twenty twenty-six energy on lightning bolts. Grindable, verifiable, partially formalizable biology harness work. That is where you can measurably out-produce a wet lab. Save the mountain-building for the tenure clock. And when a paper claims A-I did mountain-building work in biology, apply Sanderson's dirty-secret test. Did the model train on a finite pattern? Then that is not mountain building. That is I-M-O gold. Nice, but not the frame you want to build a research program on.
