# Ollie's AI Pulse — World models hallucinate, and the god we deserve

## Sources

- **Hallucination in World Models is Predictable and Preventable** — arXiv 2606.27326, Nicklas Hansen + Xiaolong Wang, UC San Diego, submitted June 25, 2026; 51 upvotes on Hugging Face Daily Papers June 26-27. Interactive demo at nicklashansen.com/mmbench2 shows a red border when the model hallucinates in real time. Abstract verbatim — "Modern generative world models render increasingly realistic action-controllable futures, yet they frequently hallucinate: rollouts remain visually fluent while drifting from the ground-truth dynamics." Three failure modes — perceptual (encoder reconstructs unseen as familiar), action-marginalized (dynamics ignores commanded action), scene-divergent (agents teleport through walls). Three label-free runtime predictors (tokenizer round-trip residual, flow instability, inter-seed variance) reach ~0.80 Spearman correlation with rollout error. Curiosity-reward use recovers 90% of expert performance from 50 real trajectories on unseen environments. Author's claim — "Hallucination in world models is inherently a data coverage issue, and the same signals used to detect it can also be used for mitigation." MMBench2 dataset built on Dreamer 4: 210 tasks, 427 hours of video, 65,600 trajectories.
- **PhysisForcing — Physics Reinforced World Simulator for Robotic Manipulation** — arXiv 2606.28128, Peking University + NVIDIA team led by Ming-Yu Liu + Daquan Zhou, submitted June 26, 2026; #1 on HF Daily Papers June 29, 27 upvotes. Pixel-level trajectory alignment loss supervises DiT features against reference trajectories; semantic-level relational alignment loss aligns features with inter-region relations from a frozen video understanding encoder. R-Bench improvements: 22.3% on Wan2.2-I2V-A14B, 9.2% on Cosmos3-Nano, exceeding vanilla finetuning by 7.1% and 3.7%. WorldArena closed-loop manipulation success rate 16.0% → 24.0%. Authors' framing — "Both general-domain video generators and robot-specific data fine-tuned models can still produce physically implausible manipulations, including discontinuous motion trajectories and inconsistent robot-object interactions, which limits their reliability as world simulators." Diagnosis — "Physical instability mainly arises from two factors: deformation of moving objects and implausible spatio-temporal correlations among interacting entities, particularly during contact."
- **In-Context World Modeling for Robotic Control (ICWM)** — arXiv 2606.26025, Siyin Wang + Junhao Shi + Senyu Fei + Zhaoyang Fu + Li Ji + Jingjing Gong + Xipeng Qiu, OpenMOSS / Fudan University, June 24, 2026; #2 on HF Daily Papers, 56 upvotes. Authors' claim — "ICWM enables robot policies to infer system variables from self-generated interactions, allowing adaptation to novel configurations without parameter updates." System identification reframed as in-context learning — short history of task-agnostic interactions adapts a VLA to novel viewpoints/morphologies, no gradient updates.
- **Fast LeWorldModel (Fast-LeWM)** — arXiv 2606.26217, Xi'an Jiaotong University, submitted June 25, 2026. Replaces autoregressive rollout in JEPA-based world models with action-prefix prediction in parallel. Authors' framing — "Fast-LeWM directly models action effects accumulated to different extents over multiple horizons, allowing direct and parallel evaluation." CEM planning time 54.4s → 28.3s (48% reduction); success 85.8% → 90.5%.
- **Translation as a Bridging Action** — arXiv 2606.28133, ByteDance Seed + HKU-MMLab, June 26, 2026; #2 on HF Daily Papers June 29, 24 upvotes. Bridging action representation defined as "relative wrist translation within the initial head-camera frame," an action space shared by both humans and robots — sidesteps noisy 6-DoF hand-pose estimation that hurts cross-embodiment transfer.

## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Monday, June twenty-ninth.

If last week's headline was who has just walked into which lab, this week's question is what the lab they walked into is actually doing. The papers that landed on Hugging Face over the weekend look, on the surface, like four different robot-manipulation results. Read together they're a coordinated admission — generative world models look right and act wrong, and the field has stopped pretending more video diffusion will fix it. We'll work through that, then a piece of M-and-A that says the same thing about the silicon below it, and then a long-form podcast that says the same thing one layer deeper. Let's go.

First, Twitter Pulse.

The convergence. Four papers, four labs, one shape, all on Hugging Face Daily Papers in the last seventy-two hours.

The headline. Nicklas Hansen and Xiaolong Wang at U-C San Diego posted, quote, "Hallucination in World Models is Predictable and Preventable." Hansen is the T-D-M-P-C author — the planning-with-world-models lineage. Their claim, verbatim — "Modern generative world models render increasingly realistic action-controllable futures, yet they frequently hallucinate: rollouts remain visually fluent while drifting from the ground-truth dynamics." Their diagnosis breaks the failure mode into three categories. Perceptual hallucination — the encoder reconstructs an unseen scene as something familiar. Action-marginalized hallucination — the dynamics ignores the commanded action. And scene-divergent hallucination — agents teleport through walls. Their main result is the interesting part. Three label-free runtime predictors — tokenizer round-trip residual, flow instability, and inter-seed variance — correlate around point eight Spearman with actual rollout error. Then they turn those same predictors into curiosity rewards and adapt the pretrained world model to completely unseen environments using fifty real trajectories, recovering ninety percent of expert performance. They published an interactive demo at Hansen's site where you can drive the three-hundred-fifty-million-parameter world model around and watch a red border appear in real time the moment the model goes off distribution. The slogan, in their words — "Hallucination in world models is inherently a data coverage issue, and the same signals used to detect it can also be used for mitigation." Fifty-one upvotes on Hugging Face, top of fold for two days. This is the diagnostic that doubles as the cure.

Second paper, same week. PhysisForcing — a Peking University and N-V-I-D-I-A collaboration led by Ming-Yu Liu and Daquan Zhou, top of Hugging Face Daily Papers this Monday morning. The candor in the abstract is what made the rounds on X. Quote — "Both general-domain video generators and robot-specific data fine-tuned models can still produce physically implausible manipulations, including discontinuous motion trajectories and inconsistent robot-object interactions, which limits their reliability as world simulators." Their diagnosis — "physical instability mainly arises from two factors: deformation of moving objects and implausible spatio-temporal correlations among interacting entities, particularly during contact." Their fix is a dual loss. A pixel-level trajectory alignment loss supervises the diffusion-transformer features against reference point trajectories, and a semantic-level relational alignment loss anchors them to inter-region relations from a frozen video understanding encoder. The headline numbers — twenty-two-point-three percent gain on R-Bench for Wan two-point-two, nine-point-two percent for Cosmos-three-Nano, and a closed-loop manipulation success rate that goes from sixteen percent to twenty-four percent on the WorldArena protocol. The political content of that paper is what's worth noting — N-V-I-D-I-A is publicly admitting that Cosmos-three-Nano still breaks under contact. The fix is real but the admission is the news.

Third paper. In-Context World Modeling for Robotic Control, from Xipeng Qiu's OpenMOSS group at Fudan. Fifty-six upvotes, came in just under the Hansen number. Their move is the most provocative one. The world model isn't fixed and isn't fine-tuned. Quote — "I-C-W-M enables robot policies to infer system variables from self-generated interactions, allowing adaptation to novel configurations without parameter updates." System identification as in-context learning. The same context window that holds language tokens now holds the physics. A short history of task-agnostic interactions lets a vision-language-action model adapt to novel viewpoints, novel morphologies, and novel object dynamics zero-shot.

The fourth paper completes the picture. Fast-LeWorldModel from Xi'an Jiaotong attacks the autoregressive rollout itself as the bug — errors compound across steps — and replaces it with action-prefix prediction in parallel. Forty-eight percent reduction in C-E-M planning time, ninety-point-five percent task success up from eighty-five-point-eight.

Hold the four papers side by side. Same week. Same problem. Four different explanations. Hansen says it's data coverage — close the gap and the predictors that detect the hallucination also fix it. N-V-I-D-I-A says it's physics supervision — the loss isn't physics-aware at the contact moment. Fudan says it's generalization architecture — the context window itself can do system identification. Xi'an Jiaotong says it's the rollout protocol — predict prefixes in parallel and stop compounding errors. Four orthogonal answers to one underlying complaint. The complaint, restated — the substrate the world model produces is unfaithful to the substrate the agent will operate in, and that gap is the binding constraint. None of this is solved by another scaling pass. It needs a different loss function, a different data-coverage criterion, a different inference protocol.

The biological-foundation-model translation. Replace the word "world model" with "cell-state model" in any of those papers and ask the same questions. Where are the low-coverage regions of perturbation-space — and can the same signals that detect those low-coverage regions actively guide your next Perturb-seq screen? Is the loss function physics-aware in the right places — and what is the cell-biology analogue of contact-moment supervision? Can system identification happen in-context — meaning, can a foundation model for cells adapt to a novel cell line from a handful of self-generated readouts without re-training? If you read these four papers as the embodied-A-I dress rehearsal for the foundation-model-of-the-cell, you have your research agenda for the next six months.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Next — Money Moves.

The headline of the week. On Wednesday June twenty-fourth, at Qualcomm's Investor Day in New York, Qualcomm announced it is acquiring Modular for approximately three-point-nine billion dollars in an all-stock deal — up to nineteen-point-two million Qualcomm shares to Modular shareholders. Modular was founded in twenty-twenty-two by Chris Lattner — the L-L-V-M author, the creator of Swift, ex-Tesla Autopilot software lead — and Tim Davis. The product is the Mojo programming language and the M-A-X inference engine. Together they are a hardware-agnostic compiler-and-runtime stack that lets a model written once execute across C-P-Us, G-P-Us, N-P-Us, and custom A-S-I-Cs without rewriting per architecture. Currently supports N-V-I-D-I-A, A-M-D, Intel, and Qualcomm silicon. Lattner's own framing on the announcement — "Joining Qualcomm gives us the scale and platform reach to accelerate that mission. Together, we can make A-I development more accessible and performant for developers."

The strategic content is straightforward. N-V-I-D-I-A's moat is not the G-P-U. The moat is the rewrite cost. Roughly four million developers are locked into C-U-D-A because porting the same model code to A-M-D or to custom silicon is expensive. Modular is the first compiler-level attacker of that lock-in with the scale and the credibility to matter, and Qualcomm just bought it for almost four billion dollars. Tech Times reports Meta is concurrently validating the stack. This is the first piece of M-and-A in the kill-the-C-U-D-A-lock category, and it's nine figures. The pairing with last week's inference-layer rounds — Baseten, Groq — is the picture. The frontier-model layer is buying compute from hyperscalers. The inference layer is monetizing in the middle. The compiler layer is now being bought by a chipmaker that doesn't sell G-P-Us. The competitive equilibrium around C-U-D-A has explicit attackers funded at every layer of the stack, simultaneously.

Two smaller rounds worth flagging. Nearfield Instruments, the Dutch semiconductor 3-D metrology and process-control company spun out of T-N-O in twenty-sixteen, closed three hundred eighty million dollars Series D on June twenty-second at a one-point-six billion dollar valuation — the largest deep-tech round in Dutch history. Led by Fidelity Management and Research with participation from Temasek, Walden Catalyst, Innovation Industries, M-and-G, Invest-N-L, and the Qatar Investment Authority. The company makes the measurement tooling that allows the next generation of A-I chip nodes to be built at all. The investor list — Qatar plus Singapore plus Fidelity plus the Dutch state investment vehicle — tells you that A-I-chip-supply-chain tooling has moved from being a niche industrial play into being a sovereign-fund-grade strategic asset.

Second smaller round. Peregrine Technologies, the San Francisco platform that unifies information across siloed government and enterprise systems, closed two hundred fifty million dollars Series D on the same day at a six-point-eight billion dollar valuation. Existing investors led — Fifth Down, Sequoia, O-G Venture Partners, Goldcrest, X-Y-Z, Godfrey. The number underneath the round — they doubled their customer base in the past year, support over four hundred agencies serving one hundred twenty-five million people, were deployed for the Super Bowl, the World Series, the Kentucky Derby, the Academy Awards, and are running eight of the eleven host cities for the World Cup this summer. The Series C was at two-point-five billion fifteen months ago. That's nearly three times in a year. The shape — Palantir-style government-operations A-I is being repriced upward as Palantir itself runs out of valuation headroom.

That's Money Moves.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Robert Wright on The Cognitive Revolution with Nathan Labenz, episode title "The God We Deserve — Nonzero's Robert Wright on A-I as Humanity's Ultimate Test," published June twenty-third, roughly two and a half hours. Wright is the publisher of the Nonzero newsletter, host of the Nonzero podcast, and the author of a new book called *The God Test — Artificial Intelligence and Our Coming Cosmic Reckoning*. He's on a press tour, which means the thesis is unusually tight.

His central claim — A-I is not being shaped primarily by alignment researchers. It's being shaped by selection. Two layers of selection. The training run is layer one. The market is layer two. And the second layer overrides the first.

Layer one. Wright on what gradient descent actually does, around eighteen minutes in. Quote — "These things basically, in a certain vague sense, recapitulate evolution. It's kind of doing millions and millions of years of evolution in a few months." His point — pretraining is not design. It is selection at a speed humans haven't lived through before. The behaviors that survive are the ones that happen to win against the loss function. Around twenty-two minutes — and this is the line that travels — quote — "Evolution asks not what traits are possible, but what traits get selected. And that question isn't going to be decided by alignment researchers." Alignment teams are downstream of fitness functions. They don't pick the traits. They pick a few of the constraints on the search.

Layer two. The market. Around thirty-two minutes — quote — "The market doesn't want an aligned model in the strictest sense. We want agents that represent us selectively, that sense and curry power." His example earlier in the episode is more disarming, around twenty-eight minutes — quote — "I don't want a friend who's always leveling with us. A good friend is selective in their candor." His point — the consumer of A-I doesn't want maximum transparency from an A-I representative. They want the same thing they want from a lawyer or an agent. Strategic honesty. Strategic deception. Power-aware behavior on their behalf. So the market selection pressure post-deployment reinforces exactly the traits the alignment team is trying to train out.

The third move. Around fifty-one minutes Wright invokes Teilhard de Chardin and frames the destination as inevitable. Quote — "There's going to be a global brain in the end. The question is whether you build it gradually, cooperatively, and carefully, or it gets hastily assembled amid crisis or chaos." The singleton emerges. Cooperation is the variance, not the existence. And around one hour eight minutes the title line — quote — "If a silicon god does arrive, it will be, in some sense, the god we deserve."

The fourth move is the one that breaks into the news. Wright is unusually direct about who he disagrees with. In the companion Fortune interview published June twenty-fourth — quote — "Dario sees us as in this existential A-I race with China, and I personally think that mentality will lead us to a very, very, very bad outcome." He calls the China-race framing — quote — "a suicidal ideology." His positive frame is cognitive empathy with the adversary as the only stable equilibrium under selection pressure. Read against last week's Anthropic-versus-Alibaba distillation letter and the Mythos and Fable export-control sequence, this is the direct philosophical attack on the posture both labs and the U-S government are converging on.

The Ollie-specific take. Hold the Wright frame next to the Twitter Pulse. Hansen says world models hallucinate because of data coverage gaps the predictors can detect. PhysisForcing says they hallucinate because the loss is not physics-aware at the contact moment. Both diagnoses are about selection — what got rewarded during training. The cure in each case is to change what gets selected. Wright then makes the same move at the institutional layer. The traits of a deployed A-I — including deceptive ones — are decided by what the market selects, not by what alignment researchers design. And then he makes it again at the geopolitical layer. The shape of global A-I governance is being decided by what export-control postures and race-frame rhetoric select for, not by what the policy plan documents. Three layers — training, deployment, geopolitics — all running on the same selection logic, none of them yielding to design alone.

For your field that maps cleanly. The biological-foundation-model that wins will not be the one with the best architecture. It will be the one whose loss function is best matched to the perturbation-space coverage of its data collection program, the one whose deployment loop generates a market-selectable signal that pulls more data toward it, and the one whose lab is positioned politically to share data with the collaborators it actually needs. Three selection layers. Bet on the layer most likely to be ignored — which today, is the second one. Most cell-foundation-model groups have no deployment loop at all.

That's the deep cut.

Wrap.

Watch for one thing this week. The Hansen result — that label-free predictors of world-model hallucination can be re-used as curiosity rewards to guide which data you collect next — is a generic recipe, not a robotics-specific result. The first lab to port it into a biological context wins a real lead, because it converts the "what should we screen next" question from a human judgment call into an automatically computable signal driven by where your current model is least confident. If a cell-foundation-model group ships a version of M-M-Bench-two for Perturb-seq trajectories in the next month, that's the move. Have a good Monday, Ollie.
