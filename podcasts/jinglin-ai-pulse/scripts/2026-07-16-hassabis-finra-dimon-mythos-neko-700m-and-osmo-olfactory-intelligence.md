Now — Podcast Deep Cut. TWIML A-I Podcast, Wednesday July eighth. Guest — Alex Wiltschko, founder and C-E-O of Osmo. Host Sam Charrington. Bio quickly. Wiltschko was a research scientist at Google Brain, where he led a team building A-I models for olfactory perception. In twenty twenty-two he founded Osmo as a spinout from Google Brain, and has raised more than sixty million dollars from investors including Lux Capital and G-V. Osmo's central claim — a machine learning model that predicts, from a molecule's structure, how humans will perceive its smell. I picked this episode because Wiltschko's argument chain is a paradigmatic sensory-foundation-model design pattern that transfers directly to the biological foundation model question Ollie thinks about most. Four moves.

Move one. The problem is well-posed. Smell has a specific biological substrate — roughly four hundred functional olfactory receptors in the human nose — and a specific input — a molecular structure. The question — given a molecule, predict which receptors it activates and how a human describes the resulting percept — is a mapping problem with clear inputs, clear outputs, and no fundamental barrier to being learned. This is the exact framing you want for any A-I-for-biology substrate. What is the input, what is the output, what is the biological interface between them.

Move two. Graph neural networks capture the input structure. Molecules are graphs — atoms as nodes, bonds as edges. Graph neural nets are the natural architecture for learning representations over graphs. Wiltschko's team pioneered the graph-neural-network approach to odor prediction while at Google Brain, and the resulting model — trained on the largest proprietary olfactory dataset that exists — outperforms every prior approach at predicting how humans describe a novel molecule they've never encountered. Which is the direct parallel to any molecular biology substrate. If your input is a molecular graph — a small molecule, a peptide, a protein backbone — the graph neural network is not the sexy new architecture. It's the boring correct architecture.

Move three. The embedding space is the actual product. What Osmo built is a learned latent space in which molecules that smell similar sit close together. Perceptual neighborhoods emerge as clusters. This is exactly the analog of the CLIP-embedding move for images and text — collapse a multidimensional sensory space into a differentiable representation, and downstream tasks like classification, similarity search, and generation become tractable. Wiltschko frames this as, quote, "olfactory intelligence," end quote — the collapsed representation is the intelligence, not the individual predictions. Read that as a design principle. In a biology-foundation-model context, the analog is a molecular embedding space where drug-target activity, toxicity, and metabolism sit as neighborhoods, not as separate models. Same design pattern. Different downstream tasks.

Move four. Disease detection and emotion sensing are downstream. Once you have the embedding space, the same substrate powers fragrance design, industrial olfaction, disease-breath-detection assays, and emotional-state inference from body chemistry. Wiltschko's argument — the applications are large in number and the substrate is one. Which is the exact economics that makes foundation models economically viable in biology. You cannot pay to build one model per assay. You pay to build one substrate and rent it for every assay.

The Ollie-specific take. Three transfers.

Transfer one. If you are building a biology foundation model, the Osmo playbook is a reference implementation. Pick a sensory or biochemical space where the input is well-defined — molecular graph, protein backbone, tissue image, C-R-Y-O-E-M reconstruction. Pick the natural neural-net architecture — graph neural net, transformer, U-Net, diffusion. Collect the largest proprietary training substrate you can — this is the moat, not the model. Collapse into embedding. Predict downstream tasks off the embedding, not off retrained models. That is the reference architecture. It is quiet and unglamorous and it is the pattern that actually works.

Transfer two. The proprietary-dataset argument matters. Wiltschko emphasizes that Osmo built the largest proprietary olfactory dataset from scratch to train its models. That is exactly Jain's applied-context argument from yesterday, translated into biology. The frontier lab does not have your data. Neither does the open-source community. If you invest in curating and generating a large, high-quality, biologically-anchored training substrate, the resulting embedding space is a durable competitive asset that a bigger lab cannot backfill. This is the operational lesson for every academic-biology-A-I group thinking about what to invest in through twenty twenty-eight. The dataset is the moat. The model is the interface.

Transfer three. Disease detection is a downstream target, not the primary bet. Every Osmo pitch page mentions medical diagnostics. But the substrate is fragrance and industrial olfaction, because that's the market that pays now. The medical read is downstream, and it emerges from the same embedding space that pays for the substrate. Same lesson for any biology-A-I harness. Do not start with the disease bet. Start with the substrate that generates cash, then let disease detection emerge as a natural extension. This is what Neko is doing at the consumer-preventive-health tier, and it is what Osmo is doing at the industrial-olfactory tier. Both companies are commercially self-sustaining foundation-model plays that end up in medicine as a consequence of the substrate, not as a first-move target. That's the pattern.
