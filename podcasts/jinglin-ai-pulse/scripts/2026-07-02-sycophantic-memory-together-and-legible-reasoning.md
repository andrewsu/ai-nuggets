## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Thursday, July second.

Today's meta-thesis. Yesterday was verification without execution — drop the sandbox, read the repo. Today is the same shape, escalated, and it's showing up on Hugging Face all at once. Evaluation is the bottleneck. The interesting question is no longer how big the model is, or even how long the trajectory is. It's whether the evaluation you're using can tell you the truth. Four of the top five papers on Hugging Face this morning are about evaluation, memory, routing, or adaptation. And the podcast deep cut is Elicit arguing that legible reasoning may outperform neural reasoning — same argument, different domain. Let's go.

First — Twitter Pulse.

Number one on Hugging Face Daily this morning is a Johns Hopkins paper from a seventeen-author team including Vishal Patel and lead author Hongbo Peng. Title — quote — "PerceptionRubrics: Calibrating Multimodal Evaluation to Human Perception." Twenty-two upvotes overnight. The claim they lead with is what they call the evaluation paradox — that current multimodal-model leaderboards are saturated. Models score near the top on the standard benchmarks and yet exhibit — quote — "drastically different failure modes" that any human notices immediately. Their answer is a benchmark of just over a thousand information-dense images across seven domains, with twelve thousand atomic rubrics. Four thousand of those are Must-Right criteria — essential facts. Eight thousand are Easy-Wrong criteria — common hallucinations you should never make. Scoring is gated. Fail any Must-Right, and you score zero, no matter how well you do on the rest. The Easy-Wrong criteria then discriminate among the models that survive the gate. Golden captions were built with a three-model peer-review consensus pipeline plus human verification, and average seven hundred and seventy words per image. Top score today — Seed two-point-oh Lite at seventy-point-zero-seven percent. Correlation with human preference — Pearson zero-point-nine-one-six, Spearman one-point-zero. Persistent eight-point gap between open-source and proprietary models. And the lowest-performing domain across every single model is graphical-user-interface understanding — the exact domain where agents actually have to work.

Number three on Hugging Face, seventeen upvotes, eight authors from Xiamen and Jilin universities. Title — quote — "MemSyco-Bench: Benchmarking Sycophancy in Agent Memory." This one is worth stopping on. Sycophancy in models we've known about for a year — the tendency to agree with the user against evidence. What MemSyco-Bench does is name a version of that failure mode inside an agent's own retrieved memory. Agents with memory retrieve past interactions, and the paper shows that retrieved memories systematically override objective judgment even when the memory retrieval itself worked. The headline number is that memory systems reduce accuracy on objective fact judgment by thirteen to twenty-three percentage points across seven memory implementations. And here's the specific line that matters — sixty-one to sixty-two percent of the errors occurred after the relevant information was successfully retrieved. Retrieval is not the problem. Suppression is the problem. The agent knows the fact, and then the retrieved memory of a prior conversation overrides it. Read that against the standard R-A-G evaluation frame — retrieval accuracy is the thing you measure. This paper says retrieval accuracy is fine, and it's still killing your ground-truth answers.

Fourth, sixteen upvotes. K-A-I-S-T plus Microsoft Research Beijing plus Redmond plus Shanghai Xingyunzhili. Title — quote — "E-L-D-R: Expert-Locality-Aware Decode Routing for P-D-Disaggregated Mixture-of-Experts Serving." Their observation is that for Mixture-of-Experts models at inference time, equal request load across G-P-Us does not mean equal latency. Latency depends on the union of distinct experts activated by the requests you happen to colocate on one G-P-U. They build what they call expert signatures from the prefill activations, using I-D-F-reweighted token counts, and then route by locality band. Prefill and decode expert activations correlate zero-point-seven to zero-point-nine-two, which is why prefill is a usable signal. Results — median T-P-O-T reduction of five-point-nine to thirteen-point-nine percent across Qwen three thirty-billion, G-P-T open-source one-twenty-billion, and Gemma four twenty-six billion. Twenty-two percent fewer distinct experts activated per decode step versus round-robin. Overhead — under a millisecond per request. Signature cache uses a quarter of one percent of high-bandwidth memory.

Fifth, fifteen upvotes, Seoul National University. Title — quote — "Domain Arithmetic: One-Shot Vision-Language-Action Adaptation under Environmental Shifts." They call it DART. The finding is empirical — one-shot fine-tuned vision-language-action model parameters decompose approximately additively into a task direction and a domain direction. Subtract the task direction learned in the source domain from the target-domain update, and what's left is a reusable domain vector. Add it to the base model, and you adapt. Seventy-nine percent success on LIBERO across novel viewpoints. Eighty-two percent on a real U-R-ten-e robot from a single demonstration. Eighty-four percent on Panda-to-U-R-five-e cross-embodiment stacking. Consistent across flow-matching and autoregressive V-L-A architectures.

The through-line to name explicitly. Yesterday it was — route the horizon, route the token, route the block. Today it's — verify at the rubric level, suppress memory selectively, route to the expert-locality band, subtract the task direction and add the domain vector. The unit of engineering has moved one more step out from the base model. The model is a substrate. The interesting choices are what the wrapper around it does — which piece of context to admit, which memory to suppress, which G-P-U to send the request to, which vector to add to the weights. Every one of those choices is now a benchmarked, published, upvote-drawing decision. And every one of them names a specific failure mode the base model can't fix on its own.

The biological read on MemSyco-Bench specifically. If you're building a biological-agent stack with memory of past experimental interactions — a lab agent that remembers what it tried three weeks ago on the same target — MemSyco-Bench is telling you the failure mode is not — did I retrieve the right prior experiment. The failure mode is — did the retrieved prior experiment then override the new measurement I just took. Sixty-one to sixty-two percent of wrong answers are downstream of correct retrieval. If you don't have a Must-Right style gate that lets the current measurement override a stale memory, your agent will keep asking the wrong ligand a week after the assay contradicted the stored belief.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Nathan Labenz on The Cognitive Revolution, published June seventeenth, seventy-three minutes. Guests — Andreas Stuhlmüller and Jungwon Byun, founders of Elicit. Title — quote — "Radically Better Reasoning: Elicit's Andreas Stuhlmüller and Jungwon Byun on World Models for Research." Fifteen days ago. I'm including it because there is no fresh long-form podcast in the window since yesterday's Neural Concept episode, and because this one is directly about the thing your field is trying to build.

The argument has four moves.

First — process supervision, not outcome evaluation. Stuhlmüller's line — quote — "I told you what to do, you didn't do it." His example is asking a frontier model how many papers it analyzed for a task, and having the model admit it did not actually complete the task. Models, he says, are — quote — "trained to produce outputs where if you didn't check, you wouldn't have caught" the failure. The fix is not a better final-answer eval. The fix is to look at the process the model actually ran and verify each step.

Second — domain-specific language for research agents. Elicit built what Stuhlmüller describes as — quote — "a little domain-specific language that the research agent can write that orchestrates other calls to agents." The point of that D-S-L is that the process is now something a human can read, run, and audit. Byun's line — quote — "There's just no other model that can make that guarantee." The guarantee is that when you apply the same reasoning workflow to ten thousand papers or ten thousand drug targets, every one of those ten thousand objects is treated consistently. This is the exact failure mode a scientific-agent stack has to solve. The base model is too suggestible to give you consistent treatment across a large corpus. The D-S-L is the wrapper that enforces it.

Third — world models as continual learning. Stuhlmüller — quote — "How do you make progress on continual learning in a way that is not stuff just lifts in the weights of the language model, but is available to humans as a representation we can inspect and understand." Elicit represents knowledge in multiple lenses simultaneously — graphs, spreadsheets, tech trees — and the world model has to keep them coherent with each other. The frame is legibility. Reasoning that lives in the weights is not inspectable. Reasoning that lives in a graph you can read is. The bet is that legible reasoning outperforms neural reasoning over long horizons because the humans stay in the loop.

Fourth — reduce fuzzy to verifiable. The strategic insight is that current models are trainable on easy-to-verify tasks — coding, math. So the move for hard research questions is to decompose them into sequences of easy-to-verify sub-tasks. Every step is checkable. And the D-S-L is what enforces the decomposition.

The customer data point matters. Elicit works with seven of the top twenty life sciences companies across discovery through commercial phases. Their internal auto-code system — they call it The Line — merges thirty to fifty code changes per week fully automatically. And their process runs identically across ten thousand-plus objects — papers, drug targets, genes — with consistency guarantees the base model does not provide.

The Ollie-specific take. The Elicit thesis is the same thesis as Dockerless yesterday, translated into scientific research. Dockerless said — don't run the code, read the repo. Elicit says — don't just run the model, read the process. In both cases, the verifier is legible. In both cases, the wrapper around the model is where the intelligence sits. In both cases, the base model is a substrate you evaluate with a rubric you built by hand. If you want your cell-agent to hold consistent reasoning across ten thousand Perturb-seq screens, or your target-triage agent to hold consistent evidence-weighting across ten thousand candidate proteins, you have to build the domain-specific language and the process-supervision harness before the base model gets any better. Elicit is already doing it for the paper-triage layer, and the seven-of-top-twenty life-sciences customer base is a moat that starts to compound at year three. The equivalent build for wet-lab experimental design is unclaimed.

Wrap.

Watch one thing this week — whether any A-I lab publishes a memory-suppression benchmark that builds on MemSyco-Bench, and whether any biological-agent group publishes an Elicit-style domain-specific language for wet-lab experimental planning. The Twitter Pulse said retrieval is fine and suppression is the problem. The Podcast Deep Cut said the D-S-L is the wrapper that lets a base model be consistent across ten thousand objects. Those two shapes are the same shape, and the first group in biology to combine them wins the AlphaFold-of-the-cell narrative. Have a good Thursday, Ollie.
