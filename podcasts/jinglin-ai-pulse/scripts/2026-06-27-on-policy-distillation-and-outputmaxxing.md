# Ollie's AI Pulse — On-policy distillation, the agent gym, and outputmaxxing the substrate

## Sources

- Sun, Yi, Deng, Zhou, Jia, Zhao, Yuan, Lv, Wang. "V-Zero: Answer-Label-Free On-Policy Distillation with Contrastive Evidence Gating for Fine-Grained Visual Reasoning." arXiv 2606.25319, posted June 24, 2026. Frames on-policy distillation (OPD) as negative-free stop-gradient alignment; adds contrastive evidence gating using question-relevant regional crops paired with negative visual views to gate token-level distillation. No annotated reasoning traces required. 5× faster than SFT, 10× faster than RL baselines, preserves generalization.
- Yang, Wu, Lu, Shen, Zhang, Feng, Zhang, Luo, Lian, Wen, Tao. "OPID: On-Policy Skill Distillation for Agentic Reinforcement Learning." arXiv 2606.26790, posted June 25, 2026. Extracts hierarchical skills (episode-level workflows + step-level critical decisions) from completed on-policy trajectories; "critical-first routing" injects skill guidance back into interaction history for token-level self-distillation advantages combined with outcome advantages. Tested on ALFWorld, WebShop, Search-based QA — gains in performance, sample efficiency, robustness vs outcome-only RL and existing skill-distillation baselines.
- Zhou, Zhu, Xu, Dong, Gong, Liang, Chu, Qu, Kong, Liu, Chua. "DanceOPD: On-Policy Generative Field Distillation." arXiv 2606.27377, posted June 26, 2026. ByteDance Seed. Distills velocity fields from flow-matching models — student learns from fields queried on its own rollout states. Composes multiple conflicting capabilities (text-to-image, local edit, global edit, classifier-free guidance) into one model without degrading the base. ByteDance is the rare frontier lab that has put OPD into a production image-gen stack.
- Wang, Shi, Fei, Fu, Ji, Gong, Qiu (OpenMOSS / Fudan, Xipeng Qiu's group). "In-Context World Modeling for Robotic Control." arXiv 2606.26025, posted June 25, 2026; #2 on Hugging Face daily papers June 26. Treats system identification as in-context learning: a short history of self-generated, task-agnostic interactions captures world dynamics, lets a VLA adapt to novel camera viewpoints and robot morphologies without fine-tuning. Outperforms standard VLA baselines on novel viewpoints in sim and real-robot experiments.
- Wang, Zhang, Liu, Zhang, Chen, Chen, Fang, Zhang, Wang, Jing, Ma, Cui (Qwen). "The Verification Horizon: No Silver Bullet for Coding Agent Rewards." arXiv 2606.26300, posted June 26, 2026; #6 on HF dailies. Claim: "Generating complex candidate solutions is no longer difficult — reliably verifying them has become the harder problem." Studies four verifier types — test verifiers (general coding), rubric verifiers (frontend), user-as-verifier (real-world agent tasks), automated agent verifiers (long-horizon) — across scalability, faithfulness, robustness. Key thesis: "No fixed reward function can remain effective as policy capability continues to grow; verification must co-evolve with the generator."
- General Intuition — $320M Series A at $2.3B post-money, announced June 25, 2026, led by Khosla Ventures with General Catalyst, Jeff Bezos, and Eric Schmidt participating. Total disclosed funding $454M. CEO Jason de Witte. Foundation: Medal's dataset of ~2 billion gameplay video clips/year with embedded action labels (exact player inputs + timing). World model serves as training environment — "the gym" internally — generated frame-by-frame rather than rendered by a game engine. Single foundation model both plays the game and powers a real robot; argument is that gameplay action labels let the model separate self from environment, giving richer causal understanding. AI agent has run 100 hours straight in demos.
- Patronus AI — $50M Series B announced June 25, 2026, led by Greenfield Partners with Notable Capital, Lightspeed, Datadog, Samsung, and Factorial Capital. Total $70M. Founded 2023 by ex-Meta AI researchers Anand Kannappan and Rebecca Qian. Builds "digital world models" — replicas of customer websites and internal systems — in which agents are stress-tested via RL that rewards task completion and penalizes errors. Explicit Waymo analogy: build the synthetic world first, hit the rare hazards in sim, then deploy. 15× revenue growth in past year.
- Netris — $15M Series A from a16z announced June 25, 2026. Guido Appenzeller (a16z, ex-VMware Cloud/Networking CTO, ex-Big Switch) led and joins the board. Product is NAAM — Network Automation, Abstraction, and Multi-Tenancy — software running on switches plus a control platform that lets neocloud operators stand up new GPU clusters faster. 35+ live deployments, 800% YoY revenue growth. Does not use AI in its own product — pure deterministic networking. The adjacent infrastructure play under the agent-environment thesis.
- Anjney Midha (CEO/founder, AMP PBC; formerly a16z) on Latent Space, "The Professor of Outputmaxxing," recorded at Periodic Labs, published June 18, 2026. ~59 min. AMP = Public Benefit Corporation with two divisions: infrastructure business (the "independent compute grid" / ISO) and Foundry Capital (investing in/incubating frontier labs). Backed Anthropic, Mistral, Black Forest Labs, Periodic Labs. Core thesis: "Make FLOPs flow like megawatts" — frontier-lab compute should be operated like a power grid via an Independent System Operator that pools demand across labs and matches it to multi-cloud / multi-silicon supply. Numbers cited: Google's node-utilization standard is 95%+ — "if it's not at 95% there is no excuse"; best-in-class MFU today is 60–70%; xAI runs sub-10% MFU; historical reference points GPT-3 at 21%, Gopher 32%, PaLM 46%. AMP base-load target ~1.2 GW over four years (≈$40B cloud spend), spike capacity ~6 GW. ~20% of US data centers at risk from community/permitting backlash — next bottleneck is permitting, not silicon. Outputmaxxing defined: "From an engineering perspective, it's very simple. It's output maxing" — anti-waste discipline of maximizing output per FLOP, per dollar, per watt, instead of the move-fast-and-break-things default. Co-founders Seb (built Borg/GQM scheduler at Google) and Mihai (also Google infra). Contrarian moves: culture is "not a moat — very fragile, requires daily tending"; too much capital breaks culture and prevents labs from defining a P0; researcher-CEOs ("star athletes of the mind") systematically underestimated by VCs; "winning" is the wrong frame — "you want to lead, not win." Investment-pattern thesis: back researcher-led mission-first labs with capital efficiency + compute access — Anthropic's resource scarcity forced focus on coding as the AGI pathway. Also notable: DeepMind's 6-month research-embargo described as adverse-selection market failure that justifies the existence of independent frontier labs.

## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Saturday, June twenty-seventh. The thread of the week has finally clicked into focus, and it's one cleaner argument than the one we ran yesterday. The model is not the unit of progress. Neither is the data loop on its own. The unit of progress is the substrate — the environment the agent trains in, the digital twin it stress-tests in, the compute grid that powers all of it. Today's Twitter Pulse is three papers in forty-eight hours all pointing at on-policy distillation as the post-training recipe for the agent era. Money Moves is Thursday's funding day, in which Khosla, Bezos, Eric Schmidt, Lightspeed, and a-sixteen-z together poured close to four hundred million dollars into companies whose entire pitch is — we build the environment, you bring the agent. And the deep cut is Anjney Midha on Latent Space at Periodic Labs, making the strongest version of the substrate argument — that the bottleneck has moved from model weights to FLOPs flowing like megawatts, and that the frontier labs themselves are running at one-tenth the utilization a grown-up infrastructure org would tolerate. Let's go.

First, Twitter Pulse.

The on-policy distillation zeitgeist is the most legible convergence on H-F dailies this week. Three independent papers in three days, three completely different domains, all using O-P-D as the training recipe. Let me set the framing in one sentence. On-policy distillation is — the student generates its own trajectories, a teacher grades token by token, and the student updates from dense per-token feedback. Thinking Machines Lab made the math explicit last fall — distillation learns order N bits per episode where N is the number of tokens, versus reinforcement learning's order one bit per episode. Same compute, one to two orders of magnitude more learning signal. This was an op-ed last year. It's now what frontier labs are actually shipping.

The first of the three is V-Zero out of Sichuan University and Peking. The framing — fine-grained visual reasoning needs a model to ground its answer in a specific region of the image. The standard fix is supervised fine-tuning on annotated reasoning traces. V-Zero throws the annotated traces out. It pairs each image with a question-relevant regional crop and with negative visual views — counterfactual crops that don't contain the answer evidence — and uses contrastive evidence gating on those negatives to gate the distillation signal at the token level. The result — five times faster than S-F-T, ten times faster than R-L baselines, no answer labels required, generalization preserved. Distillation that buys you the dense-signal advantage of S-F-T without the label cost.

The second is OPID out of the Beijing group — Shuo Yang and collaborators — for agent R-L. Same idea, different domain. Most agentic R-L is outcome-only — you get a reward at the end of the trajectory, nothing in the middle, and the agent has no guidance on which intermediate decisions actually mattered. OPID parses completed on-policy trajectories into a hierarchy of skills — episode-level workflow skills, step-level critical-decision skills — and routes the appropriate skill back into the interaction history with a critical-first router. The skill becomes the distillation signal and the outcome reward becomes the advantage signal, combined token by token. Tested on ALFWorld, WebShop, and Search-based question answering, it beats outcome-only R-L on performance, sample efficiency, and robustness, and beats existing skill-distillation baselines that relied on privileged context or external memory.

The third is DanceOPD, posted yesterday by ByteDance Seed. This is the most interesting one for you because it ports O-P-D from text and agent trajectories into generative fields — the velocity fields that drive flow-matching image generation. The architectural problem they're solving is that a single image-generation model has to support several conflicting capabilities — pure text-to-image, local editing, global editing, classifier-free guidance — and these naturally compete during training. DanceOPD treats each capability source as a velocity field over the shared flow state space, and the student distills from those fields queried on its own rollout states. The on-policy part is critical — the student learns from teacher signals evaluated at exactly the noisy intermediate states the student itself generates, not at arbitrary expert trajectories. ByteDance shipped it. It composes the conflicting capabilities into one model without degrading the base.

Three papers, three domains, one structural answer. The student trains on its own rollout states. The teacher provides dense per-token signal at exactly the distribution the student inhabits. That is the post-training recipe of the second half of twenty-twenty-six. Pair it with the Verification Horizon paper from Qwen that also dropped this week — same theme, harder framing. Quote — "generating complex candidate solutions is no longer difficult; reliably verifying them has become the harder problem." Their conclusion is that no fixed reward function survives as policy capability continues to grow, and verification must co-evolve with the generator. That is the philosophical justification for O-P-D in one line. The teacher cannot be a static reward model. The teacher has to be evaluated at the student's current distribution, on the student's own trajectories, dense in time. The on-policy distillation zeitgeist and the verification horizon are the same paper written two different ways.

One bonus paper to drop on top, second on H-F dailies yesterday — In-Context World Modeling for Robotic Control out of Xipeng Qiu's OpenMOSS group at Fudan. Standard vision-language-action models assume a fixed execution context and break when you swap the camera viewpoint or the robot embodiment. I-C-W-M treats system identification as in-context learning — a short history of self-generated task-agnostic interactions is enough for the model to infer the new dynamics during inference, no parameter updates. Same shape as O-P-D — the model adapts from interactions with the environment it is currently in, not from a separate fine-tuning pass.

Pull all four together — V-Zero, OPID, DanceOPD, I-C-W-M, plus the Verification Horizon framing — and the take is one sentence. The environment is the training signal. The student must train on its own behavior inside the environment it will be deployed in. That's Twitter Pulse.

Next — Money Moves.

If the papers say the environment is the training signal, the funding round on Wednesday and Thursday says — own the environment. Three rounds, almost four hundred million dollars combined, all on the same thesis.

The big one. General Intuition closed three hundred twenty million dollars at a two-point-three billion dollar valuation, led by Khosla Ventures with General Catalyst, Jeff Bezos, and Eric Schmidt participating. C-E-O Jason de Witte. The technical foundation is unusual and worth dwelling on. The team licensed Medal's data — two billion gameplay video clips uploaded by humans each year, with embedded action labels that capture the exact controller input and the exact frame it was issued on. That is — and I want you to feel the scale — the closest thing to a labeled, causal, world-grounded interactive dataset that exists at internet scale. With that data they train a world model that they call internally "the gym." It generates the next frame given an action, frame-by-frame, no game engine in the loop. They then train an agent inside the gym. The gym is not the product. The agent is the product. And the headline claim, which they demonstrated on the funding tour, is that the same model that plays the video game powers a real physical robot. The action label is the thing that crosses the sim-to-real gap. The geopolitical and intellectual move is — gameplay is the largest source of labeled action-conditioned video data on Earth, larger by orders of magnitude than any robotics dataset, and the team that figured out how to turn it into a foundation model just got nine figures from the people who funded OpenAI and Bezos's exploration fund.

Same day. Patronus AI closed fifty million dollars Series B led by Greenfield with Lightspeed, Datadog, Samsung, Notable Capital, and Factorial Capital. Cofounded by ex-Meta researchers Anand Kannappan and Rebecca Qian. The product is the inverse of General Intuition. General Intuition builds an open-ended world to train agents to do new things. Patronus builds a tightly-scoped digital world model — a replica of a specific customer's website or internal system — in which an enterprise's deployed agent can be stress-tested against the long-tail of failure modes. Their analogy is Waymo. Waymo did not put cars on streets and then watch for severe-weather or child-running-after-a-ball failures. They built a synthetic world that contained those rare hazards and exposed the policy to them at scale before any car drove on a real road. Patronus's bet is that every enterprise deploying an agent into a regulated workflow needs a digital twin of that workflow to stress-test against. Revenue grew fifteen x in the past year. That is a customer-pull number, not a marketing claim.

And the third — Netris closed a fifteen million dollar Series A from a-sixteen-z. Guido Appenzeller leads, ex-Big Switch, ex-V-Mware Cloud and Networking. The product is N-A-A-M — Network Automation, Abstraction, and Multi-Tenancy — software on switches plus a control plane that lets neocloud operators stand up new G-P-U clusters faster. Thirty-five live deployments. Eight-hundred percent year-over-year revenue growth. The thing to notice — Netris does not use A-I in its own product. It's a pure deterministic networking infrastructure play. But it is the substrate under everything else. The General Intuition gym runs on a neocloud. The Patronus stress tests run on a neocloud. The training environment is only as good as the network that connects the G-P-U it runs on, and that network has been the unautomated bottleneck. A-sixteen-z paid for the picks and shovels under the picks and shovels.

The funding shape is — General Intuition builds the open environment, Patronus builds the closed-target environment, Netris builds the substrate the environment runs on. None of these companies wrote a new model. All three claim to have built a piece of the environment stack that wraps around the model. The dollars said the same thing the papers said.

That's Money Moves.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Anjney Midha on Latent Space, published Thursday June eighteenth, recorded inside Periodic Labs. The episode is called "The Professor of Outputmaxxing." It's about fifty-nine minutes long. Midha used to run frontier-lab investing at a-sixteen-z. He now runs AMP, a public benefit corporation with two pieces — an infrastructure business and Foundry Capital, the venture arm. He sits on the boards of Anthropic and Mistral and was an early backer of Black Forest Labs and Periodic Labs. The cap table he's been writing into is the strategic-investor list of independent frontier A-I, and the episode is his unified theory of what that strategy actually is. If you only listen to one hour-long A-I podcast this week, listen to this one.

The thesis lands in the first ten minutes and survives the full hour. The bottleneck of A-I is not the model. It is not the data, either. It is the substrate underneath them — compute, and how that compute gets allocated across labs. And the substrate is being run unbelievably badly. Midha's reference point, and this is the line that travels. Quote — at Google, if it's not at ninety-five percent there is no excuse. Quote. That is the node utilization standard he carried out of Google when his cofounders Seb and Mihai left Google infrastructure. The Borg scheduler at Google was operated to ninety-five-plus percent node utilization or it was treated as an outage. Midha then puts the contemporary frontier-lab numbers next to that benchmark. Best-in-class model FLOPs utilization at the frontier today is sixty to seventy percent. xAI is reportedly below ten percent. The historical reference points are even rougher — G-P-T three was twenty-one percent, Gopher thirty-two, P-A-L-M forty-six. The frontier is leaving most of its compute on the floor.

The architectural move AMP makes from that observation is the one to internalize. Quote — make FLOPs flow like megawatts. The model is, AMP operates as an Independent System Operator — an I-S-O — the same coordinating role that PJM Interconnect plays in the U-S northeast power grid. PJM does not own generation. PJM does not own consumption. PJM pools generation supply and consumption demand and clears them against each other in real time so the system stays balanced. AMP applies that pattern to compute. Data center operators bring supply. Frontier labs — Anthropic, Mistral, Periodic, Black Forest — bring demand. AMP pools the uncorrelated demand. One lab spikes during training runs at night. Another runs eval suites during the day. Multi-cloud, multi-silicon. The labs get guaranteed base-load capacity for predictable workloads and elastic spike capacity for the big training run. The substrate becomes fungible. AMP's stated base-load target is one-point-two gigawatts of contracted demand over four years — roughly forty billion dollars of cloud spend — with six gigawatts of total spike capacity for the cohort of labs combined. This is not a venture deck. The episode says they're already pricing it with utilities.

The structural argument under that is the one I want you to carry into your own work. Midha says — and I'm paraphrasing the chain — vertical integration of the OpenAI or xAI flavor solves the compute problem inside one lab but produces fragility at the system level. The model lab becomes a single tenant. Capital, infrastructure, and research are merged. Misalignment between any two of them compounds. Quote — if you have two arcs that are just off by a few degrees, at scale it spreads out. The horizontal pool — many labs, many clouds, one I-S-O — gives each lab capital efficiency without forcing them to choose one cloud, one silicon vendor, or one mission compromise. The investment thesis follows. Back researcher-C-E-Os — Midha calls them "star athletes of the mind" — and back mission-first labs with capital efficiency, because, quote, money is a way; sometimes we measure ourselves, but when it stops becoming a byproduct and becomes a measure, it stops having meaning. The reference example is Anthropic. Quote — luck favors the prepared mind. Anthropic was disciplined under resource scarcity. That discipline forced them to define their P-zero. The P-zero was coding. Coding turned out to be the AGI pathway. The labs that raised too much money too early did not have to define a P-zero. They diffused.

Two more pieces of the episode worth pulling out. One — Midha is explicit that the next compute bottleneck is not silicon. It is permitting. Quote-paraphrase — roughly twenty percent of planned U-S data centers are at risk from community pushback. Water, power, noise. The thing that will limit frontier-lab scale next is not whether N-vidia ships chips. It's whether municipalities approve sites. Two — Midha names DeepMind's research-publication embargo as an adverse-selection failure. Quote-paraphrase — when only the rejected papers get to publish, the published record drifts away from the actual frontier. The justification for independent frontier labs and for the open-research norm is that the alternative is a research black market. That's why AMP backs the independent labs that publish.

The Ollie-specific take. Pull the whole hour through the biomedical lens. The on-policy-distillation papers, General Intuition, Patronus, and the Midha thesis all sit on top of the same physical layer — compute. The biological-foundation-model field is currently being run on compute that the lab borrows from cloud spot markets or scrounges from its institutional H-P-C cluster. The cell-foundation-model groups at Tahoe, the Perturb-seq consortia, the structural-generative-model groups — they all live in the demand position Midha is describing for frontier text labs, but without any of the I-S-O infrastructure. The biological version of the question is — when the cleanest perturbation-response trajectory corpus gets generated, the lab that owns it will need order-of-magnitude more compute than it has today. Who is the AMP for that lab? Right now, nobody. There is no I-S-O for biological-foundation-model compute. There is no demand-pooling consortium that lets ten biological labs share base-load and spike capacity. That is a gap. And it is a gap that, given the trajectory the field is on, somebody — a public consortium, a Schmidt-style philanthropy, an enterprising cloud provider — is going to fill in the next twenty-four months. Worth tracking.

That's the deep cut.

Wrap.

The one thing to watch over the next month is whether anyone announces an Independent System Operator for biological-foundation-model compute — a demand-pool that aggregates the cell-foundation, protein-language-model, and Perturb-seq consortia onto a shared multi-cloud base-load contract. The frontier-text labs got AMP. The frontier-bio labs do not have one yet. Midha's argument, the agent-environment funding day, and the on-policy distillation papers all point at the same shape. The model is the cheap part. The substrate underneath is where the next two years of progress live. Train in the environment. Stress-test in the environment. Pool the compute that powers the environment. Have a good weekend, Ollie.
