## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Sunday, June twenty-eighth.

The week that just closed was the most consequential week in AI talent and policy in twenty-twenty-six, and the single piece of news inside it that most directly changes the shape of your field is that the man who proved AI could solve protein structures has left Google DeepMind for Anthropic. We lead with that, follow it through the broader shape of the week, and end with the frontier-policy podcast that ties the news together. Let's go.

First, Twitter Pulse.

The headline. On Friday June nineteenth, John Jumper — co-creator of AlphaFold, twenty-twenty-four Nobel laureate in Chemistry — posted on X. Quote — "After nearly nine years, I have decided to leave Google DeepMind and join Anthropic, after taking some time to recharge. I am incredibly grateful for my time at G-D-M. Demis Hassabis took a real chance letting me lead the AlphaFold team just six months after finishing my Ph-D, and the entire G-D-M team taught me so much about how to do great science." Hassabis replied publicly within the hour. Quote — "What we achieved with AlphaFold changed the world, and showed the field what was possible with A-I for science and medicine, lighting the way for how A-I can benefit humanity." Jumper's role at Anthropic is undisclosed.

Look at the sequence Jumper is walking into. Anthropic has spent the last eight months assembling the institutional stack to do real AI-for-science work, and Jumper is the capstone. February second — founding life-sciences partnerships with the Allen Institute and Howard Hughes Medical Institute, deploying Claude into active research pipelines via instrument connectors. April third — acquisition of Coefficient Bio in a four-hundred-million-dollar all-stock deal, a stealth team whose co-founders Samuel Stanton and Nathan C. Frey came out of Prescient Design, Genentech's computational drug-discovery group. The team folded into Anthropic's Healthcare and Life Sciences division under Eric Kauderer-Abrams. May nineteenth — Andrej Karpathy joined Anthropic's pre-training team. Now Jumper. The strategic intent was made explicit by Dario Amodei in his October twenty-twenty-four essay — quote — "A-I-enabled biology and medicine will allow us to compress the progress that human biologists would have achieved over the next fifty to one hundred years into five to ten years." Kauderer-Abrams's stated operational goal — "a meaningful percentage of all of the life science work in the world to run on Claude." This is not an opportunistic hire. It is the last brick in a wall built since the start of the year.

AlphaFold is the existence proof — the one piece of work the whole AI-for-science community can point to and say, here is what AI did that human biologists could not have done in a comparable timeframe. The team that ran that program is now distributed, but the lead is at Anthropic, the same week as Alexander Pritzel, who contributed to AlphaFold and was on Gemini pretraining. The biological-foundation-model competition just stopped being a pure-research field and became a frontier-lab platform competition, and the lab most aggressively positioned for it is no longer DeepMind. It is the one Jumper picked.

The wider shape of the week. Jumper's announcement was one of four senior DeepMind departures in six days. June eighteenth — Noam Shazeer, Transformer co-author and Gemini co-lead, to OpenAI. June nineteenth — Jumper, to Anthropic. June twenty-fourth — Jonas Adler, Gemini coding lead, to Anthropic. Same day — Alexander Pritzel, Gemini pretraining specialist, to Anthropic. Three to Anthropic, one to OpenAI, all in six days. Alphabet's market cap fell roughly two hundred sixty-nine billion dollars across the week per Intellectia A-I tracking — one of the largest non-earnings tech market-cap destructions on record. The delicious wrinkle AI Twitter has not stopped discussing — Alphabet reportedly owns about fourteen percent of Anthropic, so when Jumper, Adler, and Pritzel cross the street, a non-trivial fraction of the equity upside accrues back to the company they just left. Google literally profits from losing its own people. That is a market structure that has stopped working in Alphabet's favor.

Third item — Anthropic versus Alibaba. On June twenty-fourth, Anthropic delivered a letter to the White House accusing operators affiliated with Alibaba's Qwen lab of running roughly twenty-five thousand fraudulent accounts on Claude between April twenty-second and June fifth, generating more than twenty-eight-point-eight million exchanges. The targeted topics were software engineering and agentic reasoning. The intent, per Anthropic — industrial-scale model distillation. Within forty-eight hours, the Commerce Department imposed new export controls on Anthropic's Mythos and Fable models citing Chinese military and intelligence concerns. This is the largest known model-extraction attack on a frontier lab on record, landing at the exact moment Anthropic's S-one is in front of the S-E-C with an October I-P-O target. The signal — distillation attacks have moved from theoretical to industrial. That has direct implications for any deployment of Claude-for-Life-Sciences inside a regulated workflow — the A-P-I surface is being attacked at scale, and the defensive posture of the lab is now part of the product story.

Fourth item, the political close of the week. On Friday June twenty-sixth, OpenAI previewed G-P-T five point six — three variants called Sol, Terra, and Luna. Sol is the flagship. Terra is the balanced tier. Luna is the speed-and-affordability tier. Two unusual things. First, the release is a limited preview to approximately twenty pre-approved organizations — not a public launch. Second, the pre-approval was conducted at the request of the U-S government, under President Trump's June second executive order calling for federal agencies to benchmark and assess new A-I models. On the same Friday, Anthropic's Mythos five was re-authorized for a list of roughly one hundred trusted American organizations. So both major American frontier-model releases this past Friday moved through a government-approved-recipient process simultaneously. This is the first day where frontier-model release is no longer purely a market event. It is also a state event. And the state is choosing who gets access.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Next — Money Moves.

The reverse Money Move first. Two hundred sixty-nine billion dollars of Alphabet market capitalization evaporated in six trading sessions. That is two hundred sixty-nine billion dollars the market took out of the company that, eight days earlier, the same market would have called the leader in AI for science. The catalyst was four people walking out the door. The frontier-AI market values a small number of named individuals more than the institutional infrastructure that produced them. Noam Shazeer is reportedly the most expensive individual researcher in the field — Google paid two-point-seven billion in twenty-twenty-four to bring him back from Character A-I, and he left anyway. Jumper had a Nobel Prize, and he left anyway. The binding constraint on a frontier lab is no longer compute or data. It is the loyalty of a single-digit number of researchers.

Forward Money Moves on the bio side. Two rounds worth flagging, both in the last seven days, both in your field. NewLimit — the longevity biotech backed by Coinbase's Brian Armstrong — closed four hundred thirty-five million dollars Series C, led by Founders Fund, Thrive Capital, and Greenoaks, with Kleiner Perkins and Eli Lilly Ventures participating. SonoThera, a Bay Area biotech focused on ultrasound-mediated gene-therapy delivery, raised one hundred twenty-five million dollars Series B with Vida Ventures, Leaps by Bayer, Otsuka, Johnson and Johnson Innovation, Illumina Ventures, and R-A Capital. Neither company positions itself as a foundation-model play. Both will produce exactly the kind of proprietary perturbation-response data that whichever lab — Anthropic, Genentech-via-Coefficient, or a future bio-foundation-model startup — needs to be the AlphaFold of cell biology. The investors writing these checks have noticed the talent migration. They are placing the data-asset bet, not the model-architecture bet.

Frame the whole thing in one number. Hyperscaler A-I capex for twenty-twenty-six just crossed four hundred fifty-two billion dollars across the four largest spenders per Intellectia A-I tracking — larger than the gross domestic product of Israel. Microsoft's annualized A-I revenue, roughly thirty-seven billion against ninety-seven billion cumulative spending, gives a thirty-eight-cent return on the dollar. Anthropic, by contrast, generated roughly five hundred fifty-nine million dollars of operating profit on ten-point-nine billion of quarterly revenue per its S-one. The capex is concentrated at the hyperscalers. The profit is at the labs. The talent is moving toward the labs. The data assets are being assembled outside both. Watch which infrastructure the data assets choose to run on. That is the next leg.

That's Money Moves.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Dean Ball on The Cognitive Revolution with Nathan Labenz, published June twentieth, two hours thirty-nine minutes. The episode is called "Dean Ball, on Joining OpenAI — New Power Centers, Frontier AI Policy, and Main Character Energy." Ball announces during the episode that he is leaving the Foundation for American Innovation, where he was a senior fellow, to build a frontier-A-I policy team at OpenAI. The episode is the most coherent argument I've heard about what it means for frontier labs to be the new institutions, and it sits directly on top of every story we just covered.

Ball's central thesis. Frontier A-I labs are emerging as new institutional power centers in the same sense that the chartered merchant banks of the seventeenth-century Dutch Republic and Britain emerged as new institutional power centers. They are not a regulated industry. They are something more like capital itself — an instrument the state will need to do statecraft across every domain, and an entity the state simultaneously will not have the capacity or expertise to govern from outside. The consequential decisions inside a frontier lab — capabilities roadmap, internal deployment decisions, what to ship in six months — happen before any public release and outside any current regulatory trigger. Quote — "I ultimately don't feel like I can get beyond these abstract intuitions without actually being inside the lab itself." His move is, in his own framing, the only research methodology available for the question he is trying to answer.

The second piece lands hardest in the context of Friday's G-P-T five point six release. Ball is alarmed at the speed with which the U-S government has begun monopolizing access to and authority over frontier A-I. Three specific concerns. One, the export-control regime — including the ninety-minute-notice frontier-model export ban — directly contradicts the spirit of the administration's own A-I Action Plan and confirms what foreign partners feared, that America will weaponize model access geopolitically. Two, the cyber executive order's thirty-day classified pre-deployment testing process run inside N-S-A. Quote — "a future in which models the public doesn't know exist are tested against standards that can't be disclosed." Three, the structural problem. Quote — "Government monopolization of frontier A-I is potentially how we get very scary outcomes from a civil liberties perspective, and that's a criticism I would make regardless of who the president was." His positive frame is that society itself is an information-processing system in which, quote, "all the humans in the country are parallel compute," and centralizing the relevant decisions inside a small group of cleared officials is wasteful as well as dangerous.

The third piece is his report card on the A-I Action Plan, which he helped draft during four months at the White House last year. His estimate — thirty to forty percent implemented eleven months in. Wins on nuclear-energy permitting, F-E-R-C grid interconnection reform, military adoption. The failure — quote — "It probably reads a little bit more like three dozen separate sort of thematic objectives than it does one cohesive thing unified by a common strategy." The bigger gap — the plan is silent on what to do when things get scary. The implementation is being carried out by career civil servants reasonably well; the high-level political responses to crisis events, the Fable model suspension being his example, are being made from first principles by people who haven't read the plan. On the Fable suspension and the S-K Telecom complication — S-K Telecom is part of the same conglomerate that owns S-K Hynix, the leading producer of high-bandwidth memory. Korea is, in his words, "a really important partner," and "hardening their telecommunications infrastructure seems quite reasonable." The Fable ban is being justified by shifting rationales because the rationale doesn't survive contact with what the partner country actually does for U-S compute supply.

The Ollie-specific take. Hold the Ball episode and the Jumper move side by side. Ball says the frontier labs are becoming the new banks. Jumper, in effect, just walked into one. The bio-A-I question is no longer which architecture wins. It is which lab — with which political relationship to the U-S government, with which access to which compute, with which proprietary instrument network, with which Nobel-laureate-anchor scientist — ends up sitting on the dataset that gives biology its AlphaFold-of-the-cell moment. That set of constraints is now political as much as scientific. Anthropic spent six months assembling a science stack and then spent the same week being subject to export controls and an industrial distillation attack. The decisions that determine which biological foundation models will be allowed to exist, who will be allowed to use them, and what foreign collaborators will be allowed to share data with them, are being made now. Inside the labs. Not at conferences. That is the substrate of your field.

That's the deep cut.

Wrap.

Watch whether Anthropic stages its rumored livestreamed A-I-for-science launch event in the next few weeks — tying the Allen Institute partnership, H-H-M-I, the Coefficient Bio platform, and the science division team into a single product announcement. If Jumper is on stage, the play is to make the AlphaFold lineage continuous through Anthropic, the strongest possible move into your field. If he isn't, the play is slower and more like the Karpathy hire — let the credentialed presence accrue and let the product follow. Either way, this past week is the inflection point at which biomedical A-I stopped being a science problem with industrial sponsors and became an industrial problem with scientific stakeholders. Read it that way for the next twelve months. Have a good Sunday, Ollie.
