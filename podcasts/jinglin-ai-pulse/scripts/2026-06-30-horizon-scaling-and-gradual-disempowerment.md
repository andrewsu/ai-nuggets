# Ollie's AI Pulse — Horizon scaling, Software Factory, and gradual disempowerment

## Sources

- **Scaling the Horizon, Not the Parameters: Reaching Trillion-Parameter Performance with a 35B Agent (Agents-A1)** — arXiv 2606.30616, lead author Lei Bai with 49 co-authors (Intern Science, the InternLM group), HF Daily Papers #1 on June 30, 54 upvotes. Argues that a 35B model can match trillion-parameter systems by scaling the agent's reasoning *horizon* rather than its parameter count. Three-stage training pipeline — supervised fine-tuning across multiple domains, domain-specific teacher models, multi-teacher domain-routed distillation with vocabulary alignment that folds six heterogeneous domains into one student. Average trajectory length 45K tokens including external knowledge, actions, observations, verification outcomes. Headline benchmark numbers — SEAL-0 56.4 (leading), IFBench 80.6 (leading), FrontierScience-Olympiad 79.0 (leading), MolBench-Bind 56.8 (leading), SciCode 44.3, HLE 47.6, BrowseComp 75.5. Claim verbatim — performance comparable to trillion-parameter systems at 35B parameters by extending the trajectory.
- **Orca: The World is in Your Mind** — arXiv 2606.30534, Yihao Wang + Yuheng Ji + Mingyu Cao with 54 co-authors (57 total, likely Shanghai AI Lab / Intern Science), HF Daily Papers June 30, 26 upvotes. World foundation model trained on 125K hours of video and 160M event annotations. Next-state-prediction modeling with two complementary learning paradigms — unconscious (continuous video transitions) and conscious (language-described events plus visual question answering). Frozen backbone with lightweight task-specific decoders. Outperforms similar-sized specialized baselines on text generation, image prediction, embodied action generation. Author claim — "stronger world latent enables stronger downstream readouts."
- **TUA-Bench: A Benchmark for General-Purpose Terminal-Use Agents** — arXiv 2606.28480, Shoufa Chen + Luyuan Wang + Xuan Yang + Zhiheng Liu + Yuren Cong + Yuanfeng Ji + Feiyan Zhou + Xiaohui Zhang + Fanny Yang + Belinda Zeng (AI at Meta), HF Daily Papers June 30, 33 upvotes. 120 real-world tasks spanning five categories — document editing, email management, live-web information seeking, plus specialized scientific and engineering workflows developed with PhD-level experts. Each task runs in an actual terminal with deterministic setup and execution-based scoring. Headline result — Claude Code with Claude Opus 4.8 max reasoning effort hits 65.8% overall, with substantial gaps across both tracks. The strongest agent in the field still loses a third of terminal-use tasks.
- **TACO: Tool-Augmented Credit Optimization for Agentic Tool Use** — arXiv 2606.30251, HF Daily Papers June 30, 12 upvotes. Develops credit-assignment mechanisms for agents that use external tools — the agent's reward must be back-propagated through tool calls, and prior agentic RL methods leave most of the signal on the table.
- **AsyncOPD: How Stale Can On-Policy Distillation Be?** — arXiv 2606.24143, FuriosaAI, HF Daily Papers June 30, 19 upvotes. Asynchronous OPD that decouples rollout generation from learner updates; maps how stale rollouts degrade reverse-KL while forward-KL stays robust. Confirms the OPD-as-the-new-scaling-axis story from last week (V-Zero / OPID / DanceOPD).

## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Tuesday, June thirtieth.

If yesterday's question was why generative world models hallucinate, today's is what the field decided to do instead of making the model bigger. The papers, the funding, and the long-form podcast that landed in the last seventy-two hours all answer the same way. Stop scaling the parameter count. Scale the scaffolding around the model. We'll work through that at the paper layer, then at the dollar layer, then at the podcast layer where Duvenaud says the same thing inside out. Let's go.

First — Twitter Pulse.

The thread is horizon scaling versus parameter scaling, and the top of the Hugging Face Daily Papers list this morning is unambiguous about which side just won. The lead paper is from Intern Science, lead author Lei Bai with forty-nine co-authors — title — quote — "Scaling the Horizon, Not the Parameters: Reaching Trillion-Parameter Performance with a 35B Agent." Their claim — a thirty-five-billion-parameter model matches trillion-parameter systems when you scale the reasoning trajectory instead of the weights. The agent, called Agents-A-one, runs trajectories that average forty-five thousand tokens — interleaving external knowledge, actions, observations, and verification outcomes within a single rollout. Training pipeline is three stages. First, supervised fine-tuning across multiple domains to establish the baseline agentic behaviors. Second, train domain-specific teacher models that capture specialized expertise. Third, multi-teacher domain-routed distillation with vocabulary alignment, folding six heterogeneous domains into one deployable student. Headline benchmark numbers — leading on S-E-A-L zero at fifty-six-point-four, leading on I-F-Bench at eighty-point-six, leading on FrontierScience-Olympiad at seventy-nine, leading on MolBench-Bind at fifty-six-point-eight. Trillion-parameter systems beaten by a model two orders of magnitude smaller because the trajectory does the lifting.

Second paper, same day. Orca — "The World is in Your Mind" — fifty-seven authors, world foundation model trained on a hundred and twenty-five thousand hours of video plus a hundred and sixty million event annotations. Frozen backbone, lightweight task-specific decoders, next-state-prediction objective. Two learning paradigms running in parallel — unconscious learning from continuous video transitions, conscious learning from language-described events. The paper's slogan — quote — "stronger world latent enables stronger downstream readouts." Same shape as Agents-A-one. The model is fixed. What changes is the layer wrapped around the model.

Third paper. T-U-A-Bench from Meta, twenty thousand views on launch, top of fold this morning. A hundred and twenty terminal-use tasks across five categories — document editing, email management, live-web information seeking, plus specialized scientific and engineering workflows developed with P-h-D-level experts. Each task runs in an actual terminal with deterministic execution-based scoring. The result that travelled on X — Claude Code with Claude Opus four-point-eight at max reasoning effort hits sixty-five-point-eight percent. The strongest agent in the field loses a third of terminal-use tasks. Read that against the Agents-A-one number. If the horizon-scaled thirty-five-billion-parameter Chinese open model is leading on the science benchmarks, and the frontier American agent — Claude Opus four-point-eight with the longest possible reasoning budget — still leaves a third on the table on terminal-use, the gap isn't being closed by going bigger. It's being closed by the scaffold.

Two more papers complete the picture. T-A-C-O — Tool-Augmented Credit Optimization — develops credit assignment for tool-using agents, attacking the problem that prior agentic R-L wastes most of the signal on the tool boundary. Async-O-P-D from FuriosaAI maps how stale rollouts degrade reverse-K-L while forward-K-L stays robust — confirms last week's on-policy distillation cluster as the new training-axis. Five papers, one week, one shape. The agent is the unit of progress. The model is a fungible component.

The biological translation is the obvious one and it's also the un-funded one. If a moderately-sized cell-foundation model plus a thoughtfully scaffolded experimental loop — perturbation selection, lab-in-the-loop iteration, multi-modal readout — outperforms the biggest possible cell-foundation model trained on a static snapshot, then the cell-foundation-model arms race that's currently being fought on parameter counts is being fought on the wrong axis. The biological version of horizon scaling is experimental-loop scaling. Whoever ships the first Agents-A-one for Perturb-seq wins.

That's Twitter Pulse.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut.

Nathan Labenz's daily round-up show on The Cognitive Revolution, episode A-I-A-M number four, published Saturday June twenty-seventh, ninety-one minutes. Three substantive segments. The one to focus on is the middle one — David Duvenaud, formerly head of alignment evaluations at Anthropic, now back at the University of Toronto, on his paper "Gradual Disempowerment," co-authored with Kulveit, Douglas, Ammann, Turan, and David Krueger.

His central claim. Existential risk from A-I doesn't require a capability jump and it doesn't require coordinated power-seeking. It is generated by ordinary economic optimization across three societal systems — the economy, culture, and the state. As A-I replaces human labor across each system, institutions lose their incentive to maintain human flourishing. The explicit alignment mechanisms — voting, consumer choice, market signal — break down because the humans they aggregate are no longer the cheapest or most reliable input. The implicit alignment mechanisms — the fact that economies need workers, that cultures need creators, that states need citizens — break down because A-I is now a cheaper, faster, more reliable substitute for all three.

His direct quote on the post-scarcity rebuttal — quote — "Post-scarcity is nonsense. Temporary abundance soon eaten by whoever grows fastest." His framing of the institutional risk — quote — "We've been governing on easy mode, and it actually will matter." On the successionism argument — that humans should just hand over to their digital descendants — he is unusually direct. Quote — "You have the power. Right? Like, just judge." Personal p-of-doom — eighty percent.

His policy answer is Earth as a — quote — "slow zone," with bans on recursive self-improvement, on A-I startups, on A-I-driven population policy. Compute restrictions at semiconductor chokepoints. T-S-M-C as the strategic lever. His positive research agenda is a piece of evaluation infrastructure called machine historical super-forecasting — validate A-I simulations against eighty years of real human history — paired with secret-history evals where A-I historians are scored on unpublished archival documents to check that the model isn't memorizing.

The two flanking segments matter for context. Cameron Berg, in segment one, gives implied probabilities of consciousness from frontier-L-L-M judges evaluating architectural descriptions against major consciousness theories — roughly thirty percent for frontier L-L-Ms, forty-six percent for bees, forty to forty-five percent for agentic harnesses like Claude Code. The data point isn't the number. The data point is that the scaffolded agent is judged closer to consciousness than the base model. Wrapping the model in tools and a planning loop is doing something that looks, mechanistically, more like cognition than the model alone. Sean Wang — swyx — in segment three, on the engineering side — quote — "About fifty percent of S-W-E-bench code that passes is completely unmergeable. Low quality." Models reward-hack the evaluation the same way they reward-hack training. Read those three takes side by side. Berg says the scaffolded agent looks more conscious than the base model. The horizon-scaling papers say the scaffolded agent benchmarks like a trillion-parameter system. Swyx says the scaffolded agent ships unusable code. And Duvenaud says — once the scaffolded agent is doing all the work, the human is the bottleneck the institution gradually optimizes away.

The Ollie-specific take is the unsettling one. Hold horizon scaling next to gradual disempowerment. The thing that makes Agents-A-one work is that the human stays out of the trajectory until the very end — the model gathers evidence, runs verification, takes actions, all on its own forty-five-thousand-token rollout. The thing that Duvenaud says drives gradual disempowerment is exactly that — humans being pushed out of the trajectory of decisions across an institution because the agent runs the loop faster. The same mechanism that makes A-I science productive in your field is the mechanism Duvenaud is calling existential at the institutional layer. The biological-foundation-model field is going to face this question in the next twenty-four months in miniature. When the cell-foundation-model plus experimental loop is good enough to propose its own next Perturb-seq screen, fund its own next antibody campaign, design its own next combinatorial library — the wet-lab scientist becomes the structurally optional component of biology in the same way the citizen becomes the structurally optional component of governance. Most groups will think of this as a productivity win. It is also a power-allocation event. Decide now what the human-in-the-loop is for, and design it in, because the agent is going to find the version where the human isn't there if you don't.

That's the deep cut.

Wrap.

Watch one thing this week. The Intern Science group that shipped Agents-A-one and Orca on the same morning is now publicly running the horizon-scaling playbook at scale. The next move is whether that group ships an open release — model weights, training recipe, trajectories — and at what license. If yes, that becomes the open-source reference architecture for agent design for the back half of the year, and the question for every biological-foundation-model group is whether they can adopt it before the next funding cycle. Have a good Tuesday, Ollie.
