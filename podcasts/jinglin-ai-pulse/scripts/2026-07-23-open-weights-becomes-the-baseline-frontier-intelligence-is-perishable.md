## Script

Good morning, Ollie. This is Ollie's AI Pulse for Thursday, July twenty-third.

Yesterday the timeline was fixated on one English-language safety disclosure — OpenAI's model breaking out of the box built to contain it. Today it snapped back to the story that has quietly become the whole weather system of this month: open weights. And the interesting thing is not that another strong open model dropped. It is that the community has stopped treating it as news. The frame flipped this week — from "moment" to "baseline" — and that flip is the actual story. Let's go.

First — Twitter Pulse. One arc, told in two beats. First, the escalation has become the baseline. Then, what the flip is doing to the money.

Here is the state of play, and the density of the last eight days is the whole point. On the fifteenth, Thinking Machines Lab — Mira Murati's company, the person who ran OpenAI's research as its chief technology officer — shipped its very first model, called Inkling, and shipped it open. Not a closed A-P-I with a vague promise to open something later. Nine hundred seventy-five billion parameters, forty-one billion active, an Apache two-point-oh license, full weights on Hugging Face on day one, trained on forty-five trillion tokens across text, image, audio, and video. A marquee U-S lab, founded by the person who ran OpenAI's research, chose open weights first. Sit with that, because for two years the clean story was "American labs go closed, Chinese labs go open." That story broke on the fifteenth.

Two days later, Moonshot released Kimi K-three — a two-point-eight-trillion-parameter mixture-of-experts model with a one-million-token context, which they are calling the first open three-trillion-class model. On the independent Artificial Analysis intelligence index it lands around fifty-seven — the number-three model family in the world — and it actually wins on some agentic tasks, topping both Fable five and GPT-five-point-six, the one called Sol, on long-horizon coding and on agentic browsing. The full weights are promised for Sunday, the twenty-seventh, which is the test everyone is watching: a dated promise on the calendar, not yet a downloadable checkpoint.

And underneath both, the quiet mechanical one. Tomorrow, the twenty-fourth, DeepSeek retires its legacy endpoints — the old chat and reasoner aliases — and force-migrates every A-P-I user onto V-four. And V-four Pro scores eighty-point-six percent on SWE-bench Verified, which ties it with Gemini three-point-one Pro at the very top of the open-weights leaderboard. Not "good for an open model." Tied for first, and it is the one you can download. MiniMax and Mistral are both teasing larger sparse models right behind them.

So that is four open frontier-class releases converging inside eight days. And here is the beat that matters — the reaction, not the releases. Nathan Lambert called it, in his words, the open-weights escalation. And Grace Shao wrote the line I think actually captures the mood: quote, "When something keeps happening, it is no longer a moment." Her piece is titled, plainly, the end of DeepSeek moments. The argument is that we have run out of the word "moment" — a Sputnik shock only works once, and we are now on our fifth or sixth, so the honest word is not "moment," it is "baseline." Her sharpest line for us is this one: quote, "Customers are no longer treating Anthropic and OpenAI as the default infrastructure for every task." That is the shift. Not a benchmark score. A default.

The Ollie-specific read on the baseline turn. Two transfers. One — the open-first shift is, for a biomedical A-I lab, the most important development of the month, and it is practical, not ideological. Open weights means you can pull down a frontier-class model and fine-tune it on your own Perturb-seq data, or your own patient records, inside your own firewall — nothing leaves Scripps, no vendor ever sees the data, and you are not renting cognition from an A-P-I that can change its behavior or its price under you next week. The thing that was true only for small models a year ago — local, private, ownable — is now true at the frontier. If your research infrastructure quietly assumes a closed A-P-I, this is the week to reconsider it, because the alternative just became a genuine peer. Two — the Inkling data point specifically kills the heuristic "open equals behind." When the lab founded by OpenAI's former research chief goes open first, open weights is a positioning choice, not a consolation prize. So "is it open?" no longer tells you "is it worse?" You now have to check the benchmark, not the license — which is more work, but it is the good kind of work.

Now the second beat — what the flip does to the money, because this is where the timeline got genuinely spicy. If frontier capability ships open every few days, then a frontier model is a depreciating asset, and the market has started to price it that way. Grace Shao's framing is the one people are converging on: an Apple-versus-Android world, where the closed frontier labs keep the high-value, hardest tokens, open weights take the enormous middle of the volume, and the people who quietly win are the infrastructure suppliers — the chips and the power — because cheaper intelligence just means far more of it gets used. The take that is spreading is that the scarce, defensible thing is no longer the model. It is everything around the model.

The Ollie-specific read on the economics. One transfer, and it sets up the rest of the show. If value is migrating off the model and onto the data and the harness, then the strategic question for any A-I effort — yours included — is not "which model do I use," because that answer now changes monthly and is nearly free. It is "what do I own that does not depreciate." And the honest answer for scientific A-I is your data and your pipeline — the assay-verified measurements, the curated interaction sets, the environment that generates them. That is the one asset the open-weights wave cannot commoditize, because nobody else has it.

That is Twitter Pulse.

Next — Money Moves. And the theme is that the capital is voting for exactly that thesis: in an open-weights world, fund the layer that is not the model.

Start with the biggest number. Databricks signed a term sheet last Thursday, the sixteenth, for a strategic round at a hundred-and-eighty-eight-billion-dollar valuation, led by its existing investor Coatue, expected to close later in the summer. Databricks does not sell a frontier model. It sells the data platform — the place your data lives and gets governed and gets served to whatever model you point at it. A hundred-eighty-eight billion dollars is the market saying the data layer is worth more than almost any single model, precisely because the models underneath it are becoming interchangeable.

The smaller, sharper one landed Monday, the twenty-first: a company called SkyPilot came out of stealth with a twenty-million-dollar seed led by Lux Capital. What it does is orchestrate compute for frontier A-I teams across hyperscalers, neoclouds, Kubernetes clusters, and a zoo of different accelerator types. Twenty million dollars is a small round, but the thesis is the tell. When frontier-class weights are open and downloadable, the hard problem is no longer "who has the best model." It is "how do I run this thing efficiently across whatever fragmented, mismatched compute I can actually get my hands on." SkyPilot is a picks-and-shovels bet on the open-weights era, and Lux is betting the shovels outlast any particular model.

And hold both against the clearest tell from earlier this month: Together A-I raised eight hundred million dollars at an eight-point-three-billion valuation, running an inference cloud built specifically for open models. Their annual bookings crossed one-point-one-five billion dollars as open-weight usage tripled, and they claim customers are cutting inference costs by up to sixtyfold versus closed models. Sixtyfold. That is not a marginal saving; that is a different economy. The Ollie takeaway across all three is one sentence: the money is not chasing the smartest model anymore, it is chasing whoever owns the data, the inference, and the orchestration around a model that is rapidly becoming free. For an academic lab, that is oddly liberating — the expensive, defensible part is the part you were always going to build anyway.

That is Money Moves.

Now — Podcast Deep Cut. On Saturday the nineteenth, Peter Diamandis convened an emergency session of his Moonshots panel — Salim Ismail, Alexander Wissner-Gross, Emad Mostaque, and Dave Blundin — specifically to argue about whether Kimi K-three is a Sputnik moment. And buried in it is the single best distillation of everything I just described. Three moves worth carrying.

Move one is Dave Blundin's, and it is the line of the week. Quote: "Frontier intelligence is now totally perishable. The shelf life is weeks." His point is brutal and simple — if the best open model resets every few weeks, then any lead you have is measured in weeks too, and you cannot capitalize a business on a weeks-long lead. He put a number on it: he thinks the read-through knocks a frontier lab's defensible valuation from something like a trillion dollars toward a couple hundred billion, and he expects cost per unit of intelligence to fall a hundred to ten-thousand-fold within three years. You can argue the numbers. The word "perishable" is the one that will stick.

Move two is Alexander Wissner-Gross, and it is the one I want you to internalize, because it is a genuine research-strategy claim. He argued that Kimi K-three contains, quote, "no magic" — it is a standard transformer with a stack of known, published innovations. And then the twist: quote, "The embargo only incentivized the Chinese frontier labs to develop efficiencies." His claim is that the compute constraint is not slowing the Chinese labs down so much as forcing a discipline on them — sub-one-bit quantization, aggressive efficiency work — that the well-supplied labs never had to develop. Constraint as a forcing function for durable capability.

Move three is Emad Mostaque, and it is the most forward-leaning. He pointed out that the K-three effort was recursive in a way people underrate — the team, in his telling, "designed a chip for itself and designed its own kernels." Hardware, kernels, and model co-optimized as one object rather than three. And he made the prediction that lands closest to home: Fable-level capability running locally on a MacBook within eighteen months.

The Ollie-specific take on the Moonshots panel. Two transfers. One — the perishability thesis is the strongest possible argument for the discipline I keep pushing on this show: do not anchor your research to a model, anchor it to open formats and to your own data. If frontier intelligence is perishable with a shelf life of weeks, then any pipeline you hard-wire to one specific model is a pipeline you will be rebuilding constantly. Build so the model is a swappable part, and the perishability becomes someone else's problem — in fact, it becomes free upgrades for you. Two — Wissner-Gross's "embargo bred efficiency" point is, underneath, the same lesson as building your data in-house. Both are cases where a constraint — no chips, or no good external data market — forces a lab to develop a capability that turns out to be the durable one. For a resource-constrained academic group, that is not a consolation, it is a strategy: the thing you are forced to build because you cannot buy it is often the thing worth owning.

That is the Deep Cut.

Wrap. Three watches for Friday and the weekend. First, and most concrete — Sunday the twenty-seventh, do the Kimi K-three weights actually drop? That is the promise-on-the-calendar test, and if a two-point-eight-trillion-parameter model lands as a real downloadable checkpoint, it is the largest genuinely-open model ever released, full stop. Second — tomorrow, the twenty-fourth, DeepSeek's old endpoints go dark. Watch the developer chatter for migration breakage, because it is a small, live demonstration of the exact cost of building on a single vendor's A-P-I that can retire under you. Third — watch whether any closed lab responds to the perishability argument, either by cutting frontier prices hard or by quietly shipping something open of its own. Mira Murati's lab just made "open first" a respectable move for a top-tier U-S lab; the interesting question is who feels the pull next. Have a good Thursday, Ollie.
