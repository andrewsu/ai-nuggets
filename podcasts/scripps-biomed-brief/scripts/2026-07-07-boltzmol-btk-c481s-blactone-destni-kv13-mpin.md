## Script

Welcome back to Scripps Biomedical Brief for Tuesday, July 7th. Today, four bioRxiv preprints from the last 24 to 48 hours — an AI-native virtual-screening pipeline that turned in binders on six of ten targets with a screening budget in the tens of compounds, a chiral beta-lactone warhead that resolves ibrutinib resistance and lets stereochemistry pick out kinome subsets, an engineered proximity-labeling ligase that finally extends the technique from proteomics to metabolomics, and a previously uncharacterized allosteric pocket on the potassium channel KV1.3 with a non-contact selectivity mechanism and in vivo psoriasis efficacy.

**Story one: BoltzMol-1 — an A-D-M-E-T-triaged Boltz-2 pipeline that pulled functional actives on six of ten targets with experimental budgets in the tens of compounds.**

Noah Getz, Gabriele Corso, Saro Passaro and colleagues at Boltz P-B-C — with academic co-authors including Anthony Gitter and Joshua Kritzer — push Boltz-2 out of the retrospective-benchmark regime and into prospective hit discovery, then package the result as BoltzMol-1. The core pipeline is model-driven ranking coupled to procurement from commercial catalogs, but the load-bearing piece is an early-stage triage layer for kinetic solubility, lipophilicity, and Caco-2 permeability that filters candidates before anyone spends money to synthesize or purchase — the classic developability filter, applied at ranking time. On a ten-target panel most of which had no representation in the underlying affinity training data, the pipeline identified functional actives or binders on six of ten targets, with experimental budgets from twenty-eight to ninety-six compounds per target. The interesting bit is that the hits included receptors and enzymes traditionally considered hard for structure- or ligand-based methods — which is where AI-native screening has to work if it is going to displace campaign-scale high-throughput screening rather than just supplement it. For a research center thinking about where to sit on the make-buy line for hit discovery, that is a concrete data point from a lab tightly plugged into the current wave of pretrained co-folding models.

**Story two: Chiral beta-lactone electrophiles engage wild-type BTK and the ibrutinib-resistant C481S mutant at distinct sites, and enantiomers pick out different kinome subsets.**

Chao Wang and colleagues in Ziyang Zhang's lab at U-C Berkeley, with Markus Schirle and Jeff McKenna at Novartis, introduce chiral beta-lactone electrophiles as a new covalent-warhead class for kinase targeting. The cysteine-to-serine mutation at residue 481 of Bruton's tyrosine kinase is the dominant mechanism of clinical resistance to ibrutinib in mantle cell lymphoma and chronic lymphocytic leukemia, because it eliminates the anchor cysteine that ibrutinib and every other clinical BTK covalent inhibitor requires. The useful move here is that the asymmetric beta-lactone warhead achieves stereoselective covalent modification of both wild-type BTK and the C481S mutant — but at distinct sites of reactivity, meaning the same warhead class covers wild-type and mutant enzymes through orthogonal chemistry. Extending this to kinase-directed beta-lactone probes, individual enantiomers preferentially engage different subsets of the kinome. The conceptual advance is that stereochemistry becomes an intrinsic selectivity filter in covalent drug discovery — the mirror-image of the same probe can be a different reagent — and the immediate practical output is a scaffold usable against both wild-type and ibrutinib-resistant BTK, at a moment when the covalent warhead menu has been narrow.

**Story three: DESTNI extends proximity labeling from proteomics to metabolomics — an engineered desthiobiotin ligase pulls amine-metabolite spatial signatures out of live cells.**

Chan-Mi Yoo and Hyun-Woo Rhee at Seoul National University push the TurboID lineage in a new direction. Proximity labeling has become the workhorse of spatial proteomics, but small-molecule metabolites have stayed out of reach — the labeling chemistry did not extend and the modified metabolites were hard to identify. They evolve a desthiobiotin ligase from TurboID by yeast display, name it DESTNI, and build a companion annotation framework — synthetic desthiobiotin-metabolite standards plus in vitro DESTNI profiling plus a machine-learning tandem mass spectrometry predictor — so the labeled metabolites can actually be identified. Targeted to specific organelles, DESTNI recovered reproducible compartment-specific amine-metabolite signatures, with mitochondrial-matrix enrichment of glycine, 5-aminolevulinic acid, ornithine, and spermidine adducts, and nuclear enrichment of gamma-aminobutyric acid and 5-aminovaleric acid adducts. The significance is that spatial metabolomics inside living cells now sits on the same platform footing as spatial proteomics — the enzyme is engineered, the annotation pipeline is defined, and the first compartment maps land where organelle biochemistry predicted they should.

**Story four: A previously uncharacterized extracellular allosteric pocket on KV1.3, and a state-dependent inhibitor that resolves the paralog selectivity problem through residues that never touch the ligand.**

Guoyi Luo and colleagues with Cheng Tang at Hunan Normal University report M-PiN, a highly selective, state-dependent KV1.3 inhibitor with in vivo efficacy in a mouse psoriasis model, and the structural pocket it engages. KV1.3 is a validated autoimmune drug target — every previously disclosed inhibitor has converged on the same conserved sites, with recurring selectivity and clinical failure problems. M-PiN engages a previously uncharacterized extracellular pocket framed by the turret loops P-P-1 and P-P-2, the pore helix, and the outer S-5 and S-6 helices, and it acts through a bidirectional pore-to-sensor coupling that constricts the selectivity filter and pushes the voltage sensor toward activation simultaneously. The surprising piece is how subtype selectivity is dictated across the KV1 paralogs: even though the pocket itself is highly conserved, the peripheral residues G427 and H451 define the pocket geometry without directly contacting the ligand — call it non-contact selectivity. For structure-guided immunomodulator design, this opens a distinct chemical space on a validated but historically frustrating target and gives a rational handle for next-generation selective KV1.3 drugs.

That is the briefing for July 7th. Talk to you tomorrow.
