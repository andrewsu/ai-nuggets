## Script

Welcome to the Scripps Biomedical Brief for Friday, June nineteenth. Four preprints from the past forty-eight hours, touching early-amyloid detection chemistry, structure-guided antibody alternatives, proteasomal control of transcription, and a piece of drug repurposing on a glycosylation target.

First, from the Frydman lab at Stanford, a new fluorescence assay called Q-DOAS that meaningfully extends what we can see in protein aggregation. Thioflavin T, the workhorse for amyloid kinetics for fifty years, reports only on mature cross-beta fibrils and is essentially blind to the early oligomers that most current disease models implicate. Q-DOAS instead site-specifically conjugates a single BODIPY-TMR dye to a target protein, and self-assembly is read out as proximity quenching — the dye signal drops as monomers come together, well before any cross-beta structure forms. The authors validate it on mutant huntingtin exon one and the A-fifty-three-T alpha-synuclein variant, dissect mutational effects on early oligomerization, run it as a plate-reader primary screen for protein and small-molecule inhibitors, and use it to quantify seeding activity in cellular and mouse Huntington's models. They also detect amyloid seeds directly from Parkinson's patient cerebrospinal fluid without any amplification step, which is a real difference from existing seed-amplification assays. For Huntington's, the synucleinopathies, and anyone running an aggregation-focused medicinal chemistry campaign, this looks like the cleanest available primary assay.

Second, a cryo-EM structure of LRRC15 from Shabih Shakeel's group at the Walter and Eliza Hall Institute that turns a clinical antibody story into a target-class story. LRRC15 is a leucine-rich-repeat membrane protein massively upregulated on cancer-associated fibroblasts in desmoplastic tumors — pancreatic, breast, head and neck — and the antibody-drug conjugate samrotamab vedotin is in phase one against it. The two-point-six-angstrom complex shows samrotamab engaging a membrane-proximal lateral epitope, which means the canonical concave leucine-rich-repeat surface — the face where any LRR receptor would normally dock its signaling partners — is left entirely exposed. The authors then designed, computationally and de novo, minibinders against that concave surface, and pulled out multiple nanomolar binders. The interesting implication is the next-generation playbook for cancer-associated fibroblasts. Instead of just delivering a cytotoxic payload through occupancy, you can engage the signaling-competent face directly with small designed proteins, either blocking stromal crosstalk or carrying orthogonal payloads. It is also a clean illustration of how de novo binder design now slots directly into an experimental structure pipeline within a single paper.

Third, from Michael Rape's group at Berkeley, an unusual mode of proteasomal regulation of transcription. The Groucho-TLE co-repressors sit at transcription start sites holding genes off. Rape's group shows that the cullin-RING ligase SCF-FBXL14 ubiquitinates them not by recognizing their sequence, but by recognizing the act of TLE-mediated chromatin presentation itself. The ligase effectively ubiquitinates whatever the TLEs are presenting, including the TLEs themselves, and the proteasome strips the complex off DNA — a continuous cycling that keeps transcription start sites accessible to activators. Stem cells lose lineage specification when the circuit is broken, and cancer-associated TLE-one mutations that block FBXL14 recruitment now become interpretable as loss of cycling rather than loss of repression per se. As a target class, FBXL14 looks like a node where one could rebalance transcription in cancers carrying these TLE alterations, and the broader principle — an E3 ligase that reads presentation rather than substrate sequence — is worth tracking because it implies many more presentation-dependent degrons than we currently annotate.

Fourth, a chemical-probe paper from Michael Boyce's lab at Duke worth flagging because the molecule is disulfiram. The classical alcohol-deterrent drug has been a screen hit against many things, but here a coupled luminescence screen against UDP-galactose four-prime-epimerase — GALE, the central enzyme balancing the UDP-galactose and UDP-glucose pools and their N-acetyl counterparts — finds disulfiram inhibiting through covalent modification of cysteine one-fifty-three by its diethyldithiocarbamate metabolite. Cellular treatment phenocopies genetic GALE deletion, knocking down terminally sialylated glycans and mucin-domain O-glycosylation, and galactose supplementation rescues. The therapeutic angle they raise is mucin hypersecretion in muco-obstructive lung disease and mucinous tumors, where nothing currently exists on the nucleotide-sugar side. Beyond disulfiram itself, an HTS-compatible assay is now in place for a proper GALE medicinal chemistry campaign.
