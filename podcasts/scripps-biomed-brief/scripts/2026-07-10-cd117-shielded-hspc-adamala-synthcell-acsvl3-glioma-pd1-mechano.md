## Script

Welcome back to Scripps Biomedical Brief for Friday, July 10th. Four preprints from the last 24 to 48 hours — an antibody-plus-prime-edited-stem-cell strategy that could get gene therapy off busulfan, the first synthetic cell that actually completes a full cell cycle from a chemically defined starting point, a decades-old orphan glioma-metabolism enzyme finally paired with a well-tolerated in vivo tool compound, and a mechano-immunology finding that says how a T cell squeezes through tissue directly sets its P-D-1 level.

**Story one: Toxin-free hematopoietic stem cell conditioning — a new anti-CD-117 antibody paired with prime-edited, epitope-shielded human C-D-34 cells opens a route out of busulfan for gene therapy.**

Romina Marone, Lukas Jeker and colleagues in Basel, working with David Liu at the Broad, Toni Cathomen in Freiburg, and Andre Lieber in Seattle, take direct aim at the biggest remaining obstacle in curative ex-vivo gene therapy for hemoglobinopathies — the myeloablative conditioning that clears the bone marrow niche. They generate a new C-D-117-blocking monoclonal antibody, C-I-M-058, that potently disables wild-type H-S-P-Cs, and use prime editing to introduce a single-nucleotide epitope shift into human C-D-34+ cells so the antibody no longer touches the transplanted cells. The two components are complementary: the antibody clears the endogenous niche without alkylator chemotherapy, and the edited donor cells are invisible to it, giving what the authors call in-vivo selection. Combined, they ameliorate disease phenotype in a β-thalassemia model. If this holds up in primates and eventually in humans, it directly attacks the busulfan toxicity that has kept ex-vivo sickle cell and thalassemia gene therapy from broad rollout, and it does so by putting two orthogonal engineered modalities — a shielding-epitope prime edit and a receptor-blocking antibody — into the same protocol.

**Story two: The first fully chemically defined synthetic cell that feeds, grows, replicates a 90-kilobase genome, divides, and undergoes selection — synthetic biology crosses a threshold.**

Kate Adamala and colleagues at Minnesota, with Aaron Engelhart, report a synthetic cell built from purified components that runs a complete cell cycle end to end. The genome is a 90-kilobase multi-plasmid design encoding P-U-R-E-style translation, Phi-29 rolling-circle genome replication, and — critically — a genetically encoded feeding mechanism in which an internally expressed alpha-hemolysin variant binds nickel-N-T-A lipids on incoming feeder liposomes, driving fusion that supplies fresh membrane and translation machinery. Because feeding requires an internally translated protein, a mutation that raises expression of that protein directly increases growth, offspring number, and fitness — coupling genotype to selection inside the vesicle in a way earlier minimal cells did not. They demonstrate five generations of cell cycle, spread of a beneficial mutation through a population under resource competition, and genetically encoded rather than mechanical division. The significance is less any single component than the fact that all the hallmarks — encoded metabolism, replication, growth, division, heritable selection — are running in one operational, chemically defined system. That is the object synthetic biology has been building toward, and the platform it opens for programmable therapeutic living materials and directed evolution.

**Story three: A first-in-class metabolic target for glioblastoma — very-long-chain acyl-CoA synthetase-3, druggable in vivo with a natural-product tool compound at a workable therapeutic window.**

Emily Clay and Paul Watkins at Kennedy Krieger and Johns Hopkins Neurology nominate A-C-S-V-L-3, an acyl-CoA synthetase that activates very-long-chain fatty acids, as a metabolic dependency of glioma that is largely absent in normal glia. Genetic depletion of A-C-S-V-L-3 slows U-87-M-G and Mayo-22 glioma cells and their xenografts; pharmacologically, the natural-product inhibitor grassofermata reproduces the knockout phenotype at low-micromolar concentrations, blocking beta-oxidation across C-16 to C-24 fatty acids, inducing G-F-A-P-positive astrocytic differentiation, and reducing invasion. Normal human fibroblasts are unaffected at doses that stop glioma cells. In N-O-D-S-C-I-D mice, up to 32 milligrams per kilogram per day of the drug is grossly well tolerated, and a much lower 2 milligrams per kilogram per day slows U-87 xenografts. Glioblastoma has famously few actionable dependencies; identifying a lipid-activation enzyme that is glioma-enriched and druggable in vivo with an off-the-shelf small molecule reopens a class of chemistry-forward starting points for a disease that badly needs them.

**Story four: How a T cell moves is how much P-D-1 it wears — mechanical squeezing through confined pores acutely degrades the checkpoint and rewrites the mechanobiology of adoptive cell therapy.**

Yunus Alapan, Susan Napier Thomas and colleagues at Emory and Georgia Tech show, using microphysiological transmigration devices and in vivo models, that C-D-8 T cells squeezing through confined pores lose surface P-D-1 within minutes via ubiquitin-mediated proteasomal degradation, and gain function and fitness in parallel. The mechanism is conserved across species and applies to human tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes and C-A-R T cells; the imprint correlates with outcome in human melanoma. This flips the framing on immune checkpoint expression, which has been treated as a purely molecular readout of exhaustion; here, the mechanical history of the cell — where it has been and what it squeezed through — is a co-equal determinant. Immediately practical: a mechano-priming step in the manufacturing of C-A-R T cells or T-I-L products could deliver checkpoint-blockade-like fitness without engaging the P-D-1 pathway pharmacologically. It is a rare handle on adoptive cell therapy that lives outside the target-antigen and gene-editing playbook.

That's the briefing for July 10th. Talk to you tomorrow.
