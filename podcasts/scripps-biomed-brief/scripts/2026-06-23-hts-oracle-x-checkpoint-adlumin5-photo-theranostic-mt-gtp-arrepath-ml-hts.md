## Script

Welcome to the Scripps Biomedical Brief for Tuesday, June twenty-third. Four fresh bioRxiv preprints — all posted in the last twenty-four to thirty-six hours and the bodies aren't rendered yet, so the takes below are grounded in the abstracts. A prospective deep-learning screen that pulls genuine small-molecule binders out of a one-hundred-thousand-compound library against three of the hardest immuno-oncology targets, a self-photosensitizing chemiluminescent small molecule that uses light it generates itself to degrade misfolded proteins in the Alzheimer's brain, an atomic-resolution cryo-electron-microscopy picture of how G-T-P hydrolysis actually destabilizes the microtubule lattice, and a sober industry account of what machine-learning-guided phenotypic high-throughput screening for antibiotics looks like when you actually deploy it.

First, from Somaya Abdel-Rahman and Moustafa Gabr at Weill Cornell Medicine, an A-I-guided prospective discovery of small-molecule binders against C-D twenty-eight, T-I-M three, and VISTA — three immune-checkpoint targets the field has tried and largely failed to crack with small molecules. The recognition surfaces on these costimulatory and coinhibitory receptors are shallow and featureless, and conventional high-throughput screens against them deliver characteristically low hit rates. The Gabr group built H-T-S Oracle X, a multimodal deep-learning platform that fuses ChemBERTa SMILES embeddings with extended R-D-Kit descriptors through bidirectional cross-attention, trains on continuous biophysical binding signals rather than the usual binary hit-or-miss labels, and uses Monte Carlo dropout to down-weight uncertain predictions. Training data was forty-five-thousand-seven-hundred-sixty Dianthus T-R-I-C-screened compounds per target under scaffold-aware cross-validation. They then ran prospectively against a one-hundred-thousand-compound Enamine library and selected one-hundred-fifty compounds to test. Forty-five dose-response confirmed as binders — a thirty-percent prospective hit rate, two-hundred-thirty-four to four-hundred-eight-fold over random-prospective baselines, and sixteen of those were sub-micromolar. The top hits sit at binding constants of two-hundred-thirty-three nanomolar on C-D twenty-eight, two-hundred-forty-nine nanomolar on T-I-M three, and three-hundred-forty-five nanomolar on VISTA, with on-target functional activity in immune-cell and tumor-coculture assays. The combination of training on real biophysical signals rather than thresholded labels, uncertainty-weighted selection, and immediate dose-response and functional validation gives this work a believable claim on a class of targets where A-I-led campaigns have repeatedly looked good on retrospective benchmarks and then failed prospectively.

Second, from Huizhe Wang, Shiju Gu and colleagues with Chongzhao Ran at the Martinos Center at Mass General and Harvard Medical School, a self-photosensitizing chemiluminescent small molecule, A-D-Lumin five, that degrades misfolded proteins in the brain using light it generates itself. Photodynamic and photo-degradation strategies for protein aggregates have been limited by the need for an external excitation source — brain tissue scatters and absorbs visible light, so external excitation cannot reach the parenchyma. A-D-Lumin five binds the aggregate and chemiluminescently produces its own visible light, which sensitizes singlet oxygen to oxidize and degrade the protein on contact. In vitro the chemistry oxidizes and degrades beta-amyloid, tau, alpha-synuclein, and T-D-P forty-three, confirmed by liquid-chromatography mass spec, MALDI mass spec, and Western blotting. For beta-amyloid, oxidation reduced cellular toxicity. In vivo, four months of longitudinal treatment in the five-X-F-A-D mouse model reduced beta-amyloid accumulation by molecular imaging. Because the chemiluminescence is itself the readout, A-D-Lumin five is also an imaging agent for the aggregate it is destroying — a photo-theranostic that closes the loop between target engagement, action, and readout in one molecule. The translational hook is a chemistry-first alternative to enzyme- and antibody-mediated clearance of aggregates with a working, aggregate-class-agnostic mechanism.

Third, from Juan Estevez-Gallego, Pavel Filipcik and colleagues with Michel Steinmetz at the Paul Scherrer Institute, the atomistic mechanism of microtubule destabilization by G-T-P hydrolysis, resolved by cryo-electron microscopy at one-point-nine to two-point-two angstroms across four nucleotide states. The prevailing view has been that hydrolysis of G-T-P to G-D-P in lattice tubulin drives microtubule shrinking, but the actual chemical transitions in the nucleotide pocket and how they propagate into lattice destabilization have not been visible. The new structures resolve hundreds of water molecules, ions, and side-chains carrying out the catalysis. Lattice compaction — the G-T-P-bound polymer pulling tighter into a pre-hydrolysis state — turns out to be what activates a specific catalytic water for gamma-phosphate cleavage. Hydrolysis intermediates are held together by strengthened lattice contacts, and only after the final reaction products leave the active site do those contacts break and the filament destabilize. For a target class that anchors clinical chemistry from vinca alkaloids to taxanes to maytansinoid antibody-drug-conjugate payloads, the practical implication is that this lattice-state-specific water-and-ion network is now a structural starting point for designing nucleotide-state-selective small molecules that prior moderate-resolution structures could not support.

Fourth, from Paul Lukacs and colleagues with Kurt Thorn at Arrepath, a real-world account of what happens when you actually deploy machine-learning-guided phenotypic high-throughput screening for antibiotics against E. coli. Learning-to-Rank models were trained on public and proprietary antibacterial datasets with two objectives simultaneously — maximize phenotypic growth inhibition and minimize human-cell cytotoxicity. The screen was run against a hyperpermeable L-P-T-D minus E. coli strain, a fairly typical Gram-negative discovery vehicle, and the team evaluated pre-plated commercial libraries plus a set of structurally novel cherry-picked compounds. Two headline numbers: a doubled hit rate and three-fold fewer cytotoxic hits compared with unguided screening, and the activity gains held against both wildtype E. coli and the L-P-T-D minus mutant. The model retained predictive power on compounds structurally dissimilar from training data — the most common failure mode for retrospective M-L — and the team carried hit confirmation, profiling, cytotoxicity counter-screening, and mechanism-of-action determination all the way through. The honest framing in the title is real-world results, and the take is that machine learning is now reliably useful as a hit-rate and toxicity filter on an industrial Gram-negative campaign, in a setting where every fold improvement in primary screening efficiency is real cost.
