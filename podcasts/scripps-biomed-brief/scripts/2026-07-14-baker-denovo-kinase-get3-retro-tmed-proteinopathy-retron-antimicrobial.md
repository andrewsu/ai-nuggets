## Script

Welcome back to Scripps Biomedical Brief for Tuesday, July fourteenth. Four bioRxiv preprints posted over the last twenty-four hours — de novo designed miniprotein modulators that target the kinase domain itself and toggle a kinase between inhibited and hyperactive states, a cryo-E-M mechanism that finally explains Retro-1 and Retro-2 by putting an allosteric wedge into Get3 of the G-E-T pathway, a therapeutic node built from a T-M-E-D cargo receptor complex that clears mutant client accumulation across a kidney proteinopathy and a retinitis pigmentosa model, and a bacterial retron whose activation logic is now understood well enough to computationally design synthetic triggers that turn it into a programmable antimicrobial.

**Story one: A de novo miniprotein platform gets you selective activation or inhibition of the same kinase without touching the A-T-P site.**

Bauer, Lee, Coventry, and colleagues from David Baker's Institute for Protein Design at U-W with Behnam Nabet, Michael Elowitz, and Daniel Lietha take on kinase selectivity from a different angle. Instead of another A-T-P-competitive small molecule fighting to distinguish itself among five hundred conserved active sites, they design ninety-six genetically encoded miniproteins that bind the kinase domain of focal adhesion kinase in specific conformational states. Thirty-three of them shift F-A-K activity. Of the four most potent, two are low-nanomolar inhibitors and two potentiate F-A-K by more than two-fold — activation is not a byproduct, it's a design objective they achieved. The effect transfers from biochemistry to cells; expressed inside live cells, the same designs inhibit or activate F-A-K in situ. And because conserved architecture is now leverage rather than an obstacle, they reuse the F-A-K inhibitor scaffolds against Src by retargeting, which is the point that changes what the platform means for drug discovery. You get a way to target kinases outside the A-T-P pocket, a way to discover new modulatory surfaces on the kinase domain, and — for the first time in a generalizable way — a way to design activators, not just inhibitors, on demand.

**Story two: Retro-1 and Retro-2 finally have a mechanism — they're allosteric traps of Get3.**

Lee, Gu, Chan, and colleagues from Bil Clemons at Caltech with Jason Sello and Vladimir Denic solve the long-standing mystery of how Retro-1 and Retro-2 protect cells from toxins and viruses. Both are structurally unrelated small molecules that have been useful chemical biology probes for a decade, blocking retrograde trafficking without a known target. This work identifies the target as Get3 — the A-T-Pase chaperone of the G-E-T pathway that inserts tail-anchored proteins into the E-R membrane, including the SNAREs that retrograde transport requires. Cryo-E-M shows both compounds bind a cryptic pocket in Get3 and freeze it in a stalled complex with upstream pathway components. Two things follow. First, the G-E-T pathway is now a validated therapeutic vulnerability for pathogen and toxin entry, one that no drug program has been aimed at deliberately. Second, this is a clean example of what medicinal chemists call complex stabilization — you're not blocking a site, you're gluing a productive machine into a nonproductive intermediate. The parallel to molecular glues and to allosteric splicing modulators is deliberate. With a cryptic pocket, a co-structure, and two structurally distinct hits already available, the optimization path is unusually well-lit.

**Story three: A T-M-E-D cargo receptor entrapment complex is a shared therapeutic node across genetically distinct proteinopathies.**

Riedl Khursigara, Goss, and colleagues with senior author Anna Greka at the Broad and collaborators from the Massachusetts Eye and Ear identify a hetero-oligomeric cargo receptor complex — T-M-E-D-7 paired with T-M-E-D-2, 9, and 10 — that anchors structurally and functionally diverse mutant secretory clients in the early secretory pathway by tethering to the Golgi protein G-R-A-S-P-55. The framing that matters: proteinopathies as different as autosomal-dominant tubulointerstitial kidney disease and rhodopsin-driven retinitis pigmentosa are not usually thought of as sharing a druggable mechanism. This group argues they do, because the same T-M-E-D entrapment complex is what holds toxic mutant cargoes in place long enough to damage the cell. Genetic ablation of the entrapment node clears the accumulated cargo. And a small molecule they call B-R-D-7-6-3-5 does the same pharmacologically — reversing histopathology and rescuing function in both a kidney proteinopathy mouse model and a retinitis pigmentosa mouse model, including measurable vision preservation. This is what an actionable proteostasis target looks like: a shared node, a chemical probe with in vivo activity, and two very different indications benefiting from the same intervention.

**Story four: Structural understanding of a bacterial retron enables computationally designed antimicrobial triggers.**

Hibshman, Wang, and colleagues from Eva Nogales at U-C Berkeley and Seth Shipman at Gladstone solve the cryo-E-M structure of retron-Kva2, a type nine retron from Klebsiella variicola, and use the mechanism it reveals to engineer a designer antimicrobial. Retrons are bacterial abortive infection systems: they sense phage invasion and kill the host cell before the phage can propagate. Retron-Kva2 assembles into an asymmetric higher-order ribonucleoprotein complex that sequesters a toxic dimeric HEPN R-Nase at its core, kept off until a phage protein — in this case T-5's D-5 — mimics the retron's own winged-helix fold, binds the m-s-D-N-A sensor, and structurally remodels the assembly to release HEPN and shred t-R-N-A. The key insight is that retron-Kva2 recognizes a structural fold, not a primary sequence — so any protein presenting the right helix-turn-helix architecture activates it. The team uses that as a design principle: computationally designed de novo synthetic triggers, unrelated in sequence to D-5, activate retron-Kva2 growth arrest in living bacteria. This is bacterial defense repurposed as a programmable antimicrobial modality — a class where new mechanisms are badly needed and where structure-guided design has now demonstrated it can produce triggers that are not just co-opted from nature.

That's the briefing for July fourteenth. Talk to you tomorrow.
