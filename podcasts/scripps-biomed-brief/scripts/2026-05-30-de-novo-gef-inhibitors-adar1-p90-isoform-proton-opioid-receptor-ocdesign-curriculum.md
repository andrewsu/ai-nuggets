# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-05-30

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: de novo designed protein inhibitors that selectively shut down RhoA guanine nucleotide exchange factors in living cells, an unexpected ADAR-one isoform that suppresses P-K-R signaling and drives colorectal cancer growth, structural pH-sensing in all three opioid receptors across every alternative splice variant, and a curriculum-guided protein design framework that finally cracks multi-property optimization.

First, from Mihai Azoitei's lab at Duke, an impressive piece of de novo protein design with direct intracellular signaling implications. The Dbl family of guanine nucleotide exchange factors are the main activators of RhoA-family GTPases, and they control rapid, subcellularly localized signaling events that have been essentially impossible to dissect with conventional small molecule inhibitors. The team built a generative protein design platform to make selective binders against individual Dbl-family GEFs on demand. The engineered miniproteins showed high affinity and remarkable target specificity across the family, with clean discrimination even between closely related members. They blocked GEF catalytic activity in cell-free assays and in living cells, modulating GTPase signaling reversibly. In a proof of principle, the team coupled one inhibitor to a light-activated module, so its activity could be optogenetically switched in cells. The broader point is that generative protein design is now mature enough to deliver target-class-spanning intracellular modulators against signaling proteins that small molecules have not been able to address.

Second, from Polly Chen and Leilei Chen at the Cancer Science Institute of Singapore comes a substantial revision of canonical ADAR-one biology. For decades the field has treated ADAR-one as essentially two isoforms — a nuclear p one ten and a cytoplasmic p one fifty. The Singapore team shows that intron thirteen of the ADAR-one transcript can be retained at substantial frequency, thanks to an evolutionarily conserved weaker five-prime splice site, generating two previously uncharacterized truncated isoforms, p ninety and p one thirty, which both lack the C-terminal portion of the deaminase domain. Intron retention is autoregulated by ADAR-one itself, by antagonizing hnRNPA1 binding in an editing-independent manner. Functionally, p ninety is predominantly cytoplasmic and represses P-K-R and eIF-two-alpha activation by sequestering double-stranded R-N-A — that is, it provides the canonical immune-suppressive ADAR-one function without any deaminase activity at all. In a colorectal cancer cohort, p ninety levels are elevated in most tumors relative to matched normal, and p ninety xenografts grow significantly faster than p one ten. For the growing roster of ADAR-one small molecule programs, this paper argues that deaminase-targeting and double-stranded-R-N-A-binding inhibitors will have non-equivalent therapeutic profiles depending on which isoform dominates in a given tumor.

Third, from Daniel Isom at the University of Miami Miller School, a structural and functional re-examination of the three opioid receptors — mu, delta, and kappa — under physiologically and pathologically relevant pH. Using a humanized yeast platform called DCyFIR, which isolates extracellular pH from cytoplasmic confounders, the team shows that all three opioid receptors are proton-gated coincidence detectors. Agonist efficacy falls steeply as extracellular pH drops into ranges seen in inflamed and ischemic tissue. Structure-based calculations and systematic variant profiling identify a single conserved aspartate, D one four nine in the Ballesteros-Weinstein three-point-three-two position, whose protonation breaks the salt bridge that anchors orthosteric opioid ligands. The team then profiled more than twenty mu-opioid receptor alternative splice variants and showed that every variant retaining a continuous seven-transmembrane fold preserves the pH sensor. The clinical observation that opioid analgesia fails at inflamed and acidotic sites now has a clean structural anchor, and D three-point-three-two becomes a defined target for pH-biased agonist medicinal chemistry.

And fourth, from Jianquan Zhao at the Institute of Computing Technology in Beijing, an AI protein design framework that explicitly tackles multi-property optimization. The motivating problem is that real therapeutic proteins must simultaneously satisfy solubility, structural stability, target binding affinity, and process robustness — and current one-shot generative methods almost always fail at this combinatorial optimization, requiring vast downstream screening to recover rare functional designs. OCDesign instead introduces objectives sequentially in a defined curriculum order, with experimental validation at each stage testing whether the newly introduced objective remains feasible. Using antibody-binding protein A as a test system, one-shot Pareto-style optimization yielded zero functional designs, while staged optimization — solubility and structural consistency first, then binding affinity, then alkaline resistance — produced multi-property functional proteins with substantially fewer wet-lab experiments. The bigger claim is that objective ordering is a first-class hyperparameter in high-dimensional protein design — not a nicety, but a determinant of whether the design problem has any feasible solutions at all.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about generative protein design, alternative-splicing target biology, and structural pharmacology converging on the harder problems in drug discovery. Until tomorrow.
