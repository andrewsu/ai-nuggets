## Script

Welcome to the Scripps Biomedical Brief for Friday, June twenty-sixth. Four bioRxiv preprints — two read in full, two from the abstract. Machine learning married to ultra-high-throughput droplet screening to navigate de novo enzyme space. A eukaryotic RNA-guided nuclease rewired by two mutations to acquire CRISPR-like diagnostic function. A structural account of PARP-1's invisible chromatin surveillance mode. And small molecules that selectively inhibit a bacterial deubiquitylase and cut intracellular pathogen load.

First, from Ke, Zhang, Fang, and colleagues with Longxing Cao, IMUSE — Integrating Machine Learning with Ultra-High-Throughput Screening for Enzyme space exploration. The problem is that de novo enzyme design now generates libraries far too large to test by conventional assay, and without evolutionary metadata, physics-based scorers cannot reliably separate active designs from inactive ones. IMUSE generates a synthetic fitness landscape to fill that gap. An initial library of structurally diverse de novo zinc hydrolases — designs sampling scaffold space beyond any known protein family — is screened by single-droplet double-emulsion fluorescence-activated cell sorting at millions of variants per run, recovering hundreds of active designs whose X-ray crystal structures confirmed the computational models to sub-angstrom precision. Those hits become the training set for a graph neural network that fuses ESM-2 sequence embeddings with AlphaFold-3 residue-level confidence scores and applies a specialized attention layer to the catalytic pocket. Applied prospectively to a second-generation library exploring expanded structural space, the model nearly quintupled the active-hit rate to thirty percent, and several selected designs achieved catalytic proficiency approaching native enzymes. The lesson: the bottleneck for AI-guided de novo enzyme engineering is not the design algorithms — it is the data gap, which synthetic fitness landscapes built from ultra-high-throughput screening can now fill.

Second, from Zhao, Xu, Huang, and colleagues, catalytic rewiring of Fanzor2, a eukaryotic RNA-guided endonuclease from Acanthamoeba polyphaga mimivirus, to acquire collateral trans-cleavage. Fanzors are the eukaryotic relatives of the TnpB transposons that gave rise to Cas12, but they naturally lack the bystander cleavage activity that makes Cas12a and Cas13a useful for diagnostics. The structural reason: in Fanzors, the catalytic glutamate in the RuvC-II active-site motif is displaced, creating a noncanonical arrangement. Restoring the canonical D-E-D catalytic triad with two substitutions — glycine-416 to glutamate, and glutamate-467 to proline — activates robust trans-cleavage while attenuating site-specific cis-cleavage. Systematically scanning the side-chain volume at position 467 showed that smaller residues create space inside the catalytic pocket for the reconstructed glutamate to adopt a geometry that accommodates non-specific reporter DNA after target recognition. Coupled with recombinase-aided isothermal amplification, the trans-cleaving variant drives FINDER, a Fanzor-based diagnostic platform that detected high-risk HPV at below two copies per microliter, was concordant with PCR in clinical specimens, and used a single guide RNA to cover eleven of fourteen high-risk HPV subtypes — possible because the engineered variant requires minimal target-adjacent sequence context compared to Cas12a. A mismatch-sensitive second variant enabled single-nucleotide variant genotyping confirmed against Sanger sequencing in twenty-three clinical samples.

Third, from Alexandria Fiorenza, Mahika Anand, and Karolin Luger at the University of Colorado Boulder and HHMI — going off the abstract, the body was not yet rendered — PARP-1 binds undamaged chromatin in an enzymatically inactive mode. The zinc-binding domains one, two, and three, together with the BRCT domain, cooperatively engage nucleosomal linker DNA and compact chromatin without triggering catalytic activation and without any sensitivity to PARP inhibitors. The model is that PARP-1 uses this idle pre-lesion state to surveil the genome and switches to catalytic activation only upon encountering a DNA lesion. For drug design the implication is direct: the inactive chromatin-associated pool of PARP-1 — structurally characterized here for the first time — is invisible to olaparib, niraparib, and related inhibitors that engage the active, damage-recruited form.

Fourth, from Lee, Hunt, and colleagues with Mark Hochstrasser at Yale and Jason Carlyon at Virginia Commonwealth University — also from the abstract — selective small-molecule inhibitors of OtDUB, the deubiquitylating enzyme that the obligate intracellular bacterium Orientia tsutsugamushi deploys to subvert host ubiquitin-dependent immune pathways during scrub typhus. Fluorescence-based screening identified amiloride hydrochloride and gentisic acid as selective OtDUB inhibitors with little activity against structurally related DUBs from Wolbachia and yeast. In infected cells, amiloride — an FDA-approved potassium-sparing diuretic — reduced both total cellular deubiquitylating activity and intracellular bacterial load. Scrub typhus kills tens of thousands annually across South and Southeast Asia and is accumulating antibiotic resistance. The selectivity of amiloride for the bacterial DUB over host enzymes argues for a structurally distinct active-site pocket and establishes pathogen-encoded deubiquitylases as a pharmacologically accessible drug target class for intracellular bacteria that are otherwise genetically intractable.
