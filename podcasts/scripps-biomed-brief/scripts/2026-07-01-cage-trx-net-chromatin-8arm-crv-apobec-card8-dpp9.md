## Script

Good morning. Today's Scripps Biomedical Brief covers four bioRxiv preprints from the last 48 hours: a genetically encoded photocaging strategy that turns any enzyme's active site into a light-triggered stopwatch for time-resolved crystallography, an eight-arm PEG-aminoglycoside that neutralizes neutrophil extracellular trap chromatin while retaining antibacterial activity in septic mice, a self-collapsing retrovirus that finally makes it possible to deliver viro-toxic cargoes like APOBEC3B into target cells, and a humanized-mouse reverse-genetics study that pins the pancytopenia of human DPP9 deficiency onto the CARD8 inflammasome.

**Story one: A genetically encoded photocage puts the active site on a light-triggered timer.**

Time-resolved crystallography wants to synchronize catalysis inside the crystal and then flash-freeze intermediates on the sub-second scale, but existing pump-probe schemes lean on caged substrates that have to be redesigned for each enzyme class. Clyde Smith, Ailiena Maggiolo, Alexander Deiters at Pittsburgh, Sergei Vakulenko at Notre Dame, and Aina Cohen at Stanford's SLAC now flip the strategy — instead of caging the substrate, they use genetic code expansion to install a photocaged non-canonical amino acid directly at a catalytic residue, and they call the workflow CAGE-TRX. Light removes the cage, restores the active-site residue, and starts the reaction in unison across all molecules in the crystal, enabling both pump-release-quench-probe cryocrystallography and pump-release-probe room-temperature serial crystallography. Using β-lactamases as a model, the team demonstrates efficient decaging, recovery of activity, and direct structural visualization of reaction intermediates. The reason this matters for drug discovery is that mechanistic snapshots of catalysis have historically depended on custom substrate chemistry per enzyme class — CAGE-TRX decouples reaction initiation from substrate design and makes serial time-resolved crystallography available for any enzyme where a catalytic residue can host a photocage, including the many drug-discovery-relevant enzyme families that have resisted mechanistic structural characterization.

**Story two: A multivalent aminoglycoside sponges up NET-associated chromatin while still killing bacteria.**

Neutrophil extracellular traps are useful for containing pathogens locally but disastrous when they spill into circulation during severe sepsis — the exposed DNA-histone chromatin lattice engages TLR4 and TLR9 on macrophages and drives systemic cytokine release. Chuanxu Cheng and colleagues with Kam Leong at Columbia and South China University of Technology screened a library of multivalent aminoglycoside-displayed polymers and identified an eight-arm PEG-conjugated netilmicin as the lead. Compared to two- and four-arm counterparts, the eight-arm construct binds DNA-histone chromatin with substantially higher avidity through stable, multivalent noncovalent contacts, retains full aminoglycoside antibacterial activity, and blocks NET-driven TLR4/TLR9 signaling in macrophages. In a severe septic mouse model, intravenous eight-arm netilmicin accumulates preferentially in inflamed tissue, reduces bacterial dissemination, cuts systemic cytokine output, protects multiple organs, and improves survival. The conceptual contribution is naming the NET-associated chromatin complex itself — not the neutrophil, not free DNA — as the actionable extracellular target, and demonstrating that a rationally designed multivalent material can hit that target selectively while sparing the antibacterial pharmacology of an approved drug class.

**Story three: A retrovirus that carries its own kill switch delivers viro-toxic cargoes at near-quantitative efficiency.**

Retroviral vectors break down when the cargo itself restricts retroviruses — the cytidine deaminase APOBEC3B, for example, mutates viral cDNA during reverse transcription and collapses vector titer. Christopher Mullally, Bojana Stefanovska, and Reuben Harris in the APOBEC field turn this constraint into a design principle with what they call the collapsing retrovirus. The APOBEC3B minigene is interrupted by a translation stop cassette that is itself flanked by direct repeats of the surrounding APOBEC3B sequence; the intact minigene is present in producer cells only as a disrupted, non-functional form, but during transduction the retroviral reverse transcriptase — which naturally templates high-frequency recombination across direct repeats — excises the stop cassette and reconstitutes the full-length cargo in the transduced cell. Reconstitution reaches ninety percent on its own and approaches one hundred percent when the minigene is coupled in-frame to a selectable marker. The immediate demonstration is APOBEC3B delivery for functional studies of DNA deamination, but the broader value of collapsing retrovirus is a generic strategy for delivering any cargo whose expression during vector production would otherwise self-defeat — restriction factors, toxins, cell-autonomous cytokines, gain-of-function CRISPR effectors — extending the reach of retroviral gene delivery into a class of payloads that has been effectively off-limits.

**Story four: Humanized-mouse reverse genetics reveals why DPP9 deficiency causes marrow failure in humans but not in mice.**

Loss-of-function DPP9 mutations cause Hatipoglu syndrome — a severe inflammasomopathy in which pancytopenia forces patients to bone marrow transplant — but Dpp9 mutant mice have normal hematopoiesis, so the mechanism of human cell loss has been unresolved. Tianli Xiao, James Brewer, and colleagues in Richard Flavell's lab at Yale combine CRISPR editing of primary human CD34-positive hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells with engraftment into MISTRG6 humanized mice to run reverse genetics directly on the human system. Human DPP9 deletion in these HSPCs was efficient and durable in vivo, and it faithfully reproduced peripheral and bone-marrow cytopenia through a cell-intrinsic mechanism, with almost no transcriptional response — implicating post-transcriptional regulation. Mechanistically, DPP9 loss activates the CARD8 inflammasome and drives pyroptosis of human HSPCs, while the closely related NLRP1 inflammasome is dispensable. The therapeutic lever this identifies is CARD8 — a target with small-molecule inhibitors now moving toward the clinic in other inflammasomopathy indications — and the broader methodological point is that MISTRG6 humanized-mouse reverse genetics on primary human HSPCs is now a tractable route to interrogate human-specific hematopoietic disease mechanisms that mouse knockouts miss.
