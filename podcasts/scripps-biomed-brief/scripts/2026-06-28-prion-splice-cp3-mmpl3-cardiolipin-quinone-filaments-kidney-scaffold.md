## Script

Good morning. Today's Scripps Biomedical Brief covers four stories from the past 48 hours: a splice-switching small molecule that lowers prion protein in mouse brains, a new regulatory site on tuberculosis's premier drug target, a structural discovery that rewrites how Gram-positive bacteria handle respiration, and a bioengineered kidney that integrates with the host circulation and produces urine in mice.

**Story one: A risdiplam-derived compound depletes prion protein in the brain by rerouting splicing.**

Prion diseases — including Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease and fatal familial insomnia — are uniformly fatal and have no approved treatments. The most credible therapeutic strategy is to lower cellular levels of prion protein before or early in disease, but no small molecule has been shown to accomplish this in the brain. A team led by Jingxin Wang at the University of Chicago and colleagues now reports a compound called CP3, generated by chemical modification of risdiplam — the clinically approved drug that corrects splicing in spinal muscular atrophy. Rather than correcting a defect, CP3 activates a cryptic exon in PRNP, the gene encoding prion protein, routing the resulting mRNA for degradation. The result is roughly seventy percent reduction in prion protein in cells. In working out the mechanism, the team discovered that the activity depends on an alternative splicing factor called Luc7L — a previously unknown requirement for this class of splice-switching molecule. When CP3 is co-administered with PTC258, a Luc7L activator, prion protein levels fall measurably in the brains of transgenic mice. The significance is that a clinical-grade splicing modulator framework already exists — risdiplam is in patients — and the mechanistic precedent for cooperative small-molecule splicing modulation is now established.

**Story two: Cardiolipin reveals a hidden regulatory surface on the top tuberculosis drug target.**

MmpL3 is the inner membrane transporter that flips trehalose monomycolate — a precursor of the mycolic acids that build mycobacteria's famously impermeable outer coat — from the inner membrane outward. It is essential for bacterial viability and is one of the most validated drug targets in tuberculosis biology, with multiple inhibitor programs binding inside its central channel. A new preprint from Shu-Sin Chng's group at the National University of Singapore reconstitutes MmpL3 in artificial lipid bilayers and finds that channel-binding inhibitors do not actually suppress the transporter's proton translocation activity in this system. What does suppress it completely is cardiolipin, a phospholipid present in the mycobacterial membrane. The team computationally predicted the cardiolipin-binding site on MmpL3 and built a variant with substitutions at that site. That variant is no longer modulated by cardiolipin in vitro — and it fails to support growth of Mycobacterium smegmatis, confirming that the cardiolipin-binding interaction is functionally required. The upshot: existing inhibitors are not occupying this surface, and the cardiolipin-binding site is an entirely new druggable address on a target that has already been the subject of intensive medicinal chemistry.

**Story three: Gram-positive bacteria built a protein-based membrane substitute to solve a respiration bottleneck.**

Cellular respiration depends on transferring electrons to hydrophobic quinone molecules in lipid bilayers, and the electron transport capacity available to a cell is physically limited by membrane surface area. Eukaryotes solved this by inventing mitochondria; Gram-negative bacteria evolved internal membrane invaginations. Gram-positive bacteria, which lack both, were assumed to have no comparable adaptation. New work from Rhys Grinter, Jamie Blaza, and colleagues across Monash, Edinburgh, and York reveals that Bacillus subtilis built a third solution. The NADH dehydrogenase Ndh and a quinone-transporting protein, Ncp, co-assemble with phospholipids into filamentous structures containing a continuous hydrophobic lumen that sequesters quinones and connects reaction chambers into an extended conduit. The architecture is confirmed by cryo-electron microscopy. Phylogenetic analysis shows this mechanism is widespread across Bacillota — the phylum encompassing Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Clostridium, and Bacillus. For drug discovery, the Ndh-Ncp complex is structurally unique to Gram-positive bacteria and essential for their respiratory capacity, with no human equivalent.

**Story four: A bioengineered kidney makes urine in mice after connecting to the host blood supply.**

Chronic kidney disease is among the most prevalent serious diseases globally, and the gap between patients needing transplants and available donor kidneys is measured in years of waiting. Ashwani Kumar Gupta, Jason Wertheim, and colleagues at Northwestern and the University of Arizona report progress toward transplantable kidney tissue. They seeded decellularized kidney scaffolds — donor kidneys stripped to their extracellular matrix and vascular skeleton — with two types of human pluripotent stem cell-derived progenitors: nephron progenitors and ureteric bud progenitors simultaneously. The combination drives more advanced nephrogenesis than either alone, producing interconnected nephron tubules and collecting ducts. When these reconstituted scaffolds were engrafted in mice, the graft vasculature integrated with host circulation — confirmed by mouse red blood cells entering the graft — and proteins secreted by the nephrons appeared in mouse urine. The paper also positions this platform for drug screening applications, where a vascularized, nephron-bearing tissue allows renal drug toxicity and efficacy to be assessed in a three-dimensional context.
