## Script

Good morning. Today's Scripps Biomedical Brief covers four bioRxiv preprints — a next-generation in vivo CAR-T platform that swaps the vesicular stomatitis envelope for a low-immunogenicity Piry virus glycoprotein and adds machine-learning-optimized T-cell-restricted expression, an engineered double-mutant IdeS protease that clears anti-AAV9 antibodies in pre-immunized rabbits at a submilligram dose, a cryo-EM structure of a type I-F3 CRISPR-associated transposase holo integration complex that resolves the licensing logic for RNA-guided large-cargo DNA insertion, and a first-in-class small molecule from a forty-million compound virtual screen that binds the ARF1-C9orf72:SMCR8:WDR41 interface implicated in ALS and frontotemporal dementia.

**Story one: An envelope-engineered in vivo CAR-T platform aims to make the modality safe enough to redose.**

Ruquaiya Alam, Tanveer Ahmad and colleagues built viroVbot by pairing a computational immunogenicity predictor with a broad envelope screen — twenty-two thousand five hundred and sixty-two glycoprotein sequences yielded six hundred and forty-one vesiculovirus homologs, and Piry virus glycoprotein emerged as the lead: fewer predicted MHC epitopes, lower human seroprevalence, and less T cell activation than VSV-G. They then knocked out native receptor binding and displayed CD3 or CD7 nanobodies on the envelope to target T cells, built producer cells that reject B cell transduction, and layered on machine-learning-optimized T-cell-specific promoters to restrict CAR expression to lymphocytes. In humanized xenografts, viroVbot3 generated potent BCMA and CD19 responses against multiple myeloma and Claudin-eighteen-point-two responses against gastric cancer, with sequential redosing enabled by rotating envelopes between administrations. The significance is that the current in vivo CAR-T conversation has been dominated by anti-drug immunity that caps you at a single dose — engineering the envelope for low immunogenicity from the start, combined with lineage-restricted promoters and producer-cell defenses against off-target transduction, is a coordinated attempt to make in vivo CAR-T behave like a redosable biologic rather than a one-shot procedure.

**Story two: A structure-guided double mutant makes IdeS potent enough to erase anti-AAV antibodies at a submilligram dose.**

Keyi Zhang, Xiaobing Wu and colleagues took the Streptococcus pyogenes IgG-cleaving protease IdeS and installed two mutations — lysine one-sixty-seven to arginine and aspartate two-twenty-six to glutamate — at the enzyme's Fc-binding interface. The double mutant, IdeSM33, gains a fourfold higher affinity for IgG by biolayer interferometry, cleaves serum IgG faster in vitro, and depletes circulating IgG in rabbits at doses as low as five micrograms per kilogram — forty times below wild-type. Critically, a single 0.2 milligram per kilogram dose degrades both binding and neutralizing antibodies against AAV9 within one to two days and restores hepatic AAV9 transduction in pre-immunized animals. The reason this matters is that pre-existing anti-AAV neutralizing antibodies exclude a large fraction of the eligible population from AAV gene therapies, and re-dosing after initial treatment is currently impossible for the same reason — an IgG-cleaving enzyme potent enough to work at gene-therapy-compatible submilligram exposures reopens both the treatment-naive-but-seropositive population and the redosing question, on top of the existing indications in autoantibody disease and transplant desensitization.

**Story three: A holo CRISPR-transposase structure resolves the licensing logic for RNA-guided large-cargo insertion.**

Shirin Fatma, Elizabeth Kellogg and colleagues used cryo-EM to capture the Vibrio cholerae type I-F3 CRISPR-associated transposase in its fully assembled holo integration complex, and the structure shows that catalytically competent DNA insertion is licensed only through a sequence of conformational changes as Cascade-TniQ, TnsC, and the TnsA-TnsB transposase pair come together on the on-target site. Comparing this holo complex to earlier partial assemblies across the CAST family reveals conserved architectural features, arguing for a common logic of stepwise licensing across type I-F3 systems. The significance for genome engineering is that CASTs offer something CRISPR-Cas9 fundamentally cannot — insertion of kilobase-scale cargo at a defined genomic site without double-strand breaks, without a homology-directed-repair-competent cell state, and without the mutation risk that comes with the cell's own repair machinery. What has limited them is off-target integration and low efficiency; understanding that the enzyme is essentially locked until every component is in place gives engineers a clean handle for rationally raising both fidelity and yield, which is exactly what a therapeutic large-cargo integration platform needs.

**Story four: A first-in-class small molecule binds the ARF1-C9orf72 interface implicated in ALS and frontotemporal dementia.**

Emily Dixon, Qun Lu and colleagues start with the clinical observation that phospho-ASAP1 at tyrosine seven-eighty-two — a marker of ARF1 hyperactivation — is elevated in the posterior frontal motor cortex of both sporadic ALS and C9orf72-mutant ALS, and that overexpressing C9orf72 fragments the Golgi apparatus similarly to the ARF inhibitor brefeldin A. That motivates them to target the pocket around SMCR8's catalytic arginine one-forty-seven at the ARF1-C9orf72:SMCR8:WDR41 interface. From a forty-million compound MCULE library, sequential virtual screening followed by MM/GBSA free-energy filtering and in-silico ADME/toxicity profiling narrowed the field to a lead, MCULE-5095997944, which binds purified ARF1 with a hundred-and-sixty-seven-nanomolar dissociation constant by surface plasmon resonance, reduces GTP-loaded ARF1 in cells, and rescues Golgi morphology. The conceptual contribution is a first-in-class chemical probe of an ARF1-CSW protein-protein interaction — a class of interface that has been essentially untouched by medicinal chemistry — that unifies sporadic and C9-mutant ALS under a common ARF-GAP-deficiency pharmacology, and provides a starting point for a drug-discovery campaign in a disease where the two approved therapies barely bend the survival curve.
