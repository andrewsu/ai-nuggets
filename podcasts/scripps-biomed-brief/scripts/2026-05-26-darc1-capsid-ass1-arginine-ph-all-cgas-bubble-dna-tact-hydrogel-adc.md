## Script

Welcome to the Scripps Biomedical Brief for Tuesday, May twenty-sixth. Four nuggets today: a cell-free protein capsid that delivers gene editors to dystrophic muscle, a metabolic Achilles heel in Philadelphia chromosome-positive leukemia, a new mechanism by which cGAS gets hyperactivated, and a hydrogel that finally makes subcutaneous antibody-drug conjugates safe.

First, Feng Zhang's group at the Broad has done something that should make every gene therapy program pay attention. They took the Drosophila Arc one capsid — an endogenous retroelement-derived protein nanoparticle that fly neurons use to ship RNA between cells — and they reconstituted it entirely from purified recombinant components and in vitro transcribed RNA. So this is a fully defined, cell-free assembled protein nanoparticle. By engineering the RNA-binding domain, they get efficient encapsulation of messenger RNA payloads and, importantly, Cas9 ribonucleoproteins. The capsids enter mammalian cells through a direct interaction with the surface receptor SORCS2, which is upregulated in regenerating and dystrophic tissue — so the tropism is biologically built-in. In the mdx mouse model of Duchenne, a single intramuscular injection of dArc1 capsids carrying a Cas9 gene editor produced up to eighteen percent exon skipping and restored dystrophin expression in muscle fibers. Compared with viral capsids, you avoid pre-existing immunity, the dosing is in principle redoseable, and because it's fully defined chemistry rather than producer-cell biology, manufacturing becomes a protein purification and RNA assembly problem, not a viral titer problem. This is a genuinely new third lane for in vivo macromolecular delivery, alongside lipid nanoparticles and AAV.

Second, a metabolic vulnerability in adult Philadelphia chromosome-positive acute lymphoblastic leukemia from Bela Patel's group at Barts Cancer Institute. L-asparaginase has been a cornerstone of pediatric leukemia treatment but is poorly tolerated in adults. The group asked whether an analogous arginine deprivation strategy could work, and they identified the Philadelphia chromosome-positive subgroup as a stereotypical arginine auxotroph — these cells have low expression of argininosuccinate synthase, so they cannot synthesize their own arginine and depend on extracellular supply. Pegargiminase, a pegylated arginine deiminase already in clinical development for other tumors, induced robust apoptosis in patient-derived xenografts, mechanistically through endoplasmic reticulum stress that is completely orthogonal to tyrosine kinase inhibition. So in non-genetic models of TKI resistance — which is the leading clinical problem in this subtype — pegargiminase plus a TKI eradicated the leukemia where TKI alone failed. This is exactly the kind of metabolic synthetic-lethal pairing that gives a chemotherapy-free regimen for a subset that desperately needs one.

Third, a cGAS chemical biology story from Jenny Ting's group at UNC Chapel Hill. The textbook view is that cGAS dimerizes on fully complementary double-stranded DNA and oligomerizes into phase-separated condensates. The Ting group looked at what happens when the DNA has unpaired bubble regions — the kind you actually get during transcription, replication, and recombination, including in stressed mitochondrial DNA. Bubble DNA hyperactivates cGAS, but it does so by an entirely different mechanism. Cryo-EM and single-molecule FRET show that two cGAS molecules clamp onto a single bubble-DNA, using the unpaired region as a hinge to bend the duplex into a V-shape. That two-to-one complex binds the catalytic domain much more tightly than fully paired DNA but suppresses condensation. So pattern diversity within the ligand reshapes the receptor's oligomeric state and tunes output. For anyone designing cGAS-STING modulators, this opens a structural mode of selectivity that the field has not been engaging.

Fourth, a delivery story from Alexandre Detappe's group in Strasbourg that solves a real clinical bottleneck. Every approved antibody-drug conjugate is given intravenously even though patients and oncologists overwhelmingly prefer subcutaneous administration, because the cytotoxic payloads are vesicants — release into subcutaneous tissue causes necrosis, and the hyaluronidase excipient used to disperse subcutaneous antibodies makes that worse. The group built TACT, a diacetyl-tartaric-anhydride-functionalized chitosan hydrogel that confines the ADC in a depot rather than dispersing it. It works with approved ADC formulations as-is — no drug product modification. Side-by-side with hyaluronidase co-formulation, hyaluronidase caused severe tissue necrosis and TACT prevented macroscopic injury while preserving antitumor efficacy comparable to intravenous dosing. In non-human primates, subcutaneous TACT delivered seventy-eight percent relative bioavailability for total trastuzumab and reduced peak circulating free payload exposure by nearly eight-fold. They validated across T-DM1, T-DXd, and enfortumab vedotin. This is a route-of-administration unlock for an entire drug class.
