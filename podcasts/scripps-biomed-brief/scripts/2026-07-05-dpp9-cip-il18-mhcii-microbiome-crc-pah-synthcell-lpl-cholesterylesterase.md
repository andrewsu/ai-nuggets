## Script

Welcome back to Scripps Biomedical Brief for Sunday, July 5th. Today, four bioRxiv preprints from the last 24 to 48 hours — the first genetic risk factor for checkpoint-inhibitor pneumonitis, a microbiome-driven tumor-cell antigen-presentation axis that resensitizes microsatellite-stable colon cancer to checkpoint blockade, a synthetic cell that couples metabolism to translation, and an unrecognized cholesteryl-ester hydrolase hiding inside lipoprotein lipase.

**Story one: D-P-P nine loss-of-function is the first genetic susceptibility gene for checkpoint-inhibitor pneumonitis, and I-L eighteen blockade is the mechanistically nominated intervention.**

Richard Flavell's group at Yale, with Toni Choueiri at Dana-Farber and Alexander Gusev at the Broad, ran a clinico-genomics study on roughly four thousand four hundred patients treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors and identified dipeptidyl peptidase nine — D-P-P nine — as the first susceptibility gene for checkpoint-inhibitor pneumonitis, which is the leading cause of immunotherapy-related mortality with a case-fatality rate approaching ten percent. Mechanistically, D-P-P nine suppresses the CARD-eight inflammasome and I-L eighteen secretion in human monocytes; plasma I-L eighteen was selectively elevated in patients before pneumonitis onset; and myeloid-restricted deletion of D-P-P eight and D-P-P nine in mice recapitulated the pulmonary hallmarks — granuloma formation, interferon-gamma T-cell accumulation, and monocyte-derived macrophage infiltration — all driven by excess I-L eighteen. The clinical read is direct: I-L eighteen blockade — a modality already in clinical development — is the mechanistically-rational intervention for a toxicity that is currently managed only with steroids and drug interruption. One of the cleanest human-genetics-to-druggable-pathway threads in immune-adverse-event biology in a long time.

**Story two: Microbial induction of tumor-intrinsic M-H-C class two overcomes checkpoint-inhibitor resistance in microsatellite-stable colorectal cancer.**

Semir Beyaz's group at Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory reports that a Helicobacter-containing microbiome suppresses primary tumor growth and limits metastasis in orthotopic mouse colorectal-cancer models — and the mechanism turns out to be tumor-cell-intrinsic. Microbial exposure induces M-H-C class two expression on the cancer cells themselves through the master regulator CIITA. Tumor-intrinsic CIITA knockout abrogates the microbial protection, and enforced CIITA expression is by itself sufficient to sensitize microsatellite-stable tumors to combined P-D one and C-T-L-A four blockade — the roughly ninety-percent-of-metastatic-colon-cancer patient population that currently gets almost no benefit from checkpoint inhibitors. Human microsatellite-stable patient-derived organoids replicated the phenotype: elevating cancer-cell M-H-C class two drove tumor-cell apoptosis on co-culture with autologous immune cells. The take is a microbiome-to-cancer-cell antigen-presentation axis as a resensitization strategy — not by modulating T-cell exhaustion or cold-tumor infiltration, but by turning the tumor cell itself into a competent antigen-presenting cell.

**Story three: A synthetic cell where metabolism gates translation, via a phenylalanine-hydroxylase module in a liposome compartment.**

Kate Adamala at Minnesota, with Vincent Noireaux and Aaron Engelhart, built a cell-free platform in which translation depends on tyrosine, and then imposed tyrosine scarcity as a tunable metabolic bottleneck. They alleviated the bottleneck by adding phenylalanine hydroxylase — the enzyme that converts phenylalanine to tyrosine and the one that fails in phenylketonuria — as a minimal biosynthetic module, so that phenylalanine input feeds tyrosine synthesis, which feeds translation. Encapsulated in liposome compartments, this produced three distinct synthetic-cell populations — inactive, translation-only, and metabolism-plus-translation — with the metabolically active population showing significantly higher protein-production fitness. Two ways to read this. As a synthetic-cell platform, it is a foundational step toward autonomous, self-regulating minimal cells that can be programmed to link biosynthetic modules to conditional protein expression. As an origins-of-life model, it is an experimentally tractable testbed for how the earliest cellular life may have bootstrapped internal metabolism from environmental scarcity.

**Story four: Lipoprotein lipase is a mammalian neutral cholesteryl-ester hydrolase.**

Siddhesh Kamat's group at I-I-S-E-R Pune took a systematic activity-based protein profiling and LC-mass-spec approach to identifying the neutral cholesteryl-ester hydrolases in mammals — a set of enzymes whose molecular identity has been controversial for decades. Tissue and macrophage profiling showed robust cholesteryl-ester hydrolase activity concentrated in membrane fractions and sensitive to broad-spectrum metabolic-serine-hydrolase inhibitors. Pharmacological screening excluded the leading candidates — N-C-E-H one and hormone-sensitive lipase — and instead pointed to lipoprotein lipase, which is tetrahydrolipstatin-sensitive, active on cholesteryl esters at neutral pH, and dependent on the L-M-F one maturation factor. Recombinant wild-type lipoprotein lipase, but not its catalytic serine one-fifty-nine alanine variant, efficiently hydrolyzed cholesteryl esters in vitro. This substantially expands lipoprotein lipase's functional footprint beyond triglyceride hydrolysis and reopens the medicinal-chemistry questions around lipoprotein-lipase pharmacology — including how the familial-chylomicronemia-syndrome lipoprotein-lipase-activator programs and any isoform-selective serine-hydrolase inhibitors should be read against this newly recognized cholesteryl-esterase activity.
