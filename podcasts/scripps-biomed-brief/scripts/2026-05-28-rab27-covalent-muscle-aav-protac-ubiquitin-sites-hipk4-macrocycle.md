# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-05-28

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: a first cell-active covalent toolbox for the long-undruggable RAB27 small GTPase, a muscle-tropic adeno-associated virus capsid that hits a ten-fold transduction edge in primates, a Calico study that rewrites how we think about degrader efficacy, and a macrocyclized bosutinib that finally lights up a dark kinome target.

First, from Ed Tate's group, now at Queen Mary, the first cell-active, rationally designed covalent inhibitors of the RAB27 small GTPase family. RAB27A and RAB27B regulate exocytic and endocytic vesicle trafficking, and they sit downstream of melanocyte biology, tumor metastasis, and chronic inflammation, but the protein-protein interaction surface they use to engage effectors is broad and shallow, and the GTP pocket is too conserved to drug selectively. The team ran an electrophile-first biochemical screen, hit a non-conserved cysteine flanking the effector interface, and then used X-ray crystallography to optimize a series of acrylamides that enantioselectively label that cysteine in live cells. Chemical proteomics confirmed on-target engagement, and the active probes recapitulated the RAB27 loss-of-function phenotype in melanocytes, while a matched inactive enantiomer did nothing. Just as importantly, they show that the legacy literature compound Nexinhib-twenty is non-selectively reactive and toxic, which means a generation of RAB27 papers built on that tool need a careful re-read. This is the kind of probe set that turns a long-time vesicle trafficking target into a real chemistry-led therapeutic hypothesis.

Second, a clean piece of capsid engineering from Qilu Pharmaceutical. Using a rational-design-plus-directed-evolution platform they call REACH, the team engineered a muscle-tropic adeno-associated virus capsid, called M1, and tested it head to head against AAV9 and MyoAAV in non-human primates. Systemically dosed, M1 delivered more than a ten-fold increase in skeletal muscle transduction over AAV9, and two to three-fold over MyoAAV, while cutting liver distribution by a hundred and eighty-three-fold. Off-target signal was also down sharply in dorsal root ganglia, by eleven-fold, in lung by twenty-seven-fold, and modestly in spleen and kidney. The dose-limiting toxicities that have plagued muscle-directed gene therapy are largely liver and dorsal root ganglion driven, so this is a primate-validated capsid that addresses two of the field's hard ceilings at once. Worth tracking as the muscular dystrophy programs reload around next-generation vectors.

Third, a tactical paper out of Calico from Kirill Bersuker's lab on how heterobifunctional degraders actually clear receptor tyrosine kinases at the plasma membrane. Using global ubiquitination profiling, they mapped the lysines on the EGFR and HER2 kinase domains that get ubiquitinated by VHL- and Cereblon-recruiting degraders, and found that the two ligase systems converge on a common subset of accessible lysines. Mutating those lysines away reduced degradation in proportion to how many sites you knocked out, and adding back non-native lysines elsewhere on the protein mostly failed to rescue clearance. The takeaway is that productive ubiquitination is geometrically constrained: degrader efficacy is not just a matter of forming the ternary complex, it depends on how many specific surface lysines the recruited E2 can actually reach. For target prioritization in degrader programs, that is a real design rule.

And fourth, a beautiful chemical biology vignette from Stefan Knapp and Thomas Hanke at Goethe University. HIPK4 is a dark kinome member with hints of biology in spermiogenesis and squamous cell carcinoma, but the field has lacked a clean probe. Starting from bosutinib, a broadly promiscuous kinase inhibitor, they used macrocyclization across the quinoline core to systematically prune the kinome selectivity profile. The result is AZ137, with an eleven-nanomolar biochemical IC-fifty, a seventy-six-nanomolar cellular EC-fifty, exceptional selectivity across three orthogonal kinase panels, high solubility, and no detectable cytotoxicity. Paired with a matched negative control, it gives the field a real chemical probe to test HIPK4 as a target rather than a knockout phenotype. A nice reminder that macrocyclization remains one of the most under-used selectivity levers in kinase chemistry.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about chemistry-led target validation and modality engineering. Until tomorrow.
