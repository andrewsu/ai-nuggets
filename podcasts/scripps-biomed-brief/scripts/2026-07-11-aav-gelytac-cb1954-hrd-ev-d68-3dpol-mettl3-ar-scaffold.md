## Script

Welcome back to Scripps Biomedical Brief for Saturday, July 11th. Four preprints from the last 24 to 48 hours — a gene-therapy-delivered extracellular protein degrader that maintains high-level IgG depletion for two months from a single dose, a decades-abandoned DNA-damaging prodrug repositioned as a BRCA-2-selective agent by a modern CRISPR screen, a crystallographic-fragment plus high-throughput-screen campaign against the enterovirus D-68 polymerase that surfaces two new ligandable pockets, and a paper that argues the catalytic activity of METTL-3 is the wrong hook for prostate cancer.

**Story one: Genetically encoded LYTACs — Carolyn Bertozzi's group encodes an extracellular protein degrader in an A-A-V vector and gets two months of eighty-five percent IgG depletion from a single dose.**

Yang, Loh, Sandoval Espinoza and colleagues in the Bertozzi lab at Stanford, with Karl Deisseroth, take the LYTAC concept — a bifunctional agent that redirects a soluble target to the lysosome for degradation — and rebuild it as a genetically encoded protein, then deliver it with A-A-V-8. The construct fuses murine insulin-like growth factor 2, which engages the lysosomal-trafficking cation-independent mannose-6-phosphate receptor, to an anti-IgG nanobody through a short glycine-serine linker. A single dose of the A-A-V yields sustained expression and, seventy-seven days later, roughly eighty-five to ninety percent depletion of endogenous IgG-1, IgG-2b, and IgG-2c as measured by mass spectrometry, with disease benefit in a passive-transfer rheumatoid arthritis model. Two engineering flourishes matter for the platform: a granzyme-B-cleavable variant restricts activity to sites of cytotoxic-lymphocyte activity, and a splice-switch cassette responsive to the small molecule branaplam gives on-demand small-molecule control over an otherwise-integrated therapeutic protein. Set against Biohaven's weekly-dosed GalNAc-peptide IgG degrader and Lycia's catalytic LYTACs, whose IgE rebounds by three weeks, this is a serious argument that the durability problem in extracellular targeted protein degradation can be solved genetically rather than pharmacologically.

**Story two: CB-1954, a nitrobenzamide-aziridine prodrug abandoned decades ago, is a BRCA-2-selective agent — a modern screen recovers a hidden precision-oncology drug.**

Elia, Hill and colleagues with senior authors Sundaram, Seth Herzon and Ranjit Bindra at Yale run a focused screen of DNA-damaging agents through isogenic homologous-recombination-proficient and -deficient cell pairs and find that CB-1954 selectively kills BRCA-2-deficient cells. The compound forms DNA interstrand cross-links regardless of H-R status, but the D-N-A-damage signaling, apoptosis, and clonogenic-survival loss localize specifically to H-R-deficient backgrounds. A targeted DNA-damage-response CRISPR screen pinpoints the Fanconi anemia and homologous-recombination pathways as the operative dependencies, not mismatch repair or nucleotide-excision repair. Chemistry-side, a bifurcated mechanism emerges: N-Q-O-2-dependent bioactivation drives the H-R-selective cytotoxicity, while the intrinsic aziridine reactivity accounts for the residual, non-selective activity — meaning selectivity can be dialed in medicinal-chemically. Xenografts of BRCA-2-deficient tumors show genotype-dependent antitumor activity. The lesson beyond this molecule is that the pharma back catalogue was built before biomarker-guided oncology existed and is worth re-screening against modern isogenic models.

**Story three: A crystallographic-fragment plus high-throughput screen on the enterovirus D-68 polymerase — two previously unknown pockets, and a five-aminoindazole starting point for the pediatric antiviral pipeline.**

Enterovirus D-68 causes acute flaccid myelitis in children and has no approved antiviral or vaccine. Biswas, Wang and colleagues in the lab of Francesc Xavier Ruiz at Rutgers, with Adam Renslo and Michelle Arkin at UCSF and Eddy Arnold at Rutgers, run two orthogonal campaigns against the viral 3-D polymerase. A crystallographic fragment screen of six hundred fifty fragments produces a roughly ten-percent hit rate distributed across the RNA-template channel, the active site, the RNA-primer channel, and — importantly — two previously undescribed ligandable regions the authors name Thumb Site II and the Index–Middle Finger Pocket, with inhibition confirmed for compounds binding each site. In parallel, a PicoGreen-based fluorescence H-T-S of fifty thousand ChemBridge compounds surfaces a five-aminoindazole scaffold, with lead compound seven-two-seven-five-nine-zero at an I-C-50 of twenty-five micromolar and defined structure-activity relationships. A dual-modality ligand map plus a screen-derived scaffold is the classic Scripps-style opening for a discovery program on a target that had none.

**Story four: METTL-3 is an androgen-receptor co-regulator, not a druggable methyltransferase — catalytic activity is dispensable for prostate cancer growth.**

Kostlan, Phoenix and colleagues with senior authors Sean Fanning and Steven Kregel at Loyola Chicago report that METTL-3, the catalytic subunit of the messenger-RNA m-6-A methyltransferase complex, sustains prostate-cancer growth through a non-enzymatic scaffold function. Genetic depletion of METTL-3 blocks proliferation of androgen-receptor-driven lines while sparing untransformed prostate epithelial cells; but pharmacologic METTL-3 catalytic inhibitors reduce global m-6-A on messenger RNA without affecting proliferation. A catalytically dead METTL-3 mutant rescues knockdown as well as wild-type does. Mechanistically, METTL-3 directly interacts with the androgen receptor, co-localizes with it on chromatin, and knockdown reduces A-R cistromic occupancy. The therapeutic read-through is clean: the correct modality for METTL-3 in this setting is a protein degrader or scaffold-disrupting glue, not the S-T-C-15-class catalytic inhibitors currently in the clinic.

That's the briefing for July 11th. Talk to you tomorrow.
