# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-06-04

## Script

Good morning. Today's brief: a repurposed antibiotic that selectively inhibits the JAK two V six one seven F driver of polycythemia vera, a virus-free chimeric antigen receptor T-cell manufacturing platform that solves the nuclear-import bottleneck for genome-integrating lipid-nanoparticle delivery, a Notch-based synthetic receptor that converts extracellular protease activity into programmable gene expression, and a single-stranded D-N-A strategy for intracellular production of functional R-N-As in mammalian cells.

First, from Anastasia Gumerova and colleagues with Huihui Li and Ronald Hoffman at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai, a discovery that the F-D-A-approved oxazolidinone antibiotic linezolid acts as a selective inhibitor of the JAK two V six one seven F mutation that drives roughly ninety-five percent of polycythemia vera. The clinically available J-A-K one and two inhibitors are mutant-allele agnostic — they suppress excess hematopoiesis but spare the mutant stem cell pool, and resistance ultimately emerges. In J-A-K-two-mutant human erythroleukemia cells, linezolid suppressed proliferation, reduced S-T-A-T five signaling, perturbed cell cycle progression, and increased apoptosis without affecting wild-type acute leukemia lines. Computational modeling localized the interaction specifically to the V six one seven F mutant kinase but not wild-type J-A-K two. In a mouse model of polycythemia vera that faithfully recapitulates the human disease, linezolid normalized spleen size and blood counts and selectively depleted the J-A-K-two-mutant hematopoietic stem and progenitor compartment. In ex vivo colony assays on peripheral-blood mononuclear cells from polycythemia vera patients, mutant colony numbers fell while wild-type colonies were preserved, with the most primitive progenitors as the preferred targets. The myeloproliferative neoplasm field has been waiting for a disease-modifying agent — a stem-cell-depleting, mutant-selective small molecule on a known safety profile is the closest thing the space has had to one, and linezolid becomes the de facto lead structure for next-generation selective J-A-K-two V six one seven F inhibitors.

Second, from Kazuya Inoue, Mariko Masui and colleagues with Nao Suzuki-Yamazaki at FUJIFILM Corporation, a manufacturing-platform result that attacks the dominant bottleneck in virus-free C-A-R T-cell engineering — getting donor D-N-A into the nucleus efficiently. Viral vectors deliver, but at cost, safety, and scalability constraints that have made non-viral C-A-R T-cell production a strategic priority across the field; lipid nanoparticles deliver messenger R-N-A well but typically stall in the cytoplasm with the D-N-A payload required for stable transgene integration. The team fused a nuclear localization signal directly to either a transposase or a genome-editing nuclease, used the fusion protein to actively shuttle donor D-N-A from cytoplasm to nucleus while still bound to its cargo, and packaged the system in proprietary on-demand-encapsulation ready-to-use lipid nanoparticles. The nuclear-localization-signal-fused transposase route delivered markedly higher chimeric-antigen-receptor transgene integration in primary human T cells than electroporation, with higher receptor expression and better cell viability. For site-specific integration at the T-cell receptor alpha constant locus, end-joining-based repair was substantially more efficient than homology-directed repair following lipid-nanoparticle delivery. The resulting engineered T cells exhibited potent antigen-specific cytotoxic activity. The result establishes ready-to-use lipid nanoparticles plus active nuclear shuttling as a viable virus-free C-A-R T-cell manufacturing platform — a clean architectural fix for the rate-limiting step of the modality.

Third, from James Tran, Christopher Kuffner and colleagues in John Ngo's group at Boston University, a new class of synthetic receptor. ProNotch tethers a destabilized mutant of the Notch one negative regulatory region — the autoinhibitory module that normally gates Notch activation — to inhibitory single-chain antibody fragments against that same region, joined by protease-cleavable linkers. Engagement by the antibody fragment simultaneously rescues surface trafficking of the destabilized receptor and suppresses basal signaling, until protease cleavage of the linker releases the antibody fragment and unmasks ligand-independent receptor activation that drives a user-defined transcriptional output. Linker substitution reprograms protease specificity across diverse enzymes; tandem substrate repeats raise sensitivity without raising background; single-chain designs implement A-N-D and O-R logic gates that integrate multiple protease inputs into a single output. ProNotch detected endogenous matrix-metalloproteinase fourteen activity from cancer cell lines both in cis and in trans, drove protease-dependent cell-state transitions in fibroblasts, and the antibody-fragment plus negative-regulatory-region module also worked as a soluble protease-activated pro-antibody that conditionally inhibited Delta-like-four-driven Notch signaling and ligand-independent activation of mutant Notch one in a T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia line. A new input modality for engineered cell therapies and conditional biologics in solid tumors, fibrosis, and inflammation — disease microenvironments where extracellular protease activity is the most reliable spatial marker.

Fourth, from Ahmed Mahas, Roberta Perrotta and colleagues in George Church's group at Harvard Medical School, a route to programmable intracellular R-N-A production that replaces cloning, complex R-N-A synthesis, and viral-vector delivery with directly synthesizable single-stranded D-N-A. The trick is geometric: embed an orthogonal bacteriophage R-N-A polymerase promoter inside a short hairpin so the recognition site is locally double-stranded on an otherwise single-stranded template, and you recover robust polymerase engagement. The platform produces functional C-R-I-S-P-R guides that drive adenine base editing at reporter and endogenous loci, and C-R-I-S-P-R-based transcriptional activation across multiple human cell lines. Compact, synthetically accessible inputs for intracellular R-N-A production with obvious utility across synthetic biology and genome engineering programs.

That's the brief. Four reasons today to be optimistic about a mutant-selective small molecule for myeloproliferative neoplasms, virus-free genome-integrating C-A-R T-cell manufacturing, protease-responsive synthetic receptors as a new cell-engineering input modality, and short single-stranded D-N-A as a programmable intracellular R-N-A generator. Until tomorrow.
