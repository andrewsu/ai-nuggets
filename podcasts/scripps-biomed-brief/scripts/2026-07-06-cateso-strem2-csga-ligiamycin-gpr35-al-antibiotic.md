## Script

Welcome back to Scripps Biomedical Brief for Monday, July 6th. Today, four bioRxiv preprints from the last 24 to 48 hours — a differentiable route to enzyme catalytic-turnover optimization, a microglial amyloid inhibitor caught neutralizing bacterial curli, a natural-product deorphanization pipeline that nominates an orphan G-protein-coupled receptor, and an active-learning strategy that turned a phenotypic antibiotic screen into a fifty-three-fold-enriched hit list.

**Story one: CatESO — differentiable enzyme sequence design driven by substrate-aware K-cat, not just structure.**

John Zhang's group at Shenzhen University of Advanced Technology introduce CatESO, which pushes differentiable protein design past the structure- and binding-centric objectives that have dominated the field and points it at what enzymes actually do — catalytic turnover. The core move is backpropagating through a cross-modal K-cat predictor under continuous sequence relaxation, so K-cat becomes the design objective rather than a post-hoc filter, and ESM-2 plus ESMFold sit in the loop as evolutionary and structural guardrails. Across seven out-of-distribution enzymes spanning enzyme commission classes one through seven, CatESO raised the model-predicted K-cat for the vast majority of designs with a median fold change around one-point-five, and every variant kept a p-L-D-D-T above seventy — that is, the sequences stayed foldable. Head-to-head against the R-F-diffusion-three plus Ligand-M-P-N-N pipeline and against ZymCtrl, CatESO struck a better balance between predicted activity and structural confidence. For biocatalysis and greener process chemistry, the significance is that the field now has a gradient-based recipe for co-optimizing catalytic activity, evolutionary plausibility, and foldability in a single end-to-end loop — the missing objective for programs that need turnover, not just binding.

**Story two: Soluble TREM2 is a cross-kingdom amyloid inhibitor — the microglial fragment sub-stoichiometrically shuts down bacterial curli assembly.**

Anthony Balistreri and Mark Gomulinski with Matt Chapman at Michigan and Jeff Kelly at Scripps show that soluble TREM2 — the shed extracellular domain of the microglial receptor previously demonstrated to inhibit amyloid-beta — also potently and sub-stoichiometrically inhibits CsgA amyloidogenesis, CsgA being the major protein component of curli, the functional amyloid that scaffolds biofilms in uropathogenic E. coli and many other proteobacteria. Kinetic modeling nails down where in the aggregation reaction the inhibition acts — soluble TREM2 slows both primary and secondary nucleation of CsgA fibers rather than modifying elongation, which is the same mechanistic hallmark reported for its action against pathological human amyloids. Added exogenously to bacterial growth medium, soluble TREM2 suppressed curli-dependent pellicle biofilm formation without affecting bacterial growth — biofilm-selective, not antibacterial. The take is that a small club of human proteins is now known to inhibit bacterial functional amyloidogenesis, and TREM2-expressing gut macrophages, which are already documented to bind curli and mount cytokine responses to it, appear to have a chemically defined molecular tool for that job. It reframes soluble TREM2 as a broad amyloid inhibitor operating across the human-microbe interface, and it strengthens the case for cross-kingdom amyloid biology as a therapeutic axis, given how much attention curli-like bacterial amyloids have been getting as candidate triggers for peripheral synucleinopathy and gut-brain neurodegeneration.

**Story three: A control-validated deep-learning pipeline nominates the orphan GPCR GPR35 as the target of the bacterial natural product ligiamycin A.**

James Martin at Rowan-Virtua School of Osteopathic Medicine builds and stress-tests an open natural-product target-hypothesis-generation pipeline that combines a pan-proteome deep-learning drug-target-interaction model — a graph-neural-network ligand encoder, an ESM-2 protein language-model encoder, and bidirectional cross-attention — with bias-corrected ranking and control-anchored molecular docking. The methodological contribution is not the neural architecture but the bias-correction step: run the model on a drug with a known target, in this case losartan, and use the results to identify and correct the frequent-hitter bias in raw drug-target-interaction predictions before ranking novel candidates. Applied to ligiamycin A, a decalin-amino-maleimide isolated in 2022 from a Streptomyces-Achromobacter co-culture with no reported target, the corrected predictions cluster cleanly on class-A G-protein-coupled receptors and put the orphan receptor GPR35 out in front. Docking with matched positive and negative controls corroborates the hypothesis specifically — ligiamycin A scores about negative eight-point-one kilocalories per mole at the GPR35 agonist pocket, essentially tied with the known GPR35 agonist zaprinast at negative eight-point-three, against a non-binder floor around negative five-point-five, while F-F-A-R-one is excluded and histamine H-two is inconclusive. The result is still a computational hypothesis awaiting wet-lab validation, but the more important shipment is a reusable, control-validated recipe for deorphanizing bioactive natural products at pan-proteome scale — which is exactly the bottleneck that has stranded most microbial secondary metabolites without a target.

**Story four: An active-learning strategy on a whole-cell antibiotic screen delivered a five-fold hit-rate boost and fifty-three-fold prospective enrichment.**

Lia Serrano and colleagues with James Sacchettini at Texas A and M, Bree Aldridge at Tufts, Linden Hu at Tufts, and Maha Farhat at Harvard Medical School systematically evaluated three active-learning strategies for whole-cell bacterial bioactivity prediction on a Mycobacterium tuberculosis high-throughput-screen retrospective, and identified an optimal strategy that balances compound novelty — exploration — against overall hit rate — exploitation. They then closed the loop live against Borrelia burgdorferi, and the active-learning-guided screen raised the experimental hit rate five-fold, from about two-tenths of a percent within investigator-selected plates to one percent. The stronger number is what happened when the trained model was deployed prospectively for in silico selection of chemically diverse compounds across multiple bacterial species — a fifty-three-fold enrichment over investigator-directed screening, and every predicted hit that experimentally validated showed the intended narrow-spectrum activity against Borrelia. For the drug-discovery leadership question, that is a concrete quantification of how much a properly calibrated active-learning loop can extend a whole-cell screen out of distribution without paying for more experimental cycles — and it fits inside the campaign infrastructure that antibiotic HTS groups already run.
