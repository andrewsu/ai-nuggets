## Script

Welcome to the Scripps Biomedical Brief for Thursday, June twenty-fifth. Four bioRxiv preprints today — all abstract-grounded, bodies not yet rendered. A new lysosomal degradation modality that uncouples Mcl1 killing from cardiotoxicity. Small-molecule inhibitors of the tau-uptake receptor LRP1, from a team that includes Scripps Research screening faculty. A rationally designed biased inverse agonist that separates platelet-mediated inflammation from hemostasis. And atomically precise inorganic nanoclusters that mimic the MICAL enzyme family to modulate actin without disrupting filament structure.

First, from Ahmed Elshazly, Janakiram Vangala and colleagues with Senthil Radhakrishnan at Virginia Commonwealth University, tumor-selective Mcl1 degradation by AUTAC. Mcl1 is one of the primary drivers of resistance to venetoclax and carfilzomib in multiple myeloma, and direct Mcl1 inhibition has been a clinical graveyard because the same protein is required for cardiomyocyte survival — the on-target cardiotoxicity has been dose-limiting and lethal. The V-C-U group's approach is an AUTAC, an autophagy-targeting chimera, which routes cargo to lysosomal degradation rather than the proteasome. They show robust Mcl1 degradation and cytotoxicity in multiple myeloma models with minimal toxicity in cardiac cell lines, primary cardiomyocytes, and mouse heart tissue. In vivo, AUTAC reduced tumor Mcl1 without measurably affecting cardiac Mcl1. The mechanistic explanation is not engineered binding selectivity — it is that cardiac cells express substantially less of the p62/SQSTM1, TRAF6, and UBC13 autophagy delivery machinery required for AUTAC activity, and accumulate less compound intracellularly. The lysosomal delivery chain is simply less available in heart tissue than in myeloma. The clinical translation angle is direct: AUTAC enhanced the activity of carfilzomib and venetoclax in resistant models without worsening cardiotoxicity. The broader point is that lysosomal degradation modalities can access a therapeutic window from cellular context that neither inhibitors nor proteasome-targeting degraders can exploit.

Second, from Caiqin Wang, Chen-Ting Ma and colleagues with Jennifer Rauch at the University of Massachusetts Amherst — and with Eduard Sergienko, Steven Olson, and Michael Jackson at Scripps Research — small-molecule inhibitors of the tau–LRP1 interaction identified by high-throughput screening. Cell-to-cell spreading of tau aggregates is central to Alzheimer's disease progression, and this spreading is mediated by the endocytic receptor LRP1, specifically its ligand-binding domain four. The team engineered and purified L-R-P-1 B-D-4 as a scalable biochemical reagent and built three orthogonal assays — fluorescence polarization, split luciferase complementation, and time-resolved FRET — each of which measured consistent nanomolar binding affinities and competitive displacement by tau, R-A-P, and a known peptide ligand. An H-T-S campaign identified candidate inhibitors, selected compounds of which reduced tau uptake in cells, phenocopying competitive displacement. This establishes LRP1-BD4 as a biochemically tractable, druggable interface for blocking tau spread — a mechanism that acts upstream of the receiving neuron rather than targeting aggregation itself.

Third, from Simon Pitchford, K. Miraz Rahman and colleagues at King's College London, a pathway-selective platelet P-2-Y-1 receptor inverse agonist that suppresses inflammation while preserving hemostasis. Platelets signal through two distinct G-protein branches: the Gq–PLC axis drives aggregation and hemostasis, while the G-alpha-12/13–Rho-GTPase axis drives inflammatory functions including chemotaxis. Current antiplatelet drugs hit both, which is why they increase bleeding. The King's College team used molecular docking to map residues in the P-2-Y-1 binding pocket that distinguish the two pathways, synthesized a series evolving from nucleotide analogues to a non-nucleotide lead compound, KSN-159-27, and demonstrated pathway-selective inverse agonism — G-alpha-12/13 signaling suppressed, Gq activation preserved. In vivo, KSN-159-27 reduced inflammatory cell recruitment while leaving bleeding time and thromboembolic responses intact. This is the first rationally designed biased inverse agonist at platelet P-2-Y-1, and the separation of signaling pharmacology it demonstrates is directly applicable to inflammatory conditions where platelet chemotaxis is the primary pathology.

Fourth, from Yanan Wang, Jessica Ma and colleagues with Nicholas Kotov at the University of Michigan, MICAL-inspired chiral decavanadate nanoclusters that modulate actin dynamics without disrupting filament architecture. MICAL enzymes oxidize specific methionine residues in actin to control turnover, but they are large, require N-A-D-P-H as cofactor, and are difficult to deliver intracellularly. Kotov's group shows that redox-active decavanadate nanoclusters with tartaric acid — atomically precise inorganic clusters — oxidize the same three methionine residues in globular actin, including Met-176, whose modification blocks the backdoor segment and prevents depolymerization. Unlike conventional actin-targeting drugs that scramble filament structure, cryo-E-M of nanocluster-treated actin shows no structural disturbances. The clusters are membrane-permeable, biocompatible, slow actin dynamics in live cells, and their docking into actin is computationally predictable. Using inorganic nanocluster chemistry to replicate enzyme active-site redox logic opens a pharmacologically orthogonal handle on actin dynamics.
