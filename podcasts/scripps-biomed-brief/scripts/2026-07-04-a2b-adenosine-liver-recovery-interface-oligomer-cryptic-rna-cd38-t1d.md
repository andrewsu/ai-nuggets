## Script

Welcome back to Scripps Biomedical Brief for Saturday, July 4th. Today, four bioRxiv preprints from the last 48 hours — a late-window therapeutic target for acetaminophen liver failure, an AI-native framework for designing responsive protein oligomers, a foundational result on the druggability of cryptic pockets in RNA, and a precision-immunotherapy angle on type 1 diabetes.

**Story one: Delayed activation of the A2B adenosine receptor accelerates liver recovery after an acetaminophen overdose.**

Giselle Sanchez-Guerrero, David Umbaugh and colleagues with Hartmut Jaeschke and Anup Ramachandran at the University of Kansas Medical Center make a therapeutic argument for delayed activation of the adenosine A2B receptor in acetaminophen overdose. Acetaminophen remains the leading cause of drug-induced acute liver failure in the United States, and N-acetylcysteine works only if patients arrive early — the exact window most overdose patients miss. In fasted mice given a 300-milligram-per-kilogram acetaminophen dose, activating the A2B receptor six or nine hours later, after injury is already established, accelerated liver recovery: Kupffer cells repopulated faster, macrophages migrated to necrotic areas and resolved them, and hepatocytes lit up cell-proliferation and lipid-metabolism programs, with lipid-droplet genes like Cidec and Plin2 upregulated to fuel regeneration. The shift is strategic — the mechanism is not blocking the initial toxic insult, it is reprogramming the innate-immune and metabolic response after the fact — and it opens a late-window therapeutic slot in an indication where the standard of care has essentially none.

**Story two: An interface-seeded generative framework for de novo design of responsive protein oligomers.**

Ho Yeung Chim, Mubarak Idris and colleagues with Bruno Correia at EPFL and Alena Khmelinskaia at LMU Munich and TU Dresden present a generative approach that starts from an isolated interaction module and grows a homo-oligomer around it. Prior de novo oligomer design has largely produced fixed assemblies; the twist here is designs that assemble responsively. Experimentally validated designs are accurate and explore topologies with no natural counterpart. Two functional demonstrations make the point. Chemical-trigger designs oligomerize conditionally when their small-molecule ligand is present. Phosphorylation-driven designs oligomerize reversibly under a kinase-phosphatase switch. The team then bolts these responsive assemblies to real biological outputs — ligand-dependent membrane binding, and phosphorylation-controlled gene-regulatory switches. The significance is not another protein-design demonstration; it is a route to designer protein circuits whose stoichiometry is set by an external signal, which is exactly what is missing for programmable sensors, conditional biotherapeutic assembly, and layered synthetic-biology regulatory logic.

**Story three: Cryptic RNA binding sites are energetically accessible and chemically addressable.**

Lukasz Olenginski and Robert Batey at CU Boulder go after a foundational question for RNA-targeted drug discovery — whether the transient cryptic pockets that expand druggability in proteins are also accessible in RNA. Using an env8 cobalamin riboswitch as a model, they show that beta-axial substituted cobalamin ligands bind by displacing a conserved adenosine, A20, from the RNA core, exposing a previously hidden site that the beta-substituent then occupies. Selective abasic substitutions plus isothermal titration calorimetry and fluorescence measurements quantify the energetic penalty of that base displacement at about 1.4 kilocalories per mole — a modest cost that keeps the cryptic conformer thermally accessible. Computational conformational sampling then finds analogous low-energy cryptic sites in structurally unrelated HIV-1 and HCV RNAs, and a ligand originally raised against the riboswitch cryptic site docks against the newly exposed surfaces of both. For anyone building an RNA-targeted small-molecule program, the pitch is that RNA is more druggable than its native structures suggest — cryptic base-displacement pockets are a systematically searchable class of binding surface, and both the energetic ceiling and the computational recipe for finding them are now in hand.

**Story four: CD38 selectively marks the pathogenic T-cell population in type 1 diabetes, and depleting it spares regulatory T cells.**

Sanjay Pathak, Ronia Ahmed and colleagues with Everett Meyer, Paul Utz, Mark Davis and Justin Annes at Stanford identify CD38 as a marker of the autoreactive T-cell population driving type 1 diabetes. Existing T-cell-depleting therapies delay progression of stage 2 and stage 3 disease but hit regulatory T cells as collateral damage — the wrong direction for a tolerance-restoring regimen. In non-obese diabetic mice and in recently diagnosed patients, CD38-positive T cells are the culprits: adoptive transfer of CD38-positive but not CD38-negative cells into humanized diabetic-mouse models drove disease. Depleting CD38-positive cells with an anti-CD38 monoclonal antibody prevented insulitis and diabetes onset while sparing CD4-positive CD25-positive regulatory T cells, without damaging islet function — with a bonus signal that senescent, immunogenic beta cells were also cleared. Since anti-CD38 antibodies are already clinically established in multiple myeloma, this is a fast-moving repositioning candidate as a transient tolerance-restoring intervention in early T1D — precision immunotherapy with an off-the-shelf agent.
