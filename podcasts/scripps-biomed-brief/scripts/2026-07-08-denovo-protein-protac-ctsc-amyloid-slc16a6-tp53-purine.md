## Script

Welcome back to Scripps Biomedical Brief for Wednesday, July 8th. Today, four preprints from the last 24 to 48 hours — de novo–designed protein PROTACs that get around the linker and E-3-ligase-scarcity problems that limit small-molecule degraders, a decades-old lysosomal damage reagent whose mechanism turns out to be amyloid fibril growth inside the lysosome, a long-sought orphan tyrosine transporter that gates melanosome biogenesis, and a nuclear adenosine dependency that opens a synthetic-lethal window in T-P-53–null tumors.

**Story one: De novo–designed protein PROTACs — computational protein design tackles both the linker geometry and the E-3-ligase-scarcity problems that limit small-molecule targeted protein degradation.**

Bram Mylemans, Boyana Korona and colleagues in Dek Woolfson's lab at Bristol, with Laura Itzhaki at Cambridge and Danny Huang at the CRUK Beatson, use computational protein design to build a genuinely new modality for targeted protein degradation. Small-molecule PROTACs work by co-recruiting a protein of interest and an E-3 ubiquitin ligase, but most clinical programs still lean on just two ligases — cereblon and von Hippel-Lindau — and degradation efficiency depends fussily on linker length, orientation, and the lysine geometry around the target. The authors sidestep both bottlenecks: they engineer a small, highly stable helix-turn-helix scaffold, graft in a hot-spot–driven binding site for B-C-L-x-L on one face, and on another face place a short linear motif that recruits the E-3 ligase K-L-H-L-20. The resulting bifunctional protein binds both partners in vitro and, more importantly, degrades B-C-L-x-L in cancer cells and drives apoptosis. Because the geometry of the two binding sites is dialed in by the designed scaffold rather than by an empirical linker, and because short linear motifs can in principle access a much wider slice of the human E-3 repertoire, this is a proof of concept that computational protein design can open ternary-complex modalities that were structurally out of reach for small molecules.

**Story two: A widely used lysosomal damage reagent turns out to be an amyloid-forming prodrug — Cathepsin C ligates dipeptides into cross-β-sheet fibrils inside the lysosome, and it is active fibril growth, not deposition, that ruptures the membrane.**

Ryan Elias and colleagues in Jeff Kelly's lab at Scripps, with Ben Cravatt's group, take L-leucyl-L-leucyl methyl ester — a compound many labs, including neurodegeneration groups, have used for decades to reversibly damage lysosomal membranes — and finally nail its mechanism. Cathepsin C, a lysosomal dipeptidase, uses a subtiligase-like thioester intermediate to ligate the dipeptide methyl ester into leucyl-leucyl oligopeptides that exceed a critical concentration and self-assemble into cross-β-sheet amyloid fibrils inside the lysosome. Cryo-electron tomography of affinity-isolated lysosomes shows lumenal fibrils in physical contact with broken membranes; membranolytic activity is coupled specifically to active fibril growth, and remnant fibrils after washout are essentially inert. Swapping in phenylalanine to give aromatic dipeptide variants retunes both the aggregate structure, the ESCRT lysosomal-repair response, and — critically — the fibril's ability to cross-seed cytosolic Tau P-301-S at the lysosomal membrane. Two implications: numerous prior lysosomal-damage experiments in the neurodegeneration literature need to be reinterpreted through the lens of a hidden intralysosomal amyloid load, and the Cathepsin C dipeptide ligation platform now becomes a modular chemical-biology tool for generating defined amyloid structures inside living cells — a rare experimental handle on the endolysosomal-breakout step of prion-like tau spreading.

**Story three: The orphan transporter S-L-C-16-A-6 is the missing melanosomal tyrosine transporter — a S-O-X-10–M-I-T-F–driven gene is required to load the melanosome with substrate and to build the organelle at all.**

Corey Cunningham, Alex Bott, and Charles Adelmann with the Rutter and Sabatini labs at H-H-M-I Utah identify S-L-C-16-A-6 as the long-sought tyrosine transporter that supplies melanocyte pigment factories. Tyrosine is the mandatory substrate for melanin synthesis and has to cross the melanosomal membrane, but despite decades of biochemistry the responsible transporter had stayed anonymous. Genetic screens place S-L-C-16-A-6 downstream of the S-O-X-10 to M-I-T-F master regulatory axis that runs the entire melanosomal program. Redirecting the transporter to the plasma membrane using a trafficking-disrupting point mutation lets the authors prove tyrosine transport directly and demonstrate competitive inhibition by other bulky amino acids. Depleting S-L-C-16-A-6 does not merely reduce pigment — it collapses melanosome biogenesis and depletes most melanosomal proteins, arguing that substrate loading and organelle identity are causally coupled. For a group looking at pigmentation disorders, melanoma metabolism, or the broader problem of orphan S-L-C transporter deorphanization, this is a clean example of pairing directed genetic screens with a trafficking-mutant handle to force a reluctant intracellular transporter into a measurable regime.

**Story four: T-P-53 loss creates a nuclear adenosine dependency — perturbing de novo purine biosynthesis selectively impairs P-53–null tumors in vivo and unmasks histone methylation as an intrinsic, fast nuclear stress response.**

Julia Urgel-Solas and colleagues with Sara Sdelci and Ana Janic at C-R-G Barcelona, together with Matt Vander Heiden at M-I-T, ask what metabolic dependency is unmasked when T-P-53 is lost. They identify a requirement for de novo purine biosynthesis, with a pronounced dependence on adenosine-related metabolism, in T-P-53–deficient cells; perturbing purine synthesis raises DNA damage and specifically drops nuclear ATP. The chromatin response is what surprises: histone methylation rises within minutes of perturbing purine synthesis and of acute DNA damage — fast enough that the authors argue methylation is an intrinsic, early feature of the nuclear stress response rather than downstream epigenetic remodeling, and blocking methylation itself reduces nuclear ATP and impairs DNA damage resolution. Genetic knockout of the purine-pathway enzyme P-F-A-S selectively impairs the growth of T-P-53–deficient tumors in vivo, establishing that the vulnerability is druggable rather than a cell-culture artifact. The framing worth pausing on: the nuclear compartment is treated as a metabolic entity with its own ATP pool coupled to chromatin state, and T-P-53 loss — the single most common oncogenic event — cracks open a dependency in a pathway that already has clinical inhibitors on the shelf.
