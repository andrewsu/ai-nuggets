# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-05-23

## Episode

A small-molecule strategy that selectively kills NF1-deficient tumor cells through PKC-delta-induced pyroptosis, with in vivo efficacy in neurofibroma and malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor models; a crystallography and single-molecule imaging study showing that the PD-1 checkpoint inhibits T cells through a non-catalytic SHP2 scaffolding mechanism that dismantles TCR and CD28 signaling condensates rather than through phosphatase activity; a clinical lung surfactant added to lipid nanoparticles giving an order-of-magnitude increase in particle yield and enhanced pulmonary mRNA expression in mice; and a modular mRNA platform that encodes antigen alongside PD-L1 and TGF-beta to drive antigen-specific regulatory T cell responses in autoimmunity and food allergy models.

## Script

Good morning. Today's briefing covers four moves spanning a new oncology modality, a refined checkpoint mechanism, an mRNA delivery upgrade, and an antigen-specific tolerance platform: a PKC-delta agonist that selectively kills NF1-deficient tumors through pyroptosis, a non-catalytic scaffolding mechanism for the PD-1 checkpoint that opens a different drug-target shape, a lung-surfactant-augmented lipid nanoparticle for inhaled mRNA, and a tolerogenic mRNA platform that establishes antigen-specific regulatory T cells in autoimmune and allergy models.

First, Nancy Ratner's group at Cincinnati Children's Hospital Medical Center, with Yi Zheng and collaborators, reports that activating protein kinase C delta selectively induces a lytic, immunogenic form of cell death in tumors that have lost the NF1 tumor suppressor. The mechanism is mechanistically clean. PKC delta phosphorylates KRAS at a site that traps the small GTPase in an inactive state, and the resulting collapse of RAS signaling in the NF1-null context triggers caspase activation and gasdermin-E cleavage — the molecular trigger for pyroptosis. NF1-intact cells, which retain NF1's brake on the same RAS pathway, do not undergo pyroptosis under the same PKC delta agonist exposure, giving the strategy a structural selectivity window. The in vivo arm of the paper shows that PKC delta agonism suppresses growth of both NF1-deficient plexiform neurofibroma and malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumor models — two indications where surgical resection remains the only meaningful intervention and MEK inhibitors do not durably control disease. This adds a clean synthetic-lethal-style modality to NF1 oncology, and the use of pyroptosis rather than apoptosis as the executor means the cell death generates an immunogenic signal that could engage adaptive antitumor immunity against residual disease.

Second, Panyu Fei and Wei Chen at Zhejiang University, with Michael Dustin at Oxford, used crystallography and single-molecule imaging to resolve how the PD-1 inhibitory checkpoint actually shuts down T-cell activation — and the answer is not what the textbook has been saying. The conventional model attributes PD-1's inhibitory function to the catalytic phosphatase activity of recruited SHP2 dephosphorylating downstream kinases. The Zhejiang and Oxford work shows that phosphorylated PD-1 accelerates SHP2's transition into a distinct activated conformation by at least ninety-four-fold, and that the activated SHP2's structural domains — independent of phosphatase catalysis — directly dismantle the phase-separated signaling condensates organized by phospho-TCR and phospho-CD28. The catalytic activity is not the load-bearing inhibitory function; the scaffolding function is. This reframes next-generation PD-1 modulators. Compounds that lock SHP2 in its non-activated conformation, or that prevent the condensate-dismantling protein-protein interaction without engaging the phosphatase pocket, become a distinct drug-target shape — and the SHP2 allosteric inhibitors developed for tumor-cell-intrinsic RAS pathway use may need to be reread in the immune-checkpoint context with this scaffolding mechanism in mind.

Third, Gregg Duncan's group at the University of Maryland reports that incorporating the clinical lung surfactant Poractant alfa into ionizable lipid nanoparticles materially improves pulmonary mRNA delivery. The surfactant-augmented formulation produces an order of magnitude higher particle concentration during assembly, exhibits substantially improved pH sensitivity across the endosomal range, and consequently delivers faster cytosolic release of encapsulated mRNA. In transfection assays the modified nanoparticles produced higher reporter protein expression with no detectable cell toxicity, and in mice the formulation drove enhanced mCherry expression in the lungs after pulmonary administration relative to the unmodified comparator. The translational rationale is direct. Inhaled mRNA programs — for cystic fibrosis, alpha-one antitrypsin deficiency, respiratory infections, and pulmonary tumors — have been bottlenecked by delivery efficiency in the airway environment, and the use of a clinical-grade surfactant already approved for neonatal respiratory distress syndrome as a formulation excipient is a low-friction path into IND-enabling work.

Finally, Tomoyoshi Yamano's group at the Nano Life Science Institute at Kanazawa University reports Tol-mRNA, a modular mRNA platform engineered for antigen-specific immune tolerance rather than immunization. Each Tol-mRNA construct encodes a target antigen together with two regulatory cues — the immune checkpoint ligand PD-L1 and the immunosuppressive cytokine TGF-beta — to drive antigen-specific induction of regulatory T cells while suppressing effector responses to that same antigen. In autoimmune disease models, Tol-mRNA reduced inflammation and clinical disease scores; in food allergy models, it prevented allergic symptoms and dampened type-two inflammation; and in human peripheral blood mononuclear cell experiments, the platform generated antigen-specific regulatory T cells and suppressed effector activation in vitro. The point is the modularity. The same construct architecture can be swapped for any disease-relevant antigen — autoimmune self-antigens, allergen epitopes, transplant donor antigens — making the platform a true antigen-agnostic tolerance modality in the same way LNP-mRNA vaccines became an antigen-agnostic immunization modality.

That is the brief for today. Four moves — selective pyroptosis as a synthetic-lethal-style modality for NF1-deficient cancers, a non-catalytic SHP2 scaffolding mechanism rewriting the PD-1 inhibitory checkpoint, a lung-surfactant-augmented LNP for inhaled mRNA, and a tolerogenic mRNA platform for antigen-specific regulatory T cell induction in autoimmunity and allergy. Have a productive day.
