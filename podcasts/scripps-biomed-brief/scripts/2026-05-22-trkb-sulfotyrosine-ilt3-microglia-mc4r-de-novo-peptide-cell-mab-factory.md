# Scripps Biomedical Brief — 2026-05-22

## Episode

A sulfated tyrosine modification on human TrkB explains why a selective small-protein agonist activates BDNF signaling while leaving the related receptors TrkA and TrkC alone; a high-throughput thermal-shift screen against the inhibitory immune receptor ILT3 yields a sub-micromolar small molecule that restores microglial amyloid clearance in human iPSC-derived microglia; an AlphaFold-based hallucination pipeline produces a de novo cyclic peptide agonist for the melanocortin-4 receptor with a non-canonical activation motif that suppresses acute food intake in mice; and an encapsulated cell platform produces multiple monoclonal antibodies from a single subcutaneous implant at stable titers for over six months in non-human primates.

## Script

Good morning. Today's briefing covers four moves across target biology and modality: a sulfotyrosine modification that confers TrkB-selective activation, a small-molecule modulator of an inhibitory immune receptor on Alzheimer's microglia, an AlphaFold-designed cyclic peptide agonist for the obesity receptor MC4R, and a cell-based factory for sustained antibody delivery validated through six months in non-human primates.

First, Neil McDonald's group at the Francis Crick Institute, together with Yves-Alain Barde and the team behind the TrkB agonist ZEB85, has solved the structural basis for selective activation of human TrkB by both BDNF and ZEB85. The headline finding is a sulfated tyrosine at residue four hundred in the TrkB extracellular domain that is indispensable for ZEB85 activation but is not required for BDNF binding, which instead extends into the extracellular juxtamembrane segment of the receptor. AlphaFold modeling and biophysical analysis together explain how BDNF can displace ZEB85 from TrkB through an overlapping but distinct epitope. Critically, this sulfotyrosine is not present in the related receptors TrkA or TrkC, which is the molecular handle behind ZEB85's TrkB selectivity. Because exogenous BDNF cannot be drugged into the brain and also activates a tumor necrosis factor pathway through the p75 neurotrophin receptor, a selective protein agonist of TrkB with a clean structural rationale is an attractive lever for neuroprotection programs, and the sulfotyrosine motif itself is now a defined target site for new TrkB-specific ligands.

Second, Moustafa Gabr's group at Weill Cornell ran a high-throughput cellular thermal shift screen of roughly forty thousand compounds against ILT3, the inhibitory immune receptor also known as LILRB4 that restrains microglial amyloid clearance in Alzheimer's disease. They pulled out a sub-micromolar binder, IB15C, validated direct engagement by microscale thermophoresis, surface plasmon resonance, docking, and site-directed mutagenesis, and showed in human induced pluripotent stem cell-derived microglia that the compound disrupts the ILT3 to apolipoprotein E interaction, reduces SHP1 and SHP2 phosphatase signaling, suppresses cytokine secretion, and enhances amyloid uptake. The pharmacokinetic and safety profile is favorable. This opens a small-molecule path into the inhibitory checkpoint side of the microglia program, complementary to the TREM2 agonist antibodies already in clinical development, and reinforces the idea that disinhibition rather than direct activation may be the more drug-tractable lever on microglial function in Alzheimer's.

Third, Mads Nygaard's group at Gubra in Copenhagen reports an end-to-end de novo peptide discovery pipeline, taken from AlphaFold-based structure hallucination through in vivo proof of concept, for the melanocortin-4 receptor, the validated obesity target most prominently engaged by setmelanotide. Using the ColabDesign hallucination protocol they generated over five thousand linear and head-to-tail cyclic candidate peptides aimed at the receptor's orthosteric pocket. About three-quarters of linear and a quarter of cyclic candidates showed measurable activity, and the lead cyclic agonist achieved a three hundred forty nanomolar potency despite lacking the canonical melanocortin activation motif entirely. Deep mutational scanning, a half-life extender conjugation scan, and a combinatorial maturation library together identified an alternative activation motif centered on an alanine-proline-tryptophan-arginine segment, and a single proline substitution at position five produced the variant E5P with a 6.7 nanomolar potency against the human receptor. Central administration of ten nanomoles of E5P reduced acute food intake in mice. The takeaway is that de novo computational design now reaches receptor-pocket-targeted peptide leads through to in vivo proof of concept on a single project, and crucially the optimized peptide uses an activation pharmacophore that natural melanocortins do not.

Finally, Omid Veiseh's group at Rice University, with Mick Diehl and collaborators, reports a retrievable, immunomodulatory cell encapsulation device that operates as a sustained monoclonal antibody factory from a single subcutaneous implant. A chemically modified alginate formulation, selected by immunocompetent screening in mice, sustains stable serum titers of the HIV-neutralizing antibody 3BNC117 for a full year, and single-cell RNA sequencing of the implant niche shows that the formulation establishes a local anti-inflammatory pro-resolving immune environment that attenuates the foreign body fibrotic response. The same allogeneic cell chassis is shown to produce thirteen different therapeutic antibodies including ipilimumab, pembrolizumab, adalimumab, and the broadly neutralizing PGT121, and the retrievable macrodevice format allows on-demand termination and re-implantation for dose tuning. In non-human primates, subcutaneous implants maintained stable ipilimumab titers for more than six months with no detectable toxicity, no anti-drug antibodies, and dose-proportional exposure across a three-dose escalation. This is a credible platform for replacing repeated antibody infusion with single-administration therapy across oncology, autoimmunity, and infectious disease.

That is the brief for today. Four moves across target biology, neuroimmunology, computational design, and biologics delivery — a sulfotyrosine motif that explains TrkB selectivity, a small-molecule ILT3 modulator that restores microglial amyloid clearance, an AlphaFold-designed cyclic MC4R peptide that suppresses food intake in mice, and a cell encapsulation factory for sustained monoclonal antibody delivery validated in non-human primates. Have a productive day.
