Welcome back to Scripps Biomedical Brief for Wednesday, July fifteenth. **Story one: Rifamycin B, the discarded precursor of rifampicin, retains activity against rifampicin-resistant R-N-A polymerase — and structure explains why.**

Mosaei, Shin, Kozhevnikov, and colleagues from Nikolay Zenkin at Newcastle with Katsuhiko Murakami at Penn State revisit rifamycin B, the earliest member of the rifamycin family and a precursor of the clinically used derivatives that has been sidelined for decades on grounds of chemical instability and weak antibacterial activity. Under contemporary assay conditions rifamycin B is stable enough to characterize, and it retains inhibitory activity against R-N-A polymerase variants carrying the clinically relevant rifampicin-resistance substitutions. The mechanism comes from two structures — the first crystal structure of rifamycin B on its own, and a co-structure of rifamycin B bound to bacterial R-N-A polymerase. The distinctive C-four O-carboxymethyl substituent that gives rifamycin B its identity forms an intramolecular contact in the free molecule and re-partners as a salt bridge with fork loop two of the beta subunit upon binding. That single extra interaction, absent from rifampicin, tolerates the resistance mutations, and the C-four position — which the rifamycin medicinal chemistry program long ago walked past — is now identified as an underexplored handle for rational modification. This reframes rifamycin B as a mechanistically distinct scaffold and points to a chemistry-driven route into R-N-A polymerase in resistant tuberculosis and Gram-positives.

**Story two: A PROTAC against the SAGA acetyltransferase collapses A-M-L identity by dissolving the ENL condensate that holds oncogene transcription in place.**

Anagha Deshpande, Chiang, Perales Garcia and colleagues in Aniruddha Deshpande's lab at Sanford Burnham Prebys with Kristiina Vuori and collaborators run genome-scale dependency screens across more than a thousand cancer cell lines and land on the SAGA histone acetyltransferase complex as a selective chromatin dependency in acute myeloid leukemia and other hematologic cancers. They then take a published cereblon-recruiting PROTAC — G-S-K-nine-eighty-three, paired with the closely related G-S-K-six-ninety-nine — that degrades the SAGA catalytic subunits K-A-T-two-A and K-A-T-two-B, and show broad-spectrum antileukemic activity across genetically diverse A-M-L cell lines, primary patient samples, and an isogenic K-M-T-two-A-rearranged model with cooperating oncogenic mutations. The mechanism is where this becomes a chemical biology story. K-A-T-two-A-B degradation causes a genome-wide drop in histone-H-three-K-nine acetylation, with the largest asymmetric losses concentrated at the core A-M-L oncogene loci — MYC, MYB, the HOX-A cluster. That erasure displaces the chromatin reader E-N-L from those loci, and — critically — dissolves the E-N-L-anchored phase-separated transcriptional condensates that hold the Super Elongation Complex together. On top of that, K-A-T-two-A-B directly acetylates E-N-L, A-F-F-one, and A-F-F-three, meaning the same enzyme is licensing both the histone landscape and the non-histone elongation machinery. This is a chromatin-to-condensate axis with a molecule attached, and it argues that the right way to attack A-M-L transcriptional dependency is degrader, not inhibitor.

**Story three: Cancer persister cells co-opt neuronal N-M-D-A receptors to survive ferroptosis after topoisomerase-based therapy.**

Punzi, Villanti, Gatti and colleagues in Giovanni Tonon's group at San Raffaele in Milan look at drug-tolerant persister cells left behind after standard chemotherapy and ask which programmed cell death pathway is most deregulated. Across widely used regimens the answer is ferroptosis — most notably, platinum-treated persisters become highly ferroptosis-sensitive, so pairing platinum with a ferroptosis inducer clears persister colorectal cancer cells. Topoisomerase-inhibitor persisters go the other direction — they raise intracellular iron and become ferroptosis-resistant. The mechanism is unexpected. Topoisomerase inhibitors turn on the X-c-minus antiporter axis through S-L-C-seven-A-eleven and C-D-forty-four, which pulls in cystine to load G-P-X-four and dumps glutamate outside the cell. That extracellular glutamate then binds N-M-D-A receptors on the same cancer cells — receptors these cells are not supposed to be using — driving calcium entry, an AKT to N-R-F-two signal, and a cytoprotective antioxidant program. N-R-F-two also pushes the E-R away from mitochondria and drops mitochondrial R-O-S. Blocking the X-c-minus antiporter and the N-M-D-A axis together re-sensitizes the persisters to ferroptosis. There is an existing pharmacology of N-M-D-A blockers in the neurology cabinet — memantine, ketamine, ifenprodil — and this paper is the mechanistic argument for repurposing them into oncology combinations.

**Story four: In pancreatic cancer, the plastic basal cell state maintains the tumor by secreting G-M-C-S-F to lock in immunosuppression.**

Singhal, Ryan, Rose and colleagues in Tuomas Tammela's group at Memorial Sloan Kettering use lineage tracing and genetic ablation in autochthonous pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma models to interrogate the two dominant cancer cell states — classical and basal. The plasticity is asymmetric. Basal cells are highly plastic and can regenerate classical cells; classical cells are stuck. Ablating basal cells triggers rapid, durable tumor collapse — the immunosuppressive cancer-associated fibroblast compartment goes away, tumor-associated macrophages repolarize toward pro-inflammatory, and cytotoxic T cells finish the job. Ablating classical cells does nothing comparable. The team then peels back what basal cells secrete to keep the immunosuppressive circuit in place, and lands on a single cytokine — G-M-C-S-F — as sufficient to explain most of the effect. Basal-specific G-M-C-S-F knockout recapitulates macrophage repolarization, lymphocyte recruitment, and tumor shrinkage. The frame is that a specific cell state — not a mutation, not a fibroblast subtype — is the driver of pancreatic immunosuppression, and G-M-C-S-F is a defined therapeutic node into a tumor type that has resisted essentially every immunotherapy tried against it.
