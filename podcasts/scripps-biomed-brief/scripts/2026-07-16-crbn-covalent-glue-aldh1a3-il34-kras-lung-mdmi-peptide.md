## Script

Welcome back to Scripps Biomedical Brief for Thursday, July sixteenth. Four bioRxiv preprints from the last twenty-four to forty-eight hours — a Dana-Farber cryo-E-M structure that reframes molecular glue chemistry as covalent remodeling of cereblon itself, a covalent inhibitor of an aldehyde dehydrogenase cancer stem cell target caught in two mutually exclusive conformations inside a single active site, a resident-macrophage axis in K-RAS-mutant lung cancer that connects two clinically actionable cytokines to tumor growth and ferroptosis resistance, and a structure-guided peptide design pipeline that squeezes remote functional sequence space out of tiny training sets.

**Story one: Covalent remodeling of cereblon creates a non-canonical neosubstrate interface with N-T-A-Q-one — molecular glue chemistry moves beyond the G-loop.**

De la Peña, Cruite, Che, Matyskiela, Chamberlain, Fischer, and Jones — Eric Fischer at Dana-Farber with Lyn Jones — solve the cryo-E-M structure of a ternary complex in which cereblon and its ubiquitin ligase D-D-B-one recruit the neosubstrate N-T-A-Q-one, driven not by a conventional imide but by the covalent molecular glue E-M-twelve-F-S. The interesting part is that E-M-twelve-F-S does not bind cereblon reversibly the way I-mids do. It imidazylates histidine three-fifty-three on cereblon itself, repositioning the sensor loop, eliminating a steric clash that had blocked N-T-A-Q-one engagement by conventional reversible cereblon binders. The engineered interface is stabilized by a distinctive T-shaped carbon-hydrogen to π interaction between the sulfated histidine and phenylalanine one-twenty-six on N-T-A-Q-one. Biochemistry and mutations map the ternary complex determinants. The reframe is that induced-proximity degrader pharmacology does not have to accept cereblon as it comes off the shelf. Site-specific synthetic modification of the E3 ligase itself can be used to expand neosubstrate specificity beyond the G-loop degron, and covalent engineering of the ligase is now a legitimate handle for designing new degrader modalities. For a program that has been rate-limited by which proteins present a G-loop-compatible degron, this is a new axis.

**Story two: A covalent, irreversible inhibitor of A-L-D-H-one-A-three binds in two mutually exclusive conformations inside the same active site — one seat in the substrate pocket, one in the cofactor pocket.**

Covaleda, Vizarraga, Upadhyay, Zhu, Abegg, Pequerul, Hugo, Adibekian, Fita, Parés, Avilés, Bogyo, and Farrés target aldehyde dehydrogenase one-A-three, an isoform overexpressed in a range of tumors and long identified with cancer stem cell traits and therapy resistance. They exploit the enzyme's secondary esterase activity to build an in vitro assay, then screen a thirty-two-hundred-compound cysteine-focused covalent fragment library against the highly conserved catalytic cysteine that sits at the intersection of the substrate and cofactor channels. The winner, called Z-thirty-four, is an acrylamide fragment with sub-micromolar potency against both A-L-D-H-one-A-one and A-L-D-H-one-A-three, and biophysics confirms it as a time-dependent, covalent, irreversible binder to the active-site cysteine. The cryo-E-M structure at two-point-two-six angstroms is where this becomes unusual. The same fragment, tethered by the same cysteine covalent bond, occupies two mutually exclusive conformations — in one, it fills the substrate-binding channel; in the other, it fills the cofactor-binding channel. Two productive vectors are available for elaboration off a single warhead, which is a rare gift in covalent medicinal chemistry — the fragment can be grown in either direction to buy selectivity against related isoforms or to reach beyond the immediate active site.

**Story three: A resident alveolar macrophage axis — I-L-thirty-four out of the tumor and I-G-F-one back from the macrophage — fuels K-RAS mutant lung cancer and protects it from ferroptosis.**

Zak, Chen, Wang, Ozark, Mognol, Park, and colleagues in Judith Varner's group at U-C-S-D show that K-RAS G-twelve-D lung epithelial tumors do not just tolerate the tissue-resident, embryonically derived alveolar macrophage compartment — they expand it, using tumor-cell-secreted I-L-thirty-four to drive alveolar macrophage proliferation. That is a signal borrowed from late embryonic lung development, recapitulated inside a K-RAS-mutant tumor. The macrophages return the favor by secreting I-G-F-one, which drives tumor cell proliferation and protects tumor cells from ferroptosis. Neutralizing or genetically deleting I-L-thirty-four in the mouse model collapses the loop: I-G-F-one goes down, macrophage and tumor proliferation drop, tumor progression stops. Human data lines up — high I-L-thirty-four together with high I-G-F-one correlates with poor survival in K-RAS-G-twelve-D and G-twelve-V lung adenocarcinomas and in other solid tumors. This is a therapeutic axis with two independently druggable clinical arms — I-L-thirty-four blockade cutting off the macrophage recruit, or I-G-F-one blockade cutting off the mitogenic and ferroptosis-protective return signal — into a genotype that has been very hard to attack head-on.

**Story four: A structure-guided peptide design pipeline finds remote functional sequence space from tiny training sets.**

Bayat, Perkins, Clancy, Patel, and colleagues in Keith Pardee's group build M-D-M-I — Minimal Data, Maximal Insight — a two-stage pipeline for peptide-protein interface design that leans on structure rather than sequence. Instead of feeding a machine learning model with peptide sequences alone, they extract three-dimensional interface geometry and binding energetics from predicted peptide-protein complexes and pair that structure-aware predictor with a genetic algorithm for sequence exploration. On an all-negative benchmark panel the sequence-only baseline hallucinates binders seventy percent of the time; M-D-M-I collapses that false-positive rate to near zero and produces roughly four times more high-confidence in silico binders than state-of-the-art baselines. The functional test is the split-G-F-P system, where fluorescence is a direct readout of peptide-protein complementation. In stage one, M-D-M-I finds active peptides up to thirty-eight percent diverged from wild-type; in stage two, motif-guided recombination pushes divergence past fifty percent while retaining activity, revealing two distinct functional clusters in sequence space. A top candidate expressed as a full-length G-F-P fusion retains a G-F-P-like emission profile, meaning the redesigned peptide is refolding into a fluorescent scaffold. The takeaway is that structure-informed pipelines can reach genuinely distant functional sequence space from small annotated datasets — which is exactly the regime therapeutic peptide analog discovery lives in.

That's the briefing for July sixteenth. Talk to you tomorrow.
