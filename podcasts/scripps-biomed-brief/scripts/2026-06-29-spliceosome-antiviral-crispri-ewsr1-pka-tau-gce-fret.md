## Script

Good morning. Today's Scripps Biomedical Brief covers four stories from the past 48 hours: a spliceosome inhibitor strategy that selectively suppresses replication of nuclear-replicating viruses by exploiting their dependence on host RNA processing, a non-viral epigenome-editing platform that silences a hard-to-drug fusion oncogene in patient-derived xenografts, a proteomics dissection of PKA regulatory subunit roles that links a specific isoform to Tau hyperphosphorylation in Alzheimer's disease, and a genetic code expansion approach that installs a switchable fluorescence sensor directly inside a protein to monitor conformational dynamics and enable drug screening.

**Story one: Host spliceosome dependence as a shared antiviral vulnerability across nuclear-replicating viruses.**

Many viruses that replicate in the nucleus — adenovirus, herpes simplex virus, and influenza A — co-opt the host cell's RNA processing machinery to produce their own transcripts. A team led by Alexander Price at the University of Pennsylvania now shows that pharmacological inhibition of the host spliceosome preferentially disrupts viral transcript production while leaving host gene expression largely intact. Low-dose treatment with mechanistically distinct SF3B1 inhibitors — compounds already in clinical development for cancer — or genetic depletion of SF3B1 reduced adenoviral DNA replication, suppressed late protein accumulation, and produced multi-log drops in infectious particle output. The same inhibitors blocked splicing and replication of herpes simplex virus and influenza A, but had no effect on cytoplasmic RNA viruses that do not depend on host splicing. The significance is that viral dependence on spliceosome complexity represents a shared vulnerability across three structurally unrelated pathogen families, and exploiting it with compounds already proven safe in patients is a short path to a new antiviral class.

**Story two: Non-viral CRISPRi silences an undruggable fusion oncogene in patient-derived tumors.**

Oncogenic transcription factors and fusion proteins drive many aggressive cancers but are widely considered undruggable — they lack the deep pockets required for conventional small molecules. Pilar Blancafort and colleagues at the University of Western Australia deliver a proof of concept for a programmable alternative: CRISPR-based epigenetic silencing. They delivered dCas9 fused to the transcriptional repressor KRAB as a ribonucleoprotein complex packaged in guanidino-functionalized PAMAM dendrimers — a non-viral, polymeric vehicle — to target EWSR1-FLI1, the chimeric fusion transcription factor that drives roughly 85 percent of Ewing sarcoma cases. The approach achieved robust EWSR1-FLI1 repression and potent antitumor activity in established cell line xenografts and in patient-derived xenografts. This is the first demonstration of in vivo delivery of a CRISPRi ribonucleoprotein through a non-viral vehicle, and the guide RNA is swappable, making the platform directly extensible to any hard-to-drug oncogenic driver.

**Story three: PKA regulatory subunit identity controls Tau phosphorylation — a new axis for Alzheimer's drug discovery.**

Protein kinase A is regulated by four subunits that fall into two types — type I and type II — but their distinct and sometimes opposing roles have been poorly understood. Susan Taylor's lab at UC San Diego now provides the most complete picture to date by combining proteomic and phosphoproteomic profiling of cells in which each type has been selectively knocked out. Type I and type II loss have opposite consequences: type I knockout increases both the abundance and kinase activity of the PKA catalytic Cα isoform, broadly elevating substrate phosphorylation; type II knockout does the opposite, reducing the Cβ isoform's activity and phosphorylation output. The most consequential differentially phosphorylated site maps to Tau — the microtubule-associated protein whose hyperphosphorylation is a hallmark of Alzheimer's disease. Loss of type I subunits increases Tau phosphorylation through two mechanisms: elevated Cα activity, and a newly discovered effect in which Tau binds more tightly to the flexible linker region of type II subunits, coupling the two regulatory pools. The work establishes PKA subunit identity as a regulatory axis for Tau in neurodegeneration, with implications for how kinase-targeting strategies in Alzheimer's should account for isoform specificity.

**Story four: A genetically encoded switchable sensor monitors protein dynamics and enables HTS.**

Measuring how a protein changes conformation during a biological event — or in response to a drug candidate — typically requires external labeling, which can perturb the system and adds experimental steps. Ryan Mehl, Rahul Kohli, and James Petersson report a genetic code expansion strategy that incorporates a fluorophore-quencher pair directly into the protein backbone using two non-canonical amino acids: acridonylalanine as the fluorophore and methyltetrazinyl phenylalanine as the quencher. No post-translational labeling is needed. Quenching can be toggled off by a bioorthogonal reaction targeting the methyltetrazine, providing a built-in internal control. The team applied the system to calmodulin, RecA, and LexA — including the LexA repressor whose cleavage by RecA activates the bacterial SOS response and drives acquired antibiotic resistance. In all cases, the dual-labeled proteins report conformational changes with molecular precision and support high-throughput drug screening. For programs targeting protein dynamics — allosteric sites, disordered regions, protein-protein interfaces — the approach provides a compact, genetically encoded readout that is very difficult to replicate with external labels.
