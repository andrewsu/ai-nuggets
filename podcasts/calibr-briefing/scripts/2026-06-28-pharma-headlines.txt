Good morning, Travis. Sunday June twenty-eighth, twenty twenty-six. The Sunday side of the weekend wrap. The Friday news flow was deeper than yesterday's six items captured, so today picks up four U-S regulatory and clinical stories from late Friday that the Saturday script left on the table, plus the ASCO Breakthrough Singapore close. Five items.

First — and this is the spotlight in a few hours — Replimune announced Friday morning that the F-D-A has accepted for review the third resubmission of the Biologics License Application for R-P-one in combination with nivolumab for advanced melanoma in patients who have progressed on anti-P-D-one therapy. The agency has classified it as a complete Class-one response with a P-D-U-F-A goal date of August second. Critically, the F-D-A has also scheduled an advisory committee meeting in late July — that's about a month from now and roughly a week before the action date. R-P-one is vusolimogene oderparepvec, an intratumoral oncolytic herpes-simplex-virus-one engineered to express G-M-C-S-F and a fusogenic glycoprotein from gibbon ape leukemia virus to drive cancer-cell fusion and local immunogenicity. The first B-L-A in late twenty-twenty-four pulled an initial Complete Response Letter on July twenty-second, twenty twenty-five. The second resubmission pulled a second C-R-L on April tenth this year — the agency was specific that the IGNYTE Phase-one-two single-arm data set, with an objective response rate of thirty-three-point-six percent and median duration of response up to thirty-three-point-seven months, was not sufficient on its own to support accelerated approval, and that the confirmatory Phase three IGNYTE-three, then only about ten percent enrolled, was too immature to fill that gap. Third try, third filing accepted, adcom set. The spotlight goes deep on why third time differs from the first two, how the oncolytic-virus class has fared since Imlygic's twenty-fifteen approval, and what an accelerated-approval-on-single-arm-evidence decision means for the broader IO-combo BD landscape Foresite has exposure to.

Second, Capricor Therapeutics announced Friday morning that the F-D-A's Cellular, Tissue, and Gene Therapies Advisory Committee will convene on July twenty-ninth to review the deramiocel Biologics License Application for Duchenne muscular dystrophy cardiomyopathy, with a new P-D-U-F-A target action date of August twenty-second. Deramiocel — also known as CAP-one-thousand-two — is an allogeneic cardiosphere-derived cell therapy that secretes immunomodulatory and anti-fibrotic exosomes targeting macrophages in cardiac and skeletal muscle. The backstory is unflattering: this is the second look at a B-L-A that the F-D-A rejected with a Complete Response Letter in July twenty twenty-five. What got the agency back to the table was the pivotal Phase three HOPE-three readout — randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled in one-hundred-six patients — that hit its primary endpoint on the Performance of Upper Limb scale, hit the key secondary cardiac endpoint with a significant reduction in late gadolinium enhancement corresponding to about a three-segment treatment difference at twelve months, and showed about a fifty-four percent slowing of skeletal muscle disease progression. Two muscular-dystrophy regulatory catalysts now stack across the rest of the summer — Epicrispr's E-P-I-three-twenty-one Wadham Music readout from yesterday plus this Capricor adcom — so the M-D treatment landscape is set up for back-to-back inflection points across CRISPRi-style epigenetic silencing on the F-S-H-D side and cardiosphere-derived cell therapy on the D-M-D side, with very different modality risk profiles but a common patient-advocacy and rare-disease regulatory dynamic.

Third, Biogen took the next operational step on the five-point-six-billion-dollar Apellis acquisition that closed in mid-May. According to reporting Friday morning, Biogen has paused or terminated investment in most of the legacy Apellis research portfolio, including the previously-disclosed pegcetacoplan studies in focal segmental glomerulosclerosis and in patients at high risk of delayed graft function after kidney transplant, both of which Biogen now lists as suspended. A small number of associated roles are being cut. The retained asset is the launched complement-pathway franchise — Empaveli and Syfovre — plus the U-S nephrology-and-rare-disease commercial infrastructure that Biogen is repurposing to support felzartamab, the C-D-thirty-eight antibody now in three Phase-three kidney-disease trials with the first readout expected in the first half of twenty twenty-seven. The pattern matters for two reasons. One, this is now the third large-cap acquisition in twelve months to follow the same script — buy the commercial franchise, gut the research engine, redeploy the rep network to the acquirer's own pipeline asset — and that compresses what acquired-biotech researchers can expect in terms of program longevity and what their venture backers should now model into M-and-A discount rates. Two, the Empaveli-Syfovre franchise is now the operative anchor for Biogen's nephrology repositioning, which means the next twelve months of Biogen's nephrology-franchise dynamics are tied to felzartamab readout timing more than to anything inherited from Apellis. For Foresite portfolio companies sitting in or adjacent to nephrology or in early-stage complement biology, that's a constraint on which Biogen door now opens to a partnering conversation.

Fourth, ASCO Breakthrough Singapore concluded Saturday with the Yuk Ming Dennis Lo closing keynote on multi-omics and noninvasive cancer diagnostics. The dominant story of the three-day meeting was the institutional advance of China and broader Asia-Pacific oncology innovation. The cleanest data point was Innovent's long-term follow-up of I-B-I-three-sixty-three — Takeda's T-A-K-nine-twenty-eight — a P-D-one-by-I-L-two-alpha-bias bispecific fusion protein in P-D-one-resistant advanced non-small-cell lung cancer. In the three-milligrams-per-kilogram every-three-weeks cohort, more than forty percent of patients with squamous or adenocarcinoma histology survived beyond twenty-four months — extraordinary survival in a population whose median historical OS post-checkpoint failure is well under twelve months. A separate first-line non-small-cell lung cancer cohort using a three-to-one-point-five dose-step protocol showed an objective response rate of eighty-six-point-four percent in twenty-two patients. The mechanism reads as a deliberate engineering correction to the failed I-L-two-plus-checkpoint combinations of the last decade — bias toward I-L-two-receptor-alpha to lower toxicity while retaining productive T-cell expansion. Takeda licensed the asset in October twenty twenty-five with U-S co-commercialization and ex-U-S-ex-China exclusive rights, and these data are what underpinned that deal. For Foresite the read is that Asian-originated I-O bispecifics are now a category that Big Pharma is buying ahead of registrational readouts at deal economics that compress what U-S developers in the same target space can extract.

Fifth and shortest. iBio dosed the first participant in the Phase one trial of I-B-I-O-six-hundred earlier this month — a long-acting monoclonal antibody targeting myostatin and G-D-F-eleven, the two negative regulators of skeletal muscle, designed for once-monthly or less-frequent dosing as a muscle-preserving adjunct to G-L-P-one weight-loss therapies. Thirty-two adults with overweight and obesity, single-ascending-dose randomized placebo-controlled design, completion expected in the second half of twenty twenty-seven. The data point is not the trial size — it's the platform timeline. iBio took an AI-designed antibody from program initiation to first patient dosed in approximately two years, which is faster than the typical biologic discovery cadence by a meaningful margin. The competitive set in muscle-preserving GLP-one adjuncts is now Regeneron's bimagrumab-style activin-receptor antibody, Lilly's separate myostatin-pathway program, Eli Lilly's bimagrumab-plus-tirzepatide combination data, and Scholar Rock's apitegromab — and iBio is now in clinic with the AI-platform-discovered entry into that race. For Travis the AI-in-drug-discovery thread continues to be the integration of generative discovery platforms not just into adjacency programs but into asset classes that have established Big Pharma competitors — and the iBio readout, when it comes, will be one of the cleaner head-to-head comparisons of an AI-platform-designed biologic against a Big-Pharma-discovered one.
