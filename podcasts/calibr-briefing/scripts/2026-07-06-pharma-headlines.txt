Five items.

First, Novartis buys Myricx Bio. This morning Novartis announced agreement to acquire UK biotech Myricx Bio for up to one-point-five billion dollars — one-point-one billion up front in cash plus up to four hundred million in milestone payments — closing expected in the second half of twenty twenty-six. Myricx is a twenty-nineteen spin-out from Imperial College London and the Francis Crick Institute, based in London, that had raised roughly one hundred fourteen million dollars in a Series A in July twenty twenty-four co-led by Novo Holdings and Abingworth, with Brandon Capital, Sofinnova Partners, British Patient Capital, Cancer Research Horizons, and Eli Lilly Ventures participating. The platform is N-M-T-i-A-D-Cs — antibody-drug conjugates using N-myristoyltransferase inhibitors as the cytotoxic payload, a payload class that has never previously been advanced to the clinic. Two lead assets are preclinical and target B-seven-H-three and her-two. Novartis President of Biomedical Research Fiona Marshall framed the deal as "a clear need for new payload mechanisms to overcome resistance and expand impact" in A-D-Cs — the direct reference is to emerging resistance to Enhertu-class topoisomerase-one payloads. Deep dive on this in a few hours.

Second, Amodei walks it back. This morning STAT's Matthew Herper published an interview with Dario Amodei following last week's Claude Science launch and Anthropic's own preclinical drug-discovery entry. Amodei's headline concession — "I don't think that today we can make progress at a rate of ten years per year" — is a direct walkback of the "compressed twenty-first century" framing from his October twenty twenty-four "Machines of Loving Grace" essay, which had argued A-I could deliver a century of scientific progress in a decade. Amodei attributed the reset to three factors — model limitations that are more binding than he expected on scientific reasoning versus code and language, researcher-adoption learning curves that are slower than software, and regulatory timelines that don't compress even when discovery compresses. Two reads. One, this is the C-E-O of the frontier lab with the largest publicly-stated pharma-and-science ambition — Claude Science plus internal drug-discovery programs for neglected diseases — telling the pharma buyer directly that timelines will be closer to a decade than an order of magnitude compression. That is the responsible, sales-motion-consistent version of "the models are not ready for what we said they'd do a year ago." For the A-I-in-drug-discovery peer set — Xaira, Iambic, Isomorphic Labs, Insilico, Terray, Absci — this recalibrates the top-of-market expectation on impact-per-year and probably softens near-term valuation pressure on the platforms that were being marked against the compressed-twenty-first-century thesis. Two, the walkback does not touch the direction of travel — Amodei is still building the biggest drug-discovery push of any foundation-model lab and Claude Science is still the tool most likely to become an A-I-in-pharma standard-of-care. What changes is the pace expectation. For Foresite's Xaira flagship and adjacent portfolio, the read is that the substrate-camp thesis is intact but the "years-into-a-year" narrative that supported hyper-aggressive valuations has to be replaced with a slower, still-real productivity story. That is a healthier long-term platform environment; it is a harder short-term fundraising environment.

Third, Biogen prunes Apellis. One month after closing the five-point-six-billion-dollar Apellis acquisition, Biogen has decided to pause or terminate most of the legacy Apellis preclinical research programs and eliminate a small number of research roles. The two approved products — Empaveli, the C-three complement inhibitor for paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria and now C-three glomerulopathy, and Syfovre, the C-three-targeting intravitreal for geographic atrophy — are the retained-value bucket. Everything else on the Apellis preclinical bench gets paused or killed. Two reads. One, this is the classic post-acquisition bench-clean — Biogen is buying two commercial assets and a complement-biology platform, not the whole R-and-D vision that Apellis had been running independently. The five-point-six-billion-dollar price tag reads much more coherently against Empaveli and Syfovre near-term cash flows than against Apellis's broader complement franchise. Two, for the complement-drug-development category, the message is that even a five-billion-dollar acquisition of a specialty complement leader may not carry the deeper pipeline forward. That is a cautionary read for standalone-complement-focused biotechs like Amyndas, Q32 Bio, Kira Pharma, and the second-tier complement plays that were valuing their platforms on the "someone will pay for the deep bench" thesis.

Fourth, Sanofi warning letter. The F-D-A published a warning letter dated June twenty-second to Sanofi's Genzyme Ireland facility in Waterford — reported across pharma manufacturing press over the past few days — citing violations found during a January twenty twenty-six inspection at the site that manufactures Altuviiio, the twenty twenty-three-approved efanesoctocog-alfa factor-eight replacement for hemophilia A, and Thymoglobulin, the anti-thymocyte-globulin used to prevent rejection in kidney transplantation. Specific findings include data-integrity failures — the quality unit did not exercise proper oversight of laboratory data-integrity practices, including failing to document multiple non-viable particulate excursion tests and using uncontrolled checklists for laboratory record reviews that were subsequently discarded. The F-D-A designated products manufactured at the site as "adulterated," which is the strong regulatory-status flag. Sanofi has said it does not anticipate impact on medicines supply "at this time." Two reads. One, this is the second warning letter to a Sanofi-Genzyme manufacturing subsidiary inside eighteen months — the January twenty twenty-five warning letter to the Framingham, Massachusetts Genzyme site sets the precedent that this is not a single-site quality slip but a broader quality-governance pattern. That significantly complicates Sanofi's manufacturing narrative and probably triggers additional inspections at other Sanofi-Genzyme facilities. Two, for Altuviiio specifically — the extended-half-life factor-eight product growing at high single-digit share of the hemophilia-A market — the supply-continuity question is now live even if Sanofi says supply is not at risk. If F-D-A determines further remediation is required and the Waterford site can't run at full capacity, Altuviiio moves into a supply-constrained posture right when it is establishing its share against Novo Nordisk's Esperoct, CSL Behring's Afstyla, and the gene therapies. For hemophilia B-and-A-adjacent Foresite portfolio exposure, the near-term read is manufacturing-risk-repriced across the class.

Fifth, F-D-A gene-therapy leadership. Vijay Kumar, acting director of the Office of Therapeutic Products at the Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research — the office that reviews all cell and gene therapies — is stepping down after one year in the role. Kumar had replaced former O-T-P director Nicole Verdun in June twenty twenty-five under then-C-B-E-R director Vinay Prasad. Acting C-B-E-R director Karim Mikhail takes direct oversight of O-T-P during the transition. F-D-A said it will advertise the permanent director position both internally and externally. Two reads. One, this is the second acting-O-T-P-director transition in about a year — Verdun to Kumar in mid-twenty-twenty-five, Kumar to Mikhail-oversight now — at the office that just cleared the Casgevy pediatric-two-plus label expansion under the Commissioner's National Priority Voucher pilot in fifty-three days last week and that oversees every in-vivo C-R-I-S-P-R and A-A-V program in the U-S pipeline. The turnover pattern raises the operational-continuity question for gene-therapy sponsors whose Type-A and Type-B meeting cadence depends on stable reviewer relationships. Two, for the cell-and-gene-therapy category — Vertex-C-R-I-S-P-R Therapeutics Casgevy, Bluebird's lentiviral programs, Sarepta's Elevidys under its live safety-signal remediation plan, Scribe Therapeutics on the S-one for the epigenetic-P-C-S-K-nine program, Verve Therapeutics on the base-editing recovery, and the broader in-vivo C-R-I-S-P-R and A-A-V bench — the read is that O-T-P leadership is in transition just as the Commissioner's National Priority Voucher pathway is being tested as a lane for these products. Regulatory clarity on how the voucher pathway applies to non-Casgevy assets is the near-term open question, and it is now being adjudicated under acting-director-of-acting-C-B-E-R oversight rather than a settled O-T-P director.
