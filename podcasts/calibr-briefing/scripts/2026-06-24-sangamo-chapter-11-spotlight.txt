Spotlight for Wednesday June twenty-fourth on Sangamo Therapeutics — the thirty-year-old zinc-finger pioneer that filed Chapter 11 in Delaware overnight, lined up Eli Lilly and Astellas as stalking-horse bidders for substantially all its assets, and effectively ended one of the original gene-editing pure-play storylines in biotech. Five threads — what Sangamo was, what the deal terms actually are, what Lilly is buying for fifty million dollars, what this tells you about gene-editing industry economics in mid-twenty twenty-six, and the read-through to Calibr and to Foresite's delivery-platform exposure.

First, what Sangamo was. Founded nineteen ninety-five by Ed Lanphier in Richmond, California. The original thesis was zinc-finger nucleases — engineered DNA-binding proteins that could be programmed to recognize a specific genomic sequence and, fused to a nuclease, cut at that sequence. This was a decade before CRISPR. Sangamo licensed the foundational zinc-finger patents from Johns Hopkins, MIT, and Scripps Research and spent the next twenty-five years building therapeutic programs around them. First clinical trial in HIV, using gene-edited T cells, in two thousand nine. First in-vivo human gene-editing trial — anywhere, in any modality — in twenty eighteen, targeting the IDS gene in Hunter syndrome. That program — SB-three-eighteen — eventually missed efficacy, but the regulatory precedent it set for direct-in-body gene editing was foundational for everything that came after, including the CRISPR therapeutics that would later overshadow Sangamo commercially. Big partnerships across the past decade: Biogen on beta-thalassemia and sickle cell, Pfizer on hemophilia-A starting twenty seventeen with seventy million dollars upfront and the milestone ladder, Kite on engineered T-cell receptors. Pfizer's December twenty-twenty-four termination of the hemophilia-A partnership — even after the Phase 3 trial hit its primary endpoint — was the cash-flow event that put the company on the path to today's filing. Revenue fell seventy-eight percent year over year. Net loss thirty-one million dollars on revenue of one-point-four million. Cash effectively gone by the second quarter of twenty twenty-six.

Second, the deal terms. Voluntary Chapter 11 petition in the U.S. Bankruptcy Court for the District of Delaware. Section three-sixty-three sale process with two stalking-horse bids already on file. Substantially all assets going to auction.

Lilly's bid. Fifty million dollars cash plus assumption of certain liabilities. What Lilly gets in exchange is four things. One, the AAV capsid engineering platform — the directed-evolution toolkit Sangamo built for tissue-specific capsid selection. Two, the zinc-finger protein platform itself — the foundational zinc-finger DNA-binding-protein and nuclease technology and the patent estate. Three, the Modular Integrase platform — Sangamo's newer payload-insertion technology, a serine-integrase-based system for site-directed gene insertion. Four, ST-five-oh-six, the prion-disease program, preclinical, very rare and very high unmet need but a small market.

Astellas's bid. Twenty-five million dollars at closing plus up to twenty-five million in milestone payments. What Astellas gets is isaralgagene civaparvovec — the Fabry-disease gene therapy formerly called ST-nine-twenty. Sustained alpha-galactosidase-A elevation in twenty-five treated patients through three years of follow-up. FDA aligned on an accelerated-approval pathway in twenty twenty-four. This is a clinical asset with a clear regulatory path on the table, and Astellas is paying twenty-five million dollars at closing for it. By comparison, BioMarin paid two-point-nine billion for Roctavian — also a single inherited monogenic-disease gene therapy, also AAV-delivered, also from a private company. Same modality. The mid-twenty-twenty-six pricing for a clinical-stage AAV gene therapy with a Phase-3-quality dataset and an accelerated-approval pathway is now about one percent of what BioMarin paid eight years ago for the comparable.

Not in either stalking-horse: ST-five-oh-three for chronic neuropathic pain, a clinical-stage program. Giroctocogene fitelparvovec — the Pfizer-returned hemophilia-A asset, on the cusp of an approval filing when Pfizer cut bait at the end of twenty twenty-four. And the cell-therapy and regulatory-T-cell asset bundle. Those three buckets go to the auction floor with no minimum bid set.

Third, what Lilly is actually buying for fifty million. The market consensus a few hours into the open is that Lilly is paying that fifty million almost exclusively for the AAV capsid engineering platform. Zinc-finger nucleases as a therapeutic editing tool have been outcompeted on every dimension by CRISPR base editing and prime editing — they have a defensible patent estate but no clinical path Lilly hasn't already seen done better elsewhere. The Modular Integrase platform is technically interesting but clinically unvalidated. ST-five-oh-six is a science option rather than a commercial asset. What Lilly cares about is the capsid library. Lilly has been progressively building out a gene-therapy and oligonucleotide-delivery toolkit across the past eighteen months — and the Sangamo capsid library, with established directed-evolution selections for tissue-targeted serotypes, is the third meaningful piece of that toolkit. Fifty million dollars for a directed-evolution AAV-capsid platform with established tissue-targeting selections is the cheapest meaningful platform-asset transaction in the gene-therapy delivery space this year, and arguably this cycle.

Fourth, gene-editing industry economics in mid-twenty-twenty-six. The pure-play monoculture is finished. Editas trades at roughly five percent of its twenty-twenty peak. Verve was acquired into pharma after restructuring. Beam restructured twice in the past eighteen months. Intellia trades near its cash value. CRISPR Therapeutics is propped up almost entirely by the Vertex Casgevy royalty stream and the joint-program economics. What Sangamo's Chapter 11 — and the fifty-million-dollar AAV capsid price tag — confirms is the unbundling thesis: pharma will buy delivery platforms cheap, will buy individual clinical-stage assets cheap, but is not paying a pure-play premium for an end-to-end gene-editing company anymore. The discovery layer, the delivery layer, the editing-payload layer, and the clinical-asset layer are being separated, and the discovery layer plus the delivery layer have effectively no enterprise value as standalone businesses. The clinical-asset layer retains some value, but at deeply discounted multiples relative to peak-cycle comparables. Astellas paid one percent of BioMarin-Roctavian for an arguably better clinical dataset.

Fifth, the read-through. Three threads.

One. For Calibr and Calibr-Skaggs: this matters less directly because Calibr's pipeline is not gene-editing-centric, but the structural lesson generalizes — and it's the same lesson the Boundless-Serapha reverse merger this morning is telling at a different point in the lifecycle. The path to financing for a delivery-platform or platform-engineering company is now licensing inflows from China, plus a partnered or distressed-asset transaction with pharma. The IPO-and-platform-premium pricing of the twenty nineteen to twenty twenty-one cycle is gone. For any platform-tech program in development inside Calibr-Skaggs or Foresite Labs, the planning assumption should be that the exit is increasingly the Sangamo-style asset-by-asset sale rather than the platform-premium IPO.

Two. For Foresite's delivery-platform exposure: Lilly's fifty-million-dollar Sangamo capsid acquisition is a hard price-discovery event. Any Foresite portfolio or Foresite-Labs-incubator company building tissue-targeted AAV capsids, LNP libraries, or directed-evolution delivery toolkits should now plan against the assumption that the pharma bid for the platform — absent a clinical asset attached — is in the low-tens-of-millions range, not the half-billion-plus that capsid-engineering companies were raising private rounds against three years ago. Insitro, Xaira, and other Foresite-aligned computational platforms are differently exposed — they sit upstream of the delivery layer, generating targets and ligands rather than building capsids — but any portfolio company sitting in the protein-engineering or capsid-engineering layer should benchmark against fifty million dollars cash as the realistic strategic-buyer floor.

Three. For pharma BD strategy and specifically for Lilly: the pattern this year is now clear. Lilly is assembling — quietly, asset-by-asset, at distressed pricing — what is now arguably the most diverse gene-medicine delivery toolkit at any major pharma. The Sangamo capsid platform is now the third meaningful piece. The corollary for any Calibr-Skaggs program that needs an in-vivo delivery partner for a future Calibr-Skaggs gene-medicine asset: the strategic question is whether the Lilly delivery toolkit is now broad enough that the partnering conversation starts and ends there, and whether the platform-component-by-platform-component negotiating leverage that smaller delivery companies had two years ago has collapsed.

Bottom line. Sangamo Chapter 11 is the inflection event for gene-editing pure-plays. Lilly's fifty-million-dollar acquisition of the capsid plus zinc-finger platforms is the price-discovery event for delivery technology absent a clinical asset. Astellas's twenty-five-million-dollar Fabry-program pickup is the price-discovery event for clinical-stage AAV gene therapy in twenty twenty-six. The unbundling thesis is now settled at the largest pharma scale of the cycle. For Foresite portfolio companies on the delivery-platform side, the benchmark is fifty million. For pharma BD, Lilly is the buyer to watch.
