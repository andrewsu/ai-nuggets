Good morning, Travis. It's Wednesday, May 13th, 2026, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. Here are today's top stories in biotech and pharma.

Number one. The big one. Marty Makary is out as FDA commissioner. After thirteen months marked by personnel departures, internal turmoil, and high-profile rejections, Makary resigned Tuesday. The immediate trigger was a fight over flavored e-cigarettes — the FDA approved a slate of fruit-flavored vapes from Glas Inc. on May sixth after White House pressure, and Makary reportedly resigned over having been forced to sign. Kyle Diamantas, the agency's deputy commissioner for food, steps in as acting commissioner. Diamantas is a former Jones Day partner who took the food role in February 2025 and has, by industry observers' assessment, very little experience regulating drugs or biologics. The biotech reaction was immediate. Shares of Replimune jumped over nine percent and uniQure popped more than five percent. BIO's John Crowley issued a statement calling for "strong, stable, and science-driven leadership." We'll go deep on this — and what the transition means for Calibr's pipeline strategy — in today's spotlight.

Number two. Isomorphic Labs, the DeepMind spinout, closed a two point one billion dollar Series B. Thrive Capital led the round, with Alphabet and GV continuing as investors and new money coming in from MGX, Temasek, CapitalG, and the UK Sovereign AI Fund. Total capital raised now stands at roughly two point six billion. The money is targeted at scaling what Isomorphic calls its AI drug design engine, internally branded IsoDDE, and pushing its therapeutic pipeline toward the clinic. This is the largest private AI-drug-discovery round on record and lands two weeks after Iambic announced its own multi-year discovery alliance with Revolution Medicines. The center of gravity in computational drug design is consolidating quickly around a small number of well-capitalized platforms — Isomorphic, Xaira, Iambic, and Recursion — and the rest of the field is going to have to differentiate on therapeutic insight, not just compute.

Number three. Pfizer and Arvinas have brought in Rigel Pharmaceuticals to commercialize Veppanu, their recently approved breast cancer protein degrader. Eighty-five million dollars upfront to Pfizer and Arvinas, with Rigel taking U.S. commercial responsibility. Veppanu is vepdegestrant, an oral estrogen receptor PROTAC — the first protein degrader to reach the U.S. market — approved in ER-positive HER2-negative metastatic breast cancer after one line of endocrine therapy. The decision to license commercial rights to a smaller specialty oncology player rather than running the launch in-house is a notable signal: Pfizer is concentrating sales resources on its larger oncology franchises, and Arvinas — the platform originator — gets a partner that's actually built for community oncology promotion. The deal also values Veppanu at far below expectations from twelve months ago.

Number four. BioMarin announced layoffs at the Cranbury, New Jersey headquarters of Amicus Therapeutics, just weeks after closing its four point eight billion dollar acquisition of the rare-disease company. The cuts are part of consolidation following the deal, which brought in Galafold for Fabry disease and Pombiliti plus Opfolda for late-onset Pompe. BioMarin's playbook here echoes its earlier Prosensa and Ultragenyx-era integrations — keep the commercial assets, fold R&D into the existing Novato platform, and reduce site footprint quickly. The deal closed last month at a roughly thirty percent premium over Amicus's pre-deal stock.

Number five. Alkermes reported positive topline Phase 3 results for Lumryz in idiopathic hypersomnia — the REVITALYZ study hit its primary endpoint of improvement on the Epworth Sleepiness Scale. Lumryz, an extended-release sodium oxybate, came to Alkermes via the two point four billion dollar Avadel acquisition that closed earlier this year, and the indication expansion validates that thesis. Lumryz already has approval in narcolepsy; idiopathic hypersomnia roughly doubles the addressable patient population in the U.S. Alkermes plans an sNDA filing later this year.

Number six. The European Union reached a provisional deal on the Critical Medicines Act, the EU's biggest pharmaceutical supply-chain legislation in a decade. The agreement, struck between the Council and the Parliament, creates an EU-level mechanism to identify shortages of essential medicines, coordinate procurement across member states, and incentivize on-continent manufacturing of active pharmaceutical ingredients. This sits alongside disruptions emerging from the Iran conflict — Evonik raised prices yesterday citing energy costs, and Merck KGaA reported its life-science clients are stockpiling. The supply-chain story is back in a way it hasn't been since COVID.

And finally, number seven. Takeda quietly axed another nausea and vomiting program from its pipeline, bringing its track record in that space to zero for four over the last three years. The discontinued asset, an NK1 receptor antagonist for chemotherapy-induced nausea, follows three prior failures. Takeda is now effectively out of the supportive-care oncology space it entered with significant investment back in 2022. The lesson for everyone: rebuilding a therapeutic franchise from acquired or in-licensed early-stage assets is hard, and supportive care is a graveyard most pharmas have already learned to avoid.
