Good morning, Travis. Monday June twenty-ninth, twenty twenty-six. Today's roundup leads with the largest single biotech deal of the morning — Ipsen taking out Kartos in hemato-oncology for up to one-point-seven-five billion dollars — and runs through Roche's AXELIOS one sequencing platform launch, the Larimar Therapeutics nomlabofusp investor event at seven forty-five Eastern, an Amneal C-H-M-P positive opinion in Parkinson's disease, and the BridgeBio NEJM publication on infigratinib in achondroplasia. Five items.

First — and this is the spotlight in a few hours — Ipsen announced this morning that it has agreed to acquire Kartos Therapeutics for up to one-point-seven-five billion dollars, with four-hundred-fifty million dollars upfront in cash at closing and up to one-point-three billion dollars in regulatory and commercial milestones. The deal is expected to close by end of Q-three this year. Ipsen expects accretion to core operating income from twenty twenty-nine. The headline asset is navtemadlin, an oral M-D-M-two inhibitor designed to free up the p-fifty-three tumor-suppressor pathway in myelofibrosis. The transaction matters for three reasons the spotlight will unpack. One, navtemadlin is the most advanced M-D-M-two inhibitor in oncology, in a class that has otherwise been a graveyard — Roche's idasanutlin failed Phase three in A-M-L, Daiichi Sankyo's milademetan failed in liposarcoma, Boehringer Ingelheim discontinued brigimadlin barely a year after talking it up as its most advanced oncology project. Navtemadlin's BOREAS Phase three readout in J-A-K-inhibitor-refractory myelofibrosis showed roughly a tripling of S-V-R-thirty-five rates and a doubling of T-S-S-fifty symptom-control rates over best available therapy, with bone-marrow fibrosis reduction in roughly half of patients — the first credible disease-modifying signal from an M-D-M-two inhibitor in any indication. Two, the deal teleports Ipsen into hemato-oncology and stacks alongside G-S-K's ten-point-six-billion-dollar Nuvalent purchase and AbbVie's ten-point-nine-billion-dollar Apogee close earlier this month — late-stage oncology assets are commanding premium multiples and Ipsen is the latest mid-cap to step in. Three, the registrational catalyst is the POIESIS Phase three readout in twenty twenty-seven, evaluating navtemadlin plus ruxolitinib against ruxolitinib alone in patients with suboptimal response to J-A-K monotherapy — and that's the readout that anchors the milestone tranche of the deal.

Second, Roche announced this morning the global launch of AXELIOS one — its first commercial next-generation sequencing platform built on its proprietary sequencing-by-expansion technology, or S-B-X. The headline economics are unusual for a sequencing platform launch. A standard human genome at thirty-X coverage in duplex output mode for one-hundred-fifty dollars, with end-to-end same-day whole-genome sequencing in research workflows. The underlying chemistry converts D-N-A and R-N-A into high-signal-to-noise expanded polymers Roche calls Xpandomers, which are measured by a reusable C-M-O-S nanopore sensor — fundamentally different from Illumina's sequencing-by-synthesis, PacBio's H-i-F-i, or Oxford Nanopore's direct-strand reads. Roche is launching with anchor partnerships at Hartwig Medical Foundation and Broad Clinical Labs, a collaboration with ten-X Genomics on single-cell and spatial library prep, and a free open-source bioinformatics suite called X-O-O-S. For the A-I-in-drug-discovery thread, the read is that the cost-per-genome curve just stepped down meaningfully again — roughly a fifty-percent drop versus current Illumina list pricing in a single product launch — and that's the data-supply curve underneath every A-I-platform discovery company in the portfolio. Foresite's biology-foundation-model exposures get a structural cost tailwind from this. The open question is whether AXELIOS scales out fast enough across customer sites to actually compress per-sample economics at the pharma-R-and-D-procurement level by twenty twenty-seven, or whether Illumina ratchets pricing in response and the field absorbs the technology shift without the headline cost-per-experiment economics changing as much as the launch numbers imply.

Third, Larimar Therapeutics is hosting an investor event this morning at seven forty-five Eastern to walk through the nomlabofusp clinical-development program in Friedreich's ataxia. The package they pre-announced includes a regulatory update — most importantly the status of the rolling B-L-A seeking accelerated approval, which they had targeted for initiation this month — and an update on the long-term open-label extension at the twenty-five and fifty milligram dose levels. The prior data drop showed median improvement in modified F-A-R-S of two-point-two-five at one year of treatment versus a worsening of one-point-zero observed in the natural-history F-A-C-O-M-S reference population, with directionally consistent improvements across modified F-A-R-S, F-A-R-S-A-D-L, nine-hole-peg-test, and M-F-I-S. Tissue frataxin in skin cells reached the range seen in asymptomatic disease carriers at six months of dosing. The story is two-step. The science is whether intracellular delivery of recombinant frataxin can durably restore the protein that genetic loss-of-function eliminated in Friedreich's. The regulatory question is whether the F-D-A holds open the door to skin frataxin as a reasonably-likely-surrogate endpoint for accelerated approval, which the agency pre-aligned to in February when it granted breakthrough therapy designation. A clean B-L-A acceptance with skin-F-X-N anchored as the surrogate sets up a first-half twenty twenty-seven launch and a new precedent for protein-replacement-therapy regulatory pathways in genetic disease broadly, with read-through to other monogenic loss-of-function programs in the Foresite-adjacent pipeline. Watch for timing language on the rolling B-L-A submission and any signal on F-D-A pre-B-L-A interactions.

Fourth, Amneal Pharmaceuticals announced this morning that the European Medicines Agency's C-H-M-P has issued a positive opinion recommending E-U marketing authorization for Hopledo — an extended-release oral levodopa-carbidopa formulation — for adults with Parkinson's disease who have moderate-to-severe motor fluctuations despite oral levodopa-based therapy. The drug is already approved in the U-S under the brand name C-R-E-X-O-N-T. The C-H-M-P opinion is based on the Phase three R-I-S-E-P-D readout showing significantly more Good O-N time with fewer daily doses than immediate-release levodopa-carbidopa. European Commission decision typically follows the C-H-M-P opinion by sixty-seven days. Zambon, Amneal's E-U commercialization partner, expects rolling launch across European markets starting October this year. The competitive frame is AbbVie's Vyalev twenty-four-hour subcutaneous foslevodopa-foscarbidopa infusion — the in-class benchmark for advanced motor-fluctuation Parkinson's — with Inbrija inhaled levodopa positioned as off-episode rescue. Hopledo sits in between as a once-or-twice-daily oral that simplifies the regimen without device burden. Calibr is not in Parkinson's; the Foresite read is the read-through to oral-extended-release Parkinson's market dynamics for any portfolio company in symptomatic neurology.

Fifth and shortest. BridgeBio's positive Phase three PROPEL three trial of oral infigratinib in children with achondroplasia landed in the New England Journal of Medicine yesterday afternoon. The trial showed the largest mean increase in annualized height velocity over placebo reported in any Phase three achondroplasia study — two-point-one centimeters per year — and the first statistically significant improvement in body proportionality from a placebo-controlled achondroplasia trial. Oral once-daily F-G-F-R-three inhibition versus the established BioMarin Voxzogo subcutaneous comparator, with breakthrough therapy designation and the rare-pediatric-disease voucher attached. BridgeBio plans an N-D-A filing in Q-three this year and an E-M-A submission in the second half of twenty twenty-six, with launch targeted for early-to-mid twenty twenty-seven. For Foresite's pediatric-rare-disease exposures, the read is that oral once-daily F-G-F-R-three inhibition is now the new bar in achondroplasia — and the Voxzogo subcutaneous franchise that BioMarin built into the standard of care now sees its first credible oral competitor in pivotal data. Watch BioMarin's response and the F-D-A's stance on accelerated versus traditional approval pathways for pediatric statural-growth indications under the priority-review-voucher reform window.
