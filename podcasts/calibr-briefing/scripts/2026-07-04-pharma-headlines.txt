First, Revolution Medicines zoldonrasib. Thursday at the ESMO Gastrointestinal Cancers Congress the company presented Phase one-two data on two zoldonrasib-based combinations in K-RAS G-twelve-D metastatic pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma. First-line zoldonrasib plus modified FOLFIRINOX in forty-one patients — objective response rate eighty-two percent, disease-control rate ninety-six percent. First-line zoldonrasib plus gemcitabine and nab-paclitaxel in forty patients — objective response rate sixty-one percent, disease-control rate ninety percent. Second-line-plus zoldonrasib combined with daraxonrasib — RevMed's pan-K-RAS-on inhibitor — in thirty patients — objective response rate fifty percent, median progression-free survival nine-point-six months. Two reads. One, K-RAS G-twelve-D is roughly forty percent of all K-RAS-mutant pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma and about twenty-nine percent of all RAS-driven cancers, and there is nothing approved. First-line metastatic pancreatic cancer with modified FOLFIRINOX alone runs an objective response rate around thirty percent and a median survival around eleven months. Eighty-two percent front-line response rate is roughly two-and-a-half times the historical benchmark, and if that holds up at Phase three it is a genuine before-and-after moment for a disease that has resisted every mechanism in oncology's toolkit for four decades. Two, this extends the K-RAS-generation-two win from yesterday's Roche divarasib spotlight into the G-twelve-D slice. Divarasib is G-twelve-C-only; zoldonrasib is G-twelve-D-only. The two data points together read as the K-RAS class finally scaling out of the narrow G-twelve-C non-small-cell lung cancer sub-population that has capped Amgen's Lumakras and Bristol Myers's Krazati commercially, and into the much larger G-twelve-D slice where the majority of K-RAS-driven cancer sits. Deep dive in a few hours.

Second, AstraZeneca plus CSPC. Yesterday AstraZeneca announced a licensing and research collaboration worth up to one-point-seven-seven billion dollars with China's CSPC Pharmaceutical Group to discover and develop small interfering R-N-A therapies for chronic kidney disease. Thirty million dollars upfront, up to five hundred forty million in development and regulatory milestones, and up to one-point-two billion in commercial sales milestones. AstraZeneca gets exclusive option rights to develop, manufacture, and commercialize one preclinical siRNA candidate globally, and a second candidate outside China. The work runs through CSPC's Jushi Biotech subsidiary. Two reads. One, on the AstraZeneca side, this is the growing stack of AstraZeneca-CSPC tie-ups across therapeutic areas — thirty million upfront for a global option on a preclinical siRNA plus a China-carve-out on a second is the AstraZeneca deal template applied to nephrology: pay small upfront, structure large biobucks contingent on progression, keep discovery risk on the Chinese partner side. Two, for the oligonucleotide-therapeutics class, siRNA in chronic kidney disease is a category with several plays already in the clinic — Alnylam has cemdisiran and other targets; Ionis and Novartis have I-O-N-I-S-F-B-L-Rx for I-g-A nephropathy — and AstraZeneca entering with a preclinical program signals the class is now broad enough to support a multi-target franchise rather than a single-asset play. For Foresite, siRNA in nephrology is a category worth mapping against portfolio exposure.

Third, Vertex Casgevy pediatric expansion. On Wednesday the F-D-A expanded the label for Casgevy — the Vertex and CRISPR Therapeutics ex-vivo CRISPR-Cas9-edited autologous C-D-thirty-four-plus stem cell therapy — from patients twelve years and older down to patients two years and older, in both sickle cell disease with recurrent vaso-occlusive crises and transfusion-dependent beta thalassemia. The agency cleared the pediatric expansion under the Commissioner's National Priority Voucher pilot in fifty-three days. Vertex estimates roughly five thousand five hundred additional U-S children are now eligible. Two reads. One, first-of-mechanism CRISPR therapy taking a pediatric label as young as two is a milestone — this is the first genetic medicine of any kind approved down to age two, and it is the first CRISPR-edited product to cross into the pediatric population where the disease is most disabling and the lifetime cost-effectiveness math for a one-time gene-editing intervention is most favorable. Two, for the CRISPR-therapeutic class Casgevy is the reference asset — every other in-vivo and ex-vivo CRISPR program is now evaluated relative to this one on manufacturing, conditioning-regimen tolerability, and payer access. A pediatric label expanding the addressable population is a positive lift for the class and validates the argument that ex-vivo autologous editing plus busulfan conditioning is tolerable enough for pediatric use.

Fourth, Scribe Therapeutics S-one. Yesterday Scribe Therapeutics — the Jennifer Doudna-founded in-vivo CRISPR company — filed an S-one with the S-E-C to raise up to seventy-five million dollars on Nasdaq under ticker S-C-T-X. Lead program S-T-X-eleven-fifty is an epigenetic-silencing approach that durably lowers L-D-L cholesterol by repressing P-C-S-K-nine expression without permanently altering D-N-A — currently in a first-in-human trial in Australia under T-G-A clearance, initial data expected first half of twenty twenty-seven. Two follow-on programs — S-T-X-twelve-hundred targeting lipoprotein-little-a and S-T-X-fourteen-hundred targeting triglycerides — use Scribe's X-E gene-editing technology to attack the other two atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease drivers. Phase one starts are anticipated in twenty twenty-seven and twenty twenty-eight. Two reads. One, in-vivo CRISPR for P-C-S-K-nine puts Scribe head-to-head against Verve Therapeutics' V-E-R-V-E-one-oh-two base-editor program, which ran into a Phase one dose-escalation grade-three toxicity signal in early twenty twenty-five and paused enrollment for months. Scribe's approach — epigenetic silencing rather than permanent D-N-A edit — is engineered to sidestep permanent-edit off-target risk. Whether that mechanistic bet translates to a clean safety profile in the clinic is the twenty twenty-seven readout to watch. Two, a seventy-five-million-dollar I-P-O for a preclinical-plus-early-Phase-one CRISPR company is a bellwether — the CRISPR I-P-O window has been thin since twenty twenty-four, and Scribe's pricing on the road tests whether the in-vivo CRISPR narrative gets a re-rating post-Casgevy pediatric expansion and post-Verve safety questions.

Fifth, Angitia AGA-one-eleven. Yesterday Angitia Biopharmaceuticals — the U-S-China bone-anabolic bispecific-antibody company — terminated its Phase three trial of AGA-one-eleven, a recombinant human bone morphogenetic protein six under evaluation as an adjunct to lumbar interbody fusion surgery. The termination lands about five months after Angitia closed a one-hundred-thirty-million-dollar Series D in February on the strength of its bone-building bispecific-antibody platform. AGA-one-eleven was the most-advanced clinical asset. Two reads. One, this is a B-M-P-directed osteoinductive adjunct competing against approved recombinant B-M-P-two products from Medtronic in a market that has struggled with heterotopic-ossification safety signals for two decades. Angitia has not disclosed whether termination was efficacy or safety, but the fact that the Phase three came apart right after a fresh late-stage financing suggests the sponsor concluded the data would not clear F-D-A on either axis. Two, for the bispecific-antibody bone-anabolic platform — Angitia's separate pipeline of Amgen-competing sclerostin and D-K-K-one bispecifics is a distinct story from AGA-one-eleven — the Phase three termination increases execution pressure on the platform, and the near-term catalyst for the company shifts to whichever bispecific reads out next.
