Good morning. It's Wednesday, June tenth, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. The lead story today is the largest biotech I-P-O in history — Parabilis Medicines priced six hundred seventy million dollars in an upsized initial public offering at twenty dollars a share overnight, and with the concurrent private placement, the company brought in roughly seven hundred forty-five million dollars in total. Cambridge-based, peptide-modality platform, the lead asset is a first-in-class direct inhibitor of the beta-catenin and T-C-F protein-protein interaction. The asset and the I-P-O together are today's spotlight. Six items in the roundup.

First, the Parabilis I-P-O itself. Parabilis priced at twenty dollars — above the marketed range of seventeen to nineteen dollars — for thirty-three-and-a-half million shares, for six hundred seventy million dollars in primary I-P-O proceeds, with an additional seventy-five-million-dollar concurrent private placement. The stock lists on the Nasdaq under the ticker P-B-L-S. This is the largest biotech Nasdaq debut on record, eclipsing prior multi-hundred-million-dollar peptide and cell-therapy debuts from the twenty-twenty I-P-O wave. The asset is zolucatetide, a stabilized alpha-helical peptide — what Parabilis calls a Helicon — that directly inhibits the beta-catenin and T-C-F transcription-factor interaction, a node at the center of the Wnt-driven cancer-signaling pathway that has historically been classified as undruggable. The company is the former FogPharma, founded by Harvard chemist Gregory Verdine in twenty fifteen, renamed in October of twenty twenty-four, and entering public markets after having raised more than eight hundred million dollars privately. Use of proceeds is roughly one hundred fifty million dollars to the desmoid-tumor indication for zolucatetide in Phase three, around one hundred twenty million dollars to additional zolucatetide indications including familial adenomatous polyposis and hepatocellular carcinoma, and about one hundred thirty million dollars to the broader pipeline including the Regeneron antibody-Helicon-conjugate collaboration. The spotlight goes deep on the platform, the lead asset, the Regeneron deal, and what an I-P-O of this size says about the appetite for chemistry-platform assets going after intracellular undruggable targets.

Second, two Phase three readouts out of Gilead — one a clean win, the other a clean miss. Gilead and Merck reported positive Phase three data from the ISLEND-one and ISLEND-two trials of their once-weekly oral H-I-V regimen, which combines Merck's islatravir, a nucleoside reverse-transcriptase translocation inhibitor, with Gilead's lenacapavir, a capsid inhibitor that is already approved as a long-acting injectable for pre-exposure prophylaxis and treatment of multidrug-resistant H-I-V. Both trials hit the primary efficacy endpoint of non-inferior virologic suppression compared with standard daily oral antiretroviral therapy. Once-weekly oral H-I-V — meaning two pills a week instead of fourteen — is the first new convenience-driven oral H-I-V regimen the field has had since the daily fixed-dose combinations launched in the twenty-tens, and the partners plan to file globally on the data. On the other side of the ledger, the partners discontinued the Phase three Keynote-D-forty-six and Evoke-zero-three combination study of Gilead's Trodelvy — a Trop-two antibody-drug conjugate — with Merck's Keytruda in first-line non-small-cell lung cancer for high P-D-L-one tumors. The combination did not show a statistically significant progression-free-survival benefit. This is the second high-profile lung-cancer setback for Trodelvy after the prior front-line and second-line studies missed, and it narrows the franchise's expansion strategy outside its core breast-cancer indications.

Third, the Treeline Biosciences reverse-merger announcement. The clinical-stage oncology biotech founded by former Loxo executive Josh Bilenker, which had been operating in stealth since twenty twenty-two, announced Monday that it will go public via reverse merger with Standard BioTools, the diagnostics-instrumentation company that trades on the Nasdaq under L-A-B. The combined company will operate as Treeline, take the ticker T-R-L-N, and close into the second half of this year. Treeline investors will hold approximately eighty-four-and-a-half percent of the combined company, Standard BioTools shareholders the remaining fifteen-and-a-half percent, and the combined entity will have more than nine hundred million dollars in cash at closing — funding runway into twenty twenty-nine. Treeline's pipeline is three clinical-stage oncology programs in Phase one with multiple readouts expected starting in twenty twenty-seven. This is the rare instance of a stealth biotech with significant private backing choosing the reverse-merger path rather than a conventional I-P-O — a useful reference point for how clinical-stage oncology assets are reaching public markets in the current window.

Fourth, Novo Nordisk presented Phase three data for CagriSema, the fixed-dose combination of the long-acting amylin analog cagrilintide and the G-L-P-one analog semaglutide, in type-two diabetes at the American Diabetes Association meeting yesterday. The three pivotal REIMAGINE trials — REIMAGINE one, two, and three — each met their primary endpoints for hemoglobin A-one-C reduction and confirmatory secondary endpoints for body-weight reduction across the diverse type-two-diabetes population studied. The high-dose two-point-four-milligram and two-point-four-milligram formulation drove the largest A-one-C and weight effects. CagriSema is already filed with the F-D-A in the obesity indication on earlier REDEFINE data, and Novo is targeting a U-S launch in early twenty twenty-seven. The diabetes data adds the second indication and validates the dual-mechanism amylin-plus-G-L-P-one strategy that Zealand Pharma's petrelintide and others are pursuing in parallel.

Fifth, City Therapeutics — the next-generation R-N-A-interference company we mentioned in the venture roundup yesterday — closed its ninety-nine-point-five-million-dollar Series B Monday. Viking Global Investors and Sofinnova Investments led, with Casdin Capital, A-R-C-H, Fidelity, Slate Path, Rock Springs, Regeneron Ventures, and others participating. The chemistry focus is engineered short interfering R-N-A trigger molecules paired with precision ligands designed to deliver R-N-A-i payloads to tissues outside the liver — central nervous system, lung, and muscle being the major addressable spaces. Lead program City-F-X-I is in Phase one. Two more programs are expected to enter the clinic by year-end. Founder-and-Alnylam alumnus-led, well-syndicated, and continuing the trend of capital concentrating on the small handful of R-N-A-i platforms that can credibly cross the in-vivo durability threshold outside the hepatocyte.

Sixth, a regulatory item and a deal item to close. The Pentagon yesterday added W-u-Xi AppTec — China's largest contract research and development services provider — to its national-security entities list, citing alleged military-end-user connections. The designation does not by itself impose sanctions, but it materially raises the regulatory friction for U-S sponsors whose programs sit at any W-u-Xi facility, and it amplifies the Biosecure-Act exposure the industry has been managing for the last two years. Separately, Sanofi expanded its existing collaboration with French-American A-I biotech Owkin into a multi-year agreement to co-develop autonomous artificial-intelligence agents for drug research and development across discovery, translational science, and clinical workflows. Owkin is the more clinically anchored of the well-funded A-I-discovery names, with a federated-learning platform built on real-world clinical data, and the expansion deepens Sanofi's A-I-platform exposure beyond the prior Aily-Labs and Insilico relationships.
