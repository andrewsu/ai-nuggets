Spotlight, Thursday July second. Anthropic launched Claude Science on Monday in San Francisco — an A-I workbench for scientific researchers that pulls more than sixty scientific databases into a single Claude reasoning environment — and, in a coordinated announcement, disclosed that the company has stood up its own internal preclinical drug-discovery program focused on diseases traditional pharma considers commercially unattractive. Together, those two announcements complete a three-way race — Google DeepMind and Isomorphic Labs on one side, OpenAI's GPT-Rosalind on another, and now Anthropic — in which the foundation-model labs go from selling models and A-P-Is to running direct drug programs. Seven threads.

First, what Claude Science actually is. It's an A-I workbench built around Claude with pre-configured connectors to more than sixty scientific databases across four broad families — genomics, proteomics, structural biology, and cheminformatics. The premise is that researchers today shuttle between disconnected databases, analysis tools, and pipelines to do their work, and that most of the time cost of doing computational biology sits in the plumbing and not in the reasoning. Claude Science replaces the plumbing with a single conversational layer — a researcher in Claude can query genomes, protein structures, compound libraries, and pathway databases through natural language, and can chain those calls into an analysis workflow without leaving the conversation. Structurally, this is the Model Context Protocol pattern extended into the specific vertical of scientific research, with Anthropic providing the anchor integrations rather than expecting each database to expose its own connector. The design intent is that the model — not the researcher — becomes the integration layer.

Second, the launch partners and the antibiotic-design proof point. The most concrete demonstration of what the platform can do at launch comes from Basecamp Research, a London-based metagenomics company founded in twenty nineteen. Basecamp brought two things to the launch. One, a dataset — nine-point-eight billion protein sequences collected across two hundred and thirty diverse locations in more than thirty countries, which the company describes as roughly a tenfold expansion over the union of every public sequence database combined, with sample-collection agreements that resolve the benefit-sharing questions that constrain most metagenomic datasets. Two, a foundation model — E-D-E-N, trained on that dataset, that the company deploys inside Claude Science as an antibiotic-design and vaccine-target-prediction engine. The demonstration point is a headline number — a ninety-seven percent success rate on designing functional peptides against W-H-O critical-priority multidrug-resistant pathogens. The company frames the significance in operational terms — one of the Basecamp founders said in the launch press that any clinician in the world can now chat with Claude and design an antibiotic that may work, without needing to be a computational scientist first. The University of Pennsylvania's César de la Fuente is a launch collaborator on the antibiotic side, and Basecamp has additional launch positioning in vaccine-target prediction and programmable-gene-insertion applications through a fine-tuned E-D-E-N variant.

Third, Anthropic's internal drug-discovery program. This is the announcement that changes the frame most significantly. Anthropic has hired Eric Kauderer-Abrams as head of life sciences and stood up a preclinical drug-discovery program that will pursue diseases traditional pharma considers commercially unattractive — the neglected-tropical-disease and rare-orphan space where a pure return-on-invested-capital calculation does not close the deal for a large-cap biopharma. The public argument from Anthropic is instrumental — running its own drug programs gives the company the operational understanding it needs to be a credible partner to life-sciences customers. The read behind that argument is that Anthropic has concluded — as Google DeepMind concluded in twenty twenty-one when it spun out Isomorphic Labs, and as OpenAI signalled in April with GPT-Rosalind — that the foundation-model labs cannot credibly sell into pharma without being pharma. The pharma buyer is not evaluating an A-I model as a math object; the pharma buyer is evaluating an A-I model as a component of a drug-development workflow, and the credible seller is the one that has run that workflow itself. Anthropic did not disclose specific indications, targets, or timelines for the internal program.

Fourth, the grant program. Anthropic is offering up to thirty thousand dollars in Claude credits per project across up to fifty selected projects in a Claude Science cohort, with an application window closing July fifteenth and selected projects running between September first and December first. Modal — a cloud-compute infrastructure company — is providing up to two thousand dollars in compute for select projects. The economics of the grant program are modest — thirty thousand dollars in credits will not fund a real preclinical program — but the intent is user-acquisition and case-study generation. Anthropic wants to demonstrate what independent scientific researchers can do on the platform inside a three-month window, and it wants those demonstrations to be pointed at the specific classes of computational-biology problems that Claude Science is optimized for.

Fifth, the competitive landscape read across the foundation-model labs. Three players are now running direct drug programs off foundation-model substrate. Google DeepMind is the incumbent — Isomorphic Labs was spun out in twenty twenty-one, has AlphaFold and its downstream design tools as the platform foundation, raised a six-hundred-million-dollar round in twenty twenty-four, and last month closed a one-point-two-billion-dollar Series B led by Thrive Capital that took the fresh capital past one-point-seven billion dollars — the platform is now the best-capitalized A-I-native drug-design shop in the world, with programs advancing toward clinical entry. OpenAI's GPT-Rosalind is newer — launched in April, positioned as a scientific-reasoning platform layered on the G-P-T model family, and paired with what OpenAI describes as internal drug programs of its own. Anthropic's entry today completes the trio. The strategic frame that follows is that A-I in drug discovery is no longer a platform-company-versus-pharma competitive frame — it is a foundation-model-lab-versus-platform-company frame, and the platform companies are competing not against pharma but against the substrate providers themselves.

Sixth, the read for the A-I-in-drug-discovery peer set — Xaira, Iambic, Isomorphic Labs, Terray, Absci, Recursion, Insitro, Genesis Therapeutics, Cradle. Two components. On the negative side, the foundation-model labs going direct is dilutive to the pure-play A-I platform investment case. Every dollar of pharma discovery budget that flows to Anthropic, OpenAI, or Google's direct programs is a dollar that does not flow to Xaira or Iambic. The strategic response for the platform companies is to differentiate on either wet-lab integration — where physical experimental capability at scale is still not something foundation-model labs are equipped to run — or on domain-specific vertical models trained on data the foundation-model labs cannot easily replicate. On the positive side, the substrate assumption is validated. Claude, G-P-T, and Gemini are now the operating layer for scientific research; every platform company can build against that substrate rather than replicating it. That reduces the capital burden on the platform layer even if it compresses the moat. The net directional read on the peer set is that valuation multiples on pure-A-I-model platform companies will compress relative to A-I-plus-wet-lab-integration companies over the next twelve months, and the strategic hedges Xaira, Iambic, and adjacent should already be building toward are — one, differentiated wet-lab throughput; two, proprietary data assets that foundation-model labs cannot get; and three, domain-specific fine-tuning that adds measurable capability over the substrate baseline.

Seventh, the read for Calibr and Foresite. Three specific reads. One, on Claude Science as a live tool. The grant program is on the table for Calibr researchers — thirty thousand dollars in credits per accepted project, up to fifty projects, applications through July fifteenth — and Calibr has ready-to-run computational-biology projects that would fit the September-through-December cohort window. The application-cost-to-optionality-value math is favorable, and this is the kind of low-cost strategic engagement with the substrate layer that pays off downstream regardless of whether any individual project produces publishable results. Two, on Foresite's Xaira flagship. This announcement is directionally negative for the pure-play A-I platform investment case in the short term — Anthropic is now a direct competitor rather than a passive substrate — but the practical response is to double down on the differentiators Xaira actually has. Xaira's positioning on wet-lab-integrated design, its proprietary data assets, and its established biotech-execution team are exactly the things Anthropic cannot replicate on a two-year horizon. The strategic read is to accelerate those differentiators rather than to retrench. Three, on the broader Foresite portfolio, this is the moment to think about which portfolio companies are positioned to consume Claude Science, G-P-T, and Gemini as substrate rather than compete against them — and to lean into that substrate-consumption model where it fits, because compute cost per unit of scientific insight is about to drop by an order of magnitude for teams that build on top of these platforms rather than around them.

Bottom line. Anthropic launched Claude Science on Monday, launched an internal preclinical drug-discovery program on the same day, and put a grant program in market to seed the platform's user base for the second half of the year. That completes a three-way race among Google DeepMind slash Isomorphic Labs, OpenAI slash GPT-Rosalind, and Anthropic — in which the foundation-model labs are going direct into drug development instead of only selling models. For the A-I-native drug-discovery peer set — Xaira, Iambic, Isomorphic Labs, Terray, Absci — this is directionally negative for the platform-only investment case and directionally positive for the substrate assumption, and it forces a differentiator sharpening around wet-lab integration, proprietary data, and vertical fine-tuning. For Calibr, the immediate action item is the Claude Science grant application, open through July fifteenth. For Foresite, the read is that Xaira's differentiators against foundation-model labs are the differentiators that matter most now, and that the broader portfolio should be sorted by which companies are substrate-consumers and which are substrate-competitors. Next catalysts to watch. One, Anthropic's disclosure of specific internal drug-program indications and timelines. Two, Isomorphic Labs's first announced clinical candidate — expected in the second half of this year. Three, whether OpenAI's GPT-Rosalind produces its first published drug-discovery case study inside the next six months. Four, whether any of the platform companies — Xaira, Iambic, Terray — announces a differentiated wet-lab-integrated capability as an explicit response to the foundation-model-lab entry.
