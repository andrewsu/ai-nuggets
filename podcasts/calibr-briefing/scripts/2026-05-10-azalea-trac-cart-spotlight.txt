First, the data. Six rhesus macaques received a single IV dose of Azalea's dual-vector system. No lymphodepletion — meaning they did not pre-condition the animals with chemotherapy to clear out endogenous T-cells, which is the standard pre-treatment for autologous CAR-T and which carries real toxicity. CAR-T cells were detectable in peripheral blood by day five and peaked at forty-one percent of total T-cells by day eleven. That is an extraordinary engineering efficiency for an in vivo platform. B-cell depletion exceeded ninety percent in peripheral blood in all six animals by day ten — the on-target pharmacodynamic readout you want from a CD19-directed CAR. Five of six animals also showed greater than ninety-percent B-cell depletion in the deep tissue compartments — lymph nodes and bone marrow — within two weeks. No deaths. No neurotoxicity. No off-target CAR expression. No off-target genomic integration detected in non-T-cells in the blood. That is, by every measure, the cleanest in vivo CAR-T primate dataset that has been disclosed publicly.

Second, the architecture. This is the part that matters most for the technical and competitive read. Azalea's platform is a two-component system. The first component is a CD3-targeted enveloped delivery vehicle — they call it an EDV — that delivers a transient ribonucleoprotein complex of CRISPR-Cas9 plus a guide RNA targeting the TRAC locus. TRAC stands for T-cell receptor alpha constant — it's the genomic locus that encodes part of the natural T-cell receptor, and it has been the gold-standard insertion site for ex vivo CAR-T engineering since Justin Eyquem's 2017 Nature paper from the Sadelain lab at Memorial Sloan Kettering. The second component is a T-cell-tropic AAV — they call it AAV-T — that carries the CAR transgene with no promoter of its own, but flanked by homology arms to the TRAC locus. So the EDV cuts the TRAC locus, and the AAV-delivered CAR gets inserted by homology-directed repair. The CAR ends up under the control of the cell's endogenous TCR promoter. That's important because it means the CAR is expressed at physiological levels, regulated by the cell's normal T-cell receptor signaling logic, not driven by a viral promoter to potentially supraphysiological levels.

Third — and this is the part that separates Azalea from every other in vivo CAR-T platform in the clinic or near it — this is the first in vivo CAR-T approach that combines cell-specific delivery with site-specific genomic insertion. The reason this matters technically: random lentiviral integration carries insertional mutagenesis risk. We've seen this play out historically in ex vivo settings — the SCID-X1 leukemias from gamma-retroviral integration into LMO2 in the early 2000s, the more recent CAR-T-derived T-cell malignancies that triggered the FDA's 2024 boxed warning across the autologous CAR-T class. With random integration, you're rolling the dice on insertion sites at a population level, and at high enough cell numbers, the long tail produces a problem. Site-specific TRAC integration was what made Eyquem's ex vivo work so compelling — it converted a probabilistic risk into a deterministic process. Bringing that to in vivo is a step change.
