Number one — and this is today's spotlight. Belgian biopharma UCB on Sunday agreed to acquire San Diego–based Candid Therapeutics for up to two-point-two billion dollars. Two billion upfront, two hundred million in milestones. The lead asset is cizutamig — a BCMA-by-CD3 bispecific T-cell engager in Phase 1 for autoimmune disease. Translation: a biologic that drags T cells onto plasma cells and B cells expressing BCMA, with the explicit goal of deep, durable B-cell-and-plasma-cell depletion to reset autoimmunity. We'll unpack what that means for Calibr's switchable CAR-T platform with AbbVie shortly. The deal closes Q2 to early Q3.

Number two — this is the part that matters for the Foresite portfolio thread. Candid was assembled in September 2024 from a three-way merger of Vignette Bio and TRC 2004 plus a fresh three-hundred-and-seventy-million-dollar round. Vignette Bio was incubated by Foresite Labs, with Foresite Capital, Qiming, Samsara BioCapital, and Mirae as backers. TRC 2004 was co-founded by Two River and Third Rock Ventures. Cizutamig — the asset UCB just paid two billion for — was originally licensed ex-China by Vignette Bio from EpimAb Biotherapeutics in Shanghai. So this is a Foresite Labs incubation, a Foresite Capital exit, and yet another data point that the right way to deal with the geopolitics around Chinese assets is to pull global rights out of China and run all the clinical work in the US. Worth keeping in mind given last week's House appropriations move to ban China-origin data outright.

Number three — Teva late last week announced a seven-hundred-million-dollar upfront acquisition of Emalex Biosciences for ecopipam, a selective dopamine D1 receptor antagonist that just posted positive Phase 3 data in pediatric Tourette syndrome. Up to two hundred million more in commercial milestones. NDA filing later this year, first-in-class label expected. This is part of CEO Richard Francis's pivot-to-growth playbook — Teva needs neuroscience assets to replace generics decay, and ecopipam is a clean late-stage tuck-in.

Number four — STAT broke today that Latus Bio raised forty-two million in a Series B extension to push its lead AAV gene therapy LTS-201 into the clinic for Huntington's disease. The mechanism is unusual — instead of knocking down mutant huntingtin, Latus is targeting MSH3 to reduce somatic CAG expansion, attacking the upstream driver. Worth flagging because it's another example of in vivo gene-modifying therapeutics gaining clinical-trial-ready financing on the back of the Intellia HAELO Phase 3 win. The in vivo platform thaw is real and it's flowing capital.

Number five — Endpoints out today on the Trump administration's psychedelics push. Following the April 17th executive order directing FDA to fast-track psychedelic reviews under the Kennedy MAHA agenda, the field is split between calling this "real momentum" and calling the regulatory uncertainty "absurd." Net effect for traditional small molecule and biologic developers: probably nothing direct, but it's another signal of the FDA's tilt toward unconventional pathways under current leadership, and it's another data point on regulatory unpredictability.

Number six — earnings sidebar. Vertex laid off about twenty staff in Massachusetts as part of a "functional reorganization" late last week. Amgen separately laid off twenty-two more from the Horizon Therapeutics integration. Both are routine post-deal trims, but worth noting that even with the M&A wave, headcount is still being optimized at the bigger players. Pfizer reports earnings later this week — analysts are watching for monthly VESPER-3 data on its long-acting Metsera-derived GLP-1, PF-08653944, plus updates on the dual PD-1/VEGF in colorectal that they licensed ex-China.

And number seven — quick people-and-platform note. Boehringer Ingelheim named a new corporate affairs lead on Friday. Separately, the chief scientific officer at Bayer's BlueRock cell therapy unit is exiting. BlueRock is one of the more advanced cell therapy plays in big pharma — relevant context as the in vivo cell therapy field consolidates. We'll keep an eye on whether Bayer doubles down or pulls back on cell therapy after the personnel reset.
