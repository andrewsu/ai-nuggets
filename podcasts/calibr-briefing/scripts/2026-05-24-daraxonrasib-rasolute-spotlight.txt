Today's spotlight is Revolution Medicines and the Phase three RASolute three-oh-two daraxonrasib data going to the ASCO plenary on May thirty-first. The top-line numbers — thirteen-point-two months versus six-point-seven months, hazard ratio zero-point-four-zero, in second-line metastatic pancreatic cancer — were announced in April. What is new this past Friday is the plenary placement and the full subgroup, biomarker, and safety detail dropping at the meeting. But the bigger story behind the data is the platform that produced it, the A-I partnership that supports it, and the read-throughs to Calibr's solid-tumor strategy and to the broader thesis on A-I-augmented late-stage drug development. Let me unpack it.

Start with the biology of RAS, because the RAS story is the longest-running undruggable-target story in oncology and the platform that finally cracked it is now redefining what late-stage A-I-augmented programs look like. RAS — the family of small G-T-Pases encoded by K-R-A-S, N-R-A-S, and H-R-A-S — is the most frequently mutated oncogene in human cancer. Roughly thirty percent of all cancers carry a RAS mutation. In pancreatic cancer the number is over ninety percent. In colorectal cancer it is forty-five percent. In non-small-cell lung adenocarcinoma it is thirty. For four decades RAS was considered structurally undruggable — a small protein, no obvious small-molecule binding pocket, and a dependency on guanine-nucleotide states that gave conventional kinase-inhibitor chemistry no foothold.

The breakthrough came in twenty twenty-one when Amgen's sotorasib and Mirati's adagrasib — now Bristol Myers Squibb's after the November twenty twenty-three acquisition — won F-D-A approval for K-R-A-S G-twelve-C, exploiting a cryptic cysteine pocket revealed in the active-state structure. Useful, but limited. K-R-A-S G-twelve-C is only about fifteen percent of K-R-A-S mutations and barely one percent of pancreatic cancer. The dominant mutations in pancreatic cancer are G-twelve-D, G-twelve-V, and G-twelve-R, and none of them have the reactive cysteine that the covalent G-twelve-C drugs depend on. The most clinically important RAS-driven indication — pancreatic — was structurally orthogonal to the first wave of RAS therapeutics.

That is the gap Revolution Medicines was built to fill. The Revolution platform — internally called RAS-on, in contrast to the previous generation of RAS-off inhibitors — is built on tri-complex chemistry. The drug is a small molecule that recruits the cytosolic chaperone cyclophilin-A into a complex with active, G-T-P-bound RAS, forcing a conformation that prevents downstream effector binding to R-A-F. The mechanism is allosteric, noncovalent, and importantly multi-allelic. By targeting the active state of RAS rather than a mutation-specific cysteine, the same compound can engage G-twelve-A, G-twelve-C, G-twelve-D, G-twelve-R, G-twelve-S, G-twelve-V, G-thirteen-D, Q-sixty-one mutations — and crucially, wild-type RAS. The drug-design problem stops being "find a covalent warhead for each mutant" and becomes "find a tri-complex inducer that engages every mutant the same way." That is daraxonrasib, formerly known as RMC-six-two-three-six.

Now the trial. RASolute three-oh-two enrolled five-hundred-one patients with histologically confirmed metastatic pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma who had received one prior line of standard chemotherapy. ECOG performance status zero or one. RAS mutations at codons twelve, thirteen, or sixty-one required for the primary-endpoint analysis. One-to-one randomization to oral daraxonrasib three-hundred milligrams once daily, or to investigator's choice cytotoxic chemotherapy — typically fluorouracil-and-irinotecan-based regimens, gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel, or single-agent capecitabine. The primary endpoints were progression-free survival by blinded independent review and overall survival in the RAS-G-twelve-mutated population. Secondary endpoints covered PFS and OS in the intent-to-treat population, objective response rate, duration of response, and patient-reported quality of life.

The result is the data you saw in April and that gets the plenary slot on May thirty-first. Median overall survival of thirteen-point-two months on daraxonrasib versus six-point-seven months on chemotherapy. A sixty-percent reduction in the risk of death. P less than zero-point-zero-zero-zero-one. In second-line metastatic pancreatic cancer, the historical median overall survival on standard chemotherapy is around six months and has not moved in twenty years. No targeted therapy has crossed the threshold of clinically meaningful OS gain in this setting — and now an oral small molecule has roughly doubled it. The safety profile was characterized by Revolution Medicines as "generally well tolerated" with no new safety signals beyond the rash and gastrointestinal toxicity already mapped from the Phase one and two-a portions. The F-D-A is reviewing under the Commissioner's National Priority Voucher framework. Plenary placement at ASCO is the signal that ASCO's program committee considers this practice-changing.

The plenary on May thirty-first will give the detail behind the top line — breakdown by individual RAS mutation, where the field wants to see whether G-twelve-D, G-twelve-V, and G-twelve-R patients all responded comparably; breakdown by prior-therapy regimen, whether F-O-L-F-I-R-I-N-O-X first-line patients differed from gemcitabine first-line; the duration-of-response curve; and the C-A-nineteen-nine and circulating-tumor-D-N-A dynamics that will set the framework for monitoring RAS-on therapy in real time. These details convert a positive Phase three into a launch playbook.

Behind daraxonrasib sits a vertically integrated RAS-pathway platform. Zoldonrasib — RMC-nine-eight-oh-five — is a G-twelve-D-selective RAS-on inhibitor that posted an eleven-point-one-month progression-free survival and fifty-two percent objective response rate in pretreated K-R-A-S G-twelve-D non-small-cell lung cancer at AACR last month. RMC-five-five-five-two is an m-T-O-R-C-one slash 4E-B-P-one selective inhibitor designed to combine with RAS-on agents to suppress a dominant resistance pathway. Earlier-stage RAF, S-O-S-one, and S-H-P-two assets sit behind that. Multi-selective and selective RAS-on inhibitors at the front, combination drugs in the next layer, a discovery engine refreshing the generation behind. It is the structure that justifies the roughly twenty-billion-dollar Revolution Medicines market capitalization.

The A-I component is what makes this a Calibr-relevant story specifically. Revolution Medicines signed a multi-year technology and research collaboration with Iambic Therapeutics in July twenty twenty-five. The structure — up to twenty-five million dollars in upfront and near-term milestones plus ongoing R-and-D reimbursement — looks small relative to the Revolution market cap, but the architecture is what matters. Iambic's NeuralPLexer is an A-I model for protein-ligand structure prediction. Revolution Medicines' proprietary RAS-pathway data — protein conformations, ligand binding poses, structure-activity relationships across the tri-complex chemistry class — is being used to train a bespoke version of NeuralPLexer that is uniquely tuned to RAS biology. Iambic's PropANE model — a graph neural network for lead-optimization properties — is being applied across the RAS-on portfolio for property prediction. The intellectual-property structure has both companies retaining rights to exclusive targets they nominate, with the trained models available to both. This is the platform-deal structure for A-I in drug discovery — co-trained models, target-level exclusivity, milestone payments tied to discovery output rather than software licensing. It is what Calibr's A-I partner conversations should be modeled on.

Four read-throughs for Calibr.

First, the A-I-in-drug-discovery read. Revolution Medicines is on the Calibr focus-area list for a reason — it is the rare late-stage company that is publicly modeling an A-I-augmented platform-deal structure with a credible A-I partner. The Iambic deal is small in dollar terms but rich in structural detail. Revolution is the customer that A-I drug-discovery companies want — late-stage validated chemistry, deep target ownership, willingness to share data, and willingness to publicly position the partnership. The Iambic-Revolution structure should be the template Calibr studies when thinking about its own A-I partnerships. Compare with the Lilly-Insilico Medicine two-point-seven-five-billion dollar deal, the Pfizer-Schrodinger expansion, or the B-M-S-Anthropic enterprise deal we covered Wednesday — three different structures, three different value-capture models. Revolution-Iambic is the closest to a "platform partnership with milestone-aligned upside" model. That is the structure that probably scales for a Calibr-sized internal pipeline.

Second, the subpopulation-first labeling read. We made this same point yesterday on Datroway. Revolution Medicines is doing the same thing daraxonrasib's label will end up doing — finding the highest-confidence subpopulation in a heterogeneous disease, winning label first, then expanding. RAS-positive metastatic pancreatic cancer, second-line, is the high-confidence subpopulation. The next steps — first-line in RASolute three-oh-three, second-line non-small-cell lung cancer in RASolve three-oh-one, plus combination programs in colorectal — are the expansion phase. The Calibr MASH biologic is the analogous problem. The Phase three trial design should pick its high-confidence subpopulation first — lean MASH, or steatosis-responder-but-fibrosis-plateau, or incretin-intolerant — rather than fighting Lilly's retatrutide and Novo-Akero's efruxifermin on the unselected MASH population where the dominant assets are likely to win.

Third, the chemistry-platform read. Tri-complex chemistry, Helicon stabilized helical peptides — covered in Friday's Parabilis spotlight last week — and the broader class of small-molecule and peptide protein-protein-interaction modulators is now the active frontier in oncology. The deruxtecan platform was last decade's chemistry breakthrough. Tri-complex induction and stapled-peptide P-P-I modulation are this decade's. Calibr's chemistry organization has the deep capability to play in this space — ReFRAME, Hilbert Curve, the broader Skaggs chemistry infrastructure. The question is whether any of the internal targets are amenable to a tri-complex-style approach, and whether the chemistry team is being explicitly resourced to pursue P-P-I modulation as a deliberate platform strategy. The pancreatic-cancer breakthrough on a P-P-I-modulating tri-complex is the proof-of-concept that should drive that resource allocation.

Fourth, the solo-pharma launch read. Revolution Medicines is going to launch daraxonrasib by itself. No big-pharma partner. No commercial-license arrangement. No outright acquisition. This is the third partnership model that Calibr should be looking at — not the AZ-Daiichi platform-deal model that built Enhertu and Datroway, not the Foundayo I-P-O public-listing model that we covered earlier this month, but the solo-launch-by-mid-cap-biotech model. Revolution Medicines will go from approximately twenty-billion-dollar market cap to a global-launch oncology franchise. They will hire their own oncology sales force, run their own medical-affairs organization, manage their own global supply chain, and capture all the operating margin themselves. The case for it works when the platform is deep enough that one-off licensing would leave value on the table — which is exactly Revolution's posture. Calibr's in-vivo C-A-R-T platform, if it produces durable clinical data over the next twenty-four months, will face the same option set.

A few caveats. Durability is the real question — the longest RASolute three-oh-two follow-up is around thirteen months, and the field wants twenty-four and thirty-six-month curves before settling whether this is transformative or merely very good. Resistance biology is the other unknown — S-H-P-two amplification and parallel-signaling upregulation are documented in the K-R-A-S G-twelve-C class, and the same biology probably applies to multi-selective RAS-on. The combination strategy with R-M-C five-five-five-two is the bet against that. Long-term safety — rash and gastrointestinal toxicity from Phase one and two — needs to hold up in real-world use.

The signal to take away. The first wave of RAS-targeting drugs — sotorasib and adagrasib — was a clinical proof-of-concept. The second wave, anchored by daraxonrasib's RASolute three-oh-two read, is the franchise. Revolution Medicines is on track to convert a difficult-to-drug disease biology into a multi-billion-dollar oncology franchise, and the A-I partnership architecture is part of how they did it. For Calibr the implications are concrete — A-I partnership structure, subpopulation-first label strategy, P-P-I-modulation chemistry investment, and the solo-launch-versus-partnership posture decision. Daraxonrasib is not just a pancreatic-cancer drug. It is the proof case for what an A-I-augmented, platform-deep, mid-cap biotech franchise looks like in twenty twenty-six.
