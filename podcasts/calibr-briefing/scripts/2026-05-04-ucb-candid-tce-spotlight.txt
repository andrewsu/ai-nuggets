Let me unpack the deal, the asset, the read-throughs to in vivo CAR-T, and the three structural reasons I think the switchable architecture still has a moat.

First, the basic facts. On Sunday, May 3rd, Belgian biopharma UCB announced a definitive agreement to acquire San Diego–based Candid Therapeutics for two billion dollars upfront in cash, plus up to two hundred million in milestones. Total deal size, two-point-two billion. Closing is expected by end of Q2 or early Q3 2026, subject to antitrust clearance. Candid is private. The buyers paying two billion for a Phase 1 asset.

The lead asset is cizutamig — internal designation CND106. It's a BCMA-by-CD3 bispecific T-cell engager, built on the FIT-Ig platform from EpimAb Biotherapeutics in Shanghai, with deliberately tuned-down CD3 affinity. The design intent is to maximize on-target killing of BCMA-expressing plasma cells, plasmablasts, and B cells, while minimizing the cytokine release syndrome that has plagued first-generation BCMA bispecifics like teclistamab in multiple myeloma. The clinical package: cizutamig has been dosed in over a hundred patients across multiple myeloma and autoimmune indications, and is now in Phase 1 across more than ten autoimmune diseases — lupus and lupus nephritis lead the list, with myositis, scleroderma, IgG4 disease, and refractory rheumatoid arthritis in the basket. There's also CND261, a CD20-by-CD3 bispecific from the TRC 2004 side, licensed ex-China from Genor Biopharma, behind cizutamig in the pipeline.

Now the deal narrative behind the deal. Candid was assembled in September 2024 through a three-way merger of Vignette Bio — incubated by Foresite Labs, backed by Foresite Capital plus Qiming, Samsara, and Mirae — and TRC 2004, co-founded by Two River and Third Rock. The merger was structured around three hundred and seventy million in fresh capital and the explicit thesis that bispecific T-cell engagers were going to take over the autoimmune CAR-T market. UCB's two-billion-dollar exit, eighteen months later, validates that thesis financially. It also marks Foresite Labs's largest exit of 2026 to date, and slots Foresite Capital onto a list of MASH and immunology IPO-or-acquisition winners that already includes 89bio sold to Roche for three-point-five billion and a string of GLP-1 plays.

Three things matter for Calibr.

First — the read-through to autoimmune cell therapy economics. The strategic thesis for Calibr's CLBR001 plus SWI019 program is that switchable CD19 CAR-T offers titratable, controllable, durable B-cell depletion for autoimmune disease — lupus first, then a wider basket. The competitive question UCB just sharpened with two billion dollars is whether you actually need a CAR-T at all if a BCMA bispecific can deplete the same pathogenic populations. Cizutamig already has anecdotal NEJM-grade data from Genentech's mosunetuzumab and from teclistamab off-label use showing deep B-cell-and-plasma-cell depletion in refractory lupus and myositis. The bispecific case is straightforward — repeat-dosable, no preconditioning chemotherapy, no apheresis, no cell manufacturing, no Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy. The CAR-T case is more durable, deeper depletion, longer remission. Whether the durability difference is worth the operational burden is exactly the question UCB just bet on, and exactly the question Travis and his team are answering at Calibr.

Second — and this is the underrated point — UCB also just validated the China-licensed-ex-China model, even as Washington tries to break it. Cizutamig's molecular composition was designed by EpimAb in Shanghai. Vignette Bio took ex-China rights, did the IND-enabling work and Phase 1 in the US, and the entire clinical package UCB is buying is American-conducted. This is the regulatory firewall the field built specifically to insulate against the geopolitical risk we discussed yesterday — the House appropriations push to ban China-origin clinical trial data, the FDA freeze on cross-border cell therapy, the JNJ-4496 axing because of its AbelZeta origin. The ex-China license model converts a Chinese discovery into a US-development asset, and UCB just paid two billion to prove it works. The implication for Calibr — and AbbVie — is that the cleanest competitive position is fully Western-discovered, Western-developed, with no China-origin clinical data to defend. CLBR001 plus SWI019 fits that profile. The TCE-licensed-from-China-but-developed-in-US assets are clean enough to clear an acquisition diligence today, but the political wind is still blowing the wrong direction.

Third — the modality moat. Why does a switchable CAR-T still command a premium when the bispecific antibody is sitting right there with a two-billion-dollar exit comp? Three structural reasons.

One: depth and durability. T-cell engagers like cizutamig deplete BCMA-positive cells while patients are dosed and replenish when dosing stops. CAR-T, even time-limited switchable CAR-T, drives a deeper depletion at peak and has shown — in early autologous CAR-T autoimmune data — multi-year remissions off therapy. For severe refractory lupus, that's the difference between chronic biologic dependency and an actual reset.

Two: dual targeting and combinatorial logic. Calibr's switch peptide architecture lets you sequence multiple CARs against multiple antigens — CD19 for B cells, BCMA for plasma cells, dual targeting against the relapsing populations that escape mono-targeted therapy. Bispecific TCEs are by design two-target only and scale poorly into trispecifics without losing potency. CAR-T-plus-switch is structurally extensible.

Three: control. This is the wedge that justifies the entire switchable thesis. UCB's bispecific has tuned-down CD3 to manage cytokine release. Calibr's switch lets you titrate the activation level peptide-dose by peptide-dose, in real time, in response to clinical labs. If a patient develops a hyperinflammatory response, you stop the switch peptide and the CARs go inert. With a bispecific, you stop dosing and wait for the antibody to clear. With autologous CAR-T or in vivo CAR-T, you have very limited options for stopping a runaway response. Switchable architecture solves the control problem in a way that neither bispecific TCEs nor unswitched CAR-T can match.

Now the contrary view, because we should always do this. The bear case on Calibr is that bispecific TCEs are good enough for the bulk of the autoimmune market — lupus, RA, myositis — and that the small fraction of refractory patients who would benefit from CAR-T-grade depth isn't enough to support a premium-priced cell therapy program. UCB will sell cizutamig at biologic prices, scale it to tens of thousands of patients, and capture the addressable autoimmune market before CAR-T programs cross late-stage milestones. The CAR-T assets get pushed into rare, refractory, and combination indications — real, but commercially smaller than the biologic case.

I think that view underweights two things. One — the autoimmune CAR-T data so far, from Mackensen's group, from Cabaletta, from CASTLE, suggests deep, durable, drug-free remissions that bispecific TCEs simply have not matched. If the durability gap holds at scale, the price-and-burden tradeoff favors CAR-T even at much higher per-patient cost. Two — UCB's two-billion-dollar deal puts a price floor under the autoimmune cell-and-bispecific category that did not exist last week. Every venture and corporate-development team that was on the fence about funding the next switchable CAR-T or next-gen TCE just got a clear comp. The capital flow into Calibr-adjacent programs gets easier, not harder.

The watch items from here. One — UCB's Phase 1 cizutamig data updates through the second half of 2026, particularly any lupus nephritis or myositis cohort readouts at ACR or EULAR. If cizutamig's depletion is deep and durable, the bispecific case strengthens. If it requires chronic redosing for sustained effect, the CAR-T case strengthens. Two — AbbVie's earnings calls into Q3, watching for any sCAR-T platform disclosures from the Calibr collaboration; UCB's deal raises the strategic salience. Three — does this trigger a counter-deal? Roche, AstraZeneca, Sanofi, Bristol all have autoimmune franchises and no clear BCMA TCE position. Watch for an autoimmune TCE counterbid in the next ninety days, possibly Sino-licensed similar structure. Four — and this is the long-term signal — does Foresite Labs spin out another autoimmune cell-or-bispecific play this year? A win like this attracts capital to similar plays, fast.

The bottom line, Travis. UCB's Candid acquisition is not bad news for Calibr. It is, in fact, validation of the entire autoimmune deep-immune-reset thesis at the modality level. It's a price marker that says: pharma will pay two-billion-dollar premiums for clinical-stage assets that promise durable B-cell-and-plasma-cell depletion. The strategic question is no longer whether autoimmune CAR-T is real — it's whose modality wins the durability-versus-burden tradeoff. Calibr's switchable architecture has a structurally cleaner answer to control, depth, and combinatorial extension than any bispecific can deliver. The TCE wave is going to commoditize the easy autoimmune indications. The CAR-T wave, with the right safety architecture, is going to win the refractory and the curative ones. AbbVie's job, with Calibr, is to be on the right side of that line.
