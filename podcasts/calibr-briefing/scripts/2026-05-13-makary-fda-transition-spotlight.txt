Welcome back, Travis. For today's spotlight, we're going deep on the Marty Makary resignation — because for anyone running drug development programs in the United States, this isn't a personnel story. It's a regulatory regime change in the middle of a fiscal year, and the question for Calibr isn't whether Makary leaving was good or bad. The question is what the operating environment looks like over the next twelve to eighteen months, and what to plan around.

Let me start with what actually happened.

Makary resigned Tuesday after thirteen months in the job, the second-shortest tenure of any confirmed FDA commissioner in modern history. The proximate trigger was a flavored e-cigarette dispute. On May sixth, the FDA approved several fruit-flavored vapes from Glas Inc. The Wall Street Journal reported that Makary had been refusing to approve those products and that the White House pressured him to sign off. He signed, and apparently decided that was the line. One week later he was out.

But the e-cigarette fight was just the proximate cause. The deeper backdrop is that the FDA under Makary had spent thirteen months losing the confidence of the biopharma industry, the medical community, and increasingly its own staff. Three things to anchor on.

First, the rejections. Under Makary, CBER — led until April thirtieth by Vinay Prasad — rejected at least five cell and gene therapies that most analysts believed were tracking toward approval. Replimune's oncolytic immunotherapy for melanoma was the highest-profile. RP1 had gotten a complete response letter in July 2025 over single-arm trial design, and got a second one this April. Replimune has now laid off roughly sixty percent of its workforce. uniQure's Huntington's disease gene therapy got hit with a request for more trial data that surprised the company and the field. Sarepta and Elevidys triggered a public safety spat. The pattern industry took away: under Makary and Prasad, the FDA was systematically tightening the bar on accelerated and single-arm approvals — particularly for cell and gene therapies and particularly in rare and severe diseases where the old FDA had been famously flexible.

Second, the staffing exodus. Prasad himself was already gone — his last day was April thirtieth, technically ending a one-year leave of absence from his UCSF faculty position. Katherine Szarama, his deputy, is acting CBER director. CDER has seen senior staff departures throughout the year. The result is that the senior review leadership across the agency is thin, acting, or both, exactly at the moment the top job also goes acting.

And third, the policy churn. Makary launched the Commissioner's National Priority Voucher, the CNPV — a brand-new voucher program he modeled on his experience as a surgical oncologist, promising review timelines as short as one to two months. Nine vouchers were awarded last year. The CNPV exists by commissioner discretion, not by statute. With Makary gone, the program's fate is openly uncertain. Meanwhile, the long-standing rare pediatric disease priority review voucher program — the one biotech actually depends on — is set to sunset September thirtieth, 2029 under the current Consolidated Appropriations Act, and Makary was the one publicly lobbying Congress for permanent reauthorization. Both voucher pathways are now in regulatory limbo.

Into this comes Kyle Diamantas as acting commissioner. Diamantas is a lawyer — a former Jones Day partner from Florida. He took the FDA deputy commissioner for food role in February 2025 and has, by every account, very little experience regulating drugs, biologics, or medical devices. His record at the agency is in nutrition, food safety, and the MAHA — Make America Healthy Again — agenda that HHS Secretary Kennedy has prioritized. He could be in the acting role for a long time. The Trump administration has not announced a permanent nominee.

Industry reaction has been a mix of relief and unease. Eric Schmidt called the Makary FDA a "circus." BIO's John Crowley said American biotech needed "strong, stable, and science-driven leadership" — those three words, in that order, are doing real work. Replimune was up nine-plus percent on the news, uniQure up over five percent. But the relief is tempered: nobody knows who comes next, and Diamantas as acting commissioner is not, by background, someone the drug industry has a relationship with.

Now let me get to what this actually means for Calibr's pipeline strategy. Four implications.

One: the regulatory bar for accelerated and single-arm approval is not snapping back. Even if Diamantas is replaced quickly with a more conventional commissioner, the underlying staff in CBER and CDER are now operationally inclined toward more conservative review — that's the institutional memory the last year has built. For any program where Calibr is contemplating an accelerated pathway, a single-arm registrational study, or a small confirmatory trial — assume the bar from late-2024 is not the bar today, and price in a conventional randomized confirmatory study even if early conversations suggest flexibility might be possible. This is particularly relevant for the MASH biologic. The FGF21 class — efruxifermin under Novo, efimosfermin under GSK, plus resmetirom on the market — is now in a regulatory window where the agency has a lot of comparator data and will be tougher on single-arm or short follow-up. Plan for full randomized Phase 3 against active comparator, not against placebo.

Two: in vivo cell engineering programs are in the most uncertain regulatory category in the industry right now. The Capsida gene therapy death — still under investigation, the company says it has no answers — is one data point. The broader CBER trajectory is the bigger one. Cell and gene therapies have been the rejection epicenter for the last year. For Calibr's in vivo CAR-T work, this means three things: first, the preclinical safety package needs to be belt-and-suspenders in a way that wouldn't have been required two years ago; second, the IND-enabling timeline should assume FDA pre-IND interactions will be slower and more substantive; and third, any first-in-human design that anticipates regulatory flexibility on cohort expansion or accelerated dose escalation should be reworked to the conservative case. The good news is the field's growing — Fortis just gave its inaugural grant to Deliver Biosciences for in vivo CAR-T LNPs, ViaNautis is presenting at ASGCT this week — so the agency will see multiple INDs in close succession, which usually helps the second and third filers.

Three: the voucher landscape is messier than it looks. The CNPV — Makary's signature initiative — was useful primarily for companies developing therapies in areas the commissioner personally prioritized. With Makary gone, no one should plan a development program around CNPV access. The rare pediatric voucher program, set to sunset in 2029, is more durable but politically vulnerable in the current Congress. The Calibr pediatric and rare-disease programs should be modeled with conventional priority review timelines and any voucher upside treated as a free option, not a base case.

Four — and this is the strategic one — partnering math may have just shifted. The Makary-era FDA increased regulatory risk on cell and gene programs, which depressed valuations on platform companies in those areas. We saw that in the Pfizer-Arvinas decision to license Veppanu, a first-in-class protein degrader, to Rigel at just eighty-five million upfront — a fraction of what a launch-ready breast cancer drug would have commanded eighteen months ago. With the regulatory overhang potentially lifting somewhat, valuations for early-stage assets — particularly in cell, gene, and protein degradation — could re-rate upward over the next two quarters. For Calibr's partnering conversations, this argues for patience on any deal that doesn't need to close immediately. The market may pay better in Q3 than it pays in Q2.

To summarize. The headline event is one resignation. The structural event is that the FDA's senior leadership in drug review is thin, the policy framework around vouchers is unstable, the review bar in cell and gene therapy is materially higher than it was a year ago, and no one knows who runs the agency next. The right response is not to panic — it's to assume the cautious case in every regulatory model, build a conservative confirmatory study into every program plan, and keep optionality open on partnering until the dust settles.

Watch for two things over the next two weeks. One: whether Diamantas issues any signal about CNPV continuity or new priority guidance — silence is itself a signal. Two: who Trump nominates as permanent commissioner. The shortlist has reportedly included industry-friendly names like Scott Gottlieb and Mehmet Oz, plus several academic regulators. The identity of the nominee will tell you whether the next year looks like a return to convention or another lurch in a new direction.

That's the spotlight. The FDA you're planning a 2027 IND with is not the FDA that exists today. Plan accordingly. Back tomorrow.
