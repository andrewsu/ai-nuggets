Welcome to the Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing spotlight for Tuesday, June 2nd, 2026. Today we go deep on in vivo CAR-T, and specifically on the Kelonia KLN-1010 dataset presented yesterday at ASCO, because that update is now the most mature clinical evidence in the field and it has implications for how the next two years of in vivo cell therapy investing play out. Let's unpack it.

Start with the basic problem in vivo CAR-T is trying to solve. Autologous CAR-T therapies, the Yescartas and Carvyktis and Abecmas of the world, work spectacularly well in B-cell malignancies and increasingly in multiple myeloma. But the manufacturing chain is brutal. You collect a patient's T cells via apheresis, ship the cells to a centralized GMP facility, perform a viral transduction of a chimeric antigen receptor construct, expand the engineered cells for one to three weeks, ship them back, and infuse them into a patient who in the meantime has been lymphodepleted with chemotherapy to make room for engraftment. The patient-specific manufacturing cost is in the four-hundred-thousand-dollar range per dose, the time from leukapheresis to infusion is typically three to six weeks, and a meaningful fraction of patients deteriorate or die in the waiting window. The promise of in vivo CAR-T is to replace that entire chain with a single injection of a vector that does the engineering inside the patient's body, using endogenous T cells as the substrate. No apheresis, no GMP cells, no lymphodepletion, no manufacturing delay. The hard part is getting the genetic payload selectively into T cells without engineering off-target lineages, without provoking a vector-directed immune response on the second dose, and without sacrificing the persistence and depth of response that autologous CAR-T currently delivers.

There are two architectural camps competing on this. One uses lipid nanoparticles to deliver messenger RNA encoding a CAR, producing transient CAR expression that decays over weeks. The companies in that camp include Capstan, now an AbbVie subsidiary after the two-point-one-billion-dollar acquisition last summer; Orna Therapeutics, which Lilly acquired last year for up to two-point-four billion; and a handful of others. The mRNA approach is reversible by design, which is attractive for autoimmune indications where you do not want a permanently engineered cell, but it raises the question of whether transient CAR expression is sufficient for the deep, durable remissions oncology needs. The other camp uses viral vectors, almost always lentivirus, with engineered envelope proteins that target T cells specifically. The lentiviral payload integrates into the T cell genome, producing stable CAR expression that persists for months or years, more like autologous CAR-T pharmacology. Kelonia is the most clinically advanced player in the lentiviral camp. Umoja Biopharma is another. Both are now wholly owned or about to be wholly owned by big pharma; Kelonia is in the process of closing into Lilly under the deal announced in April, and Lilly's prior Orna acquisition gives them a foot in both camps simultaneously.

Now to the Kelonia dataset specifically. KLN-1010 is a lentiviral vector with a modified envelope and tissue-specific tropism molecules designed to deliver a BCMA-targeted CAR-T construct selectively to T cells after intravenous administration. BCMA is B-cell maturation antigen, the same target as Bristol Myers Squibb's Abecma and Johnson and Johnson's Carvykti, both autologous CAR-Ts approved in relapsed and refractory multiple myeloma. The Phase 1 study, called inMMyCAR, is open-label dose escalation, designed to enroll up to forty patients. Yesterday's update brings the dosed count to eighteen, with fourteen new patients added since the first data drop at the American Society of Hematology meeting in December.

The headline numbers are striking. Overall response rate is one hundred percent in all evaluable patients. All evaluable patients are minimal residual disease negative in bone marrow at one month post infusion. Among the six patients with four or more months of follow-up, four are in stringent complete response and two in very good partial response. The first patient treated remains in deep, ongoing minimal residual disease-negative response beyond ten months. For context, autologous BCMA CAR-T programs in similar patient populations report overall response rates in the eighty to ninety-five percent range, with complete response rates around forty to sixty percent at six months. So Kelonia is, at this small sample size, matching or beating autologous benchmarks on depth of response, without the manufacturing tail.

The safety profile is the second thing that matters. Cytokine release syndrome was observed in sixteen of eighteen patients, all grade one or two, which is comparable to and arguably milder than the CRS rates with autologous BCMA CAR-T. Neurotoxicity was limited to one grade one event and one grade three event lasting three days, no delayed neurotoxicity, no Parkinsonism, no late cranial nerve issues, which are precisely the safety findings that have plagued the BCMA CAR-T class. Cytopenias were limited, grade three and four infections were limited, and no infusion-related reactions occurred once dexamethasone was added as a premedication. The most important safety signal of all may be what is absent. There is no lymphodepleting chemotherapy in this protocol. Patients are not getting fludarabine and cyclophosphamide before treatment, which removes the dominant source of grade three and four cytopenias that autologous CAR-T patients spend weeks recovering from.

There are caveats. Eighteen patients is small. The longest follow-up is just over ten months in a single patient. Durability beyond a year is genuinely unknown, and the comparison to autologous CAR-T durability, where five-year data exist in some programs, is still many years away. The dose escalation has not yet identified a maximum tolerated dose, which is good in one sense, since the lower doses are already producing complete responses, but it raises the question of whether efficacy plateaus and where the recommended Phase 2 dose actually lands. The time from consent to infusion is reported at thirteen days, which is faster than autologous, but it is not the one-day or one-week turnaround the field has sometimes been promised, because patients still need workup, vector preparation, and inpatient monitoring. And the question of repeat dosing, what happens when the patient's immune system has been primed against the lentiviral envelope and a second dose is needed, has not yet been tested.

Now the strategic layer. Lilly announced in April it would acquire Kelonia for headline consideration around three-point-two billion dollars, pending close. That values the company at roughly a hundred-and-seventy million dollars per patient dosed at the time of announcement, which is striking even by the inflated comps of 2026. Lilly already owns Orna, the leading messenger-RNA-based in vivo cell therapy player, so post-close Lilly will be the only large-cap pharma with assets in both the lentiviral and the nanoparticle camps. AbbVie has Capstan. Bristol Myers and Gilead are not yet in the in vivo space at scale. Pfizer is increasingly active through partnerships but does not own a platform. The next set of dominoes to fall will be whichever pharma picks up Umoja, the remaining significant lentiviral independent, and whether any of the three smaller mRNA players consolidate.

Here is what this means for Calibr's portfolio thinking. Calibr's in vivo CAR-T program is the asset most directly downstream of this whole debate. There are three takeaways. First, the lentiviral envelope-targeting approach is producing clinical depth that matches autologous CAR-T, which strengthens the technical case for any program that goes the viral route rather than the messenger-RNA route. The depth-of-response question, which was open at the start of the year, looks closer to settled now. Second, the safety profile, particularly the absence of lymphodepletion and the absence of delayed neurotoxicity, is the single largest clinical-economic argument for in vivo over autologous. If those safety advantages hold up at three hundred patients, in vivo CAR-T moves from a manufacturing convenience story to a genuine ambulatory therapy story, which radically expands the addressable patient population beyond academic medical centers. Third, the consolidation pace is accelerating, and the comp for a single-asset Phase 1 in vivo CAR-T platform is now three to seven billion dollars depending on data maturity. Programs that hit Phase 1 with clean data are commanding pharma-bidder attention faster than the underlying clinical evidence base would normally justify. That has implications for how Calibr times its own readouts and partnership conversations.

A final point on the broader cell therapy field. Yesterday's Kelonia dataset, the Capstan acquisition last summer, the Orna and Kelonia acquisitions by Lilly, and the Umoja delay in its China readout earlier this spring are all pieces of the same story. The field is moving from question-of-feasibility to question-of-scaling. The technical risk on whether in vivo cell engineering works clinically has substantially declined over the last twelve months. What replaces it, increasingly, is execution risk on patient throughput, on second-dose biology, on manufacturing of vector at scale, and on commercial reimbursement for a one-time infusion competing against established autologous players that have rate cards and oncology center workflows already built. The next twelve months will not answer whether in vivo CAR-T works. It will answer whether in vivo CAR-T can be delivered to ten thousand patients a year rather than fifty.
