First, the biggest story, and it's a macro one. On Tuesday, President Trump announced on Truth Social a one-hundred-percent tariff on imported generic drugs. The structure matters as much as the headline. Generics keep a zero-percent rate for two years starting August first, then the tariff jumps to one hundred percent for a year, and then to two hundred percent after that. The stated goal is to reshore generic manufacturing into the United States, with the escalating rate framed as a penalty for companies that don't build domestic plants inside that window. Here's why this lands hard: generic drugs account for about ninety percent of prescriptions filled in the U-S, and India alone supplies more than half of them, so this reaches the least profitable, most import-dependent corner of the industry. The two-year runway softens the near-term hit and reads as leverage to force factory commitments rather than an immediate cost shock, but the strategic signal is clear. After branded-drug threats earlier this year, the administration is now going after the low-cost generic supply chain, and the companies with the thinnest margins have the least room to absorb it.

Second, an approval, and it came early. On Tuesday the F-D-A approved GSK's Jideytro, chemically zidesamtinib, for adults with ROS-one-positive non-small-cell lung cancer who have already been treated with a ROS-one inhibitor. It's a ROS-one-selective tyrosine kinase inhibitor, designed to hit the resistance mutations and brain metastases that trip up the earlier drugs in this class. In the ARROS-one trial, in a hundred and seventeen previously-treated patients, the objective response rate was forty-four percent, with responses holding in about eighty-two percent of responders at six months and sixty-nine percent at twelve. Two things stand out. This is GSK's first approved medicine in lung cancer, and it arrives from GSK's roughly ten-and-a-half-billion-dollar acquisition of Nuvalent, so it's an early validation of that deal. And it cleared the agency almost two months ahead of its September target date, which is a meaningful signal of regulatory momentum for the asset.
