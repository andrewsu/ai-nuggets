Spotlight, Friday July third. Yesterday morning Roche disclosed Phase three Krascendo one data — divarasib, the company's second-generation oral K-RAS G-twelve-C inhibitor, in a head-to-head randomized trial versus Amgen's sotorasib, branded Lumakras, and Bristol Myers Squibb's adagrasib, branded Krazati, in second-line K-RAS G-twelve-C-mutant non-small-cell lung cancer. Both the primary endpoint of blinded-independent-central-review progression-free survival and the key secondary endpoint of overall survival were met with statistical significance. Full numeric detail withheld until an upcoming oncology congress. This is the first prospective randomized head-to-head win of a second-generation K-RAS G-twelve-C inhibitor over both approved first-generation drugs simultaneously, and it re-shapes a therapeutic class that has been struggling to scale. Six threads.

First, K-RAS G-twelve-C as a target. K-RAS is the most frequently mutated oncogene in human cancer — activating point mutations occur in roughly a quarter of all human tumors, including about a third of non-small-cell lung adenocarcinomas, ninety percent of pancreatic ductal adenocarcinomas, and about half of colorectal cancers. The G-twelve-C substitution — a cysteine at position twelve of the K-RAS protein — accounts for roughly thirteen percent of non-small-cell lung cancer overall, and it was for decades the paradigmatic undruggable oncoprotein. K-RAS has no obvious small-molecule binding pocket; its surface is smooth, its interactions with downstream effectors are protein-protein rather than pocket-based, and its intrinsic G-T-P-ase activity is fast. The break came in twenty thirteen from the Shokat lab at U-C-S-F — a covalent trap on the mutant-specific cysteine that captured K-RAS in its inactive G-D-P-bound state and locked it there. That structural insight motivated a decade of medicinal chemistry culminating in sotorasib in twenty twenty-one and adagrasib in twenty twenty-two — the first two irreversible covalent K-RAS G-twelve-C inhibitors. Divarasib is the second generation. Roche's design brief was greater potency and selectivity than the first-generation compounds — a more optimized covalent trap that engages K-RAS G-twelve-C more efficiently in the tumor and with less off-target effect on wild-type K-RAS or on related G-T-P-ases.

Second, why the first-generation drugs have not scaled. Both Lumakras and Krazati are approved and both are commercial, but neither has scaled the way the mechanism promised. Lumakras did about three hundred sixty-seven million dollars in twenty twenty-five with low single-digit growth. Krazati did about two hundred five million dollars but growing sixty-two percent, and that number reflects Bristol Myers's launch mechanics after acquiring Mirati more than any organic market expansion. The class is constrained by four things. One, narrow patient populations — K-RAS G-twelve-C is about thirteen percent of non-small-cell lung cancer, and testing rates are still low. Two, resistance — most patients progress within about six months, and the resistance mutations that emerge are diverse and hard to counter. Three, reimbursement — payer coverage has been uneven for an add-on to platinum-doublet chemotherapy. Four, the CodeBreaK two hundred trial for Lumakras came in weak on overall survival versus docetaxel — a technical hit on progression-free survival that failed to translate to O-S, and that ambiguity has capped adoption. The class needed a compound that delivered both a P-F-S benefit and an O-S benefit against the standard of care, and Krascendo one appears to have delivered exactly that.

Third, what Krascendo one actually shows and what it does not. What it shows — three hundred thirty-eight previously-treated K-RAS G-twelve-C non-small-cell lung cancer patients randomized to divarasib versus one of the two approved inhibitors, statistically significant improvement on blinded-independent-central-review progression-free survival, statistically significant improvement on overall survival, safety profile consistent with prior divarasib data, no new safety signals. What it does not show — Roche has not disclosed hazard ratios, median P-F-S numbers, median O-S numbers, or response rate differences. Jefferies analyst Michael Leuchten called the result a small win but premature to claim overall victory — the caution is that the P-F-S and O-S curves and the magnitude of separation matter enormously for market share. A hazard ratio of point-eight would generate very different competitive positioning than a hazard ratio of point-five-five. Until Roche presents at the congress the magnitude question is open. What is not open is directional — divarasib beat the two approved competitors, safety looks equivalent, and the second-generation-inhibitor thesis is now supported by prospective randomized data rather than cross-trial comparisons.

Fourth, Roche's broader K-RAS strategy. Divarasib is one piece. Krascendo one covered second-line monotherapy. Krascendo two is testing divarasib in combination with Keytruda in the first-line setting — the prize position, where the Roche investor-relations framing calls the opportunity five billion Swiss francs versus one to two billion for second-line monotherapy. First-line requires the combination to beat platinum-doublet-plus-immunotherapy in a head-to-head trial, and that is a much higher bar than second-line versus first-generation K-RAS inhibitors. Krascendo three is testing divarasib as adjuvant therapy in earlier-stage disease, which — if it succeeds — extends the label into the curative-intent setting where the market opportunity per patient is highest. And Roche also has pan-K-RAS-inhibitor and pan-RAS-inhibitor programs at earlier stages that target the ninety percent of K-RAS-mutant tumors carrying non-G-twelve-C mutations. The strategy is to sequence divarasib into first-line and adjuvant while the earlier-stage programs open up the rest of the K-RAS-mutant tumor spectrum. If all three Krascendo trials read out positive over the next twelve to twenty-four months and the pan-K-RAS asset delivers proof-of-concept, Roche is positioned to own the K-RAS class end-to-end.

Fifth, the read for Amgen and Bristol Myers. On the negative side, Krascendo one directly threatens second-line market share for Lumakras and Krazati. Any moderate hazard ratio on P-F-S and O-S from divarasib forces payers and clinicians to reconsider first- and second-generation positioning, and the natural evolution is that a second-generation compound with superior efficacy displaces the first-generation drugs to niche-and-legacy use. Amgen and Bristol Myers have limited runway to defend — their next-generation K-RAS G-twelve-C compounds are earlier stage and behind Roche on both timing and design. Amgen has a covalent-plus-allosteric compound in Phase one and a K-RAS-multi-mutant program at similar stage; Bristol Myers is pursuing its own second-generation candidate. Neither is close to catching Roche on a head-to-head reading. On the positive side for Amgen and Bristol Myers, the class is still small in absolute terms — Lumakras and Krazati together did well under seven hundred million dollars last year — so even a substantial share shift is not a huge P-and-L event; and both companies have broader oncology portfolios that reduce the individual asset dependency. But the strategic frame that first-generation K-RAS G-twelve-C is a durable franchise closed yesterday. The frame that survives is that this is a category where second-generation compounds displace and where pan-RAS approaches eventually dominate.

Sixth, the read for the A-I-in-drug-discovery peer set — Xaira, Iambic, Isomorphic Labs, Terray, Absci — and for Foresite. Two components. One, K-RAS is the paradigmatic case where structure-guided medicinal chemistry has produced iterative breakthroughs. The first-generation inhibitors came from human intuition about the switch-two pocket around the G-twelve-C cysteine; the second-generation came from more optimized design in the same pocket; the pan-RAS and pan-K-RAS approaches now in early development are pushing into structural regions that are harder to engage. A-I-driven design layered on this same structural foundation is the natural next step — Isomorphic Labs, Xaira, and Iambic have all specifically flagged K-RAS as a target family where their structural prediction and molecular generation platforms should compete. Whether A-I-designed K-RAS inhibitors match or exceed divarasib on the next benchmark is a genuine test question for the peer set over the next thirty-six months. Two, for Foresite specifically, Xaira is running an A-I-native discovery platform with a stated interest in oncology precision targets. K-RAS is on the differentiator list — either as a second-follower shot at the G-twelve-C generation-three compound, or as a shot at pan-RAS structural regions that first- and second-generation covalent trapping cannot reach. Divarasib re-sets the benchmark; the question is whether A-I-native design generates compounds that beat it on a matched-condition test. Also worth watching separately — yesterday's Insilico-Takeda six-hundred-million-dollar collaboration puts an actual price on A-I-driven discovery output at pharma scale, which is a different but adjacent data point for the peer set.

Bottom line. Roche disclosed Phase three Krascendo one data yesterday showing divarasib beat Amgen's Lumakras and Bristol Myers's Krazati head-to-head on progression-free survival and overall survival in second-line K-RAS G-twelve-C non-small-cell lung cancer. Full numbers wait for congress. Directionally, this is the first prospective randomized head-to-head win of a second-generation K-RAS G-twelve-C inhibitor over both approved first-generation drugs, and it establishes divarasib as the new standard-of-care candidate for second-line — with Krascendo two, first-line-plus-Keytruda, and Krascendo three, adjuvant, as the higher-value catalysts to watch over the next twelve to twenty-four months. For Amgen and Bristol Myers, this reduces the durability of the Lumakras and Krazati franchises and forces a strategic pivot to next-generation and pan-RAS programs behind schedule. For the A-I-in-drug-discovery peer set — Xaira, Iambic, Isomorphic Labs, Terray, Absci — K-RAS remains the paradigmatic structure-guided-design target, and the divarasib benchmark is the number A-I-native compounds need to beat to make the direct-substitution case in oncology. For Calibr, K-RAS is not a current pipeline priority, but the read on covalent-trap medicinal chemistry — a decade from structural insight to a second-generation win — is the analog for Calibr's own translational-development discipline in small-molecule oncology programs. For Foresite, Xaira's oncology positioning around structure-guided A-I design should include K-RAS as a differentiator target. Next catalysts to watch. One, Roche's Krascendo one full data release at oncology congress — timing not yet disclosed. Two, Krascendo two first-line readout with the Keytruda combination — expected within the next twelve to eighteen months. Three, Amgen and Bristol Myers next-generation K-RAS G-twelve-C compound Phase one data — both due late this year or early next. Four, any A-I-native peer's disclosed K-RAS pipeline compound entering the clinic — a specific test of whether A-I-driven design produces divarasib-competitive compounds in this target.
