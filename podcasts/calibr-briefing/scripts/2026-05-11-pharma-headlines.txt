Number one — and this is today's spotlight. Fractyl Health, ticker GUTS, disclosed Monday that European regulators have authorized the clinical trial application for RJVA-001, a single-administration AAV-nine gene therapy that drives a nutrient-responsive GLP-one transgene into pancreatic beta cells using a human-insulin promoter. The first-in-human study will enroll patients with inadequately controlled type 2 diabetes and obesity, dose three escalating cohorts of up to three participants each via endoscopic ultrasound-guided intrapancreatic injection, and optionally expand by ten patients. First dosing and preliminary data are guided for this year. In diet-induced obesity mice, RJVA-001 produced a twenty-percent body weight reduction and a thirty-eight-percent blood glucose drop by day twenty-one, sustained through day thirty-seven, and outperformed semaglutide head-to-head. This is the first credible attempt to obsolete the chronic-GLP-one injection franchise with a one-time procedure. Spotlight in a few minutes — what the platform is, why endoscopic-pancreatic delivery is the bear case, and what a successful gene-therapy attack on GLP-one means for combination MASH therapy.

Number two — Italian pharma Angelini announced a four-point-one-billion-dollar all-cash acquisition of Catalyst Pharmaceuticals last week, paying thirty-one dollars fifty per share — a twenty-one-percent premium to the prior close — with the deal expected to close in the third quarter. Catalyst brings three commercial CNS rare-disease assets: Firdapse, the amifampridine product for Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome approved in 2018; Fycompa, an anti-seizure medication picked up from Eisai in 2023; and Agamree, a steroid for Duchenne muscular dystrophy. Catalyst generated five hundred eighty-nine million dollars in revenue last year with 2026 guidance of six fifteen to six forty-five million. This is Angelini buying its way into the U.S. market and into brain health with three derisked franchises rather than running an originator pipeline. For the rare-disease M&A backdrop, this is the third multibillion-dollar buyout in six weeks targeting commercial-stage neurology assets, which says something about how strategics are pricing the next decade of orphan-drug pricing pressure relative to discovery risk.

Number three — Sarepta reported Q1 with Elevidys posting one hundred two million in net product revenue against a Wall Street consensus of about ninety-five million, and total net product revenue of three hundred thirty-one million also beating estimates. Full-year guidance was maintained at one-point-two to one-point-four billion. The stock dropped ten percent anyway, closing at twenty dollars sixty cents. The disconnect is the trend, not the level — Elevidys sales have now declined quarter-over-quarter consistently since the FDA restricted use following the patient deaths last year. RBC's Brian Abrahams said in his note the right move for investors is to redirect attention to Sarepta's early-stage RNA pipeline rather than the gene therapy franchise. The structural read-through for Calibr — gene therapy commercialization at scale is now visibly hard even for the platform with first-mover advantage and an established DMD patient pipeline. The pricing and durability promises have to survive a real-world safety signal and an FDA restriction, and those two things together can compound faster than the original launch curve.

Number four — Australian plasma giant CSL crashed to a nine-year low Monday after announcing a five-billion-dollar non-cash write-down and a roughly four-percent cut to full-year revenue guidance. The write-down hits a meaningful chunk of the goodwill that has accumulated across CSL's serial acquisitions over the past decade. For the immunoglobulin and plasma-fractionation supply chain — which underpins a long list of approved biologics and a longer list of pipeline assets — this is the first major financial signal that the Ig category is not the unstoppable growth franchise it was modeled as in 2022. Worth watching whether the demand softness is driven by FcRn antagonists like argenx's Vyvgart eating into chronic Ig use, or by something more cyclical on the supply side.

Number five — Eli Lilly committed another four-point-five billion dollars to Indiana manufacturing expansion on Monday, on top of the cumulative thirty-something billion the company has already announced for U.S. capacity since 2020. The bulk of this is GLP-one capacity for tirzepatide — both Mounjaro and Zepbound — and the upcoming oral orforglipron launch. Read this alongside today's spotlight. Lilly is building the biggest small-molecule and biologic peptide-manufacturing footprint in the industry at the same moment that a small biotech in Burlington Massachusetts is starting first-in-human work on a one-time pancreatic gene therapy that, if it works, makes chronic GLP-one injection volume irrelevant. The bet Lilly is placing is that the manufacturing moat survives the entire next decade of competitive pressure including any gene-therapy attack. That bet has not historically lost — but the asymmetry is interesting.

Number six — and a quick close on the gene-therapy beat going into ASGCT. Astellas confirmed Monday that it is restarting clinical development of its X-linked myotubular myopathy gene therapy program, five years after four pediatric deaths in the original Audentes AT-one-three-two trial halted the program and triggered an industry-wide reassessment of high-dose AAV safety. The restart uses a lower dose and revised eligibility criteria. Separately, AbCellera disclosed its first clinical readout from internally developed ABCL-six-three-five, a Phase 1 menopause asset, on its Q1 earnings call this morning — modest interim safety, PK, and target engagement, but a real platform milestone for the AI-and-antibody discovery shop's pivot from partnered programs to wholly-owned clinical-stage assets.
