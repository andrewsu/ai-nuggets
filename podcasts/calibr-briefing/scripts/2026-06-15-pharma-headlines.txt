Good morning. It's Monday, June fifteenth, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. The European Hematology Association annual meeting in Stockholm closed yesterday, and the late-breaking session deposited four readouts onto the Monday news cycle that will shape blood-cancer practice across the second half of the year. Today's spotlight unpacks the Karyopharm-and-Menarini SENTRY Phase three data — the first combo regimen to double frontline myelofibrosis spleen-volume response over ruxolitinib monotherapy in a fifteen-year-old treatment paradigm, plus an early overall-survival signal. Five items in the roundup.

First, the SENTRY readout itself. Three hundred fifty-three treatment-naive myelofibrosis patients were randomized to ruxolitinib alone — the JAK-one-and-JAK-two inhibitor that has been the frontline standard since twenty eleven — or to ruxolitinib plus sixty milligrams of selinexor, the oral nuclear-export protein XPO-one inhibitor that Karyopharm markets as Xpovio in the U-S and that Menarini markets as Nexpovio in Europe. The combination met the first co-primary endpoint, doubling the proportion of patients achieving at least a thirty-five-percent spleen-volume reduction at week twenty-four — about fifty percent on the combination versus twenty-eight percent on ruxolitinib alone. The absolute-symptom-score co-primary endpoint was not met — both arms showed comparable improvements. The pre-specified overall-survival secondary endpoint produced a hazard ratio of zero-point-four-three — more than a fifty-percent reduction in the risk of death — though the data are still immature. Add this to Sunday's Lilly-Ajax Type-Two-JAK-two readout in second-line myelofibrosis and the entire myelofibrosis treatment paradigm is now restructuring across both frontline and salvage settings. Today's spotlight in a few hours takes the SENTRY data apart, walks through what XPO-one as a nuclear-export target actually does in myeloproliferative biology, maps the broader frontline-myelofibrosis competitive landscape, and reads through to Calibr's small-molecule combination-discovery work.

Second, Kura Oncology and Kyowa Kirin's KOMET-zero-zero-seven update in frontline acute myeloid leukemia, also presented Sunday in Stockholm. Ninety-nine newly-diagnosed patients with NPM-one-mutant or KMT-two-A-rearranged AML were treated with the oral menin inhibitor ziftomenib — already FDA-approved as KOMZIFTI for relapsed-or-refractory NPM-one-mutant AML — combined with the standard seven-plus-three induction-chemotherapy regimen. Composite complete-response rates ranged from ninety to ninety-six percent across the molecular subgroups, with minimal-residual-disease negativity exceeding eighty percent. This is one of the largest published datasets for any menin inhibitor combined with intensive induction in frontline AML, and it sets up the randomized KOMET-zero-one-seven Phase three readout as the next anchor for the molecularly-defined-AML category that Syndax's Revuforj and Kura's KOMZIFTI have built over the past two years.

Third, a clean head-to-head Phase three loss in frontline FLT-three-mutated AML. The seven-hundred-sixty-eight-patient HOVON-one-five-six trial — sometimes referred to as PrE-zero-nine-zero-five — randomized newly-diagnosed FLT-three-mutated AML patients to induction-and-consolidation therapy plus either Astellas's second-generation FLT-three inhibitor Xospata, gilteritinib, or Novartis's first-generation FLT-three inhibitor Rydapt, midostaurin. The hazard ratio for overall survival came in at one-point-zero-two — flat. The primary endpoint missed. Event-free and relapse-free survival did trend in gilteritinib's favor and post-relapse therapy may have confounded the survival readout, but the headline for the field is that second-generation FLT-three inhibition does not displace midostaurin in the frontline on the survival metric that matters most. This is the first randomized head-to-head between any pair of FLT-three inhibitors in newly-diagnosed AML and the result reshapes how labs are going to think about FLT-three inhibitor sequencing in the chemotherapy backbone.

Fourth, an early signal that CAR-T can extend into refractory autoimmune cytopenia. A pilot study presented Sunday at EHA reported on eight patients with multi-refractory primary immune thrombocytopenia treated with an ex-vivo BCMA-directed CAR-T cell product. Four evaluable patients achieved complete platelet responses with no severe adverse events at first read, though one patient relapsed at nine months. The biological logic is direct — autoreactive antibody-producing plasma cells express BCMA, and depleting them with a BCMA CAR-T deletes the disease driver in immune thrombocytopenia the same way it deletes the malignant plasma cells in multiple myeloma. Add this to the lupus, myositis, and stiff-person-syndrome CAR-T readouts of the past eighteen months and the autoimmune-CAR-T thesis — that you can treat severe autoreactive-B-cell-mediated disease with the same engineered effectors that treat hematologic malignancy — has moved from speculation into early clinical demonstration across a widening set of indications.
