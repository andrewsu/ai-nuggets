Spotlight, Monday July sixth. Novartis this morning announced agreement to acquire Myricx Bio for up to one-point-five billion dollars — one-point-one billion up front in cash plus up to four hundred million in milestones — for a preclinical antibody-drug-conjugate pipeline built on a novel N-myristoyltransferase inhibitor payload class. Two lead assets, both preclinical, targeting B-seven-H-three and her-two. Deal closes in the second half of twenty twenty-six. This is the biggest single-billion-dollar bet on a non-topoisomerase-one payload from any large pharma to date, and it is Novartis's largest step into A-D-Cs. Six threads.

First, why the deal matters at all — Novartis has been the A-D-C laggard among big pharma. Merck did the twenty-two-billion-dollar Daiichi Sankyo deal in twenty twenty-three for three A-D-Cs — patritumab deruxtecan, ifinatamab deruxtecan, raludotatug deruxtecan — all built on Daiichi's DXd topoisomerase-one payload platform. AstraZeneca has been the Daiichi partner for Enhertu — trastuzumab deruxtecan, the DXd-payload her-two A-D-C that redefined the class — since twenty nineteen and is now sharing the same DXd payload across multiple next-in-line targets. Pfizer bought Seagen for forty-three billion dollars in twenty twenty-three to own the Padcev-and-Adcetris franchise. AbbVie paid ten-point-one billion for ImmunoGen and Elahere. Bristol Myers paid eight-hundred million up front and up to eight-point-four billion in milestones for the SystImmune bispecific-A-D-C franchise in early twenty twenty-four. Every large pharma has now made a multi-billion-dollar A-D-C move — except Novartis. Novartis's cancer franchise has been Kisqali on the C-D-K-four-slash-six side, Pluvicto on the radioligand side, and next-in-line small-molecule programs including the Antares Therapeutics undruggable-cancer discovery pact signed in late June. Nothing on A-D-Cs at scale. This morning changes that. Novartis is entering the A-D-C category with a payload nobody else has — not the DXd class, not the maytansine class, not the auristatin class. That is the specific bet.

Second, the payload — what N-M-T-i actually is and why it is a novel A-D-C-payload mechanism. N-myristoyltransferase — N-M-T — is an enzyme that adds a fourteen-carbon fatty acid, myristic acid, to the N-terminus of a specific set of proteins. Roughly one percent of the human proteome — about one hundred fifty proteins — undergoes this modification, catalyzed by two isozymes, N-M-T-one and N-M-T-two. The myristoyl modification anchors these proteins to membranes and is required for their function. The substrate list includes S-R-C-family kinases, A-R-F GTPases, calcineurin subunits, cyclic-A-M-P-dependent protein kinase catalytic subunit, and a broader set of signaling nodes central to cancer cell survival. Blocking N-M-T with a small-molecule inhibitor blocks the myristoylation of dozens of critical proteins at once — the downstream effects are on vesicle trafficking, growth-factor signaling, mitochondrial biogenesis, and cancer-cell metabolism. Myricx's founders — Professor Ed Tate at Imperial College London and the Francis Crick Institute, Roberto Solari, and Andrew Bell — spent roughly a decade developing selective N-M-T inhibitor chemistry as a systemic small molecule before recognizing that the potency and mechanism-of-action profile made it a much better fit as an A-D-C payload than as an oral drug. As a systemic drug, blocking myristoylation of one hundred fifty human proteins is too promiscuous — the therapeutic index is narrow. As an A-D-C payload conjugated to a tumor-selective antibody, the same broad mechanistic footprint becomes a feature — the antibody delivers the payload only to tumor cells, so the polypharmacology of N-M-T inhibition is what makes tumor cells more sensitive than normal cells. This is a payload-class thesis distinct from the payload classes that have driven the A-D-C category to date.

Third, the read on the DXd resistance problem — why Novartis specifically needs a non-topoisomerase-one payload. Enhertu — trastuzumab deruxtecan, D-S-eighty-two-oh-one — is the reference-case A-D-C. It uses a topoisomerase-one inhibitor, deruxtecan or DXd, an exatecan derivative, conjugated at a drug-to-antibody ratio of about eight via a cleavable tetrapeptide linker. Enhertu has been a category-defining product — it moved into her-two-low breast cancer, has been extending into gastric, biliary, lung, and now multiple pan-tumor her-two-mutation settings. But resistance is real and well-characterized. Two mechanisms in the literature — first, reduction of topoisomerase-one expression, which directly reduces payload target availability, and second, induction of sustained cellular senescence, in which tumor cells enter a non-proliferative state that resists further DXd-mediated D-N-A damage. Sustained senescence is a growing problem because it also underlies the "cold-tumor" phenotype that pushes back against immunotherapy re-challenge. A payload that could kill senescent cells — a senolytic mechanism — solves the second resistance path. Myricx's preclinical data presented at A-A-C-R showed dual senolytic-plus-cytotoxic activity for N-M-T-i-A-D-Cs in xenograft models — the payload kills proliferating cancer cells cytotoxically and clears senescent tumor cells by senolysis. That is a differentiated resistance profile against Enhertu-class payloads. For a Novartis coming late to A-D-Cs, buying into a payload that specifically addresses the primary emerging resistance mechanism of the category-defining product is a coherent strategic entry rather than a me-too move.

Fourth, the preclinical evidence Novartis is paying for. Myricx has presented at A-A-C-R positive preclinical proof-of-concept data on N-M-T-i-A-D-Cs targeting three clinically-validated tumor-associated antigens — B-seven-H-three, T-R-O-P-two, and her-two. Complete tumor regressions across a range of solid-tumor xenograft models. Tolerability profile described as clean at effective doses. Broad activity across multiple cancer cell types. The two clinical-development priorities being carried forward are the B-seven-H-three-directed A-D-C and the her-two-directed A-D-C. B-seven-H-three is an attractive target because it is broadly expressed across solid tumors — small-cell lung cancer, prostate, glioma, head-and-neck — and there are competing B-seven-H-three A-D-Cs in development from Daiichi Sankyo (ifinatamab deruxtecan) and MacroGenics. Myricx's B-seven-H-three-N-M-T-i program is preclinical, so the read-across from the Daiichi ifinatamab clinical data is that the target and antigen expression are real; the question is whether the N-M-T-i payload delivers the same or better efficacy and tolerability. Her-two is the reference target — Enhertu owns the class. Myricx's her-two-N-M-T-i-A-D-C enters against a fortified incumbent, and the differentiation argument is entirely on resistance and tolerability, not access to the target. Neither program has entered the clinic yet. First-in-human trials would be expected in twenty twenty-seven or twenty twenty-eight.

Fifth, valuation and return arithmetic. Myricx raised roughly one hundred fourteen million dollars in a Series A in July twenty twenty-four — co-led by Novo Holdings and Abingworth, joined by Brandon Capital Partners, Sofinnova Partners, British Patient Capital, Cancer Research Horizons, and Eli Lilly Ventures. The company was founded in twenty nineteen with early Cancer Research U-K support and seed financing from Brandon and Sofinnova. Two years after the Series A close, on a still-preclinical asset with no clinical trials yet initiated, the company gets acquired for up to one-point-five billion dollars — one-point-one billion up front. That is roughly a thirteen-times return on Series-A capital in twenty-four months on the up-front alone, before milestones, on a preclinical company. The one-point-one billion up front for two preclinical A-D-C programs plus a payload platform breaks down to approximately five-hundred-fifty million per lead program with the platform value on top. Compare that to the Daiichi-Merck deal — twenty-two billion dollars for three clinical-stage A-D-Cs, roughly seven-and-a-half billion per program. Or to AbbVie-ImmunoGen — ten-point-one billion for one commercial-stage product plus pipeline. The Myricx deal is priced consistent with preclinical single-billion-dollar A-D-C-platform deals like Bristol-SystImmune (eight hundred million up front on a preclinical bispecific-A-D-C platform), but at a higher up-front. The read on the market for preclinical A-D-C platforms is that pharma is paying real up-front money — one billion range — for a differentiated payload class before clinical validation. Novo Holdings, Abingworth, Brandon, Sofinnova, and Eli Lilly Ventures all realized outstanding returns.

Sixth, the read for Foresite, Calibr, and the wider A-D-C-payload category. First, the deal validates the "new-payload-class" thesis broadly. For every A-I-and-structure-guided drug discovery platform with an oncology-payload angle, and for every small-molecule oncology shop that could reposition a program into a payload role, there is now a reference-case pharma-valuation for what a differentiated payload with preclinical proof-of-concept commands — one billion up front. That directly reprices the A-D-C-payload-platform opportunity for platforms like Sutro Biopharma, Immunogen legacy franchises, Ambrx, and adjacent programs. Second, the Calibr small-molecule oncology franchise shares the structural-design discipline that produced N-M-T-i as a payload candidate — the same medicinal-chemistry playbook that identified N-M-T as a druggable target and then converted the compound into an A-D-C payload. The template for extracting more value from a small molecule than as a stand-alone oral is now well-established — myristoylation is one substrate; there are others in the covalent, kinase, and transcription-factor programs that could follow the same path. Third, for A-I-native drug-discovery peers — Xaira, Iambic, Isomorphic Labs, Insilico, Terray, Absci, and the broader substrate camp — the read is that A-D-C payload chemistry is a specific vertical where the value inflection is early, the exit multiples are ten-to-fifteen-x on Series-A capital in two years, and the payload-class innovation problem is chemistry-and-mechanism-heavy in exactly the way that structure-guided-plus-generative-model workflows are supposed to accelerate. Fourth, for large pharma A-D-C laggards — think Bayer, Takeda, Amgen — Novartis just set the precedent for a preclinical single-billion-dollar catch-up move. Expect follow-on deals in that mode. And fifth, the strategic-continuity point on Novartis specifically. Fiona Marshall — Novartis President of Biomedical Research — took over that role after Jay Bradner. Novartis oncology under Marshall is now built on four pillars — the Kisqali C-D-K-four-slash-six franchise with Vijoice on the mutant-P-I-three-K-alpha side, the Pluvicto radioligand franchise plus actinium next-gen, small-molecule undruggable oncology via the Antares Therapeutics one-point-nine-billion-dollar total-deal-value discovery pact from June, and now Myricx N-M-T-i A-D-Cs. That is a full-spectrum oncology-substrate strategy — targeted small molecule, radioligand, undruggable small molecule, A-D-C — coming from a company that eighteen months ago was widely viewed as under-invested in oncology.

Bottom line. Novartis paid one-point-one billion up front and up to four hundred million in milestones to acquire Myricx Bio and its N-M-T-i-A-D-C platform — the first single-billion-dollar bet on a novel non-topoisomerase-one payload class from any large pharma. Deal ends Novartis's tenure as the A-D-C laggard and buys into a payload with a differentiated resistance profile against the emerging Enhertu-DXd-topoisomerase-one resistance problem via dual cytotoxic-plus-senolytic mechanism. Two preclinical lead assets — B-seven-H-three and her-two — enter Novartis's oncology pipeline with first-in-human expected twenty twenty-seven to twenty twenty-eight. Return arithmetic — roughly thirteen-times on Series-A capital in twenty-four months, still preclinical — validates a rich pharma-valuation market for A-D-C payload platforms at the preclinical stage. For Foresite and Calibr, the read is that payload-class innovation is a category where pharma is paying real up-front dollars pre-clinic — worth mapping across portfolio programs and A-I-native peer programs where payload angles exist. Next catalysts. One, closing in the second half of twenty twenty-six subject to customary conditions. Two, Myricx N-M-T-i-A-D-C first-in-human filings, plausibly late twenty twenty-seven. Three, follow-on preclinical A-D-C-platform deals from Bayer, Takeda, Amgen or other A-D-C laggards on the same one-billion-up-front template. Four, Enhertu-resistance clinical data — post-progression cohorts in her-two-low breast cancer that will define the market opportunity for a non-topoisomerase-one payload. And five, A-A-C-R twenty twenty-seven — the venue where the next N-M-T-i-A-D-C preclinical dataset will land and where competing payload classes (topoisomerase-two, R-N-A-polymerase, targeted-radionuclide payloads) will position against N-M-T-i.
