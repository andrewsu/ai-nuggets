Good morning. It's Tuesday, June 2nd, 2026, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. ASCO closes today in Chicago. The plenary readouts from Sunday have been digested, two new datasets land at oral sessions this morning, and one ulcerative colitis Phase 3 readout outside the meeting has reset an entire IBD asset's valuation overnight. Here's what's on the table.

First, the Abivax story. Yesterday afternoon, the French biotech reported full results from the Phase 3 ABTECT maintenance trial of obefazimod, its first-in-class oral miR-124 enhancer in moderately to severely active ulcerative colitis. On efficacy, the readout was the cleanest the field has seen. At forty-four weeks, clinical remission was fifty-point-eight percent on the twenty-five milligram dose, fifty-one-point-three percent on fifty milligrams, against ten-point-four percent on placebo. That is a placebo-adjusted remission delta around forty percentage points, the highest in any long-term ulcerative colitis maintenance trial on record. All key secondary endpoints hit. Despite that, the stock fell more than thirty percent in post-market trading because the safety appendix included several cases of malignancy among treated patients. Abivax's own framing was that no new safety signals were observed and the events were considered unrelated to drug by investigators, with no organ clustering. A Jefferies note flagged that even if the events prove unrelated, the overhang will be real, especially because Abivax has no other value-inflecting catalysts inside the next twelve months. NDA submission is still planned for late fourth quarter. This becomes the test case for whether a clean efficacy story can survive an ambiguous malignancy signal in an IBD label, and the answer has implications for every miR-124 modulator and JAK-adjacent program that follows.

Second, today's late-breaker at ASCO is Celcuity's VIKTORIA-1 in the PIK3CA-mutant cohort. The Phase 3 evaluates gedatolisib plus fulvestrant with or without palbociclib in hormone-receptor-positive, HER2-negative metastatic breast cancer after CDK4-and-6 inhibitor and aromatase inhibitor failure. Gedatolisib is a pan-PI3K and mTOR inhibitor, distinct from Novartis's alpelisib and Roche's inavolisib, both of which are PI3K-alpha-selective. Celcuity already announced topline back in early May confirming the primary endpoint was met with a clinically meaningful improvement in progression-free survival. Today is when the curves and the magnitude get publicly disclosed. Celcuity intends to file a supplemental NDA on this PIK3CA-mutant data, and the FDA has already granted Priority Review in the PIK3CA wild-type setting, with a PDUFA date of July seventeenth. A small-cap biotech with two indications in late-stage breast cancer review at the same time is unusual, and the size of today's PFS delta determines whether gedatolisib becomes a meaningful piece of the post-CDK4-and-6 inhibitor landscape or whether the wild-type label carries the franchise alone.

Third, the plenary numbers from Sunday are now public, and HARMONi-6 cleared the bar. Akeso and Summit's ivonescimab plus chemotherapy delivered a median overall survival of twenty-seven-point-nine months against twenty-three-point-seven months for tislelizumab plus chemotherapy in first-line squamous lung cancer. That is roughly a four-month median survival benefit on top of the previously reported forty percent reduction in progression risk. Summit is now expected to take this to the FDA in the second half of this year. The two open questions, geographic generalizability of an entirely Chinese trial and the still-pending HARMONi-3 readout in nonsquamous, are not resolved by yesterday's data, but the OS magnitude is large enough that those questions become regulatory cosmetics rather than gating concerns. PROTEUS and LIBRETTO-432 also delivered. Johnson and Johnson's apalutamide significantly improved pathological response in high-risk localized prostate cancer in the perioperative setting, and Lilly's selpercatinib hit on event-free survival as adjuvant therapy in resected RET fusion-positive lung cancer.

Fourth, in vivo CAR-T. Yesterday at ASCO, Kelonia presented an updated Phase 1 dataset for KLN-1010, its lentivirally delivered in vivo BCMA CAR-T for relapsed and refractory multiple myeloma. Eighteen patients dosed, one hundred percent overall response rate, all evaluable patients minimal residual disease negative at one month, and the first patient remains in deep ongoing response beyond ten months. No lymphodepleting chemotherapy required. Sixteen of eighteen had cytokine release syndrome, all grade one or two; neurotoxicity was limited to one grade one and one short grade three event. This is the most mature in vivo CAR-T dataset in oncology to date, and we go deep on it in the spotlight episode.

Fifth, a quick licensing note. Eli Lilly took a Phase 2-stage GLP-2 agonist from Korea's Hanmi Pharm late last week. The mechanism is intestinotrophic rather than glycemic; the target indication is short bowel syndrome, where Takeda's teduglutide currently owns the market. Lilly is using the metabolic franchise it built around tirzepatide and retatrutide to bolt on adjacencies, and GLP-2 fits the gut hormone receptor superfamily story that ties the deal back to the company's existing biology.

Sixth, deal-cap watch. The Pfizer and Innovent oncology pact from last Thursday remains the largest single Chinese-IP licensing event of the year by headline value, north of twenty billion dollars including milestones, and the HARMONi-6 reception now provides the validation context that other large-cap pharmas were waiting for before committing to similar deals.
