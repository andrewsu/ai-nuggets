Good morning, Travis. Friday July tenth, twenty twenty-six. Today's roundup is dominated by Ionis Pharmaceuticals' worst day of the year — two separate program failures back to back on the same afternoon. First, AstraZeneca and Ionis announced that CARDIO-TTRansform, the fourteen-hundred-thirty-two-patient Phase 3 of Wainua — eplontersen, the T-T-R antisense oligonucleotide — failed its primary endpoint in transthyretin cardiomyopathy, sending Ionis down more than twenty percent and AstraZeneca down eight percent, while Alnylam and BridgeBio each surged double digits. That's the spotlight in a few hours. Second, Roche informed patient groups it is discontinuing both tominersen and R-G six-four-nine-six, the two Ionis-partnered Huntington's disease H-T-T-lowering programs, on the same day the Wainua data broke. Third, GSK terminated its two-point-two-billion-dollar immuno-neurology alliance with Alector — both lead assets have now failed. Fourth, A-R-P-A-H launched THRIVE, a hundred-sixty-million-dollar program funding seven teams to industrialize personalized CRISPR gene editing for rare disease, with GEMMABio and Profluent Bio picked for the A-I-native editor track. And fifth, Ipsen's Dysport delivered dual Phase 3 wins in both episodic and chronic migraine — a first for the botulinum-toxin class. Five items.

First, the Wainua CARDIO-TTRansform Phase 3 failure. Yesterday afternoon AstraZeneca and Ionis announced that CARDIO-TTRansform — the pivotal Phase 3 in adults with transthyretin amyloid cardiomyopathy — did not meet its primary endpoint of the composite of cardiovascular mortality and recurrent cardiovascular clinical events at week one-hundred-forty. Fourteen-hundred-thirty-two patients, over one-hundred-thirty sites, twenty countries. Eplontersen is a G-a-l-N-A-c-conjugated antisense oligonucleotide targeting T-T-R messenger R-N-A — the same silencer-mechanism class as Alnylam's Amvuttra vutrisiran, which succeeded in the HELIOS-B pivotal eighteen months ago. The subgroup story is what matters. In the roughly forty-three percent of patients who were not on a T-T-R stabilizer at baseline, eplontersen delivered a twenty-nine-percent relative-risk reduction on the composite endpoint — nominally significant. In the fifty-seven percent of patients who were on a stabilizer at baseline, no treatment effect. And during the trial, another twenty-four percent of patients added stabilizer on top, so by the end of one-hundred-forty weeks more than eighty percent of the CARDIO-TTRansform cohort was on background stabilizer therapy. Alnylam's HELIOS-B, which succeeded, had a materially lower fifty-three-percent stabilizer background. Market reaction was fierce. Ionis closed down more than twenty percent — one of its worst single days ever — at about sixty-four dollars and change. AstraZeneca lost eight percent. Alnylam surged roughly eighteen percent — Raymond James raised its price target from four-hundred-twenty to four-hundred-sixty-eight dollars, and Stifel's Paul Matteis said Amvuttra is now the only drug of its kind. BridgeBio closed up sixteen percent — B-of-A called it a big surprise for the stabilizer class — Attruby, acoramidis, closed twenty-twenty-five U-S sales at three-hundred-sixty-two million dollars and Q-one twenty-twenty-six at one-hundred-eighty-one million. Jefferies' Michael Leuchten framed the failure as a credibility loss for AstraZeneca on trial-design assumptions. Full spotlight in a few hours on the silencer-versus-stabilizer mechanism intuition, why the eighty-percent-stabilizer background plausibly diluted the eplontersen signal, and what it means for the silencer-class pipeline.

Second, Roche discontinues both Ionis-partnered Huntington's programs the same day. In a letter to patient groups yesterday, Roche said it is stopping tominersen — the older non-selective H-T-T-lowering antisense oligonucleotide — after the GENERATION-H-D-two Phase 2 in younger, earlier-stage patients missed its efficacy objective on functional, cognitive, and physical endpoints, despite a significant biomarker effect on target engagement. And separately, Roche is halting the Point-H-D Phase 1 of R-G six-four-nine-six — the next-generation selective mutant-H-T-T-lowering A-S-O — after only three patients were dosed, because animal-tox work showed the drug cannot be given chronically at repeated dose. Roche characterized the two decisions as independent and coincidental, but they hit Ionis the same afternoon Wainua failed. The read for H-T-T-lowering as a Huntington's mechanism: biomarker engagement is real, but downstream clinical benefit remains elusive — the field's three most-advanced A-S-O programs have now missed or been discontinued. UniQure's A-M-T one-thirty gene therapy is the last major clinical-stage H-T-T-lowering asset with active dosing.

Third, GSK ends the Alector immuno-neurology alliance. On July sixth GSK notified Alector it is terminating the seven-hundred-million-dollar-upfront and up-to-one-point-five-billion-dollar-milestone deal signed in July twenty twenty-one and amended in twenty twenty-three. Both lead assets have failed — latozinemab, the anti-sortilin-one antibody that elevates progranulin, missed cognitive and functional endpoints in the frontotemporal-dementia-progranulin Phase 3 last October, and nivisnebart, the follow-on anti-sortilin antibody for early Alzheimer's, hit a Phase 2 futility interim in April this year. Termination effective January second twenty twenty-seven — one-hundred-eighty-day notice. Alector stock is down about ninety-five percent over five years, closing at one dollar seventy after another seven-percent decline on the news. The read on immuno-neurology broadly — the progranulin-microglia-T-R-E-M-two axis was one of the cleanest translational-neuroscience mechanistic stories in the last decade, and its two most-advanced clinical assets have now failed on hard endpoints. Combined with the Huntington's A-S-O scrap this week, July eighth through tenth is one of the worst three-day windows for neuroscience-first biotech in recent memory.

Fourth, A-R-P-A-H THRIVE launches with a hundred-sixty-million-dollar program for personalized gene editing. Yesterday A-R-P-A-H announced up to one-hundred-sixty million dollars across seven research teams to industrialize N-of-one CRISPR therapy — the platform that made Baby K-J's individualized base-editing rescue possible last year. Each team must enter clinical trials by year three. Recipients include Children's Hospital of Philadelphia on rare metabolic disease, U-C Berkeley and the Innovative Genomics Institute on immune disorders, Saint Jude on bone-marrow indications, the Broad Institute on pediatric epilepsy and central-nervous-system disease, GEMMABio in partnership with Profluent Bio on A-I-designed editors for genetic heart disease, Mass General on rare vascular disease, and Stanford on rare skin disease. The read for the Foresite and Calibr audience — Profluent Bio's participation is the tell. This is federal money staking a public bet on A-I-native gene-editor design as the industrialization path for personalized CRISPR. That is directly adjacent to the platform theses at Xaira, Iambic, and Isomorphic, and it validates a fundable model for smaller A-I-native genetic-medicine startups building custom-CRISPR-per-patient as a business, not a research project.

Fifth, Ipsen Dysport delivers dual Phase 3 wins in migraine. Yesterday Ipsen reported that the BEOND Phase 3 program — E-BEOND in episodic migraine and C-BEOND in chronic migraine — hit primary endpoints in both indications on monthly migraine day reduction. This is the first time a botulinum toxin has succeeded in episodic migraine. AbbVie's Botox has been chronic-migraine-only since twenty-ten. The read is competitive. Dysport now has plausible best-in-class breadth-of-label for the botulinum-toxin class in migraine, expanding the addressable population beyond chronic-only Botox. Safety was consistent with Dysport's established profile.
