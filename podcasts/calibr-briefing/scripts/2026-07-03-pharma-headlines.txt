Good morning, Travis. Friday July third, twenty twenty-six. Today's roundup leads with Roche's divarasib beating Amgen's Lumakras and Bristol Myers Squibb's Krazati head-to-head in Phase three previously-treated K-RAS G-twelve-C non-small-cell lung cancer — the first prospective randomized head-to-head win against the two approved K-RAS G-twelve-C inhibitors, and that's the spotlight in a few hours. Then Insilico Medicine signs a strategic collaboration with Takeda potentially worth six hundred million dollars for A-I-driven drug discovery across Takeda's therapeutic areas. Then United Therapeutics acquires preclinical Thymmune Therapeutics for one hundred forty million dollars upfront and up to one hundred sixty million in milestones for i-P-S-C-derived thymic cell therapies. Then PureTech Health spins out Celea Therapeutics with a one-hundred-eighty-million-dollar Series Seed for a deuterated pirfenidone that will run the first-ever head-to-head Phase three superiority trial in idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis. And Merck kills the M-K-eleven-sixty-seven Phase two Alzheimer's study from partner Neuphoria after interim futility on the alpha-seven nicotinic acetylcholine receptor mechanism. Five items.

First, Roche's divarasib. Yesterday morning Roche disclosed Phase three Krascendo one data — three hundred thirty-eight previously-treated patients with K-RAS G-twelve-C non-small-cell lung cancer, randomized to divarasib versus Amgen's sotorasib, branded Lumakras, or Bristol Myers Squibb's adagrasib, branded Krazati. Primary endpoint blinded-independent-central-review progression-free survival — hit with statistical significance. Key secondary endpoint overall survival — also hit with statistical significance in what Roche described as a poor-prognosis patient population. Full numbers withheld until a future oncology congress. Safety profile called consistent with prior divarasib data, no new signals. Two reads. One, this is the first prospective randomized head-to-head win of any second-generation K-RAS G-twelve-C inhibitor against both approved first-generation drugs simultaneously — the field has been arguing for two years that second-generation compounds offering greater potency and selectivity would establish a new standard of care, and Krascendo one is the first hard data point. Lumakras did roughly three hundred sixty-seven million dollars in twenty twenty-five with low single-digit growth; Krazati did about two hundred five million dollars but growing sixty-two percent. Both franchises are constrained by narrow patient populations, resistance, reimbursement, and testing access — divarasib enters an underpenetrated class as the new standard-of-care candidate. Jefferies analyst Michael Leuchten called it a small win but premature to claim overall victory — the real prize is first-line, and that reads out in Krascendo two, which combines divarasib with Keytruda. Roche pegs first-line as a five-billion-Swiss-franc opportunity versus one to two billion in second-line. Two, for the A-I-in-precision-oncology thesis, divarasib is not an A-I asset per se, but it exemplifies why K-RAS remains the paradigmatic case for structure-guided design — the second-generation compound is the payoff for a decade of medicinal chemistry investment in the K-RAS G-twelve-C pocket, and it validates the case that A-I-driven design layered on that same structural knowledge can accelerate the next wave. Deep dive in a few hours.

Second, Insilico Medicine plus Takeda. Yesterday Insilico announced a strategic collaboration with Takeda potentially worth six hundred million dollars — roughly sixty million upfront in project initiation fees and near-term milestones, plus up to five hundred forty million more in preclinical, clinical, commercial, and sales milestones, plus tiered royalties. Insilico will lead A-I-driven discovery on the Pharma.AI platform to identify molecules meeting predefined scientific and early-development criteria; Takeda takes selected candidates into clinical development with exclusive worldwide rights. Two reads. One, Insilico is running an aggressive partnership stack — with this deal Insilico's total new contract value in twenty twenty-six is approaching six-and-a-half billion dollars in aggregate consideration across Lilly, Servier, S-K Biopharmaceuticals, and now Takeda. That is a specific answer to the question from yesterday's spotlight — namely, what will pharma actually pay for A-I-driven discovery output — and the answer is that Insilico has built a scalable multi-pharma template that other A-I-native shops have not yet demonstrated at this cadence. Two, for Foresite's Xaira flagship and the broader A-I-in-drug-discovery peer set, the Insilico model is a directly relevant reference for how the platform layer monetizes without going direct into drug development. Insilico is not building its own commercial infrastructure; it is layering deals with global pharma buyers on a repeatable Pharma.AI-in, licensed-out-molecule template. That is the substrate-consumer model applied at the platform level, and it is one of the responses available to platform companies confronting the foundation-model-lab entry we discussed yesterday.

Third, United Therapeutics buys Thymmune. Yesterday United Therapeutics announced acquisition of Thymmune Therapeutics for one hundred forty million dollars upfront plus up to one hundred sixty million dollars in milestones — three hundred million dollars aggregate for a preclinical-stage regenerative medicine company. Thymmune's core asset is a proprietary process for converting human induced-pluripotent stem cells into thymic epithelial cells that mature in vivo into a functional thymus. Lead candidate T-H-Y-one-hundred is in preclinical development for congenital athymia — an ultra-rare pediatric condition in which infants are born without a functional thymus and cannot generate T cells — with animal data showing i-P-S-C-derived cells reconstituting a neo-thymus capable of supporting T-cell development in vivo. Two reads. One, this fits inside United Therapeutics' regenerative-transplant strategy — the company's xenotransplantation program and its i-P-S-C-organ-generation ambitions look for platform assets that expand the pipeline beyond the pulmonary hypertension franchise. Three hundred million dollars for a preclinical i-P-S-C-thymic-cell platform is a modest bet on i-P-S-C-derived regenerative medicine as a category. Two, for the regenerative T-cell and cell-therapy field the read complements yesterday's Orca Bio Tregzi approval — that was donor-derived precision T-cell manufacturing, this is de novo T-cell reconstitution from an in-vivo-generated thymus. Two very different mechanisms attacking overlapping T-cell-restoration goals. Congenital athymia is not a Calibr indication but the platform is worth flagging for Foresite's regenerative-medicine coverage.

Fourth, Celea Therapeutics. PureTech Health spun out Celea yesterday with a one-hundred-eighty-million-dollar Series Seed Preferred financing — thirty million from PureTech and one hundred fifty million from R-A Capital Management, Leaps by Bayer, a large U-S healthcare-focused fund, and a leading sovereign wealth fund. PureTech retains thirty-five-point-four percent ownership plus non-dilutive royalties, up to one hundred ninety million in milestone payments, and twenty percent of sublicense income. The lead asset is deupirfenidone — a deuterated version of Roche's pirfenidone, branded Esbriet, one of three approved antifibrotics for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis. Proceeds fund an early third-quarter twenty twenty-six start of the SURPASS-I-P-F trial — described as the first-ever head-to-head Phase three superiority trial in I-P-F, going directly against Esbriet in a superiority design rather than the usual placebo-controlled add-on. Two reads. One, PureTech is running the same asset-vehicle playbook that delivered Karuna, which Bristol Myers acquired for fourteen billion dollars in twenty twenty-four, and Seaport Therapeutics — build a preclinical asset inside PureTech, spin it out with a top-tier syndicate as a single-asset vehicle, run one late-stage trial, monetize on a licensing deal or acquisition. The one-hundred-eighty-million-dollar effective capitalization and the head-to-head Phase three design telegraph a confidence bar and an exit-path structure that specialist life-sciences investors are willing to fund at scale for the right thesis. Two, a head-to-head superiority trial against Esbriet — if it hits — reframes I-P-F as a market where fifteen years of incremental data have not moved the needle and where a deuterated form of the incumbent could actually take the standard of care. That is a bigger claim than the pipeline usually makes at this stage.

Fifth, Merck axes M-K-eleven-sixty-seven. Yesterday Merck killed the Phase two study of M-K-eleven-sixty-seven — an oral alpha-seven nicotinic acetylcholine receptor modulator partnered with Neuphoria Therapeutics — after prespecified interim analysis showed the compound did not meet efficacy thresholds in mild-to-moderate Alzheimer's dementia when paired with a standard acetylcholinesterase inhibitor. Two reads. One, alpha-seven nicotinic modulation has been a recurring symptomatic-cognition target for two decades — encenicline from Forum Pharmaceuticals failed the same population in twenty sixteen, and EnVivo's E-V-P-six-one-two-four also failed. Merck picked M-K-eleven-sixty-seven up from Neuphoria specifically on the argument that a differentiated pharmacology could resolve the prior failures. The interim futility says that pharmacology remains the wrong lever for symptomatic cognition in Alzheimer's dementia even when the compound looks best-in-class on the target. Two, for the post-amyloid mechanism-class landscape — where AlzeCure's NeuroRestore platform sold to QuantumCell for two-point-two billion dollars on Wednesday because pharma is actively bidding for non-amyloid symptomatic-cognition mechanisms — the read on alpha-seven nicotinic is now sharply negative. The neurotrophin-potentiator category is a live alternative; the alpha-seven nicotinic category, on this second Phase two failure at Merck-Neuphoria scale, is not.
