Good morning, Travis. Thursday July second, twenty twenty-six. Today's roundup leads with Anthropic launching Claude Science — an A-I workbench for scientific researchers — and simultaneously standing up its own internal preclinical drug-discovery program focused on commercially neglected diseases; the three-way foundation-model-lab race with Google and OpenAI is now direct into drug development, and that's the spotlight in a few hours. Then Ipsen buys Swiss biotech Memo Therapeutics for up to eight hundred million dollars for a first-in-class B-K polyomavirus antibody — the second Ipsen acquisition inside of a week. Then Amgen's Tavneos comes apart — the New England Journal retracted the pivotal Phase three paper, F-D-A proposed withdrawal on hepatotoxicity, and E-M-A called for market rollback in Europe. Then Orca Bio's Tregzi wins the first F-D-A approval for a regulatory-T-cell immunotherapy in allogeneic transplant. And BridgeBio closes a one-billion-dollar equity raise from Sixth Street and K-K-R to fund the Attruby launch. Five items.

First, Anthropic. On Monday in San Francisco, Anthropic launched Claude Science — an A-I workbench for scientific researchers that integrates more than sixty scientific databases across genomics, proteomics, structural biology, and cheminformatics into a single Claude-driven reasoning layer. The platform launched with anchor partners including Basecamp Research — a London-based metagenomics shop that brought its E-D-E-N metagenomic foundation model trained on roughly nine-point-eight billion protein sequences collected across more than thirty countries, about a tenfold expansion over all public sequence databases combined, and which demonstrated a ninety-seven percent success rate on designing functional peptides against W-H-O critical-priority multidrug-resistant pathogens. The University of Pennsylvania's César de la Fuente is a launch collaborator. Underneath the platform Anthropic is doing two things worth flagging. One, the company has stood up an internal preclinical drug-discovery program under new head of life sciences Eric Kauderer-Abrams, explicitly targeting diseases traditional pharma considers commercially unattractive; the argument from Anthropic is that running its own drug programs gives the company the operational understanding it needs to be a credible partner to life-sciences customers. Two, a grant program — up to thirty thousand dollars in Claude credits per project across up to fifty selected projects, with a July fifteenth application deadline, plus up to two thousand dollars in Modal compute credits per project. Three reads. One, this is the direct-to-drug-development pivot from the foundation-model labs — Google DeepMind and Isomorphic Labs have been running that play for two years, OpenAI launched GPT-Rosalind in April, and now Anthropic completes the trio. The A-I-in-drug-discovery competitive frame is no longer platform-company versus pharma — it is foundation-model lab versus platform company. Two, this hits Foresite's A-I-drug-discovery thesis in both directions — directionally negative for the pure-play A-I platform investment case at Xaira and adjacent, because the foundation-model labs are now going direct rather than only selling models and A-P-Is; directionally positive for the substrate assumption, because Claude, G-P-T, and Gemini are becoming the operating layer for scientific research, and platforms that build on top of that substrate can now assume it. Three, for Calibr the practical read is that the Claude Science grant program is a live opportunity — thirty thousand dollars in credits per accepted project, applications open through July fifteenth, on the table for Calibr researchers who want to move internal discovery projects onto the platform this year. Spotlight in a few hours.

Second, Ipsen. The Paris-based specialty pharma announced yesterday it will acquire Swiss biotech Memo Therapeutics in an agreement valued at up to seven hundred million euros — roughly eight hundred million dollars — including two hundred million euros upfront and up to five hundred million euros more in development, regulatory, and sales milestones. The primary asset is potravitug, a first-in-class anti-B-K-polyomavirus antibody in Phase two for B-K virus-associated nephropathy in kidney transplant recipients — an indication with no approved therapy today. Closing is expected in the third quarter. Two reads. One, this is Ipsen's second acquisition in a week — the company signed for Kartos Therapeutics and its Phase three navtemadlin M-D-M-two antagonist for myelofibrosis at up to one-point-seven-five billion dollars on Sunday. That is roughly two-and-a-half billion dollars in headline acquisition value across seven days from a mid-cap specialty pharma. Ipsen is executing an aggressive rare-disease-and-hematology inorganic-growth pivot in real time. Two, for Calibr and Foresite the read on rare-disease-antibody deal terms is that specialty-pharma buyers will now pay up to eight hundred million dollars in aggregate consideration for a single Phase two rare-disease antibody with a clean first-in-class positioning and no incumbent — a directionally useful comparable when pricing rare-disease-focused programs across the portfolio.

Third, Amgen's Tavneos. Two developments landed July first. One, the New England Journal of Medicine retracted the twenty-twenty-one Phase three ADVOCATE trial paper that supported Tavneos's original approval. The reason — primary endpoint assessments in nine of the trial's patients were readjudicated after the database was locked and the trial was unblinded, and that readjudication was not disclosed in the paper. Two, F-D-A proposed withdrawal of the U-S approval on hepatotoxicity grounds — seventy-six reported cases of drug-induced liver injury, including eight deaths and seven cases of vanishing bile duct syndrome. E-M-A also called for market rollback in Europe at the end of June, citing good-clinical-practice breaches in the same ADVOCATE study. Tavneos is a C-five-a-receptor-one antagonist for A-N-C-A-associated vasculitis — specifically microscopic polyangiitis and granulomatosis with polyangiitis. Two reads. One, the class implication is immediate. InflaRx's vilobelimab and izicopan — both Phase-three-ready in A-N-C-A-associated vasculitis — become the frontline candidates to backfill Tavneos in that indication if withdrawal proceeds. Vilobelimab targets C-five-a directly rather than the C-five-a receptor, and the InflaRx position is that the differentiated engagement site avoids the hepatotoxicity signal that appears to be a Tavneos-specific liability at the receptor. Watch for InflaRx capital-markets response and analyst upgrades over the next week. Two, the scientific-integrity component — post-database-lock readjudication of primary-endpoint outcomes without disclosure — is rare at N-E-J-M for a compound with an approved indication, and the F-D-A component is not routine. Neither the retraction nor the proposed withdrawal is final regulatory action — Amgen has a chance to respond — but the direction of travel is one-sided and negative.

Fourth, Orca Bio's Tregzi. F-D-A approved Tregzi on Monday as the first-and-only precision-engineered regulatory-T-cell immunotherapy for allogeneic hematopoietic stem-cell transplantation in adult patients with hematological malignancies. The approval covers matched-donor transplants with myeloablative conditioning. Pivotal data come from the PRECISION-T trial — seventy-eight percent chronic-graft-versus-host-disease-free survival at one year on Tregzi versus thirty-eight-point-four percent on standard transplant. Wholesale acquisition cost is set at four hundred twenty-eight thousand dollars. Tregzi is manufactured individually for each patient from an eight-out-of-eight H-L-A-matched donor's peripheral blood, precision-sorted into hematopoietic stem cells and T cells including regulatory T cells, then reconstituted with fixed cellular composition. F-D-A granted orphan-drug and R-M-A-T designations during development. Two reads. One, this is a first-of-mechanism approval — the regulatory-T-cell class has been in development for years without a labeled product, and Tregzi becomes the reference asset for T-reg-directed therapeutics across autoimmune and transplant indications. For Calibr, T-reg-directed programs get a valuation-and-development-path benchmark. Two, Orca Bio is now approval-stage with a commercial cell therapy, which sets up the I-P-O question — the company remains private but the natural next twelve-to-eighteen-month event is a public offering. For Foresite, if Orca reaches an I-P-O in the current biotech-listing market it will price the acute-cell-therapy category — a directional read for cell-therapy portfolio positioning.

Fifth, BridgeBio. The company closed on one billion dollars in equity financing yesterday, led by Sixth Street Partners and K-K-R, structured to fund the ongoing Attruby launch — the transthyretin stabilizer for A-T-T-R cardiomyopathy that got F-D-A approval last November — and to progress the rest of the pipeline. Sixth Street and K-K-R are patient specialty-credit and structured-equity capital rather than crossover-generalist investors, and the read is that BridgeBio is choosing patient private capital over public equity at a moment when its stock has performed well enough that a follow-on offering was available. For Foresite, the read on specialty-capital availability to commercial-stage biotechs at the billion-dollar scale is that structured-equity terms are competitive with public-market pricing right now — which validates the private-preferred-and-structured-equity strategy for later-stage portfolio companies looking to fund commercialization without a public offering.
