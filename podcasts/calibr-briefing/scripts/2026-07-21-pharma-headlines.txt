The pitch is roughly ten times the performance per unit of energy versus the prior generation, which lets Bristol Myers scale up its own proprietary AI models to compress discovery timelines across oncology, immunology, cardiovascular, and neuroscience. Third, a deal that's squarely in your wheelhouse on drug delivery. Halozyme signed a global collaboration and license agreement with Incyte to develop subcutaneous, under-the-skin, formulations of Incyte's mutant-calreticulin antibody using Halozyme's ENHANZE technology. That antibody is a first-in-class program aimed at myeloproliferative neoplasms, a group of bone-marrow cancers, in patients whose disease is driven by mutations in the calreticulin gene. ENHANZE is the enzyme platform that lets you push large-volume antibody doses under the skin instead of into a vein, turning an infusion into a quick injection. Halozyme gets an upfront payment plus milestones and royalties, and Incyte gets an option on two more targets. This is the same playbook that turned intravenous blockbusters like Darzalex into subcutaneous franchises, and it's a reminder that convenience of delivery is now a real axis of competition, not an afterthought.

Fourth, on the capital-markets front, Latigo Biotherapeutics is heading for a Nasdaq listing. Latigo is a Thousand Oaks company developing non-opioid pain medicines that block a sodium channel called Nav one-point-eight, which carries pain signals in peripheral nerves. This is the same target Vertex validated when its drug in that class was approved. Latigo's lead candidate met its primary endpoint in a three-hundred-forty-three-patient study after abdominoplasty surgery, showing rapid onset and an opioid-sparing effect, and it has FDA Fast Track designation. A second, next-generation molecule is in a Phase 2 study in knee osteoarthritis with data expected in the second half of twenty twenty-seven. The read here is that the non-opioid pain category now has enough clinical validation that the public markets are willing to fund a second-mover.

The FDA accepted Shionogi's supplemental application for cefiderocol, its siderophore antibiotic that smuggles itself into resistant Gram-negative bacteria by hijacking their iron-uptake machinery. The expansion would cover children with hospital-acquired and ventilator-associated pneumonia and complicated urinary tract infections. Antibiotics rarely make headlines, but pediatric label expansions in resistant infections matter for a category that's been starved of investment.
