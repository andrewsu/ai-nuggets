Good morning, Travis. Wednesday June twenty-fourth, twenty twenty-six. Day three of BIO in San Diego. The big story this morning is Sangamo Therapeutics — the original zinc-finger gene-editing pioneer, founded nineteen ninety-five — filing Chapter 11 overnight and lining up Eli Lilly and Astellas as stalking-horse bidders for substantially all its assets. That goes deep in a few hours. Six items.

First — and I'll spend the spotlight on it — Sangamo Therapeutics filed for Chapter 11 in Delaware and announced asset purchase agreements with Lilly and Astellas. Lilly is the stalking-horse for the AAV capsid engineering platform, the zinc-finger protein technology, the Modular Integrase genome-editing platform, and the ST-five-oh-six prion-disease program — fifty million dollars cash plus assumption of certain liabilities. Astellas is the stalking-horse for isaralgagene civaparvovec, the Fabry-disease gene therapy formerly called ST-nine-twenty — twenty-five million at closing plus up to twenty-five million in milestones. Not in either stalking-horse: the ST-five-oh-three chronic-neuropathic-pain program, giroctocogene fitelparvovec — that's the hemophilia-A asset Pfizer terminated in late twenty twenty-four even after the Phase 3 win — and the cell-therapy and regulatory-T-cell programs. Auction to follow under section three-sixty-three. CEO Sandy Macrae stays on through the process. This is the end of a thirty-year run for the company that did the first in-vivo human gene-editing trial in twenty eighteen, and the spotlight today is on the platform unbundling that Lilly is buying for fifty million dollars.

Second, Pfizer's first ADC out of the forty-three-billion-dollar Seagen acquisition missed its primary endpoint. Sigvotatug vedotin — an integrin-beta-six-directed antibody-drug conjugate — did not significantly extend overall survival versus docetaxel in second-line-plus metastatic non-squamous non-small-cell lung cancer in the SigVie-zero-zero-two Phase 3. RBC's Trung Huynh notes Pfizer has already taken four-and-a-half billion dollars in Seagen-related write-offs and called this readout, quote, unlikely to help confidence, end quote. Leerink's David Risinger keeps the door open on the upcoming first-line trial with Keytruda — that's a different patient population and a different comparator — but the second-line Keytruda combination readout doesn't land until twenty twenty-seven. Pfizer sold integrin-beta-six at the time of Seagen acquisition as a ninety-percent-tumor-prevalence target; today the ADC field has to recalibrate what tumor-coverage prevalence actually predicts about ADC efficacy in chemo-refractory disease.

Third, NVIDIA's BioNeMo Agent Toolkit launched at BIO yesterday and is dominating the AI track at the show. Tools for AI agents — Nemotron reasoning models, NemoClaw secure agent blueprints, OpenShell execution environment, Parabricks genomics, and the existing BioNeMo model layer — packaged as agent-callable scientific skills. Fifty-plus partners at launch including Lilly, Schrödinger, Dassault Systèmes, Databricks, Snowflake, and the University of Washington Institute for Protein Design; Anthropic and OpenAI integrating directly into the agent framework. Huang's framing on stage — quote, frontier models are the brains, BioNeMo is the scientific toolbox, end quote — captures the inflection: the agent layer is now standardized, the value is moving to the skill layer. Today's main-stage panel is Katie Couric moderating a conversation between Genentech CEO Ashley Magargee and NVIDIA healthcare VP Kimberly Powell on the Genentech-NVIDIA reference partnership. Read this through for any Calibr discovery program: if you're not yet thinking about your discovery platform as a set of agent-callable skills, this is the BIO that asked the question.

Fourth, Corxel Pharmaceuticals reported positive Phase 2 topline for CX-eleven — an oral small-molecule GLP-1 receptor agonist — in obese and overweight U.S. adults. Eleven-and-a-half percent weight loss at thirty-six weeks; no hepatic safety signal across more than fifteen hundred participants studied to date between the U.S. trial and the China Phase 3 program with partner Vincentage Pharma. The hepatic-signal absence is the key headline here, because Pfizer's original oral GLP-1 lotiglipron and several other oral small-molecule GLP-1 programs across the field have fallen specifically to liver-tox flags. Corxel is heading into global Phase 3 against Lilly's orforglipron, AstraZeneca's elecoglipron — which moved to Phase 3 last week — and Novo's amycretin. This is the first oral GLP-1 from a smaller player with clean liver data at clinically interesting weight-loss magnitude, and it positions Corxel as a credible BD target if obesity dealmaking continues to consolidate.

Fifth, Boundless Bio is being reverse-merged into Serapha Bio with a two-hundred-thirty-million-dollar concurrent PIPE co-led by RTW Investments and RA Capital Management, with a top-tier mutual-fund syndicate participating. The combined company keeps the Serapha name; pre-merger Boundless shareholders end up with three-point-seven percent — that's an essentially complete write-down of Boundless's cancer extrachromosomal-DNA program in favor of Serapha's lead. That lead asset is SERP-zero-one, an in-vivo base-editing therapy for alpha-one antitrypsin deficiency, targeting the PiZZ mutation in the SERPIN-A-one gene — licensed from China's YolTech Therapeutics, where it's called YOLT-two-oh-two and is already enrolling patients in an investigator-initiated trial at Renji Hospital, Shanghai. Two read-throughs. In-vivo base editing is still investable at a quarter-billion-dollar PIPE in a market where pure-play CRISPR pricing is broken. And Chinese biotech licensing-out continues to dominate U.S. financing flow — roughly forty percent of U.S. biotech-licensing dollar value is now landing on APAC counterparties.

Sixth and shortest. Osanni Bio closed a one-hundred-ninety-million-dollar Series B led by Patient Square Capital, with the Horowitz Group, Invus Opportunities, and the Retinal Degeneration Fund participating. Ophthalmology and cardiology pipeline; the lead asset is in dry age-related macular degeneration and has wrapped Phase 1b ex-U.S. The model is the interesting part — Osanni describes itself as an innovation engine that spins out de-risked programs into affiliate companies, which is functionally the Paragon Therapeutics architecture applied to specialty therapeutic areas instead of immunology. Patient Square stepping in at one-ninety is the validation signal for that platform-spinout model in dry AMD.
