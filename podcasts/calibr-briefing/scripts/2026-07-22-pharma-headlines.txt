Second, a big pipeline casualty. GSK ended development of camlipixant in refractory chronic cough after mixed Phase 3 data, killing what a lot of analysts had penciled in as GSK's key catalyst for this year. Camlipixant is a P-two-X-three receptor antagonist. It blocks a receptor on airway sensory nerves that drives the cough reflex, and GSK acquired it when it bought Bellus Health for two billion dollars back in twenty twenty-three. In the Calm-one trial, the high dose did cut twenty-four-hour cough frequency significantly versus placebo at week twelve, but it fell short across the other endpoints, and in Calm-two the high dose was statistically no better than placebo at reducing cough at week twenty-four, so that study missed its primary endpoint. This essentially leaves the P-two-X-three chronic-cough class to Merck's gefapixant. GSK is keeping a Phase 2b study going in irritable bowel syndrome, but the blockbuster cough thesis is done, and this is effectively a write-down on the Bellus deal.

It boosts the energy metabolism of red blood cells to make them less prone to sickling. In the Phase 2, the once-daily five-milligram dose produced a hemoglobin response in eight of seventeen patients, about forty-seven percent, versus three of nine on placebo, which Agios said didn't clear the bar for a differentiated profile. The silver lining is that Agios's already-marketed pyruvate-kinase activator, mitapivat, branded Aqvesme, is under F-D-A review in sickle cell with a target decision date of November first, so the franchise now rides on the approved molecule rather than the next-generation one.
