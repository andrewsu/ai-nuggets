Good morning, Travis. Monday June twenty-second, twenty twenty-six. The BIO International Convention opens in San Diego today, and the deal calendar has front-loaded around it. Six items.

First — and I'll spend the spotlight on it — Insilico Medicine and SK Biopharmaceuticals announced an AI-driven CNS partnership at the BIO opening this morning, with a total potential value above two and a half billion dollars. Insilico will use its Pharma A-I platform to discover drug candidates in neuroinflammatory, neurodegenerative, and rare neurological disease; SK Biopharm takes them through late-stage development and commercialization. Upfront and near-term payments are capped at about eighteen million; the rest is contingent on milestones, plus single-digit royalties on net sales. The headline number is large; the structure is heavily back-loaded. This is now Insilico's biggest Asia-Pacific deal on record, and it's the cleanest AI-discovery dealmaking visual at BIO this week — so it's the right one to go deep on a few hours from now.

Second, AbbVie is reportedly closing in on an all-cash acquisition of Apogee Therapeutics — around eleven billion dollars, roughly a sixty-percent premium to last Thursday's close. Apogee's lead asset is zumilokibart, a half-life-extended anti-interleukin-thirteen antibody for atopic dermatitis. Phase 2 EASI-seventy-five numbers beat Dupixent on a placebo-adjusted basis, and the molecule is built for quarterly-to-twice-yearly dosing — positioned explicitly as a longer-acting Dupixent rival. If announced this week, it would be AbbVie's biggest bolt-on since the Allergan deal in twenty nineteen, and the clearest signal yet that the Humira-cliff hole is being filled by ultra-long-acting immunology — same architecture as the Spyre TL1A bet we covered Tuesday a week ago, different target.

Third, the BIO Convention itself. Roughly twenty-one thousand attendees, around seventy thousand scheduled partnering meetings — potentially a record. Featured sessions include a post-mortem on Novo Nordisk's Akero acquisition and the launch of BIO Partnering for Oncology. Sanofi made a pre-game move overnight, replacing its head of R and D; new CEO Belén Garijo is reorganizing the function after a string of clinical setbacks, and the timing telegraphs that Sanofi will be aggressive on the partnering floor this week. Expect more leadership and pipeline news through Thursday.

Fourth, Biogen agreed to acquire RayThera last Wednesday, up to one billion dollars including upfront plus mostly contingent milestones. RayThera is a small-molecule immunology shop with multiple anti-inflammatory assets across immune-mediated conditions; lead asset is due to start Phase 1 early in the third quarter. Worth flagging because Foresite Capital co-led the Series A — this is a near-term exit on a recent Foresite position, and it fits the new-Biogen pivot beyond MS and Alzheimer's.

Fifth, MoonLake released Week 52 data from its Phase 3 VELA program in hidradenitis suppurativa on Saturday, with an investor day this morning to follow up. Sonelokimab — an interleukin-seventeen-A-and-F nanobody — hit HiSCR-seventy-five in sixty-seven percent of patients at one year, and complete skin clearance, HiSCR-one-hundred, in thirty-three percent. Roughly ten percentage points above competitor benchmarks at the one-year mark. No new safety signals. This lines up a likely best-in-class HS biologic, and it's the latest data point that nanobody-format multi-target IL-seventeen blockade is materially differentiated from classical anti-IL-seventeen-A monoclonals — useful framing for any Calibr autoimmune work thinking through IL-seventeen-axis competitive intensity.

Sixth, and close to home — Calibr-Skaggs was awarded five point one million dollars from NIAID last Tuesday, a five-year grant for a long-acting injectable formulation of entecavir for the HIV-HBV co-infected population. About a hundred twenty thousand patients in the U.S. carry both viruses, and daily entecavir adherence is the dominant clinical failure mode. The grant funds preclinical optimization through IND-enabling work, with Anil Gupta as principal investigator. The structural read-through is the same one Nortiva's launch made explicit last week: adherence is the single biggest outcome lever in anti-infectives, and Calibr's long-acting injectable platform is institutionally bet on it.
