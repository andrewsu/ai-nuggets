Hey Travis, it's Nigel with your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing for Sunday, May 10th, 2026. Quiet weekend tape, but the week ahead is the loudest one this quarter for cell and gene therapy — the American Society of Gene and Cell Therapy annual meeting starts tomorrow in Boston with more than two thousand abstracts and a packed in vivo CAR-T track. Today's spotlight is the Azalea Therapeutics first-in-primate data on site-specific TRAC-locus in vivo CAR-T engineering, which dropped Friday afternoon ahead of the meeting. Argenx got an FDA approval that opens up generalized myasthenia gravis to all serotypes, Bristol Myers grabbed Lonza's TOPO-one ADC linker-payload platform, and Madrigal's Rezdiffra Q1 numbers reset the MASH market math. Here's the rundown.

Number one — and this is today's spotlight. Azalea Therapeutics announced Friday morning that it has been granted a late-breaking oral presentation slot at ASGCT this Friday, May 15th, to disclose first-in-primate data on its in vivo TRAC-CAR-T platform. The headline numbers from the abstract are striking. Six rhesus macaques received a single intravenous administration with no lymphodepletion. CAR-T cells peaked at forty-one percent of peripheral T cells by day eleven. B-cell depletion exceeded ninety percent in all six animals by day ten. Five of six showed greater than ninety-percent depletion in lymph nodes and bone marrow within two weeks. No deaths, no neurotoxicity, no off-target CAR expression or integration detected in non-T-cells. This is the first non-human-primate dataset for any in vivo CAR-T platform that uses site-specific gene editing — meaning the CAR is inserted at a defined safe locus, not randomly integrated by a lentivirus. The implications for the field, for AbbVie-Capstan, for Sana, for Lilly-Kelonia, and for Calibr's switchable architecture are significant. Spotlight in a few minutes.

Number two — Argenx received FDA approval Friday for a label expansion of Vyvgart and Vyvgart Hytrulo that opens generalized myasthenia gravis treatment to all serotypes — including, for the first time, anti-LRP4-antibody-positive patients and triple-seronegative patients. The expansion was based on the Phase 3 ADAPT-SERON trial, which hit its primary endpoint with a three-point-three-five-point improvement on the MG-ADL scale at week four versus placebo, p-value of zero-point-zero-zero-six-eight. The structural significance is that there was previously no approved targeted therapy for triple-seronegative gMG, which represents roughly ten percent of the patient population — patients who test negative for the three known autoantibodies and have effectively been managed with off-label immunosuppression. Argenx has now positioned efgartigimod as the first and only approved therapy across the full serological spectrum of the disease. For the broader FcRn-antagonist class — Johnson and Johnson's Imaavy and the next-wave compounds in development — this raises the bar on label breadth as a competitive parameter alongside dosing convenience and IgG-subtype effects.

Number three — the broader ASGCT meeting that starts tomorrow is the most consequential cell-and-gene venue of the year, and the in vivo CAR-T track in particular is stacked. Beyond Azalea's late-breaker on Friday, Sana Biotechnology has an oral presentation Tuesday morning on SG293, its CD8-targeted fusosome delivery platform for CD19 in vivo CAR-T, with non-human primate data and an IND filing targeted for 2027. Precision BioSciences has an oral on PBGENE-DMD with new humanized-mouse efficacy data for Duchenne muscular dystrophy. And expect data and pipeline updates from Capstan via AbbVie, from Umoja, from Outpace, and from the academic groups around the Eyquem and Marson labs. The structural takeaway for Calibr is that this is the meeting where the technological branches of in vivo CAR-T — lentiviral, fusosome, lipid nanoparticle, and now site-specific gene editing — get compared head to head on translational data for the first time. The competitive map after Friday's Azalea data plus the four days of presentations before it will look meaningfully different from the map we had a week ago.

Number four — Bristol Myers Squibb signed a single-target licensing deal Thursday with Lonza for the SYNtecan linker-payload platform, a TOPO-one inhibitor-based ADC technology. Financial terms were not disclosed beyond the standard upfront-plus-milestones-plus-royalties structure. BMS gets exclusive access to develop an ADC against an undisclosed target using the SYNtecan chemistry; Lonza retains the platform and presumably will license it to others against different targets. The competitive context is that TOPO-one inhibitors — Daiichi Sankyo's DXd is the canonical example — have become the dominant payload class in next-generation ADCs, and the linker chemistry that controls plasma stability and tumor-targeted release is the differentiator. BMS does not have a leading TOPO-one ADC franchise; Daiichi-AstraZeneca, Pfizer-Seagen, and Merck via the LaNova-MK-2010-adjacent assets do. This is BMS buying its way into a defined chemistry without paying a billion dollars for it. Read this in the context of yesterday's spotlight — Daiichi taking a nearly billion-dollar manufacturing write-down on ADC overcapacity. The chemistry is hot, the demand math is harder.

Number five — Madrigal Pharmaceuticals reported Q1 results Tuesday with Rezdiffra, the only approved MASH therapy, posting net sales of three hundred eleven million dollars, up one hundred and twenty-seven percent year-over-year, with more than forty-two thousand patients on therapy as of March thirty-first, two-and-a-half times the patient count from Q1 last year. The company also disclosed Tuesday a global licensing agreement for a clinical-stage siRNA targeting a PNPLA3 mutation that is a genetically validated driver of MASH fibrosis progression. The strategic frame Madrigal is building is unmistakable — Rezdiffra as the foundation oral therapy, a portfolio of next-generation combination assets behind it including the in-licensed PNPLA3 siRNA, an MGL-2086 oral GLP-one Phase 1 starting Q2, and an ervogastat-Rezdiffra DDI study in Q4. For Calibr's MASH biologic, the read-through is that the commercial market is real and growing fast — but the future is combination therapy, not monotherapy, and the dual-agonist FGF-twenty-one mechanism in the IND-enabling work needs to be positioned against a Rezdiffra-anchored standard of care that will look very different in 2028 than it does today.

Number six — and a quick close. AACR rolled up its 2026 meeting with strong data updates that are worth noting if you're tracking the oncology adjacency. Revolution Medicines' zoldonrasib, the RAS-on G12D-selective inhibitor, posted an eleven-point-one-month progression-free survival and fifty-two percent objective response rate in previously treated KRAS-G12D non-small-cell lung cancer — Phase 1 data, but a meaningfully better profile than what the field had pencilled in for a G12D-selective. And Merck disclosed first-in-human MK-twenty-ten data, the PD-one slash VEGF bispecific licensed from LaNova for five hundred and eighty-eight million dollars upfront — fifty-five percent response rate at the lower of two doses in untreated patients. The PD-one slash VEGF bispecific class — Akeso's ivonescimab is the leading agent — continues to look like the most credible threat to single-agent Keytruda durability that pharma has fielded.
