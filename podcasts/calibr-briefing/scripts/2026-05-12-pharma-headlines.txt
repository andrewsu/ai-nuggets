Number one. Bristol Myers Squibb just signed one of the largest China licensing deals on record. BMS and Jiangsu Hengrui announced a global strategic collaboration covering thirteen early-stage programs across oncology, hematology, and immunology. The structure: four oncology and hematology assets coming from Hengrui, four immunology assets from BMS, and five jointly discovered programs to be developed together using Hengrui's discovery engine. BMS is paying six hundred million dollars upfront, with two anniversary payments of one hundred seventy-five million each in 2027 and 2028, bringing near-term consideration to nine hundred fifty million. With options exercised and milestones hit, total deal value reaches approximately fifteen point two billion dollars, plus tiered royalties on net sales outside Greater China. BMS picks up worldwide rights ex-China; Hengrui retains the China territory and gets options on co-development. Closing is expected in the third quarter. We'll go deep on this one in today's spotlight.

Number two. Inhibrx Biosciences read out positive interim Phase 2 data yesterday for INBRX-106, a hexavalent OX40 agonist, in combination with Keytruda in first-line PD-L1-high recurrent or metastatic head and neck squamous cell carcinoma. The combination delivered a forty-four percent overall response rate versus twenty-one point four percent in the Keytruda monotherapy control arm. Three complete responses were observed in the combo arm, none in the control. The combination also drove up to a fifteen-fold increase in peripheral CD8 and CD4 T-cell proliferation, with no treatment-related deaths. This is meaningful because OX40 agonism has been a long graveyard — Genentech, GSK, AstraZeneca, and others all walked away from monovalent designs. The hexavalent multimerization approach is what Inhibrx is betting drove the differentiation. Progression-free survival data is expected in the fourth quarter, with a Phase 3 start planned for the third quarter of 2026.

Number three. Enterprise Therapeutics, a Forbion-backed UK biotech, hit the primary endpoint in a Phase 2 trial of ETD001, an inhaled epithelial sodium channel blocker, or ENaC blocker, for cystic fibrosis. The trial enrolled the roughly ten percent of CF patients who don't benefit from current CFTR modulators — the hardest-to-treat segment. ETD001 produced statistically significant improvement in lung function versus placebo over twenty-eight days of twice-daily dosing. This is the first time an ENaC blocker has shown efficacy in CF. The mechanism is mutation-agnostic, which means it could also be layered on top of Vertex's modulators. Full results will be presented at the European Cystic Fibrosis Society meeting in Lisbon in June, and Enterprise is now planning Phase 2b dose-ranging studies.

Number four. Bayer reported first-quarter earnings and used the call to confirm cuts of four pipeline programs amid what management called a slow start to the year. The casualties include a BDKRB1 receptor antagonist in Phase 2 for neuropathic pain and runcaciguat, the company's soluble guanylate cyclase activator that had been in development for chronic kidney disease. Core EPS of two seventy-one beat the consensus of two twenty-eight, helped by licensing resolution income. The pipeline pruning continues a trend across European pharma — AstraZeneca and Novartis have made similar moves this quarter — narrowing focus to higher-conviction late-stage assets ahead of pending patent exposures.

Number five. UnitedHealth's pharmacy benefits arm, Optum Rx, introduced what it's calling the industry's first transparent fee-based PBM model. Under the new structure, clients pay flat monthly per-member fees independent of manufacturer list price or prescription volume — eliminating spread pricing. Group purchasing organization revenue will fully transition to flat service fees by end of 2027. Optum is also launching consumer tools, Shop MyScript and Price Wise, that surface real out-of-pocket cost at the point of prescribing. The move arrives ahead of the FTC's ongoing PBM probe and expected congressional action — Express Scripts and CVS Caremark will face pressure to follow.

And finally, number six. At the American Society of Gene and Cell Therapy meeting in Boston, HydroGene Therapeutics presented preclinical data showing non-viral DNA delivery via hydrodynamic injection into the bile duct, achieving expression durability past six months in non-human primates — comparable to commercial AAV products — and demonstrating redosability in pig and primate models. The data targets hemophilia B as the lead indication, with first-in-human studies planned for 2027. The redosability angle is the real headline: AAV's neutralizing-antibody problem has been the central friction in liver-directed gene therapy for fifteen years.
