Welcome back. The spotlight today is on what the European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology Congress, which wraps up in London today, has done to the autoimmune B-cell-depletion field. Three readouts in the same week have repositioned the question — Cabaletta Bio's autologous C D nineteen CAR-T rese-cel and its preconditioning-free dosing in lupus, which we covered yesterday; Kyverna Therapeutics' autologous C D nineteen CAR-T miv-cel and its sixty-six point six percent ACR-seventy response at week thirty-six in refractory rheumatoid arthritis; and Cullinan Therapeutics' subcutaneous C D nineteen by C D three bispecific T-cell engager C L N nine seventy-eight, with the first company-sponsored autoimmune T-cell-engager data set out today at EULAR. Three approaches, all targeting C D nineteen, all depleting B cells, all delivered in patients who had exhausted multiple prior biologics and conventional disease-modifying therapy. The question the data are starting to answer is where those three approaches are going to compete and where they are not. There are four threads worth pulling on.

The first thread is the modality split — autologous CAR-T versus off-the-shelf T-cell engager. The autologous CAR-T programs deliver a one-time infusion that, in the lupus and dermatomyositis data sets to date, produces immune reset — meaning the post-depletion B-cell repertoire repopulates with a naive phenotype, autoantibodies fall and stay fallen, and patients sustain responses off immunomodulators for over a year. That's the curative-intent positioning. The cost is the patient journey — autologous CAR-T requires apheresis, manufacturing time of several weeks, has traditionally required lymphodepleting chemotherapy preconditioning to make space for engraftment, and the safety profile carries some risk of cytokine release syndrome and immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome. Cabaletta's preconditioning-free data from yesterday is the field's attempt to chip away at the toxicity and access barriers of that journey — and the early signal that lupus B-cell depletion translationally tracks with the preconditioned cohort is meaningful, but it's two patients at the lowest dose, not a settled question. T-cell engagers like Cullinan's C L N nine seventy-eight deliver redirected polyclonal T-cell-mediated B-cell killing without any cell manufacturing, no preconditioning at all, and crucially with subcutaneous administration. The trade-off is that they require continuous dosing — patients aren't getting a one-and-done immune reset, they're getting a repeatable depletion protocol. The clinical question that EULAR has now sharpened is whether the depth and durability of B-cell depletion you get from subcutaneous dosing of a C D nineteen T-cell engager translates into the same immune reset that one-time CAR-T can deliver, or whether it just buys you a more accessible chronic-management option. The Cullinan data flag that the early efficacy signal is there — four of five rheumatoid arthritis patients with disease-activity-score drops, and five of six lupus patients meeting the conventional clinically meaningful threshold — but the durability comparison is months to years off.

The second thread is the tissue-versus-blood depletion question. Conventional anti-C D twenty monoclonal antibody therapy with rituximab has been the cornerstone of B-cell depletion in autoimmune disease for two decades, and the reason rituximab response is often partial in lupus and rheumatoid arthritis is that tissue-resident memory B cells in lymph nodes, synovium, and bone marrow are inadequately reached by an intravenous antibody. The whole rationale for CAR-T and T-cell-engager B-cell depletion in autoimmune disease is that redirected T-cell-mediated cytotoxicity can clear B cells in tissue that antibodies cannot. Cullinan specifically called out synovial-tissue B-cell depletion and improved synovial ultrasound scores in the rheumatoid arthritis arm of OUTRACE-R-A, which is the most direct evidence yet that a T-cell engager can produce tissue-level B-cell clearance, not just peripheral. That's a real differentiation argument against rituximab-based comparators and a competitive parity argument against the CAR-T modality on the same dimension. Watch for biopsy-supported tissue-depletion data from all three modalities to become the field-standard reference point — that's where the mechanistic story actually distinguishes the new modalities from the legacy anti-C D twenty standard of care.

The third thread is the autoantibody question and the immune-reset definition. The Kyverna miv-cel data set highlighted a predominantly naive B-cell repopulation pattern after depletion, which is the strongest mechanistic argument for durable disease modification rather than chronic symptom suppression. Kyverna's COMPARE patients had anti-citrullinated-protein antibodies driving their rheumatoid arthritis — and the autoantibody declines were sustained. The deeper question this raises is whether sustained autoantibody decline plus naive B-cell repopulation actually translates into cure. The two-year and three-year follow-up data on the first cohorts of autoimmune CAR-T patients globally — the German Schett group's lupus patients in particular, who are now several years out from their first dose — are starting to suggest something close to durable disease modification at the cohort level, with most patients remaining off immunomodulators and not requiring a second infusion. If that holds in larger and randomized cohorts, the autologous CAR-T modality may end up positioned as a one-time disease-modifying intervention reserved for the most refractory severe disease, and the T-cell engager modality may end up as a more frequently dosed, more accessible alternative for moderate disease — fundamentally complementary, not competitive, positioning.

The fourth thread is what this all means for Calibr's positioning. Autoimmune CAR-T and autoimmune T-cell engagers are now the most heavily capitalized therapeutic-development thesis in autoimmune disease outside of the established T-N-F and interleukin-pathway franchises. U-C-B struck a one-point-one billion-dollar deal with Antengene for A-T-G two-oh-one, a C D nineteen by C D three bispecific T-cell engager, earlier this year. Cullinan added a seven hundred million-dollar B-C-M-A T-cell engager pickup for autoimmune disease. Cabaletta and Kyverna have repositioned their entire pipelines around autoimmune cell therapy. The lessons for Calibr's pipeline strategy are three. First, the C D nineteen B-cell depletion thesis for autoimmune disease is now de-risked at the depth-of-depletion level — that's no longer the differentiation question; the data are converging across modalities. Second, the differentiation that gets a new entrant funded is on access — preconditioning-free dosing, subcutaneous administration, manufacturing-independent off-the-shelf format, or a faster path to depth-of-depletion that doesn't require an immune-reset claim. Third, the antigen selection question is widening — B-C-M-A T-cell engagers are now being trialed in autoimmune disease specifically to deplete the longer-lived plasma cell population that C D nineteen does not reach. That's where the next mechanistic wave probably lives — tandem C D nineteen and B-C-M-A approaches, or sequential dosing strategies that engage both the short-lived memory and the longer-lived plasma-cell populations.

The bottom line: EULAR twenty twenty-six is the meeting where the autoimmune B-cell-depletion field has converged on B-cell-depletion-as-immune-reset as the central thesis, where the modality split between autologous CAR-T and off-the-shelf T-cell engagers has begun to clarify into complementary rather than directly competitive positioning, and where the tissue-level depletion question is starting to get answered with real biopsy data. The competitive intelligence for Calibr is that the depth-of-depletion question is closing, and the open differentiation lives in access, format, and antigen selection — not in whether B-cell depletion via redirected T-cell cytotoxicity works in autoimmune disease. That part is now field-standard.
