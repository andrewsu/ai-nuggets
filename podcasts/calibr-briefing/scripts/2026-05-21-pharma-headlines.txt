Number one. Infex Therapeutics, the U-K anti-infective biotech, reported positive Phase two-A data for RESP dash X, also called I-N-F-E-X seven-oh-two, in non-cystic-fibrosis bronchiectasis patients colonized with Pseudomonas aeruginosa. RESP dash X is an anti-virulence monoclonal antibody licensed from Shionogi that binds the Pseudomonas Pcr-V virulence factor. The trial randomized two doses, six milligrams per kilo and ten milligrams per kilo. The drug hit its primary safety endpoint with no infusion reactions, no anti-drug antibodies, a twenty-eight-point-eight-day half-life that supports quarterly dosing, confirmed lung deposition in epithelial lining fluid at forty-eight hours, and an early exacerbation-rate reduction signal at a p-value of zero point zero-eight versus each patient's own prior twelve months. The framing here is Insmed's Brinsupri model — non-antibiotic anti-virulence biologic for a chronic colonized lung-disease population, dosed quarterly, with the orphan-disease pricing economics. Phase three planning is next.

Number two. cAMPfield Therapeutics emerged from stealth with a one-hundred-eighty-million-dollar Series A, San Diego based, led by Andrew Gerhart, the former Prometheus chief operating officer. Backers include Novo Holdings and R-A Capital alongside seven other top-tier life-sciences investors. The pipeline focus is inflammation and immunology drugs licensed from other companies, which is now the dominant I-and-I founding template — license clinical-stage assets, syndicate top-tier capital, run them faster than the originator. The actual targets are still undisclosed. This is the second nine-figure I-and-I launch in the last quarter on that exact playbook.

Number three. Eli Lilly announced the acquisition of Engage Bio, the San Carlos non-viral D-N-A delivery startup, for up to two-hundred-two million dollars in cash, upfront plus milestones. Engage's Tethosome platform uses a lipid nanoparticle to co-deliver an engineered D-N-A payload alongside an m-R-N-A encoding a proprietary D-N-A-binding tether protein that traffics the D-N-A to the nucleus, evades cytosolic D-N-A immune sensors, and reports more than one-hundred-fold higher expression than conventional non-viral D-N-A. This is Lilly's sixth biotech acquisition in twenty twenty-six and continues the Verve, Akouos, Sigilon, Prevail, Adverum genetic-medicines build-out. We go deep on this one in today's spotlight.

Number four. Immunovant reported a meaningful Phase two readout for I-M-V-T dash one-four-oh-two, its next-generation F-c-R-n inhibitor, in difficult-to-treat rheumatoid arthritis. At week sixteen, nearly seventy-five percent of patients hit A-C-R twenty improvement and over one-third hit A-C-R seventy. Their analysts moved to a blockbuster framing. F-c-R-n inhibition has been a myasthenia-gravis and immune-thrombocytopenia story for argenx; pushing into R-A meaningfully expands the addressable market and pressures the I-L-six, J-A-K, and B-cell-depletion classes if confirmed in Phase three.

Number five. Bristol Myers Squibb signed an enterprise-wide artificial-intelligence deployment deal with Anthropic to embed Claude across drug R-and-D globally. The frame here is that AI infrastructure has bifurcated — narrow predictive vendors keep selling point models for individual problems, but the large pharmas are now buying the entire reasoning agent as horizontal infrastructure, the way they bought electronic-lab-notebook software a decade ago.

Number six. The U-S government has tapped Mapp Biopharmaceutical, the San Diego antibody developer behind the original ZMapp Ebola cocktail, to scale up M-B-P one-three-four for the Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak now spreading in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo. M-B-P one-three-four is a pan-ebolavirus antibody cocktail effective in primate challenge models against Bundibugyo, Sudan, and Zaire strains. B-A-R-D-A is coordinating the manufacturing and stockpile. There is no licensed Bundibugyo vaccine or therapeutic and W-H-O has flagged this outbreak as a public health emergency.

Number seven, and we'll close on competitive landscape. Pfizer pushed its twenty-five-valent pneumococcal conjugate vaccine into Phase three after strong Phase two infant data with no safety signals. This is the head-to-head challenge to Merck's Capvaxive and the next-generation successor to Pfizer's own Prevnar-twenty. The competitive question is whether incremental serotype coverage justifies displacement of an entrenched standard of care, especially with the post-COVID vaccine-trust headwinds.
