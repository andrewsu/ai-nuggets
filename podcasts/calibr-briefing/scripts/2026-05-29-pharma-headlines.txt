Good morning Travis. This is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing for Friday, May twenty-ninth, twenty twenty-six. Six headlines today.

First, Pfizer and Innovent Biologics. The two companies announced a global strategic collaboration covering twelve early-stage cancer programs — a mix of antibody-drug conjugates with novel differentiated payloads and multispecific antibodies. Innovent gets a six hundred fifty-million-dollar upfront cash payment, eligibility for up to nine-point-eight-five billion dollars in development, regulatory, and commercial milestones, and double-digit royalties on each approved product. Eight of the twelve programs originated at Innovent and four are de novo programs proposed by Pfizer. The portfolio splits into three buckets — four programs Pfizer takes exclusive global rights to, four programs Pfizer takes exclusive rights to outside Greater China, and four programs co-developed globally with U.S. and Europe profit-sharing. Pfizer leads all twelve through Phase one, then Innovent assumes global development. The total headline value is up to ten-point-five billion dollars, making this the largest single Chinese pharma out-licensing transaction announced this year. The deal lands into a regulatory environment that includes the BIOSECURE Act signed into law in December twenty twenty-five, a draft executive order under consideration that would route U.S. pharma licensing of Chinese drugs through a national security committee, and proposed expansions of the COINS Act to capture in-licensing arrangements. Pfizer's previous Chinese deal was the one-point-two-five-billion-dollar 3SBio PD-1 slash VEGF deal earlier this year. We go deep on what this deal says about Pfizer's post-Seagen ADC strategy, China's rising share of innovative oncology pipeline, and the implications for U.S. biotech in today's spotlight.

Second, AstraZeneca. The FDA approved Imfinzi — that's durvalumab, AstraZeneca's PD-L1 antibody — in combination with BCG induction and maintenance for BCG-naive high-risk non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer. Approval is based on the Phase three POTOMAC trial in one thousand eighteen patients, where durvalumab plus BCG reduced the risk of disease recurrence, progression, or death by roughly thirty percent versus BCG alone. The recommended dose is fifteen hundred milligrams every four weeks for thirteen cycles in combination with BCG. This is the first immunotherapy combination approved in BCG-naive high-risk NMIBC and meaningfully expands the Imfinzi label, which already covers muscle-invasive disease, several lung indications, and biliary tract cancer. The strategic threat to Merck's Keytruda franchise is the BCG-naive setting — Keytruda is approved for BCG-unresponsive high-risk NMIBC but not for the earlier BCG-naive line.

Third, AbbVie. The FDA approved Decnupaz — that's pivekimab sunirine — a CD123-directed antibody-drug conjugate, for adults with blastic plasmacytoid dendritic cell neoplasm. This is a rare, aggressive blood cancer that arises in the bone marrow and infiltrates the lymph nodes, spleen, and skin. The asset came to AbbVie through its ten-billion-dollar acquisition of ImmunoGen in twenty twenty-three. This is the second FDA approval AbbVie has now extracted from that ImmunoGen deal, the first being Elahere — mirvetuximab soravtansine — in platinum-resistant ovarian cancer, which is now reportedly tracking toward blockbuster status. Two FDA approvals from a ten-billion-dollar deal is a respectable hit rate on a transaction many analysts viewed as expensive at the time.

Fourth, Apogee Therapeutics. The Massachusetts-based clinical-stage biotech announced a one-point-three-billion-dollar strategic financing collaboration with Blackstone Life Sciences to advance Phase three development of zumilokibart, its anti-IL-13 antibody for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis. The structure is up to eight hundred million dollars of non-dilutive synthetic royalty funding from Blackstone in exchange for low-to-mid single-digit tiered royalties on worldwide annual sales over a fifteen-year term, with royalties tapering above eight billion dollars in annual sales, plus access to up to five hundred million dollars of senior corporate debt. Blackstone described this as the largest pre-Phase three royalty financing on record. The most recent Phase two readout showed sixty-five-point-nine percent of mid-dose zumilokibart-treated patients achieved a seventy-five percent or greater reduction in eczema area and severity at sixteen weeks. The drug is positioned to compete against Regeneron-Sanofi's Dupixent and Lilly's Ebglyss on a dosing-frequency wedge.

Fifth, CVS Health. CVS Caremark, the pharmacy benefit manager arm, will restore formulary coverage for Lilly's Zepbound — that's tirzepatide — starting October first, and will add Lilly's newly-approved oral GLP-1 Foundayo to its formulary effective June first for plans that elect to cover it. CVS dropped Zepbound from its template formulary in July twenty twenty-five after negotiating an exclusive deal with Novo Nordisk's Wegovy. The reversal puts Lilly back on equal footing with Novo across CVS's pharmacy-benefit network, ending an eleven-month period where roughly thirty million covered lives lost easy access to tirzepatide. Foundayo, an oral incretin Lilly received FDA approval for in April, is the first oral GLP-1 with a meaningful weight-loss label and removes the injection barrier that has constrained the broader weight-loss commercial opportunity.

Sixth, Beren Therapeutics. The FDA extended its review of Beren's New Drug Application for adrabetadex by three months, pushing the new PDUFA target action date to November seventeenth. Adrabetadex is a cyclodextrin therapy for infantile-onset Niemann-Pick disease type C — an ultra-rare neurodegenerative lysosomal storage disorder. The extension follows Beren's March eighteenth response to an FDA information request, which the agency classified as a Major Amendment, automatically triggering the three-month extension under standard PDUFA rules. The agency had originally been on track for an August seventeenth decision under priority review. Adrabetadex has been in development for over a decade across multiple sponsors. This is a small commercial opportunity by absolute revenue — but a meaningful rare-pediatric-disease priority review voucher opportunity at approval.
