Good morning. It's Saturday, June thirteenth, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. The lead story today is South San Francisco-based SonoThera's oversubscribed one-hundred-twenty-five-million-dollar Series B, syndicated by Vida Ventures and a who's-who of pharma corporate venture — Johnson and Johnson Innovation, Leaps by Bayer, Otsuka, UCB Ventures, Vertex Ventures, ARK Invest, R-A Capital, and others — to push a non-viral ultrasound-mediated gene-therapy platform into the clinic in twenty twenty-seven against Duchenne muscular dystrophy and autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease. That financing is today's spotlight. Six items in the roundup.

First, the SonoThera financing. SonoThera's RIPPLE platform pairs F-D-A-cleared diagnostic ultrasound with commercial microbubble contrast agents to mechanically open the endothelial barrier and cell membranes at a targeted tissue site, allowing naked D-N-A or R-N-A constructs to enter the local cells without any viral vector. The size ceiling that constrains A-A-V — roughly four-and-a-half kilobases of cargo — is gone. The lead D-M-D program delivers the full thirteen-kilobase dystrophin coding sequence, about three times what A-A-V can carry, and the company says it is unaware of any competitor attempting to deliver full-length dystrophin. The financing arrives the same month the F-D-A added a boxed warning to Sarepta's A-A-V-based Elevidys following multiple acute-liver-failure deaths and the same week Wave Life Sciences' R-N-A-editing alpha-one-antitrypsin program and Tessera's in-vivo gene-editing platform have been drawing pharma attention as architectural alternatives to A-A-V. SonoThera plans to file its first I-N-D in D-M-D next year, with an outpatient-style infusion plus ultrasound procedure under an hour. The spotlight in a few hours unpacks the platform, the pharma-syndicate composition, the A-A-V-alternative landscape, and the read-through to Calibr's in-vivo C-A-R-T and gene-therapy delivery work.

Second, Johnson and Johnson's MonumenTAL-three plenary readout at the European Hematology Association meeting in Stockholm. In the Phase three study, the addition of Talvey — talquetamab, a G-P-R-C-five-D-by-C-D-three bispecific T-cell engager — to Darzalex Faspro, with or without pomalidomide, in patients with relapsed or refractory multiple myeloma after at least one prior line of therapy cut the risk of progression or death by up to seventy-two percent versus the standard Darzalex-pomalidomide-and-dexamethasone control. The progression-free survival rate at twenty-four months reached eighty-one-point-three percent for the Talvey arms versus fifty-one-point-two percent for standard of care. Overall survival rates at two years were eighty-seven-point-nine percent for the doublet and eighty-nine-point-two percent for the triplet, a roughly fifty-three-percent reduction in mortality hazard versus the control. The triplet drove a much higher rate of grade three and four cytopenias — eighty-eight percent versus fifty-two percent for the doublet — so the safety story will define which arm becomes the actual standard of care. This is the first Phase three evidence that a G-P-R-C-five-D bispecific can win in earlier-line myeloma, and it consolidates Johnson and Johnson's multi-modality myeloma franchise — Darzalex on C-D-thirty-eight, Tecvayli on B-C-M-A, Talvey on G-P-R-C-five-D — into a layered displacement of the lenalidomide-and-dexamethasone post-Revlimid backbone.

Third, Rapport Therapeutics has started Phase three enrollment of R-A-P-two-one-nine in focal-onset epilepsy. R-A-P-two-one-nine is a selective negative allosteric modulator of the T-A-R-P gamma-eight subunit of A-M-P-A glutamate receptors, designed to suppress excitatory transmission only in cortical regions where T-A-R-P gamma-eight is highly expressed — hippocampus, neocortex — rather than ubiquitously across the brain like the only approved pan-A-M-P-A antagonist, Eisai's Fycompa. Phase two data showed a seventy-eight-percent median reduction in clinical seizure frequency over an eight-week observation window. The Phase three is powered around a more conservative forty-to-fifty-percent reduction. C-E-O Abraham Ceesay said Rapport is fully prepared to launch the drug independently if it clears Phase three. Rapport priced its I-P-O in twenty twenty-four and the asset has been the company's centerpiece since.

Fourth, Novo Nordisk picked up United Kingdom marketing authorization for the oral semaglutide formulation of Wegovy from the M-H-R-A this week. Novo has already secured U-S labeled-dose pricing parity with Lilly's Foundayo — Lilly's twice-daily oral G-L-P-one launched in January at thirty-day pricing of three hundred forty-nine dollars — and the U-K approval extends the oral-G-L-P-one franchise into Europe's largest obesity market by privately funded weight-loss demand. Lilly retains the chemistry edge at full-amino-acid manufacturing scale; Novo retains the brand-recognition edge from years of injectable Wegovy market education. Oral G-L-P-ones remain the single largest category-share question in pharma.

Fifth, Jazz Pharmaceuticals and PharmaMar's Zepzelca — lurbinectedin, a covalent transcription-inhibitor selective for guanine-rich D-N-A — failed the IMforte Phase three confirmatory trial in first-line maintenance for extensive-stage small-cell lung cancer. The drug was already approved by F-D-A in twenty twenty for second-line small-cell lung cancer based on accelerated-approval data, and the IMforte study was supposed to convert that to full approval and expand into the much larger first-line maintenance setting. The miss leaves the accelerated approval in jeopardy and removes a top-line-growth lever for Jazz's broader oncology rebuild after the GW Pharma acquisition. PharmaMar's small-cap valuation also takes the hit. The trial was the second high-profile small-cell-lung-cancer disappointment of the spring after the Summit-and-Keytruda first-line readout earlier this week.

Sixth and last for the day, Jeff Bezos's Prometheus closed a twelve-billion-dollar funding round at a forty-one-billion-dollar valuation to scale what the company calls an artificial general engineer — a foundation-model family trained on physics-grounded experimental data, robotics interactions, and engineering workflows rather than the text-and-image corpora behind today's general-purpose large language models. The pitch is software that can design and manufacture physical systems — jet engines, bridges, chips, and explicitly drug compounds — from physics-first principles rather than pattern-matching on prior literature. The capital came from J-P Morgan, Goldman Sachs, BlackRock, and Bezos's family office; Prometheus is roughly one hundred fifty people across San Francisco, London, and Zurich with senior hires from OpenAI, DeepMind, and Nvidia. The biotech read-through is that the next wave of generative chemistry investment will be physics-anchored rather than language-anchored, and the dollar scale at which that thesis is being capitalized is now an order of magnitude above what any biotech-specific A-I platform has raised.
