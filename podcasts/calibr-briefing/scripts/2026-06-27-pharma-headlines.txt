Good morning, Travis. Saturday June twenty-seventh, twenty twenty-six. Weekend wrap. The headline of the day broke yesterday morning out of San Francisco — Epicrispr Biotechnologies reporting the first clinical evidence that epigenome editing can rebuild muscle in human muscular dystrophy patients. That's the spotlight in a few hours. Six items.

First — and this is the lead I'll go deep on — Epicrispr reported six-month interim data Friday morning from the Phase one-two open-label first-in-human study of E-P-I-three-twenty-one in facioscapulohumeral muscular dystrophy. Nine patients dosed across two single-I-V-infusion cohorts at two-times-ten-to-the-thirteenth and four-times-ten-to-the-thirteenth vector-genomes-per-kilogram. The first three patients to reach the six-month evaluation all gained lean muscle volume — average around three-hundred-seventy milliliters, roughly eight-tenths of a pound of new muscle per patient, with the strongest individual gains hitting fifteen percent in specific muscle groups. Backed up by a novel circulating cell-free-D-N-A biomarker reflecting D-U-X-four pathway activity moving in the right direction. No serious adverse events to date. The platform is Epicrispr's Gene Expression Modulation System, the C-R-I-S-P-R-i style epigenome silencer co-founded by Stanley Qi out of Stanford — D-N-A is not cut, the D-U-X-four locus is silenced epigenetically. This is the first clinical readout of any epigenome-editing program doing what the modality was designed to do, and it lands ahead of Novartis-Avidity's del-brax FORTITUDE biomarker cohort readout in the second quarter and Arrowhead's ARO-D-U-X-four Phase one-two. Spotlight goes deep on what this means for the epigenome-editing class, the F-S-H-D competitive shape, and what Foresite and Calibr should take from a single-dose A-A-V-delivered epigenetic switch producing measurable phenotype reversal in a degenerative muscle disease.

Second, Moderna unveiled its first in-vivo C-A-R-T program at its Science Day on Thursday. M-R-N-A-six-thousand-seven is a lipid-nanoparticle-delivered, multiplexed-m-R-N-A construct that programs the patient's own T-cells in vivo to express a C-A-R targeting C-D-seven on pathogenic immune cells, with the downstream effect of deep B-cell depletion. Indication is a basket of B-cell-mediated autoimmune diseases starting with lupus. I-N-D-enabling studies are running now, first-in-human is targeted by end of twenty-twenty-seven. This is the program Moderna is positioning as the sentinel application of its in-vivo cell-therapy aspirations — and the read-through is two-fold. One, the in-vivo C-A-R-T autoimmune thesis that AbbVie validated paying eight-point-five-billion-dollars for Capstan Therapeutics last year is now Moderna's strategic frame too. Two, Moderna is making the case that targeted-L-N-P-plus-m-R-N-A is a competitive delivery vehicle against viral-vector and protein-engineered T-cell-engager approaches in this exact same space. For Foresite the read is that the in-vivo cell-therapy space is now Moderna, AbbVie-Capstan, AstraZeneca-EsoBiotec, plus Sana, Cabaletta, and the privately-funded in-vivo developers — the modality is no longer a single-company bet. The actual catalyst is twelve to eighteen months out, but the strategic-positioning announcement closes the gap between Big-Pharma in-vivo and biotech in-vivo by a full development cycle.

Third, Acadia Pharmaceuticals' Daybue — trofinetide for Rett syndrome — won a positive C-H-M-P opinion on re-examination in the European Medicines Agency's June meeting, reversing the negative opinion from February. The European Commission decision is due within sixty-seven days; if confirmed, Daybue becomes the first approved pharmacological therapy for Rett in the E-U. The drug was F-D-A-approved for pediatric and adult Rett in twenty twenty-three, generating about three-hundred-fifty-million-dollars in U-S sales last year. The win matters for two reasons — first, it is a rare reversal of a C-H-M-P negative opinion via the re-examination pathway, which is a procedural data point for anyone planning an E-M-A submission strategy on a rare-disease asset; and second, it extends the geography of one of the few approved Rett-syndrome assets at the exact moment Neurocrine, Anavex, and several gene-therapy developers are pushing follow-on Rett programs into late-stage trials.

Fourth, on the opposite side of the C-H-M-P signal book — the same June meeting recommended revoking the marketing authorization for Tavneos, Amgen's avacopan, in ANCA-associated vasculitis, following the post-marketing real-world safety review. Amgen acquired the asset in the twenty-twenty-three ChemoCentryx deal at three-point-seven-billion-dollars. There is also an F-D-A advisory-committee hearing now pending in the U-S — that turns Tavneos from a launched asset into a regulatory risk asset across both major markets simultaneously. The read-through for the C-five-a-receptor and complement-pathway competitive set is that the standard the regulators are now applying to launched complement-pathway anti-inflammatories is more aggressive than the standard at approval, which is a posture that affects every late-stage complement-pathway asset including Apellis follow-ons, the Novartis pegcetacoplan franchise, and Alexion-AstraZeneca's pipeline.

Fifth, the European Commission opened a formal antitrust investigation into Sanofi Friday over alleged disparagement of a rival's flu vaccine. The allegation is that Sanofi communications to physicians and pharmacists undercut competitor product positioning during the twenty-twenty-five-twenty-twenty-six flu season. This is the second major antitrust action of the quarter against a Big Pharma — following the C-M-A move on Roche-Genentech's distribution agreements earlier in the spring. The pattern matters because it adds a sales-and-marketing-conduct enforcement vector to the existing pricing-and-policy headwind on Big Pharma — and the structural read is that the antitrust authorities are now treating Big-Pharma promotional behavior in seasonal vaccines and in legacy franchises with the same scrutiny they apply to drug-pricing-and-formulary conduct.
