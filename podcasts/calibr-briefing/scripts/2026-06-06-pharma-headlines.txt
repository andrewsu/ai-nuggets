Good morning. It's Saturday, June sixth, twenty twenty-six, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. The Saturday roundup runs a little lighter and a little more thematic — five items today, with three of them converging on what's now the dominant clinical story of this week, which is autoimmune B-cell depletion. The European Alliance of Associations for Rheumatology Congress wraps up in London today, and the spotlight this afternoon goes deep on the question those three EULAR readouts are starting to answer together — whether autoimmune B-cell depletion is converging on autologous CAR-T cells, on off-the-shelf T-cell engagers, or on both, and where the real differentiation is going to live.

First, and this readout is happening right now as you listen — Cullinan Therapeutics is presenting initial clinical data this morning, Saturday, at the EULAR twenty twenty-six Congress, for C L N nine seventy-eight, a subcutaneously administered C D nineteen by C D three bispecific T-cell engager, in treatment-refractory rheumatoid arthritis and systemic lupus erythematosus. The two phase one studies are called OUTRACE-R-A and OUTRACE-S-L-E. The early efficacy signals across the available patient set are striking — four of five rheumatoid arthritis patients showed drops on the disease activity score for twenty-eight joints with erythrocyte sedimentation rate, and five of six lupus patients achieved a hybrid lupus disease activity index decrease of more than four points, which is the conventional threshold for a clinically meaningful response. B-cell depletion was robust in both peripheral blood and in tissue — Cullinan specifically called out synovial ultrasound improvement in the rheumatoid arthritis arm, which matters because tissue-resident memory B cells in the synovium are exactly what naked anti-C-D-twenty antibodies like rituximab have historically struggled to clear. This is the first company-sponsored data set for a C D nineteen-targeted T-cell engager in autoimmune disease, and the field is paying close attention.

Second, also at EULAR this week, Kyverna Therapeutics presented updated phase one data for miv-cel — also known as K Y V one-oh-one, their C D nineteen-targeted autologous CAR-T cell therapy — in anti-citrullinated-protein-antibody-positive, treatment-refractory rheumatoid arthritis. A single infusion produced deep C D nineteen-positive B-cell depletion, sustained autoantibody declines, and a predominantly naive B-cell repopulation pattern. The headline efficacy number was striking — two-thirds of patients, sixty-six point six percent, met the ACR-seventy response criterion by week thirty-six. The COMPARE study enrolled patients who had failed a median of six and a half prior biologic and targeted synthetic disease-modifying drugs, so that depth of response in such a refractory population is genuinely substantial. A phase two trial has now completed enrollment. The spotlight today will dig into why Kyverna's CAR-T data and Cullinan's T-cell-engager data are best read together, not as competitors.

Third, the lung-cancer event-free survival readout from ASCO twenty twenty-six that the field has been waiting for — Eli Lilly's adjuvant LIBRETTO-four-three-two phase three trial of selpercatinib, marketed as Retevmo, in patients with early-stage R-E-T-fusion-positive non-small-cell lung cancer after definitive surgery — was presented at ASCO and is now published in the New England Journal of Medicine. The trial randomized one hundred fifty-one stage one-B through three-A patients from twenty-two countries to selpercatinib or placebo for up to three years. The primary endpoint was hit by a very wide margin — selpercatinib reduced the risk of disease recurrence or death by eighty-three percent versus placebo. Twenty-four-month event-free survival was ninety-one point five percent on selpercatinib versus sixty-one point one percent on placebo. The take is that adjuvant tyrosine kinase inhibition with a selective R-E-T-fusion inhibitor is now a settled standard-of-care question, not an open one, in the small but high-need R-E-T-fusion-positive early-stage non-small-cell lung cancer population. The bigger commercial implication is for Lilly's positioning across the entire R-E-T franchise — Retevmo extends from advanced disease into the early-stage adjuvant setting, which is what the franchise has needed since Loxo's original phase three program established the advanced-stage indication.

Fourth, the Alnylam and Inceptive Nucleics strategic A-I collaboration announced earlier this week. Alnylam paid Inceptive thirty million dollars upfront in cash and equity, with up to two billion dollars in milestones plus tiered royalties, for a strategic collaboration to accelerate small interfering R-N-A design. Inceptive's foundation models will be applied to target sequence modeling, chemical modification space exploration, and preclinical candidate prediction. The structural point worth flagging: Alnylam has more than twenty years of proprietary siRNA design data, and the Inceptive deal monetizes that data asset by pairing it with generative A-I capacity that Alnylam doesn't want to build internally. This is the more disciplined version of the A-I drug-discovery deal pattern — fifteen million in cash, fifteen million in equity upfront, and the platform pays for itself only on milestone delivery. Inceptive's foundation models were originally trained for m-R-N-A design at scale and are now being repurposed for siRNA — that cross-modality transfer claim is what the industry will be watching.

Fifth and finally, Lilly's twin China-and-Korea deal day from earlier this week deserves a recap because the structural pattern is becoming more established with each iteration. Lilly licensed sonefpeglutide — Hanmi's long-acting G-L-P two analog in phase two for short bowel syndrome — for seventy-five million dollars upfront and up to a billion-point-two billion dollars in milestones. Hours later on the same day, Lilly disclosed a five-program research collaboration with China's Haisco worth up to roughly three billion dollars on eighty-seven million dollars upfront, with the program targets undisclosed. The G-L-P two analog is the headline science — long-acting G-L-P two for short bowel syndrome is the kind of niche-but-defensible nuclear-receptor-adjacent indication Lilly has been opportunistically grabbing through partnership, and Hanmi's half-life-extension platform is the technical asset. The thematic point: Lilly continues to assemble a multi-receptor gastrointestinal biologics portfolio without buying the underlying companies, partnering rather than acquiring. This is the same pattern as the prior Lilly-Innovent Tyvyt arrangement years ago and the Lilly-Roivant late-stage discovery deals of twenty twenty-five.
