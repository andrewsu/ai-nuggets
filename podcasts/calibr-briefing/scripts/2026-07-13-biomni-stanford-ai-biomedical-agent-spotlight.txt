Spotlight, Monday July thirteenth. On Thursday July ninth, Kexin Huang, Yuanhao Qu, Jure Leskovec, Le Cong, and colleagues at Stanford published in Science a paper titled Autonomous Biomedical Research with an Artificial Intelligence Agent — introducing Biomni, a general-purpose biomedical A-I research agent designed to autonomously execute a wide spectrum of scientific workflows across twenty-five biomedical subfields. Biomni is not a task-specific model. It is a generalist agentic system built on top of a frontier large language model and equipped with retrieval-augmented planning plus code-based execution over a hundred-fifty-plus bioinformatics tools, fifty-nine curated databases, and a hundred-plus molecular-modeling and analysis software packages — an entire environment discovered automatically by an action-discovery sub-agent that mined tools, databases, and protocols from tens of thousands of publications. In September twenty twenty-five Huang and Qu spun the platform out into Phylo, a Palo Alto startup that in February twenty twenty-six raised a thirteen-and-a-half-million-dollar seed co-led by Andreessen Horowitz and Menlo Ventures — Menlo through the Anthropic-linked Anthology fund — with Zetta Venture Partners, Conviction, S-V Angel, and Valkyrie joining the syndicate. The prototype has already been used by more than fifteen thousand scientists to run a hundred-thousand different research workflows in nine months, making Biomni the most widely used A-I co-scientist system in biomedicine. Seven threads on the architecture, the benchmark, the case studies, the head-to-head-in-class competition, the commercial trajectory, the read for A-I-native drug discovery, and what it changes.

First, the architectural intuition — why the generalist bet matters. The dominant A-I-in-drug-discovery story of the last two years has been proprietary task-specific platforms. Xaira Therapeutics built its virtual cell foundation model with four-point-nine billion parameters for cell-state prediction. Iambic Therapeutics focused on ligand generative models plus molecular property prediction. Isomorphic Labs owns AlphaFold Three-based structure-and-interaction prediction. Each is a bet that if you go deep on one task with proprietary data, you win. Biomni bets the other direction — that you can compose a general-purpose research agent by combining an off-the-shelf frontier language model with a large discovered library of biomedical tools and databases, and let the agent choose which tool to call at each step of a research workflow. The architecture has three pieces. One, an action-discovery agent that mines the literature — the paper reports scanning tens of thousands of publications across twenty-five biomedical subfields — to identify usable tools and their I-O contracts and add them to a unified action space. Two, a generalist agentic loop that combines large-language-model reasoning with retrieval-augmented planning — the agent retrieves relevant tools and protocols per-task at inference — plus code-based execution, meaning the agent writes and runs Python. Three, dynamic composition — no predefined templates, no rigid task flows, meaning the same underlying architecture handles causal gene prioritization on Monday and a molecular cloning protocol on Tuesday without retraining. That is the philosophical bet. The Xaira architecture is deep-and-narrow. The Biomni architecture is broad-and-composable. Both can be right for different classes of problem.

Second, the benchmark story. Systematic evaluation on a four-hundred-forty-three-question benchmark covering causal gene prioritization, drug repurposing, rare-disease diagnosis, microbiome analysis, and molecular cloning showed Biomni achieving strong generalization with zero task-specific prompt tuning — meaning the same base agent, no fine-tuning per task. Reported average accuracy runs around fifty-seven percent on that benchmark, significantly outperforming the underlying general large language model without the agentic scaffolding. That is the key comparison. The improvement over the base L-L-M is what quantifies the value the retrieval-plus-tools-plus-code-execution architecture adds. But the eye-catching result is not the aggregate accuracy — it is the zero-shot generalization across heterogeneous tasks. That is the property that lets an academic lab try one thing on Biomni today and another thing on Biomni next week without a machine-learning engineer in the loop. The failure modes are the standard L-L-M ones — hallucination in intermediate reasoning, brittleness to prompt drift, occasional wrong-tool selection — and the paper is up-front about them, but the base-rate improvement over an L-L-M with no tools is the headline.

Third, the real-world case studies. Beyond the benchmark, the paper walks through demonstrations that go beyond stylized questions. One case analyzed four-hundred-fifty-eight glucose-monitor datasets to identify thermogenic patterns — the kind of multi-modal biomedical time-series analysis that traditionally requires a bioinformatics-plus-endocrinology-team days of work. Genomic sequence analysis case studies generated hypotheses about causal genes and mechanisms that a bench scientist could go test. The molecular cloning protocol generation case — where Biomni proposes primer designs, restriction-enzyme choices, and gel-verification steps — is the one wet-bench biologists have found most useful, and is likely a big part of the fifteen-thousand-lab adoption footprint. That is the case-study pattern. Biomni is not doing prospective wet-lab discovery independently at scale, but it is compressing the interpretive-planning-and-protocol-drafting layer of research from days to minutes for a very wide range of tasks.

Fourth, the head-to-head-in-class dynamics against Google DeepMind's Co-Scientist. In May twenty twenty-six Google DeepMind published a Nature paper introducing Co-Scientist, a multi-agent Gemini-based A-I system that iteratively generates, debates, and evolves novel scientific hypotheses. Co-Scientist is architecturally similar to Biomni in that both are agentic systems that layer L-L-M reasoning over a research workflow, and it demonstrated positive initial results in three biomedical applications — drug repurposing for acute myeloid leukemia, novel-target discovery for liver fibrosis, and mechanism-of-action work on antimicrobial resistance. The liver-fibrosis case with Stanford's Gary Peltz identified a repurposing candidate that blocked ninety-one percent of a scarring-linked response in lab tests. The Co-Scientist multi-agent architecture is more explicitly debate-and-consensus — one agent proposes, another critiques, a third evolves — while Biomni is a single-agent-plus-tool-library composition play. The key differentiators. One, Biomni is open-source and Co-Scientist is a Google DeepMind proprietary system. Two, Biomni's action space is discovered automatically from published tools rather than curated internally by DeepMind. Three, Biomni ships as a usable platform for external labs today via Biomni Lab from Phylo, while Co-Scientist has been available primarily through DeepMind's academic collaborators. Four, Biomni is documented as autonomously executing complete workflows including code — file inputs to file outputs — while Co-Scientist has focused more on hypothesis generation and debate at the natural-language level. The two systems are the two dominant published architectures for a generalist biomedical A-I agent, and both are validated but with different footprints — Google DeepMind having the deeper Gemini plus TPU-plus-scale advantage, Biomni-Phylo having the openness plus already-adopted-by-fifteen-thousand-labs advantage. This is the near-term architectural competition to watch.

Fifth, the commercial trajectory. Phylo, the Stanford spinout that commercialized Biomni, raised its thirteen-and-a-half-million-dollar seed in February twenty twenty-six. The syndicate is unusually strong for a seed. Andreessen Horowitz and Menlo Ventures co-led. Menlo participated through Anthology, the Anthropic-affiliated venture fund — meaning Phylo is one of a handful of publicly identified Anthropic-adjacent biomedical bets, and the read is that Anthropic wants tools-and-tasks agentic use cases in biomedicine on top of its models. Zetta Venture Partners, Conviction, S-V Angel, and Valkyrie also joined. The scientific advisory board includes Nobel laureate Carolyn Bertozzi and CRISPR pioneer Feng Zhang — the pair signal a commitment to real-lab impact rather than pure computer-science demo. The product roadmap is Biomni Lab, the integrated A-I-driven biology workspace that combines the underlying agent with an experiment-orchestration layer, and an Academic Lab Program keeping the underlying code open source. The business model is a paid tier for pharma and biotech companies with private-data-and-tool integration and support commitments. The peer set. Chai Discovery, Recursion, and a handful of others are targeting the same general-purpose biomedical A-I space, but Phylo is the one with the Science-published architecture, the fifteen-thousand-lab footprint, and the two-Nobel-laureate-plus-CRISPR-pioneer advisory board. That is a highly credible early-stage position.

Sixth, the read for the A-I-native drug-discovery peer set. Xaira, Iambic, Isomorphic, Chai Discovery, and the newer Chinese A-I entrants have raised hundreds of millions to billions of dollars in the last two years to build proprietary A-I platforms for target-discovery-plus-molecule-design pipelines. Biomni is not a direct competitor — it does not run its own drug pipeline. But it changes the ambient economics of A-I-in-drug-discovery in three specific ways. One, base-rate. Any academic or biotech lab can now do meaningful A-I-augmented biomedical work with a free open-source generalist tool. That raises the bar for what a proprietary platform needs to deliver — you need to be measurably better than a Biomni-plus-frontier-L-L-M baseline on your target class, not merely better than a bench-only workflow. Two, tool-ecosystem. Biomni's action-discovery layer makes any published biomedical tool automatically available to fifteen thousand labs, which increases the return on releasing your platform's inference A-P-I to the community rather than keeping it fully closed — because if Xaira publishes an A-P-I, Biomni users can call it. That could shift the platform-openness gradient across the peer set. Three, target-selection versus drug design. Biomni does the interpretive-planning-and-target-selection layer well. It does not yet do de novo molecule generation at Xaira-plus-Isomorphic level. The natural stack is Biomni-plus-a-target-discovery-workflow feeding a proprietary molecule-generation platform for the medicinal-chemistry step. For the Calibr-Skaggs platform bet — hit-discovery-plus-target-selection at scale, then drug-design later — this is a favorable architecture. Biomni-plus-Calibr proprietary target biology plus a proprietary-medchem platform is a plausible stack.

Seventh, next catalysts. First, the natural next paper. Phylo is well-positioned to publish prospective wet-lab validation — Biomni proposes hypothesis, wet lab executes, hits get reported — the way DeepMind Co-Scientist's Peltz-Stanford liver-fibrosis validation ran. That is the credibility-multiplier next milestone. Second, the underlying-model upgrade. Biomni is L-L-M-agnostic, so as GPT, Claude, and Gemini improve the whole system inherits the gains. That is not linear scaling but it is a compounding tailwind. Third, competitive next moves. Google DeepMind Co-Scientist will publish more biomedical case studies. Anthropic, given its Anthology stake in Phylo, could publish something adjacent using Claude. OpenAI has been quieter in this space but is presumably watching. Fourth, adoption metrics. The fifteen-thousand-lab-hundred-thousand-workflow number is likely to double within a year given the free-open-source distribution. Fifth, regulatory and reproducibility. Journals and F-D-A-adjacent bodies are watching how A-I-generated results get logged, validated, and reproduced. Biomni's code-execution architecture inherently produces reproducible artifacts — the code and outputs are captured — which is a compliance advantage over pure-chat interfaces. Sixth, direct Phylo commercial deals. Pharma and biotech companies wanting private-data-and-tool integration are the paying customers. Watch for the first named-pharma deal announcement, which would substantially validate the commercial thesis.

Bottom line. On Thursday Kexin Huang and Jure Leskovec's group at Stanford published in Science the first widely peer-reviewed general-purpose biomedical A-I agent to demonstrate strong benchmark generalization plus large-scale real-world lab adoption. Biomni is open-source, agentic, code-executing, and equipped with a hundred-fifty-plus tools and fifty-nine databases discovered automatically from published literature. Fifteen thousand labs and a hundred-thousand workflows in nine months. The commercial spinout Phylo — a-sixteen-Z-plus-Menlo-slash-Anthropic-Anthology-backed with Bertozzi and Feng Zhang on the S-A-B — is now the leading independent A-I-co-scientist company. The architectural bet against Google DeepMind Co-Scientist is single-agent-plus-tool-library-plus-openness versus multi-agent-plus-Gemini-plus-DeepMind-scale. For the A-I-native drug-discovery peer set, Biomni raises the bar for what a proprietary platform must deliver, changes the platform-openness gradient, and complements rather than replaces molecule-design-focused platforms like Xaira, Iambic, Isomorphic, and their Chinese peers. For the Calibr-Skaggs platform bet, Biomni-plus-proprietary-target-biology-plus-proprietary-medchem is a plausible stack. Next catalysts include Phylo's first prospective wet-lab validation paper, base-model upgrades, competitive publications from DeepMind and Anthropic-linked teams, and Phylo's first named-pharma commercial deal.
