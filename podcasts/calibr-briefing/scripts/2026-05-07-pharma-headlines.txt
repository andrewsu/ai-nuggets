Hey Travis, it's Nigel with your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing for Thursday, May 7th, 2026. The big story today is GSK going back to China for siRNA — a one-billion-dollar deal for an ALK7-targeted oligonucleotide in obesity that lands directly adjacent to Calibr's MASH biologic territory. Argenx posted a blowout Q1, Bayer is buying its way into ophthalmology, and Lilly is committing another four-and-a-half billion dollars to Indiana manufacturing. Here's the rundown.

Number one — and this is today's spotlight. GSK signed a one-billion-dollar-plus licensing deal yesterday with Suzhou-based SiranBio for SA030, a small interfering RNA targeting activin receptor-like kinase 7 — ALK7, encoded by ACVR1C — currently in Phase 1 for obesity and metabolic disease. Fifty-five million dollars upfront, the rest in milestones, GSK gets ex-Greater China rights, SiranBio runs Phase 1 in China. ALK7 is the same target Arrowhead is chasing with ARO-ALK7, which started Phase 1/2a in New Zealand last year. The biology is clean — adipose-restricted expression, human loss-of-function variants drive lower abdominal fat and lower type 2 diabetes risk, preclinical models show roughly fifty percent fat-mass reduction with lean mass preservation. This is GSK's second one-billion-plus China siRNA deal in three months after the Frontier Biotechnologies agreement in February, and it's a structural signal about where big pharma thinks the post-incretin obesity market is heading. Spotlight in a few minutes on what this means for the MASH adjacency, the activin axis as a Calibr-relevant pathway, and the body-composition framework that's increasingly going to define the next-generation MASH benchmark.

Number two — Argenx reported Q1 2026 before the open and the print is one of the cleanest in big-cap biotech this year. One-point-three billion dollars in global product sales, up sixty-three percent year-over-year. Operating profit three hundred ninety-four million versus one hundred thirty-nine million in the year-ago quarter. Net profit of three hundred sixty-six million dollars and a four-point-nine billion dollar cash position. Vyvgart is still the only commercial asset and the franchise is now firmly in the durable-growth phase. The PDUFA date for AChR-antibody-negative generalized myasthenia gravis lands May tenth — Saturday — which would expand the gMG label by the seronegative, MuSK-positive, and LRP4-positive populations. On the pipeline, the ADAPT OCULUS Phase 3 readout in ocular MG was positive and was presented at AAN; the myositis ALKIVIA readout is on deck for Q3. Argenx is the case study right now of what disciplined FcRn-class commercial execution looks like.

Number three — Bayer announced plans to acquire Perfuse Therapeutics for up to two-point-four-five billion dollars, with three hundred million up front and the rest in milestones. Perfuse's lead asset is a mid-stage glaucoma and diabetic retinopathy candidate. This is Bayer's largest drug-side acquisition since the AskBio deal in 2020. Strategically the deal shores up Bayer's ophthalmology franchise — Eylea's biosimilar pressure has been intensifying — and adds a metabolic-disease-adjacent eye program in diabetic retinopathy. Read it as a shoring-up move rather than a pipeline-transforming one, but it's still the largest pharma M&A announced this week.

Number four — Angelini Pharma agreed to acquire Catalyst Pharmaceuticals for four-point-one billion dollars. Catalyst commercializes Firdapse for Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome and Fycompa for epilepsy, plus an early-stage neurology pipeline. For Italy-based Angelini this is a transformational deal that vaults them into the global specialty neurology tier. It's also the seventh biotech acquisition in the last week — the sector's M&A pace in 2026 is now tracking ahead of any year on record, with UCB-Candid, Ipsen-Albireo, Gilead-Arcellx, Bayer-Perfuse, and now Angelini-Catalyst all closing or announced inside the last fourteen days.

Number five — Eli Lilly committed an additional four-and-a-half billion dollars yesterday to expand its Indiana manufacturing footprint, with new facilities in Lebanon and Indianapolis focused on incretin and biologics manufacturing. Cumulative US manufacturing investment from Lilly is now north of fifty billion dollars over five years, almost all of it tied to the orforglipron and tirzepatide ramp. The strategic question for the rest of the industry is whether anyone outside Lilly and Novo can build the API and fill-finish capacity to support the obesity volume curve — and the answer for now is no.

Number six — Entrada Therapeutics announces topline results today from Cohort 1 of the Phase 1/2 ELEVATE-44-201 study of ENTR-601-44, an exon-44-skipping oligonucleotide for Duchenne muscular dystrophy delivered through Entrada's endosomal escape vehicle platform. Six milligrams per kilogram, eight patients, the DMC has already cleared dose escalation to twelve milligrams per kilogram in Cohort 2. The bar today is muscle dystrophin restoration that beats the approved Sarepta exon-skippers — which is a low bar mechanistically — and a clean tolerability profile through twelve weeks. The EEV platform read-through goes well beyond DMD; it's one of the leading non-LNP intracellular delivery technologies, and the data today set the credibility window for the broader oligonucleotide field.

Number seven — Gilead is trimming one hundred and eight jobs at the former Arcellx Redwood City site as the post-acquisition integration takes shape. The reduction is concentrated in early discovery and translational functions; the anito-cel BCMA CAR-T program is unaffected. The signal worth noting is that even the most successful CAR-T acquisitions are moving to consolidate manufacturing and clinical operations into the acquirer's footprint immediately — autologous CAR-T economics still demand operational scale, and standalone Bay Area sites are not surviving the integration logic. The question this poses for the in vivo CAR-T field — which Calibr is tracking actively — is whether the next generation of in vivo platforms collapses the manufacturing burden enough to make geographic decentralization viable again.

And number eight — BeOne Medicines, the rebranded BeiGene, culled five oncology programs and a Phase 2 autoimmune asset in the Q1 update yesterday. The cuts target second-generation BTK, BCL2, and pan-RAF assets that were no longer differentiated against Imbruvica, venetoclax, and the new Lilly RAF chemistry. The Brukinsa franchise remains the core, the solid-tumor pipeline pivot continues, and the headcount reduction is in low single-digit hundreds. A useful tape on what late-stage portfolio rationalization looks like when the lead asset is performing — you see the second-tier programs trimmed before the spend hits the headline.
