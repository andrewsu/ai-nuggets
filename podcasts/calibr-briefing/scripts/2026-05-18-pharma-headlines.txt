Good morning, Travis. It's Monday, May 18th, 2026, and this is your Calibr-Skaggs Daily Briefing. Markets just opened, the news cycle is back on a weekday rhythm, and there is one clean blockbuster approval today plus a Phase 3 flop, an FDA leadership story that is still developing, and a clinical hold worth watching.

Number one. AstraZeneca's baxdrostat — brand name Baxfendy — was approved by the FDA this morning for adults with hypertension that is not adequately controlled on existing antihypertensives. It is the first aldosterone synthase inhibitor ever cleared in the United States. Mechanism: it selectively inhibits CYP11B2, the enzyme responsible for aldosterone synthesis in the adrenal gland, which in this patient population is the upstream driver of treatment-resistant blood pressure. AstraZeneca picked baxdrostat up in 2023 with the one point three billion dollar acquisition of CinCor Pharma, including contingent value rights pegged to FDA submission, so today's clearance triggers a five hundred million dollar payout to legacy CinCor shareholders. Approval is supported by the BaxHTN Phase 3 — seven hundred ninety-six patients, two milligram and one milligram doses on top of standard care, statistically significant placebo-adjusted reductions in seated systolic blood pressure at twelve weeks, and all secondary endpoints met — plus the Bax24 trial, which showed a fourteen point zero millimeters of mercury placebo-adjusted reduction in twenty-four-hour ambulatory systolic blood pressure in resistant patients. Analyst peak sales estimates are four to five billion dollars. AstraZeneca used a priority review voucher to get to today. The UAE approved the drug last Friday — first regulatory clearance in the world — so the U.S. now follows. This is today's spotlight.

Number two. Regeneron's fianlimab, the LAG-3 inhibitor, failed its Phase 3 primary endpoint in first-line unresectable or metastatic melanoma. The trial randomized about fifteen hundred forty-six patients to high-dose fianlimab plus cemiplimab, low-dose fianlimab plus cemiplimab, or pembrolizumab, the Keytruda monotherapy comparator. The high-dose combination produced a numeric median progression-free survival of eleven point five months versus six point four months for pembrolizumab — a five point one month numeric advantage — but did not reach statistical significance on the primary endpoint. No new safety signals. Fierce led with this as their morning story; analysts at multiple shops were quoted as, in their words, shaking their heads, since the LAG-3 hypothesis is now zero for two in melanoma at the pivotal stage if you count the RELATIVITY-098 readout from Bristol's relatlimab combo last year. Regeneron says it will still pursue regulatory filings citing the totality of the data, and a separate Phase 3 head-to-head versus Opdualag is still running and could provide a cleaner comparison. The strategic read is that LAG-3 plus PD-1 may simply not move the needle enough over high-dose PD-1 alone in front-line melanoma, and Regeneron has now lost two defining catalysts in this pipeline within twelve months.

Number three. The FDA leadership void widened on Friday. Tracy Beth Høeg, who had been acting director of CDER for the past six months, was fired by the agency on May fifteenth. She has been replaced on an acting basis by Michael Davis, who has been deputy CDER director for about a year. Høeg told MD Reports that two FDA officials came to her office and gave her a choice between resigning and being terminated; she refused to sign and was dismissed, with the decision described as coming from, quote, way above their pay grade. She is now the fifth person to lead CDER in the fifteen months of the current administration, and her firing came just days after Commissioner Marty Makary's exit, which yesterday's spotlight covered. The practical takeaway for everyone watching pipelines is unchanged: every senior FDA seat now sits on an acting director, the Pazdur petition is still circulating, and decision-making timelines on novel modalities — gene therapies, in vivo cell therapies, ASOs — are going to keep widening until permanent leadership is named. Today's baxdrostat approval, by contrast, came through cleanly because cardiovascular small molecules are exactly the kind of well-trodden review track that CDER can run on autopilot. Novel modalities will not get that benefit.

Number four. Aardvark Therapeutics had a hard week. On May fourteenth the FDA placed a full clinical hold on ARD-101, Aardvark's lead program — a bitter-taste-receptor agonist that was in a Phase 3 trial called HERO in Prader-Willi syndrome for hyperphagia, the disabling chronic hunger that defines the disorder. The hold was triggered by a cardiac safety signal that emerged from a healthy volunteer study earlier this year, which the company had voluntarily disclosed in February. The Phase 3 trial and its open-label extension are now both suspended. Aardvark said it will unblind the HERO and OLE data to give regulators and investigators what they need to determine whether the program can continue. The stock dropped about thirty percent on the news. ARD-101 had been one of the more promising oral candidates for Prader-Willi hyperphagia — a space where Soleno's diazoxide choline is the only approved treatment — so this is also a competitive opening. Worth watching for whether the cardiac signal turns out to be a clean mechanism-related toxicity or an off-target effect that a backup molecule could resolve.

Number five. A scan of the strategic landscape and forward look. Fierce Biotech ran an analysis piece on Friday under the headline, quote, after Lilly's seven billion Kelonia deal, are there any in vivo CAR-T biotechs left to buy. The framing is that Lilly's April twentieth purchase of Kelonia Therapeutics — three point two five billion upfront and up to seven billion in total — combined with AbbVie's Capstan buyout from last summer has essentially mopped up the named in vivo CAR-T platforms that have human dosing or imminent INDs. Create Medicines, which we covered yesterday at one hundred twenty-two million Series B, is now the largest still-private clinical-stage in vivo CAR platform and would be the obvious next target if a third large pharma decides it needs in. Separately, STAT ran a special report this morning by Damian Garde under the title, quote, the China question is tearing biotech apart, on the deepening industry split over Chinese pharmaceutical opportunities versus geopolitical risk — directly relevant after last week's BMS-Hengrui fifteen point two billion dollar thirteen-asset deal. On the calendar, EASL Congress 2026 opens in Barcelona on May twenty-seventh and runs through May thirtieth, with a packed FGF21 and MASH program lined up — that is the most concentrated catalyst window for the focus area in the next two weeks.
