Welcome to the Calibr-Skaggs Spotlight for Thursday, May twenty-eighth.

Yesterday D and D Pharmatech released forty-eight-week histology data from its DD01 dash D-N dash zero two Phase 2 trial of zabopegdutide — a once-weekly GLP-1 slash glucagon dual agonist — in biopsy-confirmed F1 through F3 MASH. The numbers are striking on small sample sizes, and they land into a MASH competitive landscape that has just become considerably more crowded over the last year. Today we work through what the data say, where they sit against efruxifermin, resmetirom, tirzepatide, and the rest of the field, and what the dual-receptor design thesis implies for Calibr's IND-enabling MASH biologic.

Let's start with the data. The DD01 dash D-N dash zero two study enrolled sixty-seven overweight or obese adults with biopsy-confirmed MASH and fibrosis stage F1 through F3 across twelve U.S. centers. Patients received a two-week titration at twenty milligrams once weekly followed by forty milligrams once weekly of zabopegdutide, or matching placebo, for forty-eight weeks. The per-protocol population with paired baseline and end-of-treatment biopsies was thirty-five — sixteen on active treatment and nineteen on placebo. On the primary histological endpoint of at least one-stage fibrosis improvement with no worsening of MASH, fifty percent of zabopegdutide patients responded versus fifteen-point-eight percent on placebo. That's a placebo-adjusted effect of thirty-four percent. On MASH resolution without worsening of fibrosis, sixty-two-point-five percent versus five-point-three percent — a placebo-adjusted effect of fifty-seven percent. On the dual composite — both fibrosis improvement and MASH resolution in the same patient — thirty-seven-point-five percent versus five-point-three percent. Tolerability was consistent with the GLP-1 class. Gastrointestinal adverse events were the most common, described as mild-to-moderate and transient, with discontinuation rates matching the broader incretin field.

Now the obvious caveat. Sixteen patients on active treatment is a very small denominator. The confidence intervals around these point estimates are wide, and per-protocol exclusion of patients without paired biopsies introduces selection bias relative to an intent-to-treat analysis. This is a Phase 2 signal, not a Phase 3 efficacy estimate. But the effect sizes are large enough that even at the lower bound of plausible confidence, zabopegdutide is competitive with the strongest histological signals in the field. The data are simultaneously being presented at the EASL congress in Amsterdam this week.

Mechanism. Zabopegdutide is a GLP-1 receptor and glucagon receptor dual agonist. The GLP-1 component drives weight loss through appetite suppression and delayed gastric emptying — the same mechanism that has made semaglutide and tirzepatide commercial behemoths. The glucagon component does something different. It acts directly on hepatic glucagon receptors to increase fatty-acid oxidation and reduce hepatic steatosis. So zabopegdutide is doing two things at once — peripheral weight loss through GLP-1, and direct hepatic fat clearance through glucagon. The clinical question has always been whether you can engage glucagon enough to clear liver fat without driving hyperglycemia and undoing the metabolic benefit. Zabopegdutide's safety profile suggests they have that ratio approximately right.

Now the competitive context, because this is where it matters for Calibr. MASH has four mechanism families with histological-endpoint data in the field. First, thyroid hormone receptor beta agonists — resmetirom from Madrigal, sold as Rezdiffra, the only currently approved MASH drug, accelerated approval from March twenty twenty-four. In MAESTRO-NASH, resmetirom at the eighty-milligram dose delivered twenty-six percent fibrosis improvement versus fourteen percent placebo. Zabopegdutide at fifty percent fibrosis improvement is on its face nearly double resmetirom — though the placebo response is also higher here and the populations are not perfectly comparable. Second, FGF21 analogs. Efruxifermin from Akero — now Novo Nordisk after the five-point-two-billion-dollar acquisition last October. In HARMONY at ninety-six weeks, efruxifermin at fifty milligrams delivered forty-nine percent fibrosis improvement versus twenty-four percent placebo. Pegozafermin from 89bio had a similar signal in ENLIVEN. And efimosfermin alfa, the once-monthly long-acting FGF21 that GSK acquired from Boston Pharmaceuticals for two billion last May, delivered fibrosis improvements in Phase 2. Third, GLP-1 monotherapy. Tirzepatide from Lilly, the GLP-1 slash GIP dual, hit fibrosis endpoints in SYNERGY-NASH. Fourth, GLP-1 plus something else — semaglutide combinations, and now zabopegdutide as GLP-1 slash glucagon. The Hengrui-Kailera tri-agonist we covered in headlines — H-R-S four seven two nine — is the next escalation, adding GIP to GLP-1 plus glucagon.

The pattern is clear. The field is moving from single-receptor mechanisms toward multi-receptor designs. The thesis is that MASH is metabolically pleiotropic — fat accumulation, fibrogenic stellate-cell activation, hepatocyte injury, systemic insulin resistance — and engaging multiple receptors at once gives you a broader pharmacology that hits more of those nodes. The FGF21 class already engages multiple receptors — FGFR1c, FGFR2c, FGFR3c with beta-Klotho as the co-receptor. Dual GLP-1 slash glucagon is a smaller, more focused multi-receptor design. The Hengrui tri-agonist adds GIP. The direction of travel is more receptors, not fewer.

For Calibr's MASH biologic, which has been described as dual-acting on multiple receptors and is currently IND-enabling, this competitive picture is both validating and threatening. Validating because the dual or multi-receptor design thesis is now winning at the histological-endpoint level. Three different multi-receptor approaches — FGF21, GLP-1 slash glucagon, and GLP-1 slash GIP slash glucagon — are all generating better fibrosis numbers than single-receptor incumbents like resmetirom. Multi-receptor engagement is increasingly the consensus answer to MASH. Threatening because zabopegdutide is now a clinically de-risked precedent for the GLP-1 slash glucagon dual, efruxifermin and efimosfermin are de-risked precedents for FGF21, and tirzepatide is a de-risked precedent for GLP-1 slash GIP. If Calibr's biologic sits in an adjacent receptor space, it needs differentiation beyond just being dual-acting — differentiation on dosing frequency, on receptor selectivity, on tissue-distribution profile, or on combinability with existing GLP-1 backbones.

The dosing-frequency dimension matters a lot here. Efruxifermin is once-weekly. Efimosfermin is once-monthly — that's GSK's main differentiation lever over Akero-Novo. Zabopegdutide is once-weekly forty milligrams. Resmetirom is once-daily oral. If Calibr's biologic can credibly support every-two-week or once-monthly dosing without compromising efficacy, that's a meaningful commercial wedge in a class where patients will likely be on therapy for years, potentially indefinitely.

The combinability dimension matters even more, and it's where I think the next phase of MASH competition will be won. The interesting strategic question is what gets added on top of a base GLP-1 — likely tirzepatide, retatrutide, or semaglutide — to address the residual liver disease that GLP-1 monotherapy doesn't fully resolve. FGF21 added to GLP-1 is one combination thesis, and it's the one Akero-Novo and GSK are clearly pursuing. Activin-axis ligand traps like GSK's newly-acquired H-S two thirty-five — which we just covered in headlines — are another. And dual-acting biologics that engage liver-specific targets without overlapping the GLP-1 pharmacology are a third lane. Calibr's design choices on receptor pairing should be made with that combination architecture in mind, because monotherapy MASH is rapidly becoming the smaller commercial opportunity inside what will likely become a combination paradigm by the time the first wave of biologics launches.

Three takeaways. One, the multi-receptor MASH thesis is becoming the consensus answer. Zabopegdutide's forty-eight-week histology numbers are another data point in a growing pile, even if the per-protocol N of thirty-five is small enough that Phase 3 will be the real test. Two, the competitive moat in this class is shifting from "do you have fibrosis-improvement data" to "what is your dosing frequency, what is your tolerability, and what is your combinability with GLP-1 backbones." Three, watch the readouts from efimosfermin's Phase 3 once-monthly program, from efruxifermin's SYNCHRONY studies, and from the early Hengrui tri-agonist MASH work. Those are the data points that will decide which dual or multi-receptor design becomes the standard-of-care MASH backbone by twenty twenty-eight, and they're the competitive references Calibr's biologic will be measured against at every regulatory and commercial inflection from IND forward.
