Spotlight, Saturday July fourth. Thursday at the ESMO Gastrointestinal Cancers Congress, Revolution Medicines presented Phase one-two data on zoldonrasib — the company's oral K-RAS G-twelve-D-selective covalent RAS-on inhibitor — in two combinations for RAS G-twelve-D metastatic pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma. First-line zoldonrasib plus modified FOLFIRINOX in forty-one patients delivered an objective response rate of eighty-two percent and a disease-control rate of ninety-six percent. First-line zoldonrasib plus gemcitabine and nab-paclitaxel in forty patients delivered an objective response rate of sixty-one percent and a disease-control rate of ninety percent. Second-line-plus zoldonrasib combined with daraxonrasib — RevMed's pan-K-RAS-on inhibitor — in thirty patients delivered an objective response rate of fifty percent and a median progression-free survival of nine-point-six months. This is the biggest single data point ever recorded in K-RAS G-twelve-D pancreatic cancer, and it lands in a disease that has resisted every mechanism in oncology's toolkit for four decades. Six threads.

First, the disease and why the numbers are so striking. Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma kills roughly fifty thousand Americans a year and is the third-leading cause of cancer death in the United States. About eighty percent of patients are diagnosed at advanced or metastatic stage. First-line standard of care is modified FOLFIRINOX or gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel — both are decades-old chemotherapy regimens. Modified FOLFIRINOX alone in first-line metastatic pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma delivers an objective response rate of roughly thirty percent and a median overall survival around eleven months. Gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel is similar. Nothing added on top — not immunotherapy, not P-A-R-P inhibitors outside the small B-R-C-A-mutant subset, not K-RAS-targeted therapy — has moved the needle. An eighty-two percent objective response rate in first-line zoldonrasib plus modified FOLFIRINOX is roughly two-and-a-half times the historical control benchmark. If that magnitude holds up at the Phase three level, it is a genuine mechanistic breakthrough for a disease that has been the graveyard of drug-development programs since the nineteen-eighties.

Second, K-RAS G-twelve-D specifically. K-RAS is mutated in about ninety percent of pancreatic cancers, and the G-twelve-D substitution — a glycine-to-aspartate change at position twelve — accounts for about forty percent of those K-RAS-mutant pancreatic cases and about twenty-nine percent of all RAS-driven cancers across tumor types. Roughly sixty-one thousand new RAS G-twelve-D cancer patients per year in the United States. Nothing is approved for G-twelve-D. The Shokat-lab-derived first-generation covalent K-RAS G-twelve-C inhibitors — sotorasib and adagrasib — do not touch G-twelve-D because they rely on the mutant-cysteine side chain that only exists in G-twelve-C. G-twelve-D presents an aspartate — a negatively-charged carboxylate — instead. That difference is a fundamentally different medicinal-chemistry problem. Mirati's MRTX-eleven-thirty-three was the first serious G-twelve-D attempt — a non-covalent switch-two-pocket binder — but the compound has struggled with formulation, pharmacokinetics, and clinical profile. Zoldonrasib solves the chemistry problem from a completely different angle by targeting the RAS-on state rather than the G-D-P-bound RAS-off state.

Third, the RAS-on inhibitor concept. Traditional K-RAS G-twelve-C inhibitors — sotorasib, adagrasib, and yesterday's spotlight compound divarasib — trap K-RAS in its inactive G-D-P-bound state. That mechanism depends on two things: the intrinsic G-T-P-ase cycling that constantly returns K-RAS to the G-D-P-bound conformation, and the mutant cysteine at position twelve to form the covalent bond. RevMed's RAS-on inhibitors instead engage K-RAS in its active G-T-P-bound state — the on-state, where K-RAS is signaling to R-A-F and P-I-three-K. The compound is a tri-complex — it recruits cyclophilin-A as a chaperone protein to form a ternary complex with K-RAS-G-T-P, and the ternary complex blocks effector binding directly at the on-state interface. That mechanism does not require a mutant cysteine, which is what unlocks the door to non-G-twelve-C mutants. Zoldonrasib is the G-twelve-D-selective variant of the platform; daraxonrasib is the pan-K-RAS-on inhibitor that hits multiple G-twelve mutants including G-twelve-D, G-twelve-V, G-twelve-R, and G-twelve-A. Daraxonrasib has been the primary RevMed development asset and already has AACR-scale monotherapy data in pancreatic cancer; zoldonrasib layers on the G-twelve-D-selective specificity for the largest single mutation slice.

Fourth, the two combinations and what each tells you. The zoldonrasib-plus-chemotherapy Phase one-two data shows that layering the RAS-on inhibitor on top of the two standard-of-care chemotherapy backbones — modified FOLFIRINOX and gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel — gives you a large additive response benefit without unmanageable added toxicity. Grade-three-plus treatment-related adverse events were sixty-one percent in the modified FOLFIRINOX combination and eighty percent in the gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel combination — both consistent with the underlying chemotherapy safety profile. The toxicity increment from adding zoldonrasib on top is small. That is what makes the eighty-two percent response rate a plausibly-repeatable number rather than a small-sample cherry-pick — the compound is not driving the toxicity, so its efficacy signal is not being confounded by patient selection through tolerability. The zoldonrasib-plus-daraxonrasib doublet is a completely different bet: a chemotherapy-free combination of two RAS-on inhibitors, one G-twelve-D-selective and one pan-K-RAS. Second-line objective response rate fifty percent and median progression-free survival nine-point-six months, in patients who have already progressed on chemotherapy, is a striking result for a chemo-free approach. Grade-three-plus treatment-related adverse events were thirty-five percent — much lower than the chemotherapy combinations. If the doublet holds up at higher patient numbers, RevMed has a chemotherapy-free first-line path to compare against gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel, which is the RASolute three-oh-nine trial design.

Fifth, the Phase three program and regulatory posture. RASolute three-oh-five is the ongoing Phase three trial testing zoldonrasib plus chemotherapy versus placebo plus chemotherapy in first-line RAS G-twelve-D metastatic pancreatic cancer — currently enrolling. RASolute three-oh-nine is a planned Phase three testing zoldonrasib plus daraxonrasib versus gemcitabine plus nab-paclitaxel in first-line RAS G-twelve-D metastatic pancreatic cancer. The two trials cover the two strategic paths — chemotherapy-augmentation and chemotherapy-replacement. First readouts likely twenty twenty-eight for three-oh-five, later for three-oh-nine given later start. On regulatory posture, RevMed has flagged that accelerated approval is on the table if the Phase one-two data are compelling and Phase three interim reads confirm the direction. An eighty-two percent response rate is precisely the kind of number that motivates an accelerated-approval conversation, and F-D-A has historically been willing to move fast on drugs in cancers with no approved targeted therapy and dismal current outcomes.

Sixth, competitive and platform read. On direct competitors — Mirati, now inside Bristol Myers Squibb after the twenty twenty-four acquisition, has MRTX-eleven-thirty-three, the non-covalent G-twelve-D inhibitor, and next-generation programs. Bristol Myers has had a hard time getting MRTX-eleven-thirty-three to a competitive clinical profile — formulation constraints and pharmacokinetic limits have held it back. Roche's divarasib — yesterday's spotlight — is G-twelve-C-only, so it does not touch this indication directly. Roche has separate pan-K-RAS and pan-RAS programs at earlier stages that will eventually enter the G-twelve-D space, but they are Phase one at best right now, so RevMed has a two-to-three-year lead on that front. Amgen's next-generation programs are earlier still. On the A-I-in-drug-discovery peer set — Xaira, Iambic, Isomorphic Labs, Terray, Absci — K-RAS remains the paradigmatic example of structure-guided medicinal chemistry producing iterative wins, and now RevMed's tri-complex mechanism adds a second structural approach — cyclophilin-mediated ternary complex formation on the on-state — that neither the covalent-trap G-twelve-C class nor the non-covalent switch-two-pocket G-twelve-D class had used. Zoldonrasib was not A-I-designed; it emerged from cyclophilin biology and structure-guided compound design in the same medicinal-chemistry lineage as sotorasib and divarasib. Whether A-I-native design can produce a K-RAS G-twelve-D compound that matches or exceeds zoldonrasib on a matched-condition test is the direct benchmark question for the A-I-native class over the next thirty-six months. Isomorphic Labs and Xaira have both flagged K-RAS as a target family; the eighty-two percent front-line response rate is the new number to beat.

Bottom line. Revolution Medicines presented Phase one-two data Thursday at ESMO GI showing zoldonrasib — the oral K-RAS G-twelve-D-selective RAS-on inhibitor — delivering an eighty-two percent objective response rate in first-line metastatic pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma combined with modified FOLFIRINOX, plus a fifty percent second-line objective response rate and a nine-point-six-month median progression-free survival in a chemotherapy-free doublet with daraxonrasib. This is the biggest single data point in K-RAS G-twelve-D pancreatic cancer to date, and it extends the K-RAS-generation-two win from yesterday's Roche divarasib spotlight into the forty-percent slice of K-RAS-mutant pancreatic cancer that is G-twelve-D. RASolute three-oh-five in first-line pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma is enrolling; RASolute three-oh-nine is planned. For Bristol Myers, the MRTX-eleven-thirty-three franchise moves from behind to further behind. For Roche, the divarasib win in G-twelve-C non-small-cell lung cancer is real but zoldonrasib in G-twelve-D pancreatic cancer opens a second front where the Krascendo program does not compete. For the A-I-in-drug-discovery peer set — Xaira, Iambic, Isomorphic Labs — zoldonrasib is the new benchmark in G-twelve-D and the direct-substitution test question for structure-guided A-I-native design remains open. For Foresite and Calibr, pancreatic cancer with a targeted-therapy backbone is now a category and not a hypothesis — the Calibr small-molecule oncology programs share the covalent-and-structural-design discipline that produced this result, and RevMed's tri-complex mechanism is a new template worth adding to the medicinal-chemistry playbook. Next catalysts. One, RevMed presents mature Phase one-two data on both combinations at a fall oncology congress with more patient follow-up. Two, RASolute three-oh-five interim analysis timing — RevMed has not disclosed. Three, Bristol Myers responds with an MRTX-eleven-thirty-three data update or a next-generation G-twelve-D compound Phase one entry. Four, an A-I-native peer — Isomorphic Labs, Xaira, or Iambic — announces a K-RAS G-twelve-D program entering the clinic as the direct-substitution test of A-I-driven design against the zoldonrasib benchmark.
